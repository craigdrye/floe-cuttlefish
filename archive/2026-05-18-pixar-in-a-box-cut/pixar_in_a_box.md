# Pixar in a Box
**ID**: `pixarInABox` - **Discipline**: Arts/Maths - **Year**: 11 (ages 15-16)

## Course Aim
Explore how animated films are built through the combined work of story artists, modelers, animators, technical directors, simulation artists, lighting teams, and reviewers. Students use the Pixar in a Box approach to connect creative choices with mathematics, physics, computer science, visual design, and revision.

## Course Design Notes
Keep the course studio-based, production-oriented, and accessible to students who may identify as artists, writers, programmers, designers, or problem solvers. Every chapter should connect a creative goal to a technical method: a character needs a clear want, a curve needs control points, a motion needs timing, a material needs light behavior, and a shot needs critique. Route questions here when they involve animation storytelling, storyboards, character design, modeling, curves, rigging, simulation, rendering, lighting, production pipelines, critique, or iteration.

## Chapter 1: Story Seeds, Characters, and Emotional Stakes
- **Key concepts**: premise, protagonist, want, need, obstacle, theme, logline, pitch, emotional arc, conflict, audience expectation, and story spine.
- **Practice questions**: What does the character want on the surface? What might the character need underneath? How does one choice reveal personality, conflict, or theme?
- **Student output**: A one-page story seed with a logline, character want and need, central obstacle, and two possible ending directions.

## Chapter 2: Storyboards, Visual Language, and the Story Reel
- **Key concepts**: storyboard panel, shot, beat, staging, composition, silhouette, camera angle, continuity, visual contrast, color script, timing, and story reel.
- **Practice questions**: Which frame gives the audience the clearest information? How do camera angle, color, and composition change the emotion of a scene? What panel can be cut without losing the beat?
- **Student output**: A storyboard sequence of 8 to 12 panels with notes on staging, camera position, emotion, and one revision after peer critique.

## Chapter 3: Shape, Modeling, and Virtual Sets
- **Key concepts**: polygon, mesh, vertex, edge, face, silhouette, scale, proportion, subdivision, topology, environment model, reference image, and production constraint.
- **Practice questions**: How can a complex object be built from simpler shapes? Where does topology need to support bending or close-up detail? What makes a set readable from the camera's point of view?
- **Student output**: A low-poly model plan or paper prototype for a character prop or set piece, including reference sketches, shape breakdowns, and notes on what details matter most.

## Chapter 4: Curves, Interpolation, and Motion Paths
- **Key concepts**: coordinate plane, function, parabola, cubic curve, Bezier curve, control point, tangent, interpolation, weighted average, easing, and motion path.
- **Practice questions**: How do control points change the shape of a curve? Where should motion speed up or slow down? How can a weighted average create an in-between position?
- **Student output**: A curve study showing at least three motion paths or shape outlines, with annotations explaining the mathematical choices and the intended visual effect.

## Chapter 5: Animation Principles, Keyframes, and Rigging
- **Key concepts**: keyframe, in-between, pose, timing, spacing, squash and stretch, anticipation, follow-through, overlapping action, rig, joint, constraint, forward kinematics, and inverse kinematics.
- **Practice questions**: Which poses are essential for the action to read? How does spacing affect weight and speed? When would inverse kinematics make a character easier to animate?
- **Student output**: A short animation plan for a bouncing ball, walk, jump, or reach, including key poses, timing notes, and a critique of how believable the motion feels.

## Chapter 6: Simulation, Effects, and Physical Believability
- **Key concepts**: simulation, particle system, force, gravity, friction, collision, spring, constraint, cloth, hair, fluid, smoke, procedural rule, and art direction.
- **Practice questions**: Which variables control the look of cloth, hair, fire, or water? When should a simulation be physically accurate, and when should it be art-directed? What can go wrong when collisions are not handled well?
- **Student output**: An effects design brief for one animated scene, naming the forces, constraints, variables, references, and artistic goal behind the simulation.

## Chapter 7: Materials, Lighting, Cameras, and Rendering
- **Key concepts**: virtual camera, focal length, perspective, depth of field, light source, shadow, reflection, refraction, shader, texture, material, ray tracing, render time, and visual mood.
- **Practice questions**: What lighting choice tells the audience where to look? How should plastic, metal, skin, glass, or fabric respond to light? Why can a single finished frame take so much computation?
- **Student output**: A lighting and materials plan for one storyboard frame, with a before-and-after explanation of how the shot's clarity and emotion changed.

## Chapter 8: Pipeline, Review, Iteration, and Final Pitch
- **Key concepts**: production pipeline, department handoff, asset, version, dailies, note, constraint, iteration, polish, audience feedback, revision log, and final pitch.
- **Practice questions**: What feedback is actionable rather than vague? Which problem belongs to story, animation, lighting, or simulation? How can a team revise without losing the original intent?
- **Student output**: A production packet that combines story, design, technical notes, critique history, and a final pitch for a short animated moment.

## Capstone
Students design a short animated scene or proof-of-concept package. The final product should include a story premise, storyboard or animatic plan, character or set design, at least one mathematical or scientific technique used intentionally, a lighting or effects plan, critique notes, and a revision reflection explaining how feedback improved the work.

## Assessment Ideas
- Story pitches and storyboard checks graded on clarity, emotional stakes, visual communication, and revision.
- Technical mini-tasks on curves, interpolation, motion paths, keyframes, simulation variables, lighting, and rendering vocabulary.
- Studio critiques assessed for specificity, evidence, kindness, and the ability to turn notes into better choices.
- Capstone rubric covering creative coherence, technical understanding, production documentation, iteration, and final presentation.
