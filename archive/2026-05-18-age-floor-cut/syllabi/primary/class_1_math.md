# Class 1 Math
**ID**: `col-class-1-math` | **Discipline**: Maths | **Year**: 1 (ages 5-6)
**Frameworks**: UK KS1 National Curriculum, US Common Core Grade 1, ACARA v9 Year 1, India NCERT Class 1

## Course Aim
Build early mathematical confidence through counting, talking, moving, drawing, sorting, comparing, and playing with real objects. Children learn that numbers describe quantities, shapes have properties, measures help us compare the world, and word problems can be acted out before they are written down.

## Course Design Notes
Class 1 maths should feel concrete, visual, and safe to try. Lessons begin with manipulatives: counters, cubes, bead strings, ten frames, number cards, blocks, clocks, coins, shape tiles, classroom objects, and drawings. Each lesson uses short loops: notice, build it, say it, draw it, write it, check it. Number talk is central, with children explaining "how I know" in simple language before speed is expected.

## Chapter 1: Counting, Matching, and Number Talk
- **Key concepts**: count, quantity, one-to-one match, more, fewer, equal, before, after.
- **Practice activities**: count classroom objects; match number cards to sets; count forward and backward from different starting points; play "show me" with fingers, dots, and counters.
- **Teacher prompts**: How did you keep track? What number comes next? Is this group more, fewer, or equal?
- **Work product**: a number talk page showing one chosen number with objects, dots, tally marks, a word, and a numeral.

## Chapter 2: Numbers to 100 and Place Value
- **Key concepts**: numeral, number word, tens, ones, bundle, digit, one more, one less.
- **Practice activities**: build teen numbers with ten frames; bundle sticks into tens; make two-digit numbers with base-ten blocks; order number cards to 100.
- **Teacher prompts**: How many tens and ones can you see? What changes when we add one more? Which number is greater, and how do you know?
- **Work product**: a place value mat for three numbers, using blocks, drawings, numerals, and tens-and-ones labels.

## Chapter 3: Addition as Joining
- **Key concepts**: add, join, altogether, total, count on, number bond, equals.
- **Practice activities**: join two groups of counters; use ten frames for bonds to 10 and 20; roll dice and add with cubes; act out addition stories.
- **Teacher prompts**: Which group will you start with? Can a number bond help? What does the equals sign show?
- **Work product**: an addition strategy strip showing one problem with counters, a drawing, a number sentence, and an answer sentence.

## Chapter 4: Subtraction as Taking Away and Comparing
- **Key concepts**: subtract, take away, left, difference, fewer, count back, compare.
- **Practice activities**: remove counters from a set; jump backward on a number line; compare two towers; match subtraction facts to pictures.
- **Teacher prompts**: Are we taking away or comparing? How many are left? Could counting back or counting up help?
- **Work product**: a subtraction story drawing with objects crossed out or compared, a number sentence, and a check.

## Chapter 5: Shape, Position, and Drawing
- **Key concepts**: circle, triangle, square, rectangle, cube, cuboid, sphere, cylinder, cone, above, below, beside, between.
- **Practice activities**: sort 2D and 3D shapes; build shapes with sticks and clay; find shapes in the room; give partner position clues on a simple map.
- **Teacher prompts**: What makes this shape a triangle? Can it turn and still be the same shape? Where is it compared with the other object?
- **Work product**: a shape hunt sheet with drawings, labels, and one position sentence.

## Chapter 6: Measurement Through Comparing
- **Key concepts**: longer, shorter, taller, heavier, lighter, full, empty, estimate, measure, unit.
- **Practice activities**: compare lengths with cubes or paperclips; balance objects on scales; order containers by capacity; estimate before checking.
- **Teacher prompts**: What could we use as a fair unit? Which is longer or heavier? Was your estimate close?
- **Work product**: a comparison record with three classroom objects, an estimate, a non-standard measure, and a sentence.

## Chapter 7: Time, Money, and Everyday Maths
- **Key concepts**: day, week, month, o'clock, half past, coin, note, price, total.
- **Practice activities**: sequence daily routines; make times on teaching clocks; identify coins and notes; play a small shop game with simple totals.
- **Teacher prompts**: What happens before lunch? Where should the hour hand point? Which coins could make this amount?
- **Work product**: an everyday maths strip showing one time, one coin amount, and one routine or shop problem.

## Chapter 8: Word Problems, Games, and Checking
- **Key concepts**: story, question, information, model, answer, check, explain, reasonable.
- **Practice activities**: act out word problems with objects; draw quick pictures; choose addition or subtraction; play board games that require counting on and back.
- **Teacher prompts**: What is happening in the story? What do we need to find out? Does the answer make sense?
- **Work product**: a solved word-problem card with a drawing, number sentence, answer sentence, and one check.

## Capstone
Create a "Maths Around Our Classroom" trail. Children design five small stations: count a set, build a two-digit number, solve an addition or subtraction story, identify shapes and positions, and compare or measure an object. Each station includes a manipulative model, a drawing, a number sentence or label, and a short explanation to a partner.

## Assessment Ideas
- Observe whether children count accurately with one-to-one matching and can compare groups using more, fewer, and equal.
- Use short interviews to hear place-value language for tens and ones, especially teen numbers and two-digit numbers.
- Review addition and subtraction work for matching objects, drawings, number sentences, and answer sentences.
- Check shape and measurement understanding through practical sorting, building, estimating, and comparing tasks.
- Grade the capstone on clear models, sensible drawings, correct language, and confident mathematical talk rather than speed alone.
