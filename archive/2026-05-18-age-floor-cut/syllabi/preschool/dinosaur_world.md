# Dinosaur World (Pre-school, ages 3-5)
**ID**: `dinosPreschool` | **Status**: playable

## Course Aim
Invite young children into dinosaur science through pretend digs, footprints, fossils, size play, sorting games, museum displays, stories, songs, and gentle wondering about long-ago life. The course builds early ideas about evidence, animal features, plant eaters and meat eaters, tracks, bones, habitats, change over time, and how people learn from fossils.

## Course Design Notes
This is a playful, concrete preschool dinosaur course. Children do not need to memorize long names or geologic periods. Adults support learning with toy dinosaurs, picture books, sand trays, clay fossils, footprint paths, measuring strings, magnifiers, dress-up tools, museum labels, and repeated talk prompts. "Long ago" is treated gently as a story of the Earth before people, with fossils as clues rather than scary remains. Activities avoid exam tone and invite children to point, sort, stomp, build, brush, compare, draw, and tell an adult what they notice. Each chapter includes a small science idea, a pretend-play routine, and a class artifact.

## Chapter 1: Welcome to Dinosaur World
**Circle-time invitations**
- Share dinosaur toys, photos, silhouettes, storybooks, and a class "long ago" blanket or dig mat.
- Ask: "What do you notice?", "How might this dinosaur move?", and "What do you wonder?"
- Introduce dinosaurs as animals that lived a very, very long time ago, before people.

**Pretend journey**
- Begin the paleontologist ritual: put on pretend field hats, choose careful tools, and step softly into the dig site.

**Playful output**
- Start a Dinosaur Wonder Wall with child drawings, adult-scribed questions, favorite dinosaur pictures, and noticing words.

## Chapter 2: Big, Small, Tall, and Tiny
**Circle-time invitations**
- Compare dinosaur sizes with blocks, string, body poses, toy animals, and familiar objects such as a chair, child, door, or bus picture.
- Ask: "Which is taller?", "Which is longer?", and "Can a dinosaur be tiny?"
- Include small dinosaurs as well as giant ones so children meet variety.

**Tiny investigation**
- Line up toy dinosaurs by size, then make footprint paths with big, medium, and small paper prints.

**Playful output**
- Create a Dino Size Parade strip with child-made prints ordered from tiny to enormous.

## Chapter 3: Teeth, Food, and Sorting
**Circle-time invitations**
- Look at simple pictures of flat teeth, sharp teeth, leaves, fruit, meat, and pretend dinosaur meals.
- Ask: "What might a plant eater need?", "What might a meat eater need?", and "How can teeth be clues?"
- Keep terms friendly: plant eater, meat eater, and sometimes both.

**Tiny investigation**
- Sort dinosaur picture cards by clues such as long neck, sharp teeth, beak, claws, flat teeth, or leaf-eating pose.

**Playful output**
- Build a Dino Cafe sorting mat with sections for leafy snacks, crunchy plants, and meat-eater clues.

## Chapter 4: Feet, Tracks, and Movement
**Circle-time invitations**
- Compare footprint shapes: three-toed tracks, round heavy tracks, claw marks, bird tracks, and child shoe prints.
- Ask: "Who might have made this mark?", "Was it walking or running?", and "What does your foot leave behind?"
- Move safely like stompers, tiptoers, slow giants, quick runners, and quiet nest watchers.

**Tiny investigation**
- Press toy dinosaur feet, shoes, and hands into sand, clay, or play dough to see what marks are left.

**Playful output**
- Make a Track Detective Trail across paper or fabric with labels for big, tiny, three toes, claws, slow, and fast.

## Chapter 5: Fossils Are Clues
**Circle-time invitations**
- Show pictures or replicas of bones, shells, leaves, teeth, footprints, and eggs preserved as fossils.
- Ask: "What clue can we see?", "What tool should we use gently?", and "Who studies these clues?"
- Introduce paleontologists as careful dinosaur detectives who study fossils.

**Pretend dig**
- Brush sand away from buried toy bones, laminated fossil pictures, shells, or clay impressions using soft brushes and trays.

**Playful output**
- Create a Fossil Clue Gallery with rubbings, clay fossils, photos from the dig, and adult-scribed child observations.

## Chapter 6: Nests, Eggs, and Dinosaur Homes
**Circle-time invitations**
- Look at dinosaur nest pictures, egg shapes, leaves, rocks, muddy places, forests, plains, and water-edge habitats.
- Ask: "Where might this dinosaur feel safe?", "What would a baby need?", and "What could we build?"
- Compare dinosaur care carefully with familiar animal care, without overpromising what scientists know.

**Tiny investigation**
- Build pretend habitats with blocks, fabric, leaves, rocks, nests, water trays, trees, and toy dinosaurs.

**Playful output**
- Make a Dinosaur Habitat Mat with spaces for food, water, shelter, eggs, tracks, and safe watching.

## Chapter 7: Long Ago Earth
**Circle-time invitations**
- Use a picture sequence to show today, when grandparents were little, before people, and dinosaur time as "very, very long ago."
- Ask: "Are dinosaurs alive today?", "What clues are left?", and "What animals remind us of dinosaurs?"
- Share that birds are related to dinosaurs, and that many ancient animals are known from fossils.

**Tiny model**
- Walk a simple classroom timeline with stepping stones or mats: now, long ago, very long ago, dinosaur time.

**Playful output**
- Create a Long Ago Path with drawings for present-day animals, fossils, volcanoes, plants, dinosaurs, and museum clues.

## Chapter 8: Dinosaur Museum Play
**Circle-time invitations**
- Visit a classroom "museum" setup with display trays, labels, careful-looking rules, tickets, guide badges, and a gift-shop drawing table.
- Ask: "What should visitors see first?", "What label can we make?", and "How do we care for museum things?"
- Practice museum words: display, label, clue, fossil, visitor, guide, careful, and question.

**Pretend journey**
- Children take turns as museum guides, fossil preparers, ticket helpers, label makers, and visitors.

**Playful output**
- Prepare a class Dinosaur Museum with selected artifacts from the whole course.

## Capstone
Children host a "Tiny Dinosaur Museum and Dig Day." The room may include the Dinosaur Wonder Wall, Dino Size Parade, Dino Cafe sorting mat, Track Detective Trail, Fossil Clue Gallery, Dinosaur Habitat Mat, Long Ago Path, and museum displays. Children choose jobs such as dig helper, track detective, size sorter, habitat builder, fossil artist, museum guide, or label maker, then show a trusted adult or another small group one favorite clue and one favorite dinosaur idea.

## Assessment Ideas
- Adult observation notes on noticing, comparing size, sorting by visible clues, using gentle dig tools, making tracks, talking about long ago, and cooperating in museum play.
- Photo portfolio of footprint paths, clay fossils, pretend digs, size comparisons, habitat builds, sorting mats, timeline walks, and museum displays.
- Adult-scribed child voice records using stems such as "I found...", "This track looks...", "This dinosaur might...", "A fossil is...", and "Long ago means..."
- Small-group conversation during digging, building, sorting, story, and museum play rather than quizzes or formal naming tests.
- Family connection prompt: invite caregivers to share a dinosaur book, visit a museum website or local display, make a footprint trail, or notice birds as living animals with dinosaur connections.
