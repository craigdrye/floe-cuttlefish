# Big Questions: Year 6
**ID**: `philosophyYear6` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 6 (ages 10–11)

## Course Philosophy
Year 6 philosophy is the culmination of primary-level critical thinking. Students are challenged to examine their own biases, understand complex political and ethical systems, and apply logical frameworks to difficult, real-world problems. The focus is on robust debate and understanding the difference between a sound argument and a persuasive, but flawed, one.

## Module 1: Logic & Arguments (20%)
- What makes a good argument? (Premises and Conclusions)
- Identifying common logical fallacies:
  - *Ad hominem* (attacking the person instead of the argument)
  - *Straw man* (misrepresenting the argument to make it easier to attack)
  - *Slippery slope* (assuming one small step will lead to an extreme outcome)
- The difference between correlation and causation
- Confirmation bias: Why do we only notice evidence that supports what we already believe?
- **Questions**: Identifying logical fallacies in text, distinguishing correlation from causation, evaluating argument soundness

## Module 2: Ethics & Action (Utilitarianism vs Deontology) (20%)
- Utilitarianism (Jeremy Bentham/John Stuart Mill): "The greatest good for the greatest number." When does this work? When does it fail?
- Deontology (Immanuel Kant): Duty and rules. "Is it ever okay to lie, even to save someone?" (The Categorical Imperative)
- The Trolley Problem: Examining active vs. passive harm
- Virtue Ethics (Aristotle): Focusing on being a good person rather than following strict rules
- **Questions**: Applying ethical frameworks to dilemmas, analyzing the trolley problem

## Module 3: Political Philosophy & The State (20%)
- Why do we have governments? (Thomas Hobbes' "Leviathan" vs. John Locke's "Social Contract")
- What is freedom? (Negative freedom: no one stopping you vs. Positive freedom: having the resources to do what you want)
- Censorship and free speech: Should people be allowed to say things that are offensive or dangerous? Where is the line?
- Meritocracy: Is it fair that people who work harder get more? What if they had a head start?
- **Questions**: Analyzing the social contract, evaluating free speech limits, meritocracy vs equity scenarios

## Module 4: The Philosophy of Mind & AI (20%)
- What is consciousness? How do you know anyone else is conscious? (The Problem of Other Minds)
- The Turing Test: If a computer can trick you into thinking it's human, is it thinking?
- John Searle's "Chinese Room" thought experiment (syntax vs. semantics)
- Will AI ever be able to feel emotion or be creative? Should we treat advanced AI with respect?
- **Questions**: Turing Test analysis, evaluating AI consciousness, ethics of artificial intelligence

## Module 5: Meaning & The Good Life (20%)
- What makes a good life? Is it happiness, achievement, helping others, or something else?
- Hedonism: Is pleasure the only thing that matters? (Robert Nozick's "Experience Machine" thought experiment)
- Existentialism (Sartre): "Existence precedes essence." Do we create our own meaning in a universe without rules?
- How does the awareness of mortality (death) affect how we live?
- **Questions**: Analyzing the "Experience Machine", evaluating what constitutes a meaningful life

## Stretch Zone
- Introduction to Nihilism and Absurdism (Camus' Myth of Sisyphus)
- Advanced logic: Modus ponens and Modus tollens
- Non-western philosophy: Introduction to Taoism (wu wei/non-action) or Ubuntu (I am because we are)
