# Big Questions: Year 6
**ID**: `philosophyYear6` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Year**: 6 (ages 10-11)

## Course Aim
Prepare Year 6 students to reason with maturity, humility, and courage as they move toward secondary school. Students learn to analyse arguments, recognise bias and persuasion, compare ethical frameworks, debate questions about power and justice, and express well-supported views on identity, media, technology, rights, environment, and the good life without reducing complex issues to slogans.

## Course Design Notes
Year 6 philosophy should give pupils more formal tools while keeping discussion grounded in age-appropriate scenarios. Lessons can draw on P4C enquiry, SAPERE-style community norms, simple logic diagrams, short case studies, children's rights materials, media literacy routines, citizenship themes, and reflective writing. Controversial topics should be framed through principles, evidence, rights, responsibilities, and competing goods rather than party politics or adult culture-war positions. Students should leave with habits of civil disagreement: define terms, ask for reasons, test examples, consider who is affected, and revise claims when the argument demands it.

## Chapter 1: Logic, Claims, and Evidence
- **Key concepts**: claim, premise, conclusion, evidence, validity, soundness, assumption, example, counterexample.
- **Practice questions**: What makes an argument strong? Can a conclusion be persuasive but poorly supported? What hidden assumptions are inside everyday arguments? How can a single counterexample change a claim?
- **Student output**: An argument clinic sheet that identifies a claim, premises, evidence, one assumption, one possible counterexample, and a revised stronger version.

## Chapter 2: Bias, Fallacies, and Fair Debate
- **Key concepts**: bias, confirmation bias, ad hominem, straw man, slippery slope, correlation, causation, charity, rebuttal.
- **Practice questions**: Why do people notice evidence that supports what they already think? How can we spot a weak attack on a person instead of an idea? When does a warning about consequences become a slippery slope? How can we represent an opponent's view fairly?
- **Student output**: A fallacy and fairness notebook page with three labelled examples, corrected versions, and one sentence showing the strongest fair version of an opposing view.

## Chapter 3: Ethical Frameworks and Difficult Choices
- **Key concepts**: consequence, duty, virtue, harm, intention, responsibility, trade-off, principle, moral dilemma.
- **Practice questions**: Should we judge actions by outcomes, rules, character, or all three? Is it ever right to lie to prevent harm? Does doing nothing make us responsible when we could have helped? Can the same action be brave in one situation and reckless in another?
- **Student output**: An ethics comparison table applying consequence-based, duty-based, and virtue-based reasoning to one classroom-safe dilemma, followed by a justified judgement.

## Chapter 4: Power, Democracy, and the Common Good
- **Key concepts**: power, authority, democracy, representation, consent, freedom, security, common good, minority voice.
- **Practice questions**: Why do communities need rules and leaders? What makes authority legitimate? How should a class or society protect both majority decisions and minority voices? When can freedom be limited to protect others?
- **Student output**: A democratic decision design for a school issue, explaining who gets a voice, how evidence is gathered, how the decision is made, and how fairness is protected.

## Chapter 5: Rights, Justice, and Repair
- **Key concepts**: human rights, children's rights, justice, punishment, restoration, dignity, equality, equity, accountability.
- **Practice questions**: Are rights invented, discovered, or agreed? What should happen after someone causes harm? When is punishment fair, and when is repair more useful? How can a system be unfair even when no single person means to be unfair?
- **Student output**: A justice response plan comparing punishment, restoration, prevention, and support in a realistic school or community scenario.

## Chapter 6: Media, Information, and Influence
- **Key concepts**: source, credibility, misinformation, disinformation, algorithm, framing, audience, verification, digital responsibility.
- **Practice questions**: How do platforms decide what we see? Why might two reports describe the same event differently? What is the difference between being misled by mistake and spreading falsehood on purpose? What should responsible citizens do before sharing information?
- **Student output**: A media investigation log with a focused question, source comparison, credibility checks, fact/opinion labels, and a careful conclusion about what can and cannot be known.

## Chapter 7: Mind, AI, and Personhood
- **Key concepts**: consciousness, mind, personhood, intelligence, emotion, creativity, Turing test, respect, responsibility.
- **Practice questions**: How do we know other minds exist? If a machine speaks like a person, is it thinking like a person? Can something be intelligent without feelings? What responsibilities do humans have when they build powerful tools?
- **Student output**: A personhood thought-experiment response that gives criteria for personhood, tests them against humans, animals, and AI, and notes one problem with the criteria.

## Chapter 8: Meaning, Identity, and the Good Life
- **Key concepts**: meaning, happiness, purpose, achievement, service, friendship, identity, mortality, reflection, flourishing.
- **Practice questions**: What makes a life go well? Is happiness enough, or do challenge, responsibility, and contribution matter too? How do communities shape what people value? Can people create meaning when circumstances are difficult?
- **Student output**: A good-life essay plan or speech outline with a clear thesis, three values, examples, a counterpoint, and a personal reflection written at an appropriate level of privacy.

## Capstone
Create a "Year 6 Big Questions Symposium." Students choose a substantial but classroom-safe enquiry question, research background ideas from age-appropriate sources, prepare an argument map, take part in a chaired seminar or structured debate, respond to questions from peers, and submit a final position paper or presentation that includes evidence, ethical reasoning, a counterargument, and a reflection on how their thinking changed.

## Assessment Ideas
- Assess logic work through argument maps that separate claims, reasons, evidence, assumptions, objections, and conclusions.
- Use debate observation notes to evaluate listening, fair representation of opposing views, respectful rebuttal, and use of examples.
- Review ethics tasks for accurate comparison of consequences, duties, virtues, rights, responsibilities, and repair.
- Evaluate media literacy work for source checking, awareness of framing and algorithms, fact/opinion distinction, and appropriate caution under uncertainty.
- Grade the symposium on question focus, preparation, evidence quality, balance, oral clarity, response to challenge, and reflective maturity.

## Research Notes
- Philosophy for Children and P4C research-informed practice supports enquiry communities where pupils generate questions, give reasons, listen carefully, and build shared understanding: https://www.sapere.org.uk/
- CASEL's social and emotional learning framework aligns with philosophical discussion through self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making: https://casel.org/fundamentals-of-sel/
- The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child gives a shared, non-partisan reference point for discussing participation, protection, provision, dignity, and responsibilities: https://www.unicef.org/child-rights-convention
- UNESCO media and information literacy guidance supports teaching source evaluation, media influence, responsible sharing, and informed participation: https://www.unesco.org/en/media-information-literacy
- Primary citizenship and PSHE frameworks support age-appropriate work on democracy, rules, rights, responsibilities, respectful relationships, online safety, wellbeing, and community participation.
