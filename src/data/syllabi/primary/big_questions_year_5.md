# Big Questions: Year 5
**ID**: `philosophyYear5` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Year**: 5 (ages 9-10)

## Course Aim
Develop confident upper-primary thinkers who can ask precise philosophical questions, listen to competing viewpoints, and build fair arguments about identity, justice, technology, media, the environment, and global responsibility. Students learn to move beyond "I think" by giving reasons, testing examples, noticing assumptions, and revising their views when discussion reveals stronger evidence or a better principle.

## Course Design Notes
Year 5 philosophy should feel ambitious but classroom-safe: real enough to matter, structured enough to remain respectful, and carefully balanced so pupils can examine ideas without being pushed toward partisan conclusions. Lessons use Philosophy for Children enquiry circles, short stimulus texts, stories, news-like scenarios, rights cards, image prompts, and simple argument maps. Students should practise speaking with evidence, disagreeing without personal attack, separating a person from an idea, and recognising that fairness sometimes involves competing values rather than one obvious answer.

## Chapter 1: Identity, Memory, and Change
- **Key concepts**: identity, self, memory, body, personality, continuity, change, belonging, perspective.
- **Practice questions**: What makes you the same person over time? If someone changes their interests, friends, or beliefs, are they still the same self? How much do family, culture, school, and community shape who we become? Can a person choose who they are?
- **Student output**: An identity map and short reasoning paragraph explaining which feature of identity matters most, using at least one example and one possible objection.

## Chapter 2: Fairness, Equality, and Justice
- **Key concepts**: fairness, equality, equity, need, desert, rule, law, justice, responsibility.
- **Practice questions**: Is treating everyone the same always fair? When should people get extra help? What is the difference between a school rule, a law, and a moral duty? Can a rule be fair for a group but unfair to one person?
- **Student output**: A fairness case file comparing two possible decisions in a classroom, sports, or community scenario, with reasons for and against each decision.

## Chapter 3: Rights and Responsibilities
- **Key concepts**: right, responsibility, dignity, protection, participation, voice, safety, education, play.
- **Practice questions**: What rights should every child have? Do rights always come with responsibilities? When should adults make decisions for children, and when should children have a say? Can two rights ever conflict?
- **Student output**: A children's rights discussion card linking one right from the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child to a realistic responsibility and a classroom example.

## Chapter 4: Truth, Evidence, and Reliable Sources
- **Key concepts**: truth, belief, evidence, source, reliability, bias, fact, opinion, uncertainty, correction.
- **Practice questions**: Is seeing always believing? How can we know something happened if we were not there? What makes a source trustworthy? When should we change our minds?
- **Student output**: A source-checking sheet that compares two accounts of the same event, labels facts and opinions, and explains which source seems more reliable and why.

## Chapter 5: Media, Persuasion, and Online Choices
- **Key concepts**: media, message, audience, persuasion, algorithm, privacy, rumour, image, influence, digital citizenship.
- **Practice questions**: How do headlines, images, and comments shape what we believe? Is sharing a rumour ever harmful if we are not sure it is false? Should privacy be protected even when people say they have "nothing to hide"? How can online spaces be both free and safe?
- **Student output**: A media reflection poster or slide analysing one age-appropriate message for audience, purpose, evidence, emotional appeal, and a responsible response.

## Chapter 6: Technology, AI, and Human Choices
- **Key concepts**: technology, tool, artificial intelligence, automation, creativity, privacy, benefit, risk, responsibility.
- **Practice questions**: Is a smart machine really thinking or only following instructions? Who is responsible when technology causes harm? Should robots or AI systems ever have rights? How can technology help people without replacing human judgement?
- **Student output**: A balanced technology briefing with one possible benefit, one possible risk, one unanswered question, and one rule that would make the technology fairer or safer.

## Chapter 7: Environment, Resources, and Future People
- **Key concepts**: environment, resource, stewardship, sustainability, trade-off, future generation, common good, local action.
- **Practice questions**: Who owns air, water, forests, oceans, and space? Do people today have duties to people who are not born yet? How should communities balance jobs, cost, convenience, and environmental care? Does one person's action matter?
- **Student output**: An environmental dilemma grid showing stakeholders, possible choices, short-term effects, long-term effects, and a justified recommendation.

## Chapter 8: Dialogue, Debate, and Better Arguments
- **Key concepts**: claim, reason, example, evidence, counterargument, assumption, respectful challenge, conclusion, revision.
- **Practice questions**: What makes an argument strong rather than just loud? How can we challenge an idea without attacking a person? What should we do when two good values point in different directions? When is it wise to change your mind?
- **Student output**: A revised argument map with a claim, two reasons, evidence or examples, a counterargument, and a final reflection on how peer discussion improved the argument.

## Capstone
Create a "Big Questions Enquiry Portfolio." Students choose a classroom-safe question about fairness, identity, media, technology, rights, or the environment; gather stimulus material from stories, images, data, or age-appropriate sources; lead a short enquiry discussion; and submit an argument map, a reflection on changed thinking, and a final spoken or written position that recognises at least one serious counterargument.

## Assessment Ideas
- Assess discussion habits through listening, turn-taking, building on others' ideas, asking clarifying questions, and disagreeing respectfully.
- Review argument maps for clear claims, relevant reasons, examples, counterarguments, and willingness to revise.
- Use short written responses to check whether students can distinguish fairness, equality, rights, responsibilities, facts, opinions, and values.
- Evaluate media and technology tasks for source awareness, balanced reasoning, privacy language, and practical judgement.
- Grade the capstone on question quality, enquiry preparation, use of evidence, balance of viewpoints, clarity of conclusion, and reflection on the discussion process.
