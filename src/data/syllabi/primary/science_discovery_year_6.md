# Science Discovery: Year 6
**ID**: `scienceYear6` - **Discipline**: Science - **Year**: 6 (ages 10-11)
**Frameworks**: UK KS2 Science Year 6, US NGSS Grade 6, ACARA Science Year 6

## Course Aim
Prepare learners for the transition from primary science into more formal secondary science by strengthening classification, systems thinking, careful modelling, data interpretation, and evidence-based argument. Children investigate classification, microorganisms, the circulatory system, health, evolution and inheritance, light, electricity, and independent enquiry through practical work, diagrams, models, research, and precise scientific language.

## Course Design Notes
Year 6 science should balance ambitious ideas with concrete experience. Learners can now justify classification choices, interpret secondary sources, evaluate models, use standard circuit symbols, draw light-ray diagrams, compare data sets, and explain how evidence supports a scientific claim. Lessons should include structured practical investigations, fieldwork or digital data where useful, sensitive body science, discussion of historic scientists, and explicit preparation for secondary-school expectations.

## Chapter 1: Classification Systems and Keys
**Key concepts**: organism, classify, characteristic, vertebrate, invertebrate, plant, animal, fungus, microorganism, species, genus, Linnaean system, branching key.

**Practice questions**
- Which observable characteristics are useful for classifying living things?
- How can a branching key help another person identify an organism?
- Why do scientists revise classification systems when new evidence appears?

**Student output**
- A classification field guide with a branching key, labelled examples from several groups, reasons for each division, and a short note on the simplified Linnaean system.

## Chapter 2: Microorganisms and Scientific Evidence
**Key concepts**: microorganism, bacterium, fungus, yeast, mould, virus, decomposer, growth, hygiene, useful microorganism, harmful microorganism, evidence, control.

**Practice questions**
- Are all microorganisms harmful?
- What conditions help yeast or mould grow?
- How can we design a safe investigation about microorganisms without culturing unknown samples?

**Student output**
- A safe microorganism investigation or research report, such as yeast respiration or bread mould observation, with controls, data table, risk note, and a balanced useful/harmful microorganisms explanation.

## Chapter 3: Circulatory System and Transport
**Key concepts**: heart, blood, blood vessel, artery, vein, capillary, pulse, oxygen, nutrients, water, transport, lungs, muscle, system.

**Practice questions**
- What jobs do the heart, blood vessels, and blood do?
- How are oxygen, nutrients, and water transported around the body?
- How can pulse data help us understand exercise and the circulatory system?

**Student output**
- A circulatory system model or diagram with labelled parts, a pulse-rate investigation table and graph, and an explanation linking exercise to increased oxygen demand.

## Chapter 4: Health, Lifestyle, and Body Function
**Key concepts**: diet, exercise, sleep, hydration, medicine, drug, alcohol, tobacco, lifestyle, risk, evidence, correlation, body function.

**Practice questions**
- How do diet, exercise, sleep, and hydration affect body systems?
- What is the difference between a medicine used safely and a drug that can cause harm?
- How should scientists use evidence when discussing health choices?

**Student output**
- A health evidence briefing that explains one lifestyle factor, uses data or trustworthy sources, avoids blame, and gives a science-based recommendation for upper-primary learners.

## Chapter 5: Evolution, Fossils, and Deep Time
**Key concepts**: fossil, sediment, extinction, variation, inheritance, adaptation, environment, natural selection, evolution, evidence, deep time.

**Practice questions**
- How do fossils provide evidence about living things from the past?
- Why are offspring similar to parents but not identical?
- How can environmental change make some variations more useful than others?

**Student output**
- An evidence board showing fossil information, inherited variation, adaptation to environment, and a simple natural selection explanation using a model organism or case study.

## Chapter 6: Adaptation, Inheritance, and Scientific Models
**Key concepts**: inherited trait, acquired characteristic, variation, adaptation, population, survival, reproduction, model, limitation, Darwin, Wallace.

**Practice questions**
- Which traits are inherited, and which are learned or acquired during life?
- How can a classroom model show selection without pretending that organisms choose to change?
- What did scientists such as Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace contribute to evolutionary thinking?

**Student output**
- A natural selection model report that identifies inherited variation, explains the model's rules, records class data, notes one limitation, and connects the model to Darwin and Wallace.

## Chapter 7: Light, Sight, and Shadows
**Key concepts**: light source, reflection, ray, straight line, eye, opaque, transparent, translucent, shadow, angle, spectrum, prism, refraction.

**Practice questions**
- How does light travel from a source to an object and then to the eye?
- Why do shadows have the same outline shape as the object that blocks the light?
- What can prisms and water drops show about white light?

**Student output**
- A light investigation portfolio with ray diagrams, shadow-size measurements, a reflection or periscope challenge, and a labelled explanation of a spectrum demonstration.

## Chapter 8: Electricity, Components, and System Troubleshooting
**Key concepts**: cell, voltage, current, complete circuit, component, lamp, buzzer, switch, motor, series circuit, circuit symbol, brightness, volume, fault.

**Practice questions**
- How does changing the number or voltage of cells affect lamps, buzzers, or motors?
- How do standard circuit symbols make diagrams easier to share?
- What evidence helps locate a fault in a circuit?

**Student output**
- A circuit investigation report with standard symbols, predictions, results for changes in cells or components, troubleshooting notes, and safety rules for classroom electricity.

## Capstone
Create a "Year 6 Evidence Expo." Learners choose a question from classification, microorganisms, circulation, health, evolution, light, or electricity and produce a secondary-ready science presentation. Each exhibit must include a clear question, accurate vocabulary, practical or research evidence, data display, labelled model or diagram, explanation of limitations, and a short spoken defence of the claim.

## Assessment Ideas
- Review classification work for accurate observable characteristics, usable keys, reasoned groupings, and awareness that scientific classification changes with evidence.
- Assess microorganism tasks for safety, clear controls, data recording, and balanced discussion of useful and harmful microorganisms.
- Check circulatory and health work for correct system diagrams, careful pulse data, respectful health language, and evidence-based recommendations.
- Evaluate evolution and inheritance work through fossil evidence, variation examples, adaptation reasoning, model limitations, and avoidance of common misconceptions.
- Assess light investigations through ray diagrams, shadow measurements, reflection explanations, spectrum vocabulary, and safety around bright light and the Sun.
- Grade electricity work for correct circuit symbols, complete-circuit reasoning, systematic troubleshooting, evidence from component tests, and safe handling.
- Use the Evidence Expo to judge independent enquiry, scientific accuracy, data quality, model use, explanation of uncertainty, and readiness for secondary science.
