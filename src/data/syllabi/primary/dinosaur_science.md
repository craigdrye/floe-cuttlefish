# Dinosaur Science (Primary, ages 5-11)
**ID**: `dinosPrimary` | **Status**: playable

## Course Aim
Help primary learners investigate dinosaurs as real animals known through evidence, not just as giant storybook creatures. Children build a sense of deep time, compare bodies and behaviours, read fossils as clues, model ancient environments, and explain how scientists revise ideas when new discoveries appear.

## Course Design Notes
Dinosaur science should feel like detective work with bones, rocks, footprints, maps, measurements, and careful imagination. Lessons should balance awe with accuracy: pupils can wonder about a roaring Tyrannosaurus while also asking what the teeth, skull, trackways, rock layers, and living bird relatives actually show. Activities should include fossil casts or images, simple timelines, body plans, habitat models, evidence cards, drawing from observation, and age-appropriate discussion of extinction, evolution, and uncertainty.

## Chapter 1: Deep Time and the Dinosaur World
**Key concepts**: deep time, Mesozoic Era, Triassic, Jurassic, Cretaceous, million years ago, timeline, Pangaea, continent, climate, environment.

**Practice questions**
- How can a timeline help us understand events that happened millions of years ago?
- What changed from the Triassic to the Jurassic and Cretaceous periods?
- Why were the continents and climates different during dinosaur times?

**Student output**
- A classroom-scale Mesozoic timeline with period labels, sample dinosaurs, continent sketches, and one sentence explaining why deep time is hard to picture.

## Chapter 2: What Makes a Dinosaur a Dinosaur?
**Key concepts**: dinosaur, reptile, archosaur, hip socket, upright legs, biped, quadruped, herbivore, carnivore, omnivore, extinct, living relative.

**Practice questions**
- Why is a pterosaur or marine reptile not a dinosaur, even though it lived at the same time?
- What body clues help scientists sort dinosaur groups?
- How are birds connected to theropod dinosaurs?

**Student output**
- A "dinosaur or not?" sorting board with evidence labels for dinosaurs, pterosaurs, marine reptiles, crocodile relatives, and modern birds.

## Chapter 3: Fossils as Evidence
**Key concepts**: fossil, body fossil, trace fossil, footprint, coprolite, sediment, mineralisation, rock layer, preservation, evidence, inference.

**Practice questions**
- What steps can turn a dead animal or plant into a fossil?
- What can footprints tell us that bones cannot?
- Why do scientists separate what they observe from what they infer?

**Student output**
- A fossil evidence notebook page with a four-step fossil formation sequence, labelled examples of body and trace fossils, and two careful inferences from a footprint trackway.

## Chapter 4: Dinosaur Bodies and Adaptations
**Key concepts**: skeleton, skull, teeth, claw, tail, armour, plate, horn, crest, feather, adaptation, function, compare, model.

**Practice questions**
- How can teeth give clues about what an animal ate?
- What possible jobs might plates, horns, crests, armour, or feathers have done?
- Why should scientists test more than one explanation for a body feature?

**Student output**
- A labelled dinosaur anatomy diagram that links at least six body features to possible functions and marks which ideas are strongly supported by evidence.

## Chapter 5: Food, Movement, and Behaviour Clues
**Key concepts**: predator, prey, herbivore, food web, trackway, stride, speed, herd, nest, egg, behaviour, pattern, claim.

**Practice questions**
- How can tracks help scientists estimate how an animal moved?
- What evidence might suggest nesting, herding, hunting, or scavenging?
- Why is behaviour harder to prove than body shape?

**Student output**
- A dinosaur behaviour case file using trackway or nest evidence, including a claim, at least three observations, and a cautious conclusion.

## Chapter 6: Ancient Habitats and Ecosystems
**Key concepts**: habitat, ecosystem, producer, consumer, decomposer, fern, conifer, cycad, river, floodplain, desert, coast, niche, food web.

**Practice questions**
- What plants and other animals shared dinosaur habitats?
- How did rivers, forests, deserts, and coasts shape where dinosaurs lived?
- What would happen to an ecosystem if one important organism disappeared?

**Student output**
- A Mesozoic habitat model or poster showing plants, herbivores, carnivores, decomposers, water sources, and evidence-based notes about the environment.

## Chapter 7: Dinosaur Discoveries and Field Science
**Key concepts**: palaeontologist, field site, excavation, plaster jacket, museum collection, fossil prep, map, grid, specimen, description, peer review.

**Practice questions**
- What careful steps protect a fossil from field site to museum drawer?
- Why do scientists record exactly where a fossil was found?
- How did people such as Mary Anning help change ideas about ancient life?

**Student output**
- A mock field report with a site map, grid location, specimen sketch, safe excavation notes, and a short museum label written for younger visitors.

## Chapter 8: Extinction, Survivors, and New Questions
**Key concepts**: extinction, K-Pg boundary, asteroid impact, Chicxulub crater, iridium, climate change, survivor, mammal, crocodilian, bird, evidence, scientific debate.

**Practice questions**
- What evidence supports the asteroid impact explanation for the end-Cretaceous extinction?
- Why did some groups disappear while others survived?
- How do new fossils, better tools, and new questions change dinosaur science?

**Student output**
- An extinction evidence board that connects the crater, iridium layer, shocked rocks, fossils, and survivor groups to a clear explanation written in primary-friendly language.

## Capstone
Create a "Dinosaur Evidence Museum." Teams design a small exhibit about one dinosaur, fossil site, habitat, or extinction question. The exhibit must include a timeline, map or habitat model, labelled fossil or anatomy evidence, a claim supported by observations, one uncertainty scientists still discuss, and an interactive visitor task such as matching tracks, sorting teeth, or building a food web.

## Assessment Ideas
- Check timelines for correct ordering of Triassic, Jurassic, Cretaceous, major changes, and realistic scale language.
- Review sorting tasks for accurate distinctions among dinosaurs, pterosaurs, marine reptiles, crocodile relatives, and birds.
- Assess fossil work for clear formation steps, correct body and trace fossil examples, and separation of observation from inference.
- Use anatomy and behaviour products to evaluate evidence-based reasoning, labelled diagrams, vocabulary, and caution around uncertain claims.
- Assess habitat models for producers, consumers, environmental features, and realistic connections between organisms and place.
- Evaluate extinction explanations for age-appropriate accuracy, use of multiple evidence types, and understanding that birds are living dinosaurs.
- Grade the capstone on scientific accuracy, clarity of evidence, careful drawing or modelling, teamwork, visitor communication, and revision after feedback.
