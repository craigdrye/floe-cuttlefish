# 6th Grade Reading and Vocab
**ID**: `col-6th-grade-reading-and-vocab` | **Discipline**: English | **Year**: 6 (ages 10-11)
**Frameworks**: US Common Core ELA Grade 6

## Course Aim
Help students cross from upper primary reading into confident middle-school analysis. Students build fluency, stamina, morphology, academic vocabulary, literary interpretation, informational reading, argument evaluation, research habits, and discussion routines so they can read harder texts with warmth, precision, and evidence.

## Course Design Notes
This course keeps reading joyful while making the work more analytical. Lessons balance shared reading, close reading, vocabulary study, short written responses, discussion, and independent reading. Students repeatedly move from "What does the text say?" to "How do I know?" and "Why did the writer make that choice?" Vocabulary instruction uses context, Greek and Latin roots, word families, connotation, figurative language, and academic terms students can reuse in speaking and writing.

## Chapter 1: Reading Stamina, Fluency, and Evidence
**Key concepts**: fluency, prosody, stamina, annotation, gist, evidence, inference, quotation, paraphrase, objective summary.

**Essential questions**
- What helps a reader stay with a demanding text?
- Which details prove an idea rather than merely decorate it?
- How can a summary be accurate without becoming a personal reaction?

**Skills**
- Read grade-level prose and poetry with accuracy, expression, and attention to punctuation.
- Annotate for main ideas, confusing moments, important words, and evidence.
- Cite textual evidence for explicit meaning and reasonable inference.
- Write concise objective summaries.

**Assessment idea**
- Fluency-and-evidence conference: students read a short passage aloud, annotate it, answer an inference question, and explain which evidence is strongest.

## Chapter 2: Morphology and Academic Vocabulary
**Key concepts**: root, prefix, suffix, affix, base word, word family, morphology, context clue, synonym, antonym, connotation, denotation, register.

**Essential questions**
- How can word parts help without replacing context?
- Why do similar words carry different feelings or levels of formality?
- How do academic words travel across subjects?

**Skills**
- Use common Greek and Latin roots and affixes to predict word meaning.
- Check a predicted meaning against sentence and paragraph context.
- Build word webs that connect roots, related words, synonyms, antonyms, and examples.
- Choose precise academic vocabulary in discussion and written responses.

**Assessment idea**
- Morphology notebook check: students unpack unfamiliar words from current readings, test meanings in context, and use selected words in original analytical sentences.

## Chapter 3: Literature, Plot, and Character Change
**Key concepts**: plot, episode, conflict, resolution, character trait, motivation, decision, change, theme, setting, narrator.

**Essential questions**
- How does a story move from problem to resolution?
- What causes a character to change, resist change, or reveal hidden traits?
- How do setting and conflict shape what characters can do?

**Skills**
- Track plot episodes and explain how each contributes to the whole story.
- Analyze character responses, motivations, and changes using evidence.
- Distinguish a topic from an emerging theme.
- Discuss stories with accountable talk moves and textual support.

**Assessment idea**
- Character-change map: students chart key scenes, decisions, evidence, and a claim about how a character develops.

## Chapter 4: Poetry, Figurative Language, and Word Choice
**Key concepts**: line, stanza, speaker, imagery, metaphor, simile, personification, tone, mood, repetition, rhythm, figurative meaning.

**Essential questions**
- How does a poem create meaning with fewer words?
- How do sound, line breaks, and repetition affect feeling and emphasis?
- What changes when a word is read literally, figuratively, or connotatively?

**Skills**
- Interpret common figures of speech in context.
- Analyze how specific words and phrases affect meaning and tone.
- Explain how a stanza, scene, chapter, or sentence fits into a larger structure.
- Compare how two texts approach a similar topic or theme.

**Assessment idea**
- Poetry close-read: students annotate one poem for imagery, figurative language, structure, and tone, then write a short evidence-based interpretation.

## Chapter 5: Informational Text Structures and Central Ideas
**Key concepts**: central idea, detail, text structure, cause and effect, compare and contrast, problem and solution, chronology, domain vocabulary, section heading.

**Essential questions**
- How does the structure of an article guide the reader's thinking?
- Which details develop the central idea, and which are less important?
- How do technical words help a writer be exact?

**Skills**
- Determine central ideas and explain how details develop them.
- Identify and use common informational text structures.
- Analyze how a paragraph, section, graphic, or heading contributes to the whole text.
- Integrate information from words, charts, images, and other media.

**Assessment idea**
- Structure detective task: students label the structure of a nonfiction passage, summarize its central idea, and explain how one section advances that idea.

## Chapter 6: Argument, Claims, and Evidence
**Key concepts**: claim, reason, evidence, warrant, source, credibility, relevant, sufficient, counterclaim, bias, unsupported claim.

**Essential questions**
- What makes evidence strong enough to support a claim?
- How can readers tell the difference between a reason and an example?
- When should a reader trust, question, or reject a source?

**Skills**
- Trace an author's argument from claim to reasons and evidence.
- Distinguish supported claims from claims that lack relevant evidence.
- Notice loaded language, weak support, and missing information.
- Use evidence from a text to write a clear response to an argument.

**Assessment idea**
- Claim check: students mark a short argument for claim, reasons, evidence, and unsupported leaps, then write a verdict on its strength.

## Chapter 7: Research Reading and Source Synthesis
**Key concepts**: research question, keyword, source, note, paraphrase, quotation, citation, fact, opinion, synthesis, plagiarism.

**Essential questions**
- How does a good research question focus reading?
- What notes help a writer think instead of copy?
- How can several sources build a fuller answer?

**Skills**
- Generate focused research questions and useful search terms.
- Take notes by paraphrasing, quoting sparingly, and recording source information.
- Compare sources for agreement, difference, emphasis, and reliability.
- Combine information from multiple sources into a short explanation.

**Assessment idea**
- Mini-research brief: students answer one question using two or three sources, with notes, source labels, and a short synthesis paragraph.

## Chapter 8: Discussion and Writing About Reading
**Key concepts**: discussion norm, accountable talk, claim, evidence, explanation, transition, paragraph, revision, audience, reflection.

**Essential questions**
- How does discussion change or sharpen interpretation?
- What makes a reading response more than a list of facts?
- How can revision make evidence and explanation clearer?

**Skills**
- Prepare for discussion with notes, questions, and evidence.
- Build on, challenge, and clarify classmates' ideas respectfully.
- Write claim-evidence-explanation paragraphs about literature and nonfiction.
- Revise for clarity, precise vocabulary, transitions, and textual support.

**Assessment idea**
- Seminar-to-paragraph cycle: students join a text discussion, gather one improved idea, and revise a written response using evidence from the conversation.

## Capstone
Create a "Reading Investigator Portfolio" around a shared theme such as courage, invention, justice, survival, or belonging. Each student reads at least one literary text and two informational sources, builds a vocabulary/morphology page, writes a literary analysis paragraph, evaluates one argument or source, synthesizes research notes, and leads a short discussion segment with prepared questions and evidence.

## Assessment Ideas
- Use fluency conferences, reading logs, and short comprehension checks to monitor accuracy, stamina, and literal understanding.
- Review annotations and notebooks for evidence selection, inference quality, vocabulary reasoning, and morphology transfer.
- Assess literary work through character analysis, theme development, structure, figurative language, and precise text evidence.
- Assess informational reading through central idea, text structure, argument tracing, source reliability, and synthesis.
- Grade discussion and writing on preparation, evidence use, listening, response to others, clarity, and revision.

## Research Notes
- Common Core ELA Grade 6 emphasizes citing textual evidence, determining theme and central idea, analyzing structure and point of view, evaluating claims, vocabulary in context, morphology, and academic discussion: https://www.corestandards.org/ELA-Literacy/grade-6/
- The UK National Curriculum for English in upper Key Stage 2 and Key Stage 3 emphasizes fluent reading, wide literature, inference, vocabulary, discussion, formal presentation, and increasingly analytical response: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/national-curriculum-in-england-english-programmes-of-study
- Education Endowment Foundation reading guidance informed the focus on vocabulary, fluency, comprehension strategies, discussion, and structured support for struggling readers: https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/education-evidence/guidance-reports/literacy-ks2
- Morphology, repeated exposure to academic words, and active discussion help students connect word knowledge with comprehension rather than treating vocabulary as isolated memorization.
