# 4th Grade Reading and Vocab
**ID**: `col-4th-grade-reading-and-vocab` | **Discipline**: English | **Year**: 4 (ages 8-9)
**Frameworks**: US Common Core ELA Grade 4

## Course Aim
Build confident, curious readers who can read grade-level literature and nonfiction with stamina, accuracy, and purpose. Students strengthen fluency, use morphology and context to unlock vocabulary, summarize with precision, cite evidence, interpret figurative language, and write clear responses that show what they noticed, wondered, and understood.

## Course Design Notes
This course keeps reading warm, text-rich, and strategy-explicit. Each week blends shared read-aloud, short complex passages, partner rereading, vocabulary notebooks, morphology practice, discussion, and written response. Students learn to pause, reread, annotate, ask questions, track ideas across paragraphs, and explain their thinking with sentence frames that gradually become independent habits.

## Chapter 1: Reading Identity, Fluency, and Stamina
- **Key concepts**: fluency, accuracy, rate, expression, rereading, stamina, purpose, just-right challenge.
- **Practice activities**: read short passages more than once for smoother expression; mark punctuation for phrasing; keep a reading stamina log; compare "fast reading" with "thinking reading"; practice partner feedback that names one strength and one next step.
- **Teacher prompts**: Where did your voice show the meaning? Which sentence needed a reread? What helped you keep going when the text became harder?
- **Work product**: a fluency reflection with one recorded or teacher-observed passage, two rereading goals, and a short note about how fluency supported comprehension.

## Chapter 2: Morphology and Word Learning
- **Key concepts**: prefix, suffix, root, base word, Greek root, Latin root, context clue, synonym, antonym, nuance.
- **Practice activities**: build word webs from common roots such as photo, graph, tele, struct, port, and form; sort words by affix meaning; use context clues before checking a dictionary; collect academic words from science, history, and literature passages.
- **Teacher prompts**: Which word part gives you a clue? What does the sentence suggest before and after the unknown word? How is this word similar to, but not exactly the same as, another word you know?
- **Work product**: a vocabulary detective page with five words, word-part clues, context clues, student-friendly meanings, and one original sentence for each.

## Chapter 3: Fiction Comprehension and Character Thinking
- **Key concepts**: character, setting, event, motivation, trait, conflict, point of view, inference, evidence.
- **Practice activities**: track a character's thoughts, words, and actions; infer motivation from details; compare two scenes in a story; discuss how point of view changes what the reader knows; annotate evidence with "I think... because the text says..."
- **Teacher prompts**: What does the character want? Which detail proves that trait? What can we infer that the author does not say directly?
- **Work product**: a character evidence chart explaining one character's trait, motivation, challenge, and change using quoted or paraphrased details.

## Chapter 4: Nonfiction Main Ideas and Summaries
- **Key concepts**: topic, main idea, key detail, summary, paraphrase, section, heading, diagram, caption.
- **Practice activities**: identify the topic and main idea of paragraphs; match headings to sections; use a main idea plus three details frame; paraphrase important information; turn diagrams, captions, and charts into sentences.
- **Teacher prompts**: What is this section mostly teaching? Which details are important enough to keep? What can we leave out of a summary?
- **Work product**: a nonfiction summary card with a main idea, three supporting details, one visual-text connection, and a concise summary paragraph.

## Chapter 5: Text Structure and Thinking Across Paragraphs
- **Key concepts**: chronology, cause and effect, compare and contrast, problem and solution, description, signal word, connection.
- **Practice activities**: highlight signal words; sort passages by structure; build simple graphic organizers; explain events, procedures, and concepts in order; compare firsthand and secondhand accounts of the same event.
- **Teacher prompts**: How did the author organize the information? Which signal words helped? How does this structure help the reader understand the topic?
- **Work product**: a text-structure map for one informational passage, including the structure, signal words, key details, and a written explanation of why the structure fits.

## Chapter 6: Theme, Figurative Language, and Author Choices
- **Key concepts**: theme, lesson, poem, drama, prose, simile, metaphor, idiom, adage, proverb, personification.
- **Practice activities**: compare how stories, poems, and dramas present similar ideas; interpret similes and metaphors in context; collect idioms and explain their real meanings; connect a theme to characters' choices and story events.
- **Teacher prompts**: What lesson grows from the details? What image does this figurative phrase create? How does the author's word choice shape the mood?
- **Work product**: a theme-and-language response explaining a theme and interpreting two examples of figurative language with evidence from the text.

## Chapter 7: Evidence, Discussion, and Writing About Reading
- **Key concepts**: claim, evidence, explanation, quote, paraphrase, discussion, agree, build, challenge, revise.
- **Practice activities**: answer questions using text evidence; practice discussion stems; write short constructed responses; revise vague answers by adding stronger details; compare two possible answers and decide which is better supported.
- **Teacher prompts**: Where does the text support your answer? How does that evidence connect to your idea? What could you add to make your response clearer?
- **Work product**: a polished reading response with a clear answer, at least two pieces of evidence, and explanation in the student's own words.

## Chapter 8: Reading Across Texts and Building Knowledge
- **Key concepts**: source, topic, account, perspective, fact, opinion, compare, integrate, synthesize.
- **Practice activities**: read two short texts on the same topic; compare firsthand and secondhand accounts; gather facts from print and digital sources; create a two-column evidence chart; explain how texts agree, differ, or add new information.
- **Teacher prompts**: What did both texts teach? What is different about each account? How can we combine the information without copying?
- **Work product**: a cross-text knowledge note with facts from two sources, a comparison statement, and a short paragraph explaining the topic knowledgeably.

## Capstone
Create a "Reading Explorer Portfolio" around one rich topic or author set. Students choose literature, nonfiction, and a poem or drama excerpt; keep vocabulary and morphology notes; write one fiction response, one nonfiction summary, one figurative language analysis, and one cross-text explanation; then present the portfolio through a short book talk or gallery walk.

## Assessment Ideas
- Listen for fluency growth through accuracy, phrasing, expression, and purposeful rereading rather than speed alone.
- Check vocabulary work for useful context clues, accurate word-part reasoning, and flexible use of academic words in speech and writing.
- Use short written responses to assess inference, theme, main idea, summary, evidence selection, and explanation.
- Observe discussions for listening, turn-taking, text reference, respectful agreement, and building on another student's idea.
- Grade the capstone on reading range, vocabulary strategy use, evidence, summary quality, cross-text thinking, and confidence explaining choices.

## Research Notes
- Common Core ELA Grade 4 emphasizes close reading of literature and informational text, explaining explicit meaning and inferences, determining theme and main idea, summarizing, text structure, vocabulary, and evidence-based responses: https://www.corestandards.org/ELA-Literacy/RI/4/
- The UK National Curriculum English Key Stage 2 expects pupils to build positive reading habits, understand increasingly challenging texts, discuss words and phrases, retrieve and record information, infer, summarize, and justify views with evidence: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/national-curriculum-in-england-english-programmes-of-study
- EEF guidance on improving literacy in Key Stage 2 highlights explicit vocabulary instruction, reading comprehension strategy instruction, purposeful talk, and structured writing about reading: https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/education-evidence/guidance-reports/literacy-ks2
- Reading confidence grows when students meet worthwhile texts with visible strategies, frequent low-stakes practice, teacher modelling, peer talk, and chances to reread for meaning.
