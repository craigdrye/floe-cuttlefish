# 4th Grade Reading and Vocab
**ID**: `col-4th-grade-reading-and-vocab` · **Discipline**: English · **Year**: 4 (ages 8–9)
**Frameworks**: US Common Core ELA Grade 4

## Course Philosophy
In 4th grade, the shift from "learning to read" to "reading to learn" is complete. Students must comprehend complex informational texts and nuanced literature. Vocabulary acquisition moves toward academic language and domain-specific terms.

## Module 1: Reading Literature (25%)
- Refer to details and examples in a text when explaining what the text says explicitly and when drawing inferences
- Determine a theme of a story, drama, or poem from details in the text
- Describe in depth a character, setting, or event in a story or drama, drawing on specific details in the text (e.g., a character's thoughts, words, or actions)
- Explain major differences between poems, drama, and prose
- Compare and contrast the treatment of similar themes and topics and patterns of events in stories, myths, and traditional literature
- **Questions**: Inference MCQs, theme identification, character motivation analysis, text type comparison

## Module 2: Reading Informational Text (25%)
- Determine the main idea of a text and explain how it is supported by key details; summarize the text
- Explain events, procedures, ideas, or concepts in a historical, scientific, or technical text, including what happened and why
- Determine the meaning of general academic and domain-specific words or phrases
- Describe the overall structure (e.g., chronology, comparison, cause/effect, problem/solution) of events, ideas, concepts, or information in a text
- Compare and contrast a firsthand and secondhand account of the same event
- Interpret information presented visually, orally, or quantitatively (e.g., in charts, graphs, diagrams)
- **Questions**: Main idea summary, text structure identification, vocabulary in context, visual data interpretation

## Module 3: Vocabulary Acquisition & Use (25%)
- Use context as a clue to the meaning of a word or phrase
- Use common, grade-appropriate Greek and Latin affixes and roots as clues to the meaning of a word (e.g., telegraph, photograph, autograph)
- Demonstrate understanding of figurative language, word relationships, and nuances in word meanings
- Explain the meaning of simple similes and metaphors (e.g., as pretty as a picture) in context
- Recognize and explain the meaning of common idioms, adages, and proverbs
- Demonstrate understanding of words by relating them to their opposites (antonyms) and to words with similar but not identical meanings (synonyms)
- **Questions**: Root/affix meaning, simile/metaphor identification, idiom meaning, synonym/antonym matching

## Module 4: Language Conventions (25%)
- Use relative pronouns (who, whose, whom, which, that) and relative adverbs (where, when, why)
- Form and use the progressive verb tenses (e.g., I was walking, I am walking, I will be walking)
- Use modal auxiliaries (e.g., can, may, must) to convey various conditions
- Order adjectives within sentences according to conventional patterns (e.g., a small red bag rather than a red small bag)
- Form and use prepositional phrases
- Produce complete sentences, recognizing and correcting inappropriate fragments and run-ons
- Correctly use frequently confused words (e.g., to, too, two; there, their, they're)
- Use correct capitalization and punctuation, including commas and quotation marks to mark direct speech
- **Questions**: Grammar fill-in, sentence correction, punctuation placement, homophone selection

## Stretch Zone
- Analyze how a narrator's point of view influences how events are described
- Integrate information from two texts on the same topic in order to write or speak about the subject knowledgeably
- Understand personification and hyperbole
