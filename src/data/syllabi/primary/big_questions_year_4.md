# Big Questions: Year 4
**ID**: `philosophyYear4` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Year**: 4 (ages 8-9)
**Frameworks**: P4C/SAPERE enquiry routines, CASEL SEL framework, UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, UK KS2 citizenship and PSHE, primary media literacy

## Course Aim
Deepen children's ability to reason about intentions, consequences, truth, fairness, freedom, wealth, creativity, and shared life. Year 4 students learn to build clearer arguments, notice counterexamples, compare more than one value at a time, and use evidence from stories, school life, family and community situations, and child-friendly news without losing the warmth of a discussion circle.

## Course Design Notes
Year 4 philosophy should feel like serious thinking made child-sized. Lessons use picture books, short news items, fictional family and school dilemmas, simple data, artwork, adverts, voting games, and community examples. Students practise "I think... because... for example...", "A counterexample is...", "The strongest reason is...", and "I used to think... now I think..." Teachers should keep political and social questions balanced, concrete, and safe: use public scenarios, avoid asking children to reveal family finances or beliefs, and invite uncertainty as part of good enquiry.

## Chapter 1: Intentions, Outcomes, and Repair
**Key concepts**: intention, outcome, accident, choice, harm, apology, repair, responsibility.

**Practice questions**
- Is accidentally causing harm different from causing harm on purpose?
- Can a good intention still lead to a bad result?
- What makes an apology or repair fair to the person who was hurt?

**Student output**
- An intention-and-outcome grid comparing accident, carelessness, pressure, and deliberate choice.

## Chapter 2: Fairness, Equality, and Need
**Key concepts**: fairness, equality, equity, need, advantage, disadvantage, access, justice.

**Practice questions**
- Is fair always the same for everyone?
- Should games, classrooms, or competitions ever give extra support to some people?
- How can we design a fair rule if we do not know which person we will be?

**Student output**
- A fair-game redesign with one rule, one reason, and one explanation of who the rule helps.

## Chapter 3: Truth, News, and Media
**Key concepts**: fact, opinion, bias, source, evidence, headline, image, reliable.

**Practice questions**
- How can a headline make people feel before they know the whole story?
- What is bias, and does having a point of view always make someone wrong?
- What should we check before we share something online or repeat it at school?

**Student output**
- A media-check bookmark: source, evidence, date, purpose, and "what might be missing?"

## Chapter 4: Wealth, Poverty, and Helping Well
**Key concepts**: wealth, poverty, need, want, charity, justice, opportunity, responsibility.

**Practice questions**
- Why might some people have more choices than others?
- What is the difference between helping one person now and changing a rule so more people are helped?
- Do people have responsibilities to others beyond their own family or friends?

**Student output**
- A help-now/change-the-system T-chart using a fictional school, community, or news example.

## Chapter 5: Freedom, Safety, and Shared Rules
**Key concepts**: freedom, safety, law, rule, privacy, protection, trust, limit.

**Practice questions**
- When should rules limit what people are free to do?
- Would a school with no rules be more free, more unfair, or both?
- How should a community balance privacy with keeping people safe?

**Student output**
- A freedom-and-safety scale placing scenario cards and adding one reason for each placement.

## Chapter 6: Human Nature and Cooperation
**Key concepts**: selfish, generous, cooperation, trust, conflict, law, habit, common good.

**Practice questions**
- Are people naturally kind, selfish, or a mixture?
- What happens when a group has shared resources but no shared rules?
- Why do people cooperate even when nobody is forcing them?

**Student output**
- A group-choice reflection showing what helped cooperation, what made it harder, and one improved rule.

## Chapter 7: Art, Beauty, and Value
**Key concepts**: art, beauty, taste, value, purpose, usefulness, creativity, judgement.

**Practice questions**
- Does something have to be beautiful to be art?
- Can two people disagree about art and both give good reasons?
- Does something have to be useful to be valuable?

**Student output**
- A gallery label for an artwork, object, song, poem, or design explaining one judgement with evidence.

## Chapter 8: Stronger Arguments and Open Minds
**Key concepts**: argument, reason, evidence, example, counterexample, assumption, revise, conclusion.

**Practice questions**
- What is the difference between a strong reason and a loud opinion?
- How can a counterexample test an argument?
- When is changing your mind a sign of good thinking?

**Student output**
- A mini argument map with claim, two reasons, one counterexample, and a revised conclusion.

## Capstone
Create a "Big Questions Assembly" or classroom exhibition. Groups choose a question from the course, such as whether intentions matter more than outcomes, how to check a news story, whether fair means equal, or when rules should limit freedom. Each group prepares a child-friendly scenario, a balanced explanation of two views, an argument map, and a discussion prompt for visitors. Students finish by writing or recording a short reflection on one claim they can now support with reasons and one question that remains open.

## Assessment Ideas
- Observe whether students can make a claim, give more than one reason, use an example, respond to a counterexample, and revise a view.
- Review intention grids, fair-game designs, media bookmarks, help-now/change-system charts, freedom scales, cooperation reflections, gallery labels, and argument maps.
- Use discussion-circle rubrics for listening, building on others, asking clarifying questions, and disagreeing with reasons.
- Check scenario work for age-appropriate understanding of intention, outcome, fairness, reliability, need, freedom, safety, value, and cooperation.
- Assess the capstone on balanced presentation, argument structure, evidence use, respectful facilitation, and thoughtful final reflection.
