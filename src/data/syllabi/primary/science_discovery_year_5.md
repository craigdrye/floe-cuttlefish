# Science Discovery: Year 5
**ID**: `scienceYear5` - **Discipline**: Science - **Year**: 5 (ages 9-10)
**Frameworks**: UK KS2 Science Year 5, US NGSS Grade 5, ACARA Science Year 5

## Course Aim
Develop confident upper-primary scientists who can plan fair tests, handle data carefully, use models to explain hidden processes, and connect evidence to clear scientific vocabulary. Learners investigate life cycles, reproduction, human development, materials, reversible and irreversible changes, Earth and space, forces, mechanisms, and environmental problem solving through practical enquiry and careful recording.

## Course Design Notes
Year 5 science should feel investigative, precise, and hands-on. Children are ready to separate observation from inference, identify variables, repeat measurements, choose suitable graphs, and evaluate whether evidence is strong enough to support a conclusion. Lessons should include practical tests with classroom materials, model building, science notebooks, data tables, labelled diagrams, respectful discussion of human development, and explicit vocabulary routines.

## Chapter 1: Working Scientifically with Fair Tests
**Key concepts**: testable question, prediction, independent variable, dependent variable, control variable, fair test, repeat, accuracy, anomaly, evidence, conclusion.

**Practice questions**
- What will we change, measure, and keep the same?
- Why might repeated measurements make a result more trustworthy?
- How can a scientist decide whether a result is an anomaly or part of a pattern?

**Student output**
- An investigation plan for a simple fair test, including a prediction, variables, safe method, repeated results table, graph choice, and conclusion that quotes evidence.

## Chapter 2: Life Cycles of Plants and Animals
**Key concepts**: life cycle, birth, growth, adult, reproduction, mammal, amphibian, insect, bird, metamorphosis, pollination, seed, germination.

**Practice questions**
- How is the life cycle of a butterfly different from the life cycle of a mammal?
- Which stages are shared by many living things, and which stages are special to one group?
- How can a diagram show change over time clearly?

**Student output**
- A comparative life-cycle display for a mammal, amphibian, insect, and bird, with labelled stages, similarities, differences, and one evidence-based explanation.

## Chapter 3: Reproduction in Plants and Animals
**Key concepts**: sexual reproduction, asexual reproduction, pollen, ovule, fertilisation, seed, fruit, runner, bulb, tuber, cutting, offspring, inherited features.

**Practice questions**
- How do flowering plants make seeds?
- How can some plants reproduce without seeds?
- Why do offspring resemble their parents but still show variation?

**Student output**
- A plant reproduction notebook page with a labelled flower diagram, a model or observation of pollination, examples of asexual reproduction, and a short comparison of plant and animal reproduction.

## Chapter 4: Human Development and Healthy Change
**Key concepts**: gestation, infancy, childhood, adolescence, puberty, adulthood, old age, growth, development, hormones, body systems, health, respect.

**Practice questions**
- What changes happen as humans develop from birth to old age?
- Which changes are growth, and which changes are development?
- How can scientists describe puberty accurately and respectfully?

**Student output**
- A human development timeline with major stages, measured or researched milestones, factual vocabulary, and a respectful explanation of how bodies change during adolescence.

## Chapter 5: Properties and Uses of Materials
**Key concepts**: material, property, hardness, flexibility, transparency, thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, magnetism, absorbency, durability, suitability.

**Practice questions**
- Which properties make a material suitable for a particular job?
- How can we compare materials fairly?
- Why might two materials be useful for different parts of the same object?

**Student output**
- A material testing report with at least four properties tested, a results table, clear ranking evidence, and a recommendation for a real design problem such as a lunchbox, shelter, handle, or window.

## Chapter 6: Solutions, Mixtures, and Separation
**Key concepts**: mixture, solution, dissolve, soluble, insoluble, solvent, solute, filter, sieve, evaporate, recover, particle model, variable.

**Practice questions**
- How do we know when a substance has dissolved?
- Which separation method works best for each mixture, and why?
- How can evaporation help recover a dissolved solid?

**Student output**
- A separation challenge record showing how to separate mixed materials using sieving, filtering, magnetism, and evaporation, with diagrams and reasons for each method.

## Chapter 7: Reversible and Irreversible Changes
**Key concepts**: reversible change, irreversible change, physical change, chemical change, melting, freezing, dissolving, mixing, burning, gas production, new material.

**Practice questions**
- Which changes can be reversed by cooling, heating, filtering, or evaporation?
- What evidence suggests that a new material has formed?
- Why should burning be discussed through teacher demonstration or secondary evidence rather than pupil experimentation?

**Student output**
- A change classification table with examples, evidence for each decision, particle-model sketches for reversible changes, and a safe explanation of an irreversible reaction such as acid with bicarbonate of soda.

## Chapter 8: Earth, Space, Forces, and Mechanisms
**Key concepts**: Sun, Earth, Moon, planet, spherical body, rotation, orbit, day, night, year, gravity, friction, air resistance, water resistance, lever, pulley, gear.

**Practice questions**
- How does Earth's rotation explain day and night?
- How can a model show the Moon orbiting Earth and Earth orbiting the Sun?
- How do friction, air resistance, water resistance, levers, pulleys, and gears change motion or force?

**Student output**
- A linked models portfolio with a Sun-Earth-Moon explanation, a force investigation such as parachute surface area or shoe friction, and a mechanism diagram explaining how a smaller force can have a larger effect.

## Capstone
Design a "Mission Lab: Improve a Scientific System." Teams choose a practical challenge connected to the Year 5 course, such as improving a seed dispersal model, selecting materials for a shelter, separating a mystery mixture, reducing air resistance, or explaining day and night to younger pupils. The final lab must include a testable question, model or prototype, fair-test plan, data table, graph, labelled diagram, vocabulary glossary, safety note, and conclusion that uses evidence.

## Assessment Ideas
- Review investigation plans for clear variables, safe methods, repeated measurements, useful tables, and evidence-based conclusions.
- Assess life-cycle and reproduction work for accurate stages, comparisons across animal groups, plant reproduction vocabulary, and clear diagrams.
- Check human development work for factual accuracy, respectful language, correct sequencing, and distinction between growth and development.
- Evaluate materials investigations for fair comparisons, property vocabulary, justified material choices, and careful handling of results.
- Use mixtures and changes tasks to assess separation methods, reversible and irreversible change reasoning, particle-model explanations, and safety awareness.
- Assess Earth, space, forces, and mechanisms through models, data from practical tests, labelled diagrams, and explanations of cause and effect.
- Grade the capstone on scientific accuracy, practical design, quality of data, teamwork, communication, and willingness to improve the method after evidence.
