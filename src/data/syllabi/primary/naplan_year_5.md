# NAPLAN Prep (Year 5)
**ID**: `naplanYear5` | **Discipline**: Exam Prep | **Year**: 4 (shown in Year 4 as prep for the Year 5 NAPLAN test)
**Framework**: Australian NAPLAN

## Course Aim
Prepare students for Year 5 NAPLAN by strengthening the reading, writing, language, and numeracy skills they will use across school. The course treats NAPLAN as one useful checkpoint, not the whole purpose of learning: students build stamina, accuracy, flexible strategies, and confidence with computer-based question formats while practising how to explain, revise, estimate, and check.

## Course Design Notes
This course is designed for Year 4 learners preparing for the Year 5 NAPLAN assessment in March of Year 5. Lessons should balance explicit strategy teaching with rich primary learning: varied texts, shared annotation, modelled writing, vocabulary talk, sentence combining, editing routines, number talks, visual models, data interpretation, and timed-but-low-stress practice. Students should meet NAPLAN-style items often enough to remove mystery, while teachers keep the focus on transferable Australian Curriculum English and Mathematics skills.

## Chapter 1: Reading Across Text Types
**Key concepts**: imaginative text, informative text, persuasive text, main idea, detail, sequence, evidence, purpose, audience.

**Practice questions**
- What is the main idea of this text, and which details support it?
- How does the structure help the reader follow the information or story?
- Which answer is best supported by the text rather than by a guess?

**Student output**
- A text-type comparison chart that identifies purpose, audience, key details, and one evidence-backed answer for three short texts.

## Chapter 2: Inference, Author Choices, and Vocabulary
**Key concepts**: inference, motive, tone, context, synonym, connotation, figurative language, simile, metaphor, effect.

**Practice questions**
- What can we infer about a character, speaker, or situation from the clues?
- How does a word, phrase, simile, or metaphor affect the reader's understanding?
- Which meaning of the word fits this context best?

**Student output**
- An inference trail showing quoted clues, a supported conclusion, one vocabulary-in-context answer, and a short note on author choice.

## Chapter 3: Visual, Digital, and Data-Rich Texts
**Key concepts**: diagram, map, table, graph, caption, hyperlink, sidebar, layout, comparison, source.

**Practice questions**
- What information comes from the visual feature rather than the main paragraph?
- How do the layout and labels guide the reader?
- Which conclusion is supported by both the written text and the data display?

**Student output**
- A visual-text response page with a labelled feature analysis, two data-based answers, and one explanation that combines words and visuals.

## Chapter 4: Narrative Writing With Control
**Key concepts**: prompt, orientation, complication, climax, resolution, character, setting, dialogue, paragraph, cohesion.

**Practice questions**
- What problem will drive the story, and how will it be resolved?
- Where should a new paragraph begin because time, place, speaker, or focus changes?
- Which sentence adds detail without slowing the story too much?

**Student output**
- A planned narrative draft with a clear arc, purposeful paragraphs, at least one moment of dialogue or description, and a revision note explaining an improvement.

## Chapter 5: Persuasive Writing With Evidence
**Key concepts**: position, argument, reason, evidence, example, audience, introduction, paragraph, linking word, conclusion.

**Practice questions**
- What position will you take, and which two or three reasons support it?
- Which example makes the argument more convincing for the audience?
- How can linking words help the reader follow the argument?

**Student output**
- A persuasive response with an introduction, organised reasons, evidence or examples, linking words, and a conclusion that strengthens the position.

## Chapter 6: Language Conventions and Editing
**Key concepts**: spelling pattern, prefix, suffix, tense, subject-verb agreement, clause, phrase, pronoun reference, comma, quotation mark, apostrophe.

**Practice questions**
- Which spelling choice follows the pattern or word meaning?
- Which sentence has correct tense, agreement, or pronoun reference?
- Where should punctuation be added to make the sentence clear?

**Student output**
- An edited paragraph with marked corrections for spelling, grammar, punctuation, and sentence clarity, plus a short explanation of three choices.

## Chapter 7: Number, Fractions, Decimals, and Money
**Key concepts**: place value, operation, estimate, mental strategy, written method, fraction, equivalent, decimal, money, change, word problem.

**Practice questions**
- Which operation or combination of operations matches the problem?
- How can equivalent fractions, decimals, or money amounts be compared?
- What estimate helps you decide whether the answer is reasonable?

**Student output**
- A numeracy reasoning page with one multi-step whole-number problem, one fraction or decimal comparison, one money problem, and an estimate-and-check note.

## Chapter 8: Measurement, Space, Statistics, and Probability
**Key concepts**: perimeter, area, angle, 2D shape, 3D object, net, time, duration, column graph, dot plot, table, chance.

**Practice questions**
- How can a diagram help solve a perimeter, area, angle, or 3D object problem?
- What does the graph or table show, and what comparison can you make?
- Which everyday event is impossible, unlikely, likely, or certain?

**Student output**
- A mixed-domain problem set with a measurement solution, a geometry explanation, a data interpretation, and a probability statement justified in words.

## Capstone
Create a "NAPLAN Skills Studio" portfolio. Students assemble a balanced sample pack with one reading text and questions, one visual or data-rich text response, one narrative or persuasive writing piece, one edited conventions passage, and a numeracy set covering number, measurement, geometry, data, and chance. Each section includes a strategy note that explains how the student approached the task, checked the answer, or revised the work.

## Assessment Ideas
- Use reading responses to assess main idea, detail location, inference, vocabulary in context, author choice, and synthesis across written and visual information.
- Review writing for planning, structure, paragraphing, sentence variety, vocabulary, audience awareness, and meaningful revision.
- Assess language conventions through focused spelling, grammar, punctuation, and editing tasks that require students to justify selected corrections.
- Check numeracy for strategy choice, accuracy, efficient use of diagrams or models, estimation, multi-step reasoning, and clear answer sentences.
- Include short online-style practice sets so students become comfortable with the computer-based format, pacing, review screens, and mixed item types.
- Grade the capstone on domain coverage, accuracy, evidence of strategy use, quality of explanations, independence, and calm readiness.
