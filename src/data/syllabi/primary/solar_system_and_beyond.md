# Solar System & Beyond (Primary, ages 5-11)
**ID**: `planetsPrimary` - **Status**: playable

## Course Aim
Grow thoughtful young space scientists who can use models, observations, data, and scale thinking to explain the Solar System, the Moon, stars, galaxies, and space exploration. Learners build physical and drawn models, read real mission images carefully, compare planetary evidence, and practise saying "the model helps here, but it is not the real size or distance" when science needs caution.

## Course Design Notes
Primary astronomy should feel vast and inviting while staying scientifically careful. Many ideas are too large, far away, or slow to observe directly, so lessons should make model limits explicit and connect classroom demonstrations to real evidence from telescopes, spacecraft, and sky observations. Use safe investigation routines: never look at the Sun without approved specialist equipment, use indirect Sun observations only, avoid eclipse viewing without certified protection, and treat online images as evidence that needs source checking. Younger pupils can focus on day, night, Moon phases, and planet order; older pupils can add gravity, scale, light years, mission timelines, and evidence-based explanations.

## Chapter 1: Looking Up Like a Scientist
**Key concepts**: astronomy, observation, pattern, sky, horizon, Sun safety, model, evidence, scale, question, source.

**Practice questions**
- What can we observe in the sky safely during the day and at night?
- How is an observation different from a story, wish, or guess?
- Why do astronomers use models, photographs, measurements, and repeated observations?

**Student output**
- A sky scientist notebook page with one safe observation or sourced image study, labelled features, three evidence statements, one question, and a note about what cannot be concluded yet.

## Chapter 2: Our Solar System Neighbourhood
**Key concepts**: Sun, planet, dwarf planet, asteroid, comet, orbit, inner planet, outer planet, rocky planet, gas giant, ice giant, astronomical unit.

**Practice questions**
- What objects belong in the Solar System besides the eight planets?
- How can we group planets by position, size, surface, or composition?
- Why do classroom Solar System models often get size or distance wrong?

**Student output**
- A Solar System sorting display that orders the planets from the Sun, groups them by type, includes dwarf planets and small bodies, and labels at least two limits of the model.

## Chapter 3: Earth as a Planet
**Key concepts**: Earth, rotation, axis, day, night, atmosphere, water, land, orbit, year, tilt, season, hemisphere.

**Practice questions**
- How does Earth's rotation explain day and night?
- How does Earth's tilt help explain seasonal patterns?
- Why are seasons not caused by Earth being simply "nearer" or "farther" from the Sun?

**Student output**
- A Sun-Earth model explanation with labelled rotation, orbit, tilted axis, day-night boundary, and a short seasons explanation that uses light angle and day length.

## Chapter 4: The Moon, Phases, Tides, and Eclipses
**Key concepts**: Moon, satellite, phase, new Moon, crescent, quarter, gibbous, full Moon, orbit, tide, solar eclipse, lunar eclipse, shadow.

**Practice questions**
- Why does the Moon seem to change shape during the month?
- How are solar and lunar eclipses different?
- How does the Moon's gravity connect to ocean tides, and why is the full pattern more complex than one simple pull?

**Student output**
- A Moon phases and eclipse model set with labelled positions, phase names, shadow diagrams, one tide explanation, and a safety warning about never viewing the Sun directly.

## Chapter 5: Gravity, Motion, and Orbits
**Key concepts**: gravity, mass, weight, pull, orbit, motion, speed, path, microgravity, satellite, probe, rover.

**Practice questions**
- Why do objects have different weights on Earth, the Moon, Mars, or Jupiter?
- How can gravity and sideways motion work together to make an orbit?
- Why do astronauts in orbit feel weightless even though gravity is still acting?

**Student output**
- A gravity and orbit explainer using a labelled diagram, a comparison table for weight on different worlds, and a model note explaining what the classroom demonstration can and cannot show.

## Chapter 6: Stars, Constellations, and Galaxies
**Key concepts**: star, constellation, pattern, apparent brightness, distance, light year, Milky Way, galaxy, nebula, telescope, culture.

**Practice questions**
- Why are constellations patterns people imagine, not groups of nearby stars?
- How is the Sun like other stars, and how is it special to Earth?
- Why do astronomers use light years as a distance unit?

**Student output**
- A star and constellation fact sheet that distinguishes real stars from human-made patterns, explains light years as distance, and places the Sun inside the Milky Way galaxy.

## Chapter 7: Space Exploration and Mission Evidence
**Key concepts**: spacecraft, satellite, telescope, space station, rover, lander, probe, astronaut, mission, data, image, timeline, engineering.

**Practice questions**
- What can telescopes, orbiters, landers, rovers, and astronauts each help us learn?
- How did missions such as Apollo, Hubble, the International Space Station, Mars rovers, James Webb Space Telescope, and Artemis change the questions scientists can ask?
- Why should a mission image include a source, date, and explanation of what instrument collected it?

**Student output**
- A space mission evidence card set with at least five missions or tools, each showing the agency or team, date range, target, evidence collected, and one discovery or engineering challenge.

## Chapter 8: Beyond the Solar System
**Key concepts**: exoplanet, habitable zone, star system, nebula, stellar life cycle, supernova, black hole, universe, Big Bang model, evidence, uncertainty.

**Practice questions**
- How do scientists find planets around other stars when they are so far away?
- What is a habitable zone, and why does it not guarantee life?
- How can scientists talk about the Big Bang, black holes, and stellar life cycles using evidence while admitting what is still uncertain?

**Student output**
- A "beyond our Solar System" research poster explaining one big idea, such as exoplanets, star birth, supernovae, black holes, or galaxies, with a sourced image, careful vocabulary, and a section titled "What scientists know and what they are still studying."

## Capstone
Create a "Planetarium and Mission Briefing." Teams design a walk-through or tabletop exhibit that teaches younger pupils how we know about space. The exhibit must include a scale-aware Solar System model, a Sun-Earth-Moon model, a planet classification display, one gravity or orbit explanation, a mission evidence card, a beyond-the-Solar-System research note, safe sky-observation guidance, and a short presenter script that separates evidence, model, and imagination.

## Assessment Ideas
- Review sky observation notebooks for safe methods, accurate labels, evidence statements, and honest uncertainty.
- Assess Solar System sorting displays for correct planet order, planet groups, dwarf planets or small bodies, and model-limit notes.
- Check Earth and Moon models for accurate rotation, orbit, tilt, phases, eclipses, tides, and Sun safety warnings.
- Use gravity and orbit explainers to assess mass, weight, gravity, motion, and careful language about microgravity.
- Evaluate star and galaxy work for correct distinction between stars, constellations, the Sun, the Milky Way, and light years.
- Assess mission evidence cards for accurate dates or date ranges, agency names, instruments or spacecraft types, and links between data and discoveries.
- Grade the capstone on scientific accuracy, model clarity, source quality, teamwork, presenter explanation, and ability to name what each model leaves out.
