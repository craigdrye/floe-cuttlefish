# Ocean Science (Primary, ages 5-11)
**ID**: `oceanPrimary` | **Status**: playable

## Course Aim
Help primary learners understand the ocean as a living, moving Earth system that can be observed, measured, modelled, and protected. Children investigate ocean zones, water movement, marine food webs, animal adaptations, coasts, deep-sea exploration, and human impacts through concrete observations, simple experiments, maps, data, and careful science talk.

## Course Design Notes
Ocean science should feel wide, sensory, and evidence-rich: salt water on a spoon, light fading through blue water, shells sorted by shape, currents traced with colour, and maps showing how one ocean connects the planet. Lessons should use safe classroom models, NOAA and NASA images or data, videos from research vessels when direct observation is impossible, field sketches near local water if available, and frequent links between living things, physical conditions, technology, and stewardship.

## Chapter 1: One Ocean, Many Places
**Key concepts**: ocean, sea, coast, basin, continent, map, salt water, freshwater, shoreline, tide pool, open ocean, deep sea.

**Practice questions**
- How do maps show that Earth's ocean is connected?
- What is different about a coast, a coral reef, the open ocean, and the deep sea?
- Why is most ocean water salty?

**Student output**
- A labelled world ocean map with major ocean basins, one coast, one reef, one open-ocean area, one deep-sea feature, and a short note about connected water.

## Chapter 2: Ocean Zones and Light
**Key concepts**: sunlight zone, twilight zone, midnight zone, abyss, trench, pressure, temperature, photosynthesis, bioluminescence, adaptation.

**Practice questions**
- How do light, temperature, and pressure change as depth increases?
- Why do many plants and algae need the sunlight zone?
- How can deep-sea animals survive where sunlight does not reach?

**Student output**
- An ocean-depth model showing zones, depth ranges, light levels, sample organisms, and one evidence-based adaptation for each zone.

## Chapter 3: Waves, Tides, and Currents
**Key concepts**: wave, tide, current, wind, gravity, Moon, Sun, surface current, deep current, upwelling, model, pattern.

**Practice questions**
- What causes waves, and how are waves different from tides?
- How do the Moon and Sun help create tides?
- Why do currents matter for weather, nutrients, animals, and people?

**Student output**
- A water movement investigation page with a labelled wave or current model, observations from a tray or bottle activity, and a diagram explaining one real ocean movement.

## Chapter 4: Marine Food Webs and Ecosystems
**Key concepts**: producer, phytoplankton, algae, seagrass, coral reef, kelp forest, plankton, consumer, predator, prey, decomposer, food web.

**Practice questions**
- Why are tiny producers such as phytoplankton important to large ocean animals?
- How is a food web different from a simple food chain?
- What might happen if one part of a reef or kelp forest changes?

**Student output**
- A marine ecosystem food web for a reef, kelp forest, open ocean, or tide pool, with arrows showing energy flow and one change prediction.

## Chapter 5: Marine Animal Adaptations
**Key concepts**: fish, mammal, reptile, invertebrate, gill, lung, fin, flipper, blubber, camouflage, shell, tentacle, migration, behaviour.

**Practice questions**
- How do fish, whales, sea turtles, and octopuses meet their needs in different ways?
- Which adaptations help animals move, breathe, stay warm, hide, hunt, or migrate?
- Why should we compare animals by evidence rather than by first impressions?

**Student output**
- A marine adaptation comparison chart with at least four animals, labelled body features, habitat notes, and one claim about how each animal survives.

## Chapter 6: Coral, Kelp, Coasts, and Change
**Key concepts**: coral polyp, reef, symbiosis, bleaching, kelp, holdfast, coast, erosion, deposition, storm, habitat change, resilience.

**Practice questions**
- Why are corals animals even though reefs can look like rocks or plants?
- How do kelp forests and coral reefs give shelter and food to other organisms?
- How can storms, warming water, pollution, or erosion change coastal habitats?

**Student output**
- A habitat change case study with before-and-after diagrams, cause-and-effect arrows, and one realistic action that could reduce harm.

## Chapter 7: Exploring and Measuring the Ocean
**Key concepts**: observation, measurement, sonar, satellite, buoy, submersible, remotely operated vehicle, sample, data, map, model, uncertainty.

**Practice questions**
- Why is the deep ocean difficult for humans to explore directly?
- How do tools such as sonar, satellites, buoys, and remotely operated vehicles help scientists collect evidence?
- What can a data map show that one photograph cannot?

**Student output**
- An ocean explorer mission plan with a research question, tool choices, safety notes, expected data, and a labelled diagram of how one tool works.

## Chapter 8: Ocean Care and Climate Connections
**Key concepts**: conservation, pollution, plastic, microplastic, overfishing, protected area, sea level, warming, carbon dioxide, climate, stewardship, solution.

**Practice questions**
- How can plastic, overfishing, and warming seas affect ocean life?
- What is the difference between a local action and a global ocean problem?
- How can evidence help people choose fair and useful ocean solutions?

**Student output**
- An ocean action brief that explains one problem, shows evidence with a simple graph or diagram, compares two possible solutions, and recommends one practical step.

## Capstone
Create an "Ocean Research Station." Teams choose a question about an ocean zone, animal, food web, current, coastal habitat, exploration tool, or conservation problem. The station must include a map, labelled model, observation or data display, vocabulary card set, evidence-based explanation, and a hands-on visitor activity such as sorting adaptations, modelling currents, testing floating debris, or building a food web.

## Assessment Ideas
- Check maps for accurate ocean basin labels, coast and deep-sea features, and understanding that the ocean is connected.
- Assess zone models for correct depth order, light changes, pressure and temperature ideas, and animal adaptations matched to conditions.
- Review water movement investigations for clear distinction among waves, tides, and currents, and for careful observations from classroom models.
- Use food web work to evaluate producer-consumer relationships, arrows for energy flow, ecosystem vocabulary, and change predictions.
- Assess animal comparison charts for accurate breathing, movement, body covering, temperature, camouflage, and migration explanations.
- Evaluate habitat change and conservation briefs for cause-and-effect reasoning, age-appropriate climate connections, evidence use, and realistic stewardship.
- Grade the capstone on scientific accuracy, model quality, data or observation use, teamwork, visitor communication, and thoughtful revision.
