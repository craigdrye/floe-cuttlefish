# Entomology for Kids (Primary, ages 5-11)
**ID**: `bugsPrimary` - **Status**: playable

## Course Aim
Build curious, careful young entomologists who can observe small animals respectfully, classify arthropods from evidence, explain insect body plans and life cycles, and investigate how insects fit into ecosystems. Learners use hand lenses, drawings, simple models, field notes, safe outdoor observation, and student-made displays to connect wonder with accurate science.

## Course Design Notes
This course treats insects as living organisms to study, not toys or pests to handle roughly. Lessons should privilege observation before naming, comparison before memorising, and evidence before guessing. Use clear safety routines: observe outdoors with adult permission, avoid stinging or biting animals, return any temporary specimens to their habitat, wash hands after fieldwork, and use photos or museum resources when live observation is unsuitable. Younger pupils can focus on noticing, sorting, and drawing; older pupils can add variables, food-web reasoning, conservation evidence, and short research notes.

## Chapter 1: Becoming an Insect Scientist
**Key concepts**: entomology, observation, evidence, habitat, field note, hand lens, specimen, respect, safety, question, classification.

**Practice questions**
- What can you observe before you decide what an animal is?
- Which details belong in a useful field note: place, weather, behaviour, body parts, or guesswork?
- How can we study a tiny animal without harming it or its habitat?

**Student output**
- A fieldwork notebook page with a labelled observation drawing, location and date, three evidence-based observations, one question for further study, and a safety promise for respectful investigation.

## Chapter 2: Arthropods and Insects
**Key concepts**: arthropod, exoskeleton, jointed legs, insect, arachnid, myriapod, crustacean, segment, antenna, wing, classify.

**Practice questions**
- Which features do all arthropods share?
- How can you tell an insect from a spider, centipede, millipede, or woodlouse?
- Why is counting legs useful, and why should scientists also look for other evidence?

**Student output**
- A classification chart sorting common arthropods into insects, arachnids, myriapods, and crustaceans, with at least two evidence features for each decision.

## Chapter 3: Insect Body Plans
**Key concepts**: head, thorax, abdomen, antennae, compound eye, mouthpart, six legs, wing, spiracle, exoskeleton, moult.

**Practice questions**
- Which insect body parts help with sensing, moving, feeding, and breathing?
- Why do insects need to moult as they grow?
- How are the body plans of a beetle, butterfly, bee, and fly similar and different?

**Student output**
- A labelled insect anatomy model or diagram showing head, thorax, abdomen, legs, antennae, wings if present, eyes, and one note about how structure helps survival.

## Chapter 4: Life Cycles and Metamorphosis
**Key concepts**: life cycle, egg, larva, pupa, adult, nymph, complete metamorphosis, incomplete metamorphosis, growth, change, survival.

**Practice questions**
- How is complete metamorphosis different from incomplete metamorphosis?
- Why might a caterpillar and an adult butterfly eat different foods?
- What evidence would help you decide whether a young insect is a larva or a nymph?

**Student output**
- A life-cycle comparison wheel for two insects, one with complete metamorphosis and one with incomplete metamorphosis, including labelled stages and a short explanation of how the young and adults differ.

## Chapter 5: Insect Families and Orders
**Key concepts**: order, Coleoptera, Lepidoptera, Hymenoptera, Diptera, beetle, butterfly, moth, bee, ant, wasp, fly, feature, identification key.

**Practice questions**
- What clues help identify a beetle, butterfly, moth, bee, ant, wasp, or fly?
- How can a simple yes-or-no key help scientists classify an unknown insect?
- Why do scientists sometimes revise an identification after looking more closely?

**Student output**
- A classroom mini field guide with four insect orders, one example for each, a labelled feature drawing or photograph, and a simple identification key using observable traits.

## Chapter 6: Pollination, Decomposition, and Food Webs
**Key concepts**: pollination, pollen, flower, nectar, decomposer, predator, prey, herbivore, food chain, food web, ecosystem, interdependence.

**Practice questions**
- How can insects help some flowering plants make seeds?
- What roles do insects play as pollinators, decomposers, predators, prey, and herbivores?
- What might happen in a food web if one important insect group became much rarer?

**Student output**
- An ecosystem role map linking at least five insects or arthropods to plants, soil, birds, mammals, or other animals, with arrows that explain energy flow or pollination links.

## Chapter 7: Social Insects and Collective Behaviour
**Key concepts**: colony, queen, worker, drone, nest, pheromone, communication, division of labour, bee, ant, termite, cooperation.

**Practice questions**
- What jobs can different members of a bee, ant, or termite colony do?
- How can insects communicate without human language?
- Why is a termite mound or ant nest a structure made by many small actions?

**Student output**
- A colony systems poster showing roles, communication signals, nest structure, and one careful comparison between a bee colony, ant colony, or termite colony.

## Chapter 8: Adaptation, Threats, and Conservation
**Key concepts**: adaptation, camouflage, mimicry, warning colour, pollinator decline, habitat loss, pesticide, light pollution, citizen science, conservation.

**Practice questions**
- How can camouflage, warning colours, mimicry, or body shape help an insect survive?
- Which human activities can make life harder for insects?
- What can a school or family do that genuinely helps insects without creating unsafe conditions?

**Student output**
- A safe insect-help action plan, such as a pollinator planting proposal, night-light audit, leaf-litter habitat note, or bug hotel design, with reasons, risks, and a way to observe whether it helps.

## Capstone
Create an "Insect Investigation Museum." Teams choose a local insect or arthropod question, such as which microhabitat has the most visible arthropod diversity, how body parts help feeding or movement, or how a pollinator visits flowers. The final exhibit must include field notes or photo evidence, a classification decision, a labelled body diagram, a life-cycle explanation, an ecosystem role map, a conservation note, and a student-made model, guide, or display for younger visitors.

## Assessment Ideas
- Review field notes for accurate observation, dated location details, safe methods, and separation of evidence from guesses.
- Assess classification work for correct arthropod groups, observable traits, and careful use of identification keys.
- Check anatomy models for correct insect body regions, six legs attached to the thorax, antennae, eyes, wings if present, and function notes.
- Use life-cycle tasks to assess sequencing, complete versus incomplete metamorphosis, and comparison across species.
- Evaluate ecosystem maps for accurate pollination, decomposition, predator-prey, and food-web relationships.
- Assess colony work for clear roles, communication ideas, and avoidance of human-like explanations when simpler biological explanations fit.
- Grade conservation plans on scientific accuracy, safety, feasibility, evidence of local observation, and respectful treatment of living things.
