# Big Questions: Year 2
**ID**: `philosophyYear2` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Year**: 2 (ages 6-7)
**Frameworks**: P4C/SAPERE enquiry routines, CASEL SEL framework, UK KS1 citizenship and PSHE, early primary speaking and listening

## Course Aim
Strengthen children's confidence as young thinkers who can ask big questions, give reasons, notice evidence, listen to different views, and care about fairness. Year 2 keeps philosophy concrete and story-led while inviting children to compare ideas about justice, courage, belief, difference, imagination, art, community, and classroom democracy.

## Course Design Notes
Year 2 philosophy uses short stories, picture books, dilemmas, role-play, images, objects, puppets, and classroom incidents that have been safely fictionalised. Children still answer through drawing, movement, talk partners, sorting cards, and class charts, but they begin to build two-step reasons: "I think... because... and..." The teacher protects emotional safety by using imagined characters, offering opt-out choices, and valuing "not sure yet" as real thinking. Each chapter ends with a small artifact children can revisit before the capstone.

## Chapter 1: Fair, Equal, and What People Need
**Key concepts**: fair, equal, need, same, different, share, turn, justice.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Share a story where treats, turns, attention, or help are not given in the same way.
- Ask: "Is fair always the same?" and "When might someone need something different?"
- Compare equal sharing with helpful sharing using counters, snacks in pictures, or playground turns.

**Practice routine**
- Children place scenario cards under "same for everyone", "different because someone needs it", or "not sure yet."

**Tiny output**
- A fairness chart with drawings for same, need, turn, and reason.

## Chapter 2: Courage, Fear, and Wise Choices
**Key concepts**: brave, scared, safe, risk, help, stand up, reckless, wise.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Read a story where a character feels scared but still does something important.
- Ask: "Can you be brave and scared at the same time?" and "Is every risky choice brave?"
- Explore standing up for someone, asking for help, telling an adult, or choosing not to join a dangerous dare.

**Practice routine**
- Children sort examples into brave, unwise, kind help, or needs an adult.

**Tiny output**
- A tiny reflection card: "Brave can look like..." with a drawing or adult-scribed sentence.

## Chapter 3: Knowing, Believing, Guessing, and Checking
**Key concepts**: know, believe, guess, evidence, check, source, see, hear.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Present a mystery bag, changed classroom object, or picture with missing information.
- Ask: "How do we know?" and "What could we do if we are not sure?"
- Compare seeing, hearing from someone, reading, remembering, guessing, and testing.

**Practice routine**
- Children use evidence cards: I saw, someone told me, I read, I guessed, I checked.

**Tiny output**
- A "how I know" chart for future discussions and classroom investigations.

## Chapter 4: Community and Helping Each Other
**Key concepts**: community, job, help, responsibility, neighbour, classroom, common good.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Map people who help in school, at home, in the street, or in a story village.
- Ask: "Why do people need each other?" and "What happens if nobody helps?"
- Discuss shared places: classroom, library, playground, park, bus, path, or lunch table.

**Practice routine**
- Children build a web of helpers and draw lines showing who helps whom.

**Tiny output**
- A community helper web with one sentence stem: "We help by..."

## Chapter 5: Difference, Respect, and Prejudice
**Key concepts**: respect, difference, same, group, unfair judgement, include, learn.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Use a story where someone is judged before others know them.
- Ask: "Can we know what someone is like just by looking?" and "What is a fair way to find out?"
- Explore differences in games, languages, food, families, bodies, beliefs, and ideas using care and privacy.

**Practice routine**
- Children practise respectful questions and noticing statements: "I notice...", "I wonder...", "Can you tell me about...?"

**Tiny output**
- A class respect agreement with child-drawn examples of listening, including, and not teasing.

## Chapter 6: Promises, Sorry, and Second Chances
**Key concepts**: promise, trust, mistake, apology, repair, forgive, second chance, consequence.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Tell puppet stories about a broken promise, an accident, a repeated problem, and a real repair.
- Ask: "What makes a sorry helpful?" and "Should everyone get another chance?"
- Compare punishment, protection, teaching, fixing, and making things right.

**Practice routine**
- Children choose repair cards for scenarios: tell the truth, return it, clean it, comfort, give space, try a new plan.

**Tiny output**
- A repair path strip: stop, tell, listen, fix, try again.

## Chapter 7: Imagination, Possibility, and Better Worlds
**Key concepts**: imagine, possible, impossible, change, invention, hope, consequence.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Ask playful "what if?" questions: no rules for a day, everyone could fly, toys could talk, or no one ever made mistakes.
- Explore which changes would be fun, fair, confusing, unsafe, or helpful.
- Ask: "Could the world be different?" and "What would you change to help people?"

**Practice routine**
- Children draw one imagined change and add one consequence with a partner.

**Tiny output**
- A "better world" drawing with the sentence stem: "I would change... because..."

## Chapter 8: Art, Choice, and Class Democracy
**Key concepts**: art, choice, vote, voice, reason, majority, minority, decide, respect.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Show varied child-safe images or classroom creations and ask, "Is this art? Who decides?"
- Connect choice-making to class life: books, games, jobs, displays, rules, or project themes.
- Ask: "Should children get a vote?" and "How do we care for people who voted differently?"

**Practice routine**
- Children hold a low-stakes class vote, give reasons before voting, and reflect on how the decision felt.

**Tiny output**
- A voting-and-voice chart showing proposal, reasons, vote, decision, and respect for the minority.

## Capstone
Create a "Young Thinkers Fair" for another class, family group, or buddy audience. Small groups prepare one big-question station using a story card, two possible answers, a reason stem, a drawing or sorting activity, and a gentle talk move. Stations may include fairness, courage, checking truth, community, respect, second chances, imagination, art, or voting. Each child practises explaining one idea and asking visitors, "What do you think, and why?"

## Assessment Ideas
- Observe whether children can give a reason, add a second reason, ask for evidence, and respond respectfully to a different view.
- Review class charts, sorting mats, "how I know" cards, repair strips, respect agreements, and better-world drawings.
- Use paired-talk snapshots to note listening, turn-taking, building on another idea, and use of "not sure yet."
- Check scenario responses for fair reasoning about needs, safety, repair, inclusion, and consequences.
- Assess the capstone on clarity of question, two possible answers, reason-giving, audience listening, and respectful facilitation.
