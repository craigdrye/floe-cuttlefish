# Money Basics
**ID**: `col-money-basics` | **Discipline**: Tech & Life | **Year**: 1 (ages 5-6)
**Frameworks**: Early financial literacy, CFPB Money as You Grow, JumpStart/CEE National Standards, classroom numeracy links

## Course Aim
Help children notice what money is for: choosing, paying, saving, sharing, and keeping track. Learners use play shops, coins, drawings, stories, and role-play to build confidence with simple money choices.

## Course Design Notes
At ages 5-6, money learning should stay concrete. Children handle coins, sort picture cards, count small amounts, act out buying and selling, and talk about fair choices. The course avoids formal budgeting language and instead builds habits: pause, count, choose, save, give, and check.

## Chapter 1: What Money Does
- **Key concepts**: coins, notes, price, buy, sell, pay, exchange, value.
- **Practice activities**: sort real or play coins; match classroom objects to price tags; act out swapping money for an item.
- **Teacher prompts**: What is the money for? Which item has a price? What happens after we pay?
- **Work product**: a picture page showing three things people can do with money.

## Chapter 2: Coins, Notes, and Values
- **Key concepts**: same/different, more/less, worth, coin size, coin value, note value.
- **Practice activities**: sort coins by colour, size, and number; order a small set of coins by value; make coin rubbings or drawings.
- **Teacher prompts**: Which coin is worth more? Can a smaller coin be worth more? How do you know?
- **Work product**: a coin museum mat with labels and value order.

## Chapter 3: Counting Small Amounts
- **Key concepts**: count on, total, altogether, equal amount, different ways.
- **Practice activities**: count pennies or counters into pots; make 5, 10, and 20 using different coin sets; clap-count totals with a partner.
- **Teacher prompts**: How much is altogether? Can you make the same amount another way? Did we count every coin once?
- **Work product**: a "ways to make 10" money mat.

## Chapter 4: Needs and Wants
- **Key concepts**: need, want, choice, enough, not enough, important, extra.
- **Practice activities**: sort picture cards into needs and wants; pack a pretend shopping basket for a family meal; choose one treat from three options.
- **Teacher prompts**: Do we need it or want it? Can something be both? What should we buy first?
- **Work product**: a needs-and-wants poster with drawings and simple labels.

## Chapter 5: Saving for Later
- **Key concepts**: save, spend, wait, goal, jar, bank, later.
- **Practice activities**: add tokens to a class saving jar; draw a saving goal; sequence cards showing "want it, save, wait, buy."
- **Teacher prompts**: What are we saving for? How many more tokens do we need? Why might waiting help?
- **Work product**: a simple saving strip with spaces to colour in as money is saved.

## Chapter 6: Playing Shop
- **Key concepts**: customer, shopkeeper, price tag, pay, receipt, check, fair.
- **Practice activities**: run a classroom shop with labelled items; practise asking "How much?"; check that the customer has enough coins.
- **Teacher prompts**: What does the customer want? How much does it cost? Did the shopkeeper give the right item?
- **Work product**: a labelled shop role-play card for customer or shopkeeper.

## Chapter 7: Change and Giving
- **Key concepts**: change, left over, give, share, charity, thank you.
- **Practice activities**: pay 10 for an item that costs 6 and count what is left; use counters to show giving one coin to help; role-play a donation box.
- **Teacher prompts**: How much is left? Who could this help? How can we check the change?
- **Work product**: a three-part money drawing: spend some, save some, give some.

## Chapter 8: Cards, Phones, and Digital Money
- **Key concepts**: card, phone pay, account, tap, safe, adult help, money still counts.
- **Practice activities**: compare coin-paying and pretend card-paying; sequence "tap, check, receipt"; sort safe and unsafe money choices.
- **Teacher prompts**: If we tap a card, is it free? Who should help with card or phone money? How can we check what was paid?
- **Work product**: a digital money safety card with three child-friendly rules.

## Capstone
Create and run a tiny class shop. Children design price tags, choose needs and wants for sale, take turns as customer and shopkeeper, count small payments, decide whether to save or spend tokens, and draw one thing they learned about money.

## Assessment Ideas
- Observe whether children can sort coins or pictures by simple features and explain one choice.
- Use role-play to check "enough/not enough," paying, and simple change with counters.
- Ask children to sort needs and wants, then explain one item in child language.
- Review the capstone shop mat for price labels, counting, and a clear save/spend/give choice.

## Research Notes
- UK financial education guidance often places money learning inside mathematics, PSHE, and everyday classroom contexts for younger children.
- CFPB Money as You Grow emphasizes age-appropriate family and classroom activities for building money habits: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/consumer-tools/money-as-you-grow/
- JumpStart/CEE National Standards for Personal Financial Education support early, repeated learning about earning, spending, saving, and decision-making: https://www.jumpstart.org/education/national-standards/
- Young children benefit from concrete manipulatives, storybooks, play shops, and repeated talk about choices before abstract finance vocabulary.
