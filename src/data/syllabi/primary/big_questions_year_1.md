# Big Questions: Year 1
**ID**: `philosophyYear1` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Year**: 1 (ages 5-6)
**Frameworks**: P4C/SAPERE enquiry routines, CASEL SEL framework, EYFS communication and personal development, UK primary citizenship and PSHE

## Course Aim
Help children move from "I feel..." toward "I think... because..." through warm, story-led philosophy circles. Children wonder about rules, kindness, truth, living things, feelings, identity, and friendship while learning to listen, give small reasons, change their mind gently, and notice that good questions can have more than one thoughtful answer.

## Course Design Notes
Year 1 philosophy should feel like circle time with better questions. Lessons begin with a picture book, puppet problem, classroom story, object, photo, or simple "what if?" prompt. Children answer by talking, drawing, sorting cards, standing on opinion lines, whispering to a partner, or adding to a class chart. The teacher models sentence stems such as "I think... because...", "I agree with...", "I wonder...", and "Can you say more?" No child is pushed to share private family experiences; imagined characters and classroom scenarios keep discussion safe.

## Chapter 1: Wondering Together
**Key concepts**: question, wonder, idea, reason, listen, agree, disagree, maybe.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Share a strange object, picture-book page, or puppet question and collect children's wonders.
- Sort questions into "quick answer" and "big question" piles.
- Ask: "What makes this a question we can think about together?"

**Practice routine**
- Children use paired talk before sharing: one child says an idea, the other asks, "Why do you think that?"

**Tiny output**
- A class "wonder wall" with drawings, sticky notes, or adult-scribed questions.

## Chapter 2: Rules That Help
**Key concepts**: rule, safe, fair, unfair, choice, consequence, responsibility, classroom.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Use a story where a character breaks, follows, or changes a rule.
- Compare rules that keep people safe with rules that only make someone bossy.
- Ask: "Who does this rule help?" and "Could children help make it better?"

**Practice routine**
- Children vote with thumbs, corners, or picture cards: fair rule, unfair rule, or not sure yet.

**Tiny output**
- A class chart of "rules that help us learn, play, and feel safe."

## Chapter 3: Friendship and Belonging
**Key concepts**: friend, kind, include, left out, different, same, repair, forgive.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Pause a friendship story before the problem is solved and ask what each character might need.
- Explore whether friends must like the same games, clothes, food, or ideas.
- Ask: "Can someone be kind even when they say no?"

**Practice routine**
- Pairs practise tiny scripts: "Can I play?", "Let's make room", "I need a turn", "I am sorry. Can I fix it?"

**Tiny output**
- A friendship phrase poster with child drawings and adult-scribed words.

## Chapter 4: Feelings and Reasons
**Key concepts**: feeling, body clue, reason, choice, calm, upset, proud, sorry.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Use face cards, story scenes, or puppets to notice feelings and clues.
- Ask: "Can two people feel different things about the same event?"
- Wonder about whether feelings can be wrong, or whether choices can be better or worse.

**Practice routine**
- Children complete sentence stems orally or with cards: "The character might feel... because..." and "A kind choice could be..."

**Tiny output**
- A feelings-and-reasons matching mat or class chart.

## Chapter 5: Truth, Mistakes, and Trust
**Key concepts**: true, false, lie, mistake, guess, promise, trust, check.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Tell small puppet scenarios: a wrong answer, a pretend story, a hidden accident, a promise forgotten.
- Sort examples into lie, mistake, guess, joke, or pretend.
- Ask: "Why can telling the truth be hard?" and "How can we check?"

**Practice routine**
- Children practise gentle checking language: "How do you know?", "Did you see it?", "Could it be a mistake?"

**Tiny output**
- A "truth and checking" card with pictures for saw, heard, guessed, and checked.

## Chapter 6: Caring for Living Things
**Key concepts**: living thing, need, care, harm, gentle, animal, plant, place.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Use a nature photo, school plant, toy animal, or story about a park, garden, pet, or bug.
- Ask: "Do animals have feelings?" and "What do plants and animals need from us?"
- Talk about small choices: picking flowers, wasting water, litter, loud noises, or touching creatures.

**Practice routine**
- Children place scenario cards on a care line from "helps" to "harms" and give one reason.

**Tiny output**
- A child-drawn care promise for people, animals, plants, or classroom spaces.

## Chapter 7: Me, You, and What Makes Us Us
**Key concepts**: name, body, family, memory, favourite, change, same, different, respect.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Read a story about growing, moving, changing, or being different.
- Ask: "What makes you you?" and "Are you still you if your favourite thing changes?"
- Notice that people can share some things and differ in other things.

**Practice routine**
- Children draw a "same and different" pair portrait with a partner, then share one respectful noticing.

**Tiny output**
- A reflection card: "I am me because..." with drawing, symbols, or adult-scribed words.

## Chapter 8: Good Reasons and Gentle Disagreement
**Key concepts**: reason, example, because, agree, disagree, listen, change my mind, build.

**Circle-time invitations**
- Revisit a favourite big question from the course and collect two or three different answers.
- Model the difference between "I just want it" and "I think it is fair because..."
- Ask: "What did someone say that helped your thinking?"

**Practice routine**
- Use discussion tokens for talk moves: agree, disagree, add, ask, and change my mind.

**Tiny output**
- A class sentence-stem chart for philosophical talk.

## Capstone
Create a "Big Questions Circle Book" as a class. Each child contributes one page with a drawing, a big question, a sentence stem, and one reason. The book can include pages on fair rules, friendship, truth, feelings, caring for nature, and identity. Children share selected pages in a final circle, practise listening to another answer, and add one new wonder to the class wonder wall.

## Assessment Ideas
- Observe participation in circle routines: listening, turn-taking, using sentence stems, asking questions, and responding to classmates.
- Collect drawings, sorting mats, paired-talk notes, tiny reflection cards, and class charts as evidence of thinking.
- Use short adult-child conferences: "What do you think?", "Why?", "Can someone think something different?"
- Track whether children can distinguish a lie, mistake, guess, pretend story, and checked fact in simple scenarios.
- Assess the capstone on curiosity, one clear reason, respectful listening, and willingness to revisit or add to an idea.
