# Big Questions: Year 3
**ID**: `philosophyYear3` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Year**: 3 (ages 7-8)
**Frameworks**: P4C/SAPERE enquiry routines, CASEL SEL framework, UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, UK KS2 citizenship and PSHE, primary media literacy

## Course Aim
Help children become thoughtful discussion-circle members who can ask big questions about rights, responsibility, fairness, power, history, truth, and the planet. Year 3 keeps examples concrete and close to children's lives, while introducing beginning argument structure: claim, reason, example, and careful response to someone else's idea.

## Course Design Notes
Year 3 philosophy works best through short stories, school and community dilemmas, images, simple news extracts, role-play, maps, classroom votes, and teacher-made characters. Children practise saying "My claim is...", "My reason is...", "For example...", "I agree because...", and "I disagree because..." without turning discussion into a debate to win. Sensitive topics such as poverty, conflict, family life, rights, or the environment should be explored through fictionalised scenarios and age-appropriate public examples, with no child asked to speak for a group or disclose private experiences.

## Chapter 1: Rights, Needs, and Responsibilities
**Key concepts**: right, need, want, responsibility, respect, voice, safety, education.

**Practice questions**
- What is the difference between something you want and something every child needs?
- Should children have a right to be heard in school decisions?
- If we have a right to learn, what responsibilities might come with it?

**Student output**
- A rights-and-responsibilities matching chart using classroom, family, playground, and community examples.

## Chapter 2: Fair Choices for the Planet
**Key concepts**: environment, care, consequence, choice, pollution, climate, shared place, trade-off.

**Practice questions**
- Does one person's small action matter if lots of other people do nothing?
- Who should help look after a park, beach, street, river, or school garden?
- Can a choice be good for some people now but harmful later?

**Student output**
- A consequence chain showing one everyday choice, who it affects, and one reason for a better option.

## Chapter 3: History, Memory, and Stories
**Key concepts**: past, memory, evidence, story, source, monument, perspective, remember.

**Practice questions**
- Why do people tell stories about the past?
- Can two people remember the same event differently?
- Who decides what a school, town, or country should remember?

**Student output**
- A source-sorting mat that separates fact, memory, myth, opinion, and "need to check."

## Chapter 4: Power, Rules, and Authority
**Key concepts**: power, authority, rule, law, permission, democracy, leader, fairness.

**Practice questions**
- What makes a rule fair: who made it, why it exists, or how it treats people?
- When should children follow an instruction, and when should they ask a question?
- Is voting always the fairest way to decide?

**Student output**
- A classroom authority map showing different decision-makers and the reasons their power can be fair or unfair.

## Chapter 5: Truth, Evidence, and Checking
**Key concepts**: truth, evidence, source, claim, opinion, rumour, check, reliable.

**Practice questions**
- How can a rumour spread at school or online before anyone checks it?
- What is the difference between a fact, an opinion, and a guess?
- What makes a source more trustworthy?

**Student output**
- A "check before sharing" card with three steps: pause, ask for evidence, compare with another source.

## Chapter 6: Loyalty, Honesty, and Hard Choices
**Key concepts**: honesty, loyalty, friendship, harm, intention, consequence, bystander, courage.

**Practice questions**
- If a friend has done something wrong, what do you owe your friend and what do you owe others?
- Can telling the truth ever feel unkind?
- Are you responsible if you see something unfair and do nothing?

**Student output**
- A dilemma ladder that names the choice, the people affected, two values in tension, and a possible action.

## Chapter 7: Community, Belonging, and the Common Good
**Key concepts**: community, belonging, common good, neighbour, service, public place, inclusion.

**Practice questions**
- What makes a classroom, club, street, or town feel like a community?
- Should everyone help with shared jobs, even if they did not make the mess?
- How can a group include someone without forcing everyone to be the same?

**Student output**
- A community promise poster with one claim, one reason, and one practical example.

## Chapter 8: Building Better Arguments
**Key concepts**: claim, reason, example, evidence, counterexample, agree, disagree, revise.

**Practice questions**
- What makes a reason stronger than "because I like it"?
- Can a good example change someone's mind?
- How do we disagree with an idea while still respecting the person?

**Student output**
- A four-part argument strip: claim, because, for example, and "someone might think..."

## Capstone
Create a "Rights and Reasons Forum" for classmates, families, or a buddy class. Small groups choose one big question from the course, such as whether children should help make school rules, whether one small environmental action matters, or when honesty is difficult. Each group prepares a short scenario, two possible answers, one claim with a reason and example, and one question for visitors. The final circle asks children to name one idea they kept, one idea they changed, and one question they still have.

## Assessment Ideas
- Observe use of claim, reason, example, listening, turn-taking, and respectful disagreement during enquiries.
- Review rights charts, consequence chains, source mats, authority maps, checking cards, dilemma ladders, community posters, and argument strips.
- Use short conference prompts: "What is your claim?", "What reason supports it?", "What example helps?", and "What might someone else say?"
- Check whether students can distinguish fact, opinion, memory, myth, guess, rumour, and checked evidence in age-appropriate scenarios.
- Assess the capstone on clarity of question, balanced scenario, reasoned answer, use of example, and ability to invite others into discussion.
