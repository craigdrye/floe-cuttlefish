# Big Questions: Year 3
**ID**: `philosophyYear3` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 3 (ages 7–8)

## Course Philosophy
Year 3 marks the start of KS2 philosophy. Children are now capable of sustained reasoning across several steps. The focus shifts to **constructing arguments, evaluating evidence, and exploring moral complexity**. Topics connect to the broader world — environment, history, justice — not just personal experience.

## Module 1: Rights & Responsibilities (20%)
- What is a "right"? Something every person deserves
- Children's rights: UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (simplified)
- With rights come responsibilities — you have a right to education, and a responsibility to learn
- "Should children have the same rights as adults? Why or why not?"
- Right to play, right to safety, right to be heard
- **Questions**: Rights identification, responsibility matching, dilemma reasoning

## Module 2: The Environment & Our Choices (20%)
- Do humans have a responsibility to protect the planet?
- Climate change in simple terms: the Earth is getting warmer because of what humans do
- Individual vs collective action: "Does it matter if just I recycle?"
- Endangered species: should we save them? Why?
- Trade-offs: jobs vs environment (a factory that pollutes but employs people)
- **Questions**: Environmental dilemmas, action-consequence reasoning, trade-off evaluation

## Module 3: History, Memory & Stories (20%)
- Why do we learn about the past?
- Who decides which stories get told? (History is written by the winners)
- Myths and legends vs historical facts — what's the difference?
- Monuments and memorials: what should we remember and why?
- "If you could go back in time and change one thing, what would it be?"
- **Questions**: Fact vs myth sorting, perspective reasoning, historical judgment

## Module 4: Power & Authority (20%)
- Who has power? (Parents, teachers, government, police, older siblings)
- Is all power fair? What makes authority legitimate?
- Democracy: people choosing their leaders
- Dictators and kings: what happens when one person has all the power?
- Rules vs laws: school rules vs country laws — what's the difference?
- **Questions**: Authority identification, fairness of power scenarios, democracy reasoning

## Module 5: Complex Moral Reasoning (20%)
- "The ends justify the means" — is it okay to do something wrong for a good reason?
- Stealing bread to feed your family: right or wrong?
- Loyalty vs honesty: your friend did something wrong — do you tell?
- Bystander responsibility: if you see something wrong and do nothing, are you partly responsible?
- The golden rule across cultures: "Treat others as you want to be treated"
- **Questions**: Multi-layered dilemmas, competing-values scenarios, cultural comparison

## Stretch Zone
- Introduction to logical arguments: premise + premise = conclusion
- Identifying weak arguments: "That's what everyone thinks" (appeal to popularity)
- Philosophy of language: "What do words mean? Who decides?"
- Utilitarianism for kids: "The greatest good for the greatest number — is that always right?"
- Thought experiment: "The ship of Theseus" — if you replace every plank, is it the same ship?
