# Bugs and Minibeasts
**ID**: `bugsPreschool` | **Status**: playable | **Level**: Preschool / Early Years (ages 3-5)

## Course Aim
Invite young children to notice, wonder about, and care for tiny living things. Children learn that minibeasts have different bodies, homes, movements, patterns, and needs, and that gentle observation helps us understand them without hurting them.

## Course Design Notes
This course is playful, sensory, and adult-facilitated. Learning happens through story baskets, outdoor looking walks, magnifiers, model minibeasts, movement games, sorting trays, drawing, clay, songs, and short conversations. Adults model safe handling: look first, touch only when invited and safe, use soft brushes or pots, return creatures to their homes, and wash hands afterwards. The emphasis is curiosity and care, not fear, collecting, or memorising long facts.

## Chapter 1: Tiny Creature Detectives
**Story and activity invitations**
- Start with a picture book, puppet, or pretend magnifier hunt for "small things that move."
- Explore the idea of minibeasts: insects, spiders, worms, snails, slugs, woodlice, centipedes, and millipedes.
- Look closely at photos, models, shells, shed skins, leaves with holes, soil, bark, and garden clues.

**Adult prompts**
- What do you notice first?
- Where might this tiny creature like to hide?
- How can we look carefully and kindly?

**Tiny output**
- Add one drawing, photo, or adult-scribed noticing sentence to a class "tiny creature detective" wall.

## Chapter 2: Bodies, Legs, and Sorts
**Story and activity invitations**
- Count legs on safe pictures or models: insects have 6 legs, spiders have 8, worms have none, and woodlice have many.
- Build minibeast bodies with playdough, loose parts, pipe cleaners, buttons, and natural materials.
- Sort creatures by "has wings," "has shell," "many legs," "no legs," "long body," or "round body."

**Adult prompts**
- What body parts can you see?
- How many legs should we count together?
- What is the same and what is different?

**Tiny output**
- Create a simple sorting mat with child-placed creature cards and one shared rule.

## Chapter 3: Safe Looking and Gentle Handling
**Story and activity invitations**
- Practise a "look, ask, scoop, watch, return" routine with toy creatures before going outdoors.
- Use magnifiers, white trays, bug viewers, clipboards, and photo cards with adult supervision.
- Discuss which creatures are for looking only, such as bees, wasps, unknown spiders, or anything moving quickly away.

**Adult prompts**
- Does this creature want space?
- What can we use instead of fingers?
- Where should we put it back?

**Tiny output**
- Make a class care code with pictures for soft hands, quiet watching, home returning, and handwashing.

## Chapter 4: Homes Under, Over, and Around
**Story and activity invitations**
- Search for habitats: under logs, in soil, on leaves, near flowers, in compost, by damp stones, and in long grass.
- Match minibeasts to homes: worm and soil, bee and flower, spider and web, snail and damp leaf, ant and nest.
- Build a pretend habitat tray with leaves, pebbles, bark, soil, sticks, and paper flowers.

**Adult prompts**
- What does this place give the creature: food, hiding, water, warmth, or safety?
- Is it damp or dry? Light or dark?
- What would happen if we moved its home?

**Tiny output**
- Add one habitat square to a shared minibeast map of the garden, park, or classroom tray.

## Chapter 5: Moving Like Minibeasts
**Story and activity invitations**
- Move like ants marching, butterflies fluttering, worms wriggling, beetles crawling, spiders tiptoeing, and snails sliding.
- Compare fast, slow, smooth, wiggly, jumpy, crawling, flying, and still bodies.
- Make movement trails with ribbon, chalk, finger paint, or toy tracks.

**Adult prompts**
- How does this creature move without bumping?
- Which body part helps it move?
- Can your body show "crawl," "flutter," "wiggle," or "slide"?

**Tiny output**
- Create a class movement chart with marks, photos, or drawings for three minibeast moves.

## Chapter 6: Colors, Patterns, and Camouflage
**Story and activity invitations**
- Notice stripes, spots, shiny shells, soft bodies, wings, and colors that help creatures hide or warn.
- Hide paper minibeasts on bark, leaves, flowers, stones, and fabric to test camouflage.
- Paint butterfly wings with folded paper, make ladybird spots, or print beetle patterns with blocks.

**Adult prompts**
- Which creature is easiest to find? Which one hides well?
- What pattern can you see?
- Why might a tiny creature need to blend in?

**Tiny output**
- Make a pattern card showing spots, stripes, matching colors, or a hiding place.

## Chapter 7: Life Cycles and Growing Up
**Story and activity invitations**
- Sequence simple life cycles: egg, caterpillar, chrysalis, butterfly; egg, larva, ladybird; egg, baby, adult for familiar creatures.
- Use scarves, hoops, blocks, or body poses to act out change over time.
- Compare "baby looks like adult" and "baby changes a lot" using photos and story cards.

**Adult prompts**
- What comes first? What changes next?
- Does the baby look like the grown-up?
- What does a growing creature need?

**Tiny output**
- Build a three- or four-part life-cycle strip with pictures, loose parts, or adult-scribed labels.

## Chapter 8: Caring for Tiny Living Things
**Story and activity invitations**
- Explore helpful minibeast jobs: bees visit flowers, worms help soil, ladybirds eat aphids, spiders catch small insects, snails recycle soft leaves.
- Plant flowers, care for a wormery with expert guidance, keep a damp leaf corner, or design a simple bug hotel with safe natural materials.
- Talk gently about fears: some people feel nervous around small creatures, and we can still be kind and careful.

**Adult prompts**
- How can we help this creature stay safe?
- What should we leave alone?
- What can our class do for flowers, soil, and hiding places?

**Tiny output**
- Choose one class action: plant, water, return, tidy carefully, make a shelter, or make a "please look gently" sign.

## Capstone
Create a "Minibeast Care Walk." Children help prepare looking tools, choose a safe observation route, notice at least three minibeast clues, use the class care code, and return any moved materials. Back indoors, the group builds a shared display with habitat maps, movement marks, body sorts, pattern cards, and one caring action for the outdoor space.

## Assessment Ideas
- Observe children using gentle looking routines, including asking before touching and returning creatures or materials.
- Listen for concrete vocabulary: legs, wings, shell, web, soil, flower, damp, hide, crawl, flutter, wriggle, egg, caterpillar, butterfly.
- Use photo portfolios of sorting mats, habitat trays, life-cycle strips, and movement charts.
- Invite children to explain one minibeast need with a drawing, gesture, model, or adult-scribed phrase.
- Review the capstone for careful observation, safe behaviour, simple classification, habitat awareness, and kindness toward living things.
