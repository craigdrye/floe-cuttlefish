# Primary Foundations
**ID**: `primary` | **Status**: playable | **Discipline**: Academics
**Frameworks**: EYFS, EYLF, NAEYC Developmentally Appropriate Practice, Common Core K Foundational Skills and Mathematics

## Course Aim
Help preschool children feel ready for the routines, language, numbers, movement, curiosity, and social habits of early primary school. The course builds school readiness through play, sensory exploration, shared talk, songs, stories, movement games, object handling, pretend classroom routines, and gentle adult-facilitated practice.

## Course Design Notes
Primary foundations should feel like a well-held bridge, not an early test. Children learn by touching, moving, listening, looking, copying, wondering, choosing, and trying again with a trusted adult nearby. Each chapter uses predictable routines: gather, notice, play, say, make, move, and reflect. Learning spans emergent literacy, early number, shape and space, social participation, physical confidence, self-help routines, and curiosity about the world. Recording is light and concrete: photos, drawings, adult-scribed words, sorting mats, block builds, movement games, and small artifacts children can revisit.

## Chapter 1: Welcome to Our Learning Day
**Circle-time invitations**
- Practice arrival routines: greeting, finding a name card, choosing a place, putting belongings away, and joining the group.
- Use picture cards for "first, next, then" so children can see the shape of the day.
- Ask: "What helps you feel ready?" and "Who can help if something feels tricky?"

**Play and practice**
- Act out school routines with puppets: saying hello, asking for help, waiting, tidying, choosing an activity, and coming back to the group.
- Sing a welcome song with names, claps, rhythm sticks, or soft movement.

**Tiny output**
- Build a class routine strip with photos, drawings, or symbols for arrival, play, snack, story, outdoor time, and goodbye.

## Chapter 2: Listening, Speaking, and Story Sharing
**Circle-time invitations**
- Share short picture books, oral stories, puppets, and familiar songs with repeated phrases.
- Invite children to listen for names, sounds, rhymes, actions, and story clues.
- Ask: "What happened first?" "What might happen next?" and "How did the character feel?"

**Play and practice**
- Retell a simple story with three picture cards, props, or small-world toys.
- Practice turn-taking talk with a talking object, partner echo, or "my turn, your turn" chant.

**Tiny output**
- Make a story path with beginning, middle, and end pictures plus adult-scribed child words.

## Chapter 3: Marks, Letters, and Names
**Circle-time invitations**
- Notice that print carries meaning on name cards, labels, signs, books, menus, maps, and class jobs.
- Explore letters through sand trays, chalk, paint, magnetic letters, air writing, and name puzzles.
- Ask: "What do you notice about your name?" and "Where else have you seen this letter?"

**Play and practice**
- Match name cards to photos, build names with loose letters, and hunt for beginning sounds in classroom objects.
- Offer mark-making choices: big paper, clipboards, envelopes, pretend lists, labels, cards, and whiteboards.

**Tiny output**
- Create a name-and-mark page with a photo, name card, child-made marks, and one adult-scribed sentence.

## Chapter 4: Counting With Bodies and Objects
**Circle-time invitations**
- Count steps, claps, jumps, fingers, snack pieces, blocks, buttons, toy animals, and children in the group.
- Compare small sets using more, fewer, same, enough, not enough, one more, and one less.
- Ask: "How can we make sure each object gets one count?" and "How many are there altogether?"

**Play and practice**
- Use counters, shells, cubes, beads, ten frames, dot cards, and finger patterns for counting and subitising to small amounts.
- Play hide-and-count games where children check whether the last number tells "how many."

**Tiny output**
- Make a number treasure mat for one chosen number, showing objects, dots, fingers, marks, and a numeral when ready.

## Chapter 5: Shapes, Patterns, and Spatial Words
**Circle-time invitations**
- Find circles, squares, triangles, rectangles, lines, corners, curves, towers, roads, boxes, and wheels in the room.
- Build and copy AB, ABB, and ABC patterns with beads, blocks, sounds, actions, colors, or natural objects.
- Ask: "What is the same?" "What changes?" and "Where is it: under, beside, between, inside, or behind?"

**Play and practice**
- Sort loose parts by color, shape, size, texture, or use, then invite children to explain the sorting rule.
- Build obstacle paths and block scenes that require positional language.

**Tiny output**
- Create a class shape-and-pattern board with photos of child-built patterns, towers, tracks, and sorted collections.

## Chapter 6: Moving, Drawing, and Fine-Motor Confidence
**Circle-time invitations**
- Connect whole-body movement to learning: cross the midline, balance, crawl, stretch, jump, trace big shapes, and move to rhythm.
- Explore hand strength and coordination through dough, tweezers, pegs, threading, pouring, cutting with supervision, and finger songs.
- Ask: "What can your body do carefully?" and "What helps your hand get ready to draw or write?"

**Play and practice**
- Rotate sensory stations: dough letters, bead strings, water pouring, chalk paths, brush painting, peg boards, and construction.
- Use large-to-small mark making: floor paths, wall paper, table paper, then small cards.

**Tiny output**
- Collect a movement-and-marks strip showing one big movement, one fine-motor task, and one drawing or mark.

## Chapter 7: Friends, Feelings, and Classroom Choices
**Circle-time invitations**
- Name common feelings and classroom needs: happy, sad, angry, worried, tired, excited, hungry, calm, help, space, turn, stop.
- Use puppets for everyday problems: a grabbed toy, a knocked tower, a waiting turn, a loud voice, or a friend left out.
- Ask: "What happened?" "Who needs care?" and "What could help now?"

**Play and practice**
- Practice simple joining and repair scripts: "Can I play?", "My turn next?", "Stop please", "I need help", "Let's fix it."
- Use timers, visual turn cards, calm choices, and adult coaching during real play.

**Tiny output**
- Build a class friendship toolkit with feeling cards, help cards, turn-taking cards, and repair pictures.

## Chapter 8: Curious Questions About the World
**Circle-time invitations**
- Invite noticing about plants, animals, weather, materials, light, sound, water, magnets, family routines, and community helpers.
- Ask: "What do you see?" "What do you wonder?" "What could we try?" and "What changed?"
- Treat children's questions as the starting point for shared investigation.

**Play and practice**
- Run small, safe explorations: sink and float, wet and dry, soft and hard, loud and quiet, light and shadow, seed and sprout.
- Record observations with photos, drawings, object displays, and adult-scribed words.

**Tiny output**
- Start a Wonder Wall with child questions, photos of investigations, and simple "we noticed" captions.

## Capstone
Children help create a "Ready for Learning Day" exhibition. The room includes the routine strip, story path, name-and-mark pages, number treasure mats, shape-and-pattern board, movement-and-marks strips, friendship toolkit, and Wonder Wall. Children guide a trusted adult or older buddy through the day: greeting, story, counting, building, moving, helping a puppet solve a problem, and sharing one question about the world.

## Assessment Ideas
- Adult observation notes on joining routines, following simple directions, listening, oral language, counting, comparing, sorting, movement, and help-seeking.
- Portfolio evidence from photos, drawings, mark making, name work, number mats, pattern builds, and adult-scribed child language.
- Short child conferences using prompts such as "I can count...", "I noticed...", "I need help when...", and "I wonder..."
- Routine-based checks during arrival, snack, story, outdoor play, tidy-up, and small-group exploration rather than formal testing.
- Family connection prompts asking caregivers to share home routines, favorite songs, counting games, name-writing attempts, and comfort strategies.
