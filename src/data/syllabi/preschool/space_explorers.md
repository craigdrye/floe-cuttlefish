# Space Explorers (Pre-school, ages 3-5)
**ID**: `planetsPreschool` | **Status**: playable

## Course Aim
Invite young children into the wonder of sky and space through looking up, asking big questions, making simple models, singing, storytelling, and pretend journeys. The course builds gentle early ideas about day and night, the Sun, Moon, stars, Earth, planets, astronauts, rockets, and caring for our home planet.

## Course Design Notes
This is an awe-first preschool space course. Children do not need to memorize planet facts or give test-style answers. Adults support learning with sky watching, picture books, movement games, shadow play, cardboard rockets, planet balls, moon art, songs, sensory trays, and repeated talk prompts. Space is kept concrete: what children can see in the sky, model with a lamp and ball, act out with bodies, or imagine safely through story. Each chapter includes a tiny science idea, a pretend-play routine, and a visual or tactile artifact for the room.

## Chapter 1: Looking Up
**Circle-time invitations**
- Share photos or window observations of blue sky, clouds, sunsets, night sky, Moon, and stars.
- Ask: "What have you seen in the sky?" and "What do you wonder about up there?"
- Practice safe sky watching: never look directly at the Sun, use shade, and notice with adult help.

**Pretend journey**
- Use a blanket, flashlight stars, soft music, and a countdown to begin the class space explorer ritual.

**Playful output**
- Start a Sky Wonders wall with drawings, photos, and adult-scribed questions.

## Chapter 2: Day, Night, and Shadows
**Circle-time invitations**
- Compare morning, afternoon, evening, and night routines with picture cards.
- Ask: "What do we do in the daytime?" and "What changes when it gets dark?"
- Notice that shadows need light and an object.

**Tiny model**
- Use a lamp and toy figure to make, move, stretch, and shrink shadows.

**Playful output**
- Make a day-night sorting mat with pictures for waking, playing, dinner, pajamas, bedtime, stars, and streetlights.

## Chapter 3: The Sun Helps Earth
**Circle-time invitations**
- Talk about the Sun as a star that gives Earth light and warmth.
- Ask: "What feels warmer in sunlight?" and "What helps us stay safe outside?"
- Connect Sun care to hats, shade, water, sunscreen from caregivers, and never staring at the Sun.

**Tiny model**
- Place one toy in warm light and one in shade for a short adult-led comparison, noticing warm and cool by touch when safe.

**Playful output**
- Create a Sun safety picture strip: shade, hat, water, grown-up help, eyes away.

## Chapter 4: Moon Shapes
**Circle-time invitations**
- Look at Moon photos and name shapes such as circle, crescent, half, almost full, and tiny sliver.
- Ask: "Did the Moon look the same last night?" and "What shape can your hands make?"
- Read a Moon story and move like a sleepy Moon crossing the sky.

**Tiny model**
- Use paper circles, clay, or cookie-cutter shapes to build a changing Moon sequence without requiring exact phase names.

**Playful output**
- Add Moon shape art to a class night-sky mural.

## Chapter 5: Stars and Sky Pictures
**Circle-time invitations**
- Notice that stars are very far away and look tiny from Earth.
- Ask: "What pictures can people imagine in the stars?" and "What stories could the sky tell?"
- Share that many cultures have sky stories, handled respectfully and briefly.

**Pretend journey**
- Make constellations with stickers, hole-punched cards and flashlights, peg boards, or child-safe beads and string.

**Playful output**
- Create a class star picture gallery with each child's invented sky picture and adult-scribed name.

## Chapter 6: Our Home Planet Earth
**Circle-time invitations**
- Look at Earth images from space and notice blue water, land, clouds, and round shape.
- Ask: "What do living things need on Earth?" and "How can we care for our home?"
- Connect Earth to air, water, plants, animals, people, weather, and favorite outdoor places.

**Tiny model**
- Use a globe, ball, or blue-green collage circle to show land and water.

**Playful output**
- Make an Earth care badge or mural with pictures for water, plants, animals, clean places, and gentle choices.

## Chapter 7: Planet Parade
**Circle-time invitations**
- Meet the eight planets as neighbors moving around the Sun, using balls, hoops, or picture cards.
- Ask: "Which planet looks red?", "Which one has rings?", and "Which one looks very big?"
- Keep facts simple: Mercury is close to the Sun, Mars is red, Jupiter is huge, Saturn has rings, and Earth is our home.

**Tiny model**
- Children carry planet cards in a slow orbit parade around a pretend Sun, with lots of room and adult guidance.

**Playful output**
- Build a planet ribbon or floor path with child-made planet circles.

## Chapter 8: Rockets, Astronauts, and Space Helpers
**Circle-time invitations**
- Explore what astronauts do: travel, float, observe, fix things, exercise, work together, and look back at Earth.
- Ask: "What would a space helper need?" and "What job could you do on our pretend spacecraft?"
- Notice rocket parts in simple terms: pointy nose, strong body, engines, window, and crew space.

**Pretend journey**
- Build a cardboard spacecraft or mission control with buttons, helmets, maps, checklists, and countdown songs.

**Playful output**
- Create a class mission patch with drawings of the crew, rocket, Moon, stars, and Earth.

## Capstone
Children host a "Tiny Space Mission" in the classroom. The mission may include the Sky Wonders wall, day-night mat, Sun safety strip, Moon mural, star gallery, Earth care mural, planet parade path, and cardboard spacecraft. Children choose jobs such as sky watcher, Moon artist, planet mover, rocket builder, Earth carer, or mission helper, then act out a short journey from countdown to safe landing.

## Assessment Ideas
- Adult observation notes on sky vocabulary, day-night sorting, safe Sun habits, Moon shape noticing, model use, and cooperative pretend play.
- Photo portfolio of shadow play, Moon art, star pictures, Earth collage, planet parade, and spacecraft construction.
- Adult-scribed child voice records using stems such as "I saw...", "I wonder...", "The Moon looks...", and "On Earth we can care by..."
- Conversation during play, art, and sky watching rather than recall quizzes.
- Family connection prompt: invite caregivers to look for the Moon, notice day-night routines, or share a simple sky story from home.
