# Social and Emotional Learning
**ID**: `col-social-emotional-learning` | **Discipline**: Kids Discovery | **Level**: Preschool / Early Years

## Course Aim
Help young children notice feelings, care for bodies, join play, repair small hurts, and make kind classroom choices. The course treats social and emotional learning as something children practice with trusted adults through stories, routines, songs, puppets, picture cards, sensory regulation, and repeated everyday moments.

## Course Design Notes
This preschool SEL course is aligned with early childhood versions of self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making. Learning is adult-facilitated, concrete, and emotionally safe: children can point, move, choose a picture, whisper to a puppet, draw, or pass. No child is asked to perform distress or talk about private family experiences. Each chapter pairs a small concept with a predictable routine, a pretend-play scenario, and a tiny classroom artifact that can be revisited.

## Chapter 1: Hello, Feelings
**Circle-time invitations**
- Use face cards, mirrors, puppets, or picture-book pauses to name feelings.
- Ask: "What clues tell us this person might feel happy, sad, angry, scared, worried, proud, or calm?"
- Offer a choice-based feelings check-in with colors, weather, animals, or simple gestures.

**Practice routine**
- Children point, sign, gesture, or say a feeling, then choose "same," "different," or "not sure" for a story character.

**Tiny output**
- Add one feeling face or color to a shared feelings board.

## Chapter 2: Feelings in the Body
**Circle-time invitations**
- Notice body clues: tight hands, fast heart, warm cheeks, wiggly legs, sleepy eyes, butterflies in the tummy.
- Compare high-energy, low-energy, and just-right bodies through safe movement.
- Ask: "What does your body need before play, rest, snack, or tidy time?"

**Practice routine**
- Try a body scan with hands on heart, tummy, shoulders, and knees.

**Tiny output**
- Build a "body clues" picture strip for the classroom calm space.

## Chapter 3: Calm-Down Tools
**Circle-time invitations**
- Explore calming choices: breathe, count, squeeze playdough, ask for space, hug a cushion, look at a book, drink water, or sit near an adult.
- Use a puppet who feels too buzzy, too mad, or too worried to join play.
- Ask: "Which tool might help this body get ready?"

**Practice routine**
- Practice one calming tool when everyone is already calm, so it feels familiar later.

**Tiny output**
- Choose class calm-space cards with pictures rather than long instructions.

## Chapter 4: Waiting, Trying, and Trying Again
**Circle-time invitations**
- Act out waiting for a turn with a toy, swing, snack helper job, or favorite book.
- Read a story where a character finds something hard and keeps going.
- Ask: "What can we do while we wait?" and "What can we say when it is tricky?"

**Practice routine**
- Use a timer, song, waiting spot, or "first, next" card.

**Tiny output**
- Make a "try again" card with child-drawn ideas: ask for help, take a breath, watch, try a smaller step.

## Chapter 5: Other People Have Feelings Too
**Circle-time invitations**
- Pause in stories to ask: "What might this friend feel? What clue do you see?"
- Use back-facing or partial picture cards to show that we do not always know how someone feels.
- Ask: "How could we check?"

**Practice routine**
- Try kind noticing words: "Are you okay?", "Do you want help?", "Do you want space?"

**Tiny output**
- Create a class helper phrase chart with pictures for help, space, wait, and play.

## Chapter 6: Joining Play and Being a Friend
**Circle-time invitations**
- Pretend a block tower, tea party, train track, or painting table is already busy.
- Ask: "How can someone join?" and "What can the players say back?"
- Explore friendship as kind actions, not always wanting the same game.

**Practice routine**
- Role-play joining scripts: "Can I play?", "What are you building?", "Can I be next?", "Let's make room."

**Tiny output**
- Add child-chosen joining-play cards to the play areas.

## Chapter 7: Repair After a Problem
**Circle-time invitations**
- Use puppets for everyday problems: a snatched toy, knocked block tower, loud voice, spill, accidental bump, or left-out friend.
- Ask: "What happened? Who needs care? What can help now?"
- Keep apologies concrete: listen, return, fix, clean, comfort, try again.

**Practice routine**
- Practice a repair path: stop, breathe, check, help, try a new plan.

**Tiny output**
- Make a repair map for the classroom, using icons for adult help, words, clean up, space, and new choice.

## Chapter 8: Our Caring Classroom
**Circle-time invitations**
- Look at shared spaces: snack table, book corner, outdoor area, water tray, dress-up clothes.
- Ask: "How do we care for people, things, animals, plants, and places?"
- Invite children to notice helpful jobs and gentle leadership.

**Practice routine**
- Choose a daily caring job: tidy helper, plant watcher, welcome wave, book rescuer, kindness spotter.

**Tiny output**
- Build a class care agreement with photos or drawings of what care looks like.

## Capstone
Children create a "Feelings and Friendship Toolkit" for the room. It may include feeling cards, calm-down choices, joining-play scripts, repair icons, and class care agreements. The group uses puppets to act out one familiar classroom problem, then chooses tools from the kit to help everyone feel safe, included, and ready to try again.

## Assessment Ideas
- Adult observation notes on emotion naming, body awareness, waiting, help-seeking, joining play, and repair attempts.
- Portfolio photos of calm-space cards, role-play props, helper charts, and class agreements.
- Child voice records: adult-scribed phrases such as "I can try...", "My body might need...", or "A friend might feel..."
- Routine-based check-ins during play, snack, transitions, outdoor time, and tidy-up.
- Family connection prompt: invite caregivers to share one calming routine, comfort song, or helping phrase used at home.
