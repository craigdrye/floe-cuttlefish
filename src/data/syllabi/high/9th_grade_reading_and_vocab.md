# 9th Grade Reading and Vocab
**ID**: `col-9th-grade-reading-and-vocab` | **Discipline**: English | **Year**: 10 (ages 14-15)
**Frameworks**: US Common Core ELA Grade 9-10

## Course Aim
Prepare students for high school English by building disciplined close reading, literary analysis, rhetorical analysis, research habits, academic vocabulary, and writing about evidence. Students learn to read complex texts with patience, notice how authors create meaning, and develop claims that can stand up to scrutiny.

## Course Design Notes
The course bridges middle-grade reading confidence into high school analytical independence. Pair substantial literary works with speeches, essays, articles, and research sources. Keep vocabulary embedded in authentic reading and writing so students connect morphology, syntax, tone, and academic word choice to interpretation and argument.

## Chapter 1: High School Close Reading
- **Key concepts**: strong and thorough evidence, inference, annotation systems, ambiguity, implication, objective summary, interpretive claim, and explanatory commentary.
- **Practice questions**: What evidence is strongest and why? Which details complicate the first reading? How does commentary explain rather than repeat evidence?
- **Student output**: a close-reading paragraph with a claim, embedded quotation, and commentary that traces one inference.

## Chapter 2: Complex Character and Theme
- **Key concepts**: complex character, conflicting motivation, foil, relationship, plot advancement, theme emergence, theme refinement, and specific detail.
- **Practice questions**: How do a character's motives conflict? How does an interaction advance plot or theme? Which details refine the theme across the work?
- **Student output**: a character-theme chart that tracks change over multiple scenes or chapters.

## Chapter 3: Structure, Time, and Author's Craft
- **Key concepts**: narrative structure, pacing, flashback, foreshadowing, parallel plot, scene order, tension, mystery, surprise, voice, and setting.
- **Practice questions**: Why might the author reveal information in this order? How does pacing create tension? What is the cumulative effect of repeated craft choices?
- **Student output**: a craft analysis explaining how structure or time manipulation shapes a reader's response.

## Chapter 4: Poetry, Drama, and Literary Tradition
- **Key concepts**: speaker, imagery, figurative language, sound, meter at an introductory level, dramatic structure, soliloquy, cultural experience, allusion, and tradition.
- **Practice questions**: How does figurative language sharpen meaning? What does the speaker reveal indirectly? How does cultural or literary context affect interpretation?
- **Student output**: a comparative annotation of a poem, drama excerpt, or world-literature passage focused on language and perspective.

## Chapter 5: Rhetoric and Seminal Nonfiction
- **Key concepts**: purpose, audience, point of view, claim, line of reasoning, rhetoric, diction, syntax, parallelism, anaphora, ethos, pathos, logos, and occasion.
- **Practice questions**: How does the speaker advance a purpose? Which rhetorical choices shape tone and credibility? How do two documents address related themes?
- **Student output**: a rhetorical analysis paragraph on a speech, essay, letter, or foundational document.

## Chapter 6: Argument Evaluation and Research Reading
- **Key concepts**: valid reasoning, relevant and sufficient evidence, false statement, fallacy, counterclaim, source credibility, bias, synthesis, citation trail, and research question.
- **Practice questions**: Does the evidence prove the claim? What assumption does the argument depend on? How do sources agree, extend, or challenge one another?
- **Student output**: a source evaluation matrix for three texts connected to one research question.

## Chapter 7: Academic Vocabulary and Language in Context
- **Key concepts**: context clues, word families, morphology, part of speech shifts, academic verbs, domain vocabulary, euphemism, oxymoron, nuance, register, and precision.
- **Practice questions**: How does the word's form signal its function? Which synonym best fits the tone? How does figurative or technical language affect meaning?
- **Student output**: an academic vocabulary set that includes morphology, text evidence, connotation, and a precise original use.

## Chapter 8: Writing About Evidence
- **Key concepts**: analytical thesis, topic sentence, quotation integration, paraphrase, explanation, synthesis, transitions, counterargument, revision, and academic style.
- **Practice questions**: Is the claim arguable and specific? Does each quotation need more context or commentary? How can paragraphs move from summary to analysis?
- **Student output**: a revised literary or rhetorical analysis essay section with an evidence audit and reflection.

## Capstone
Students produce a high school readiness reading dossier on a literary work and a connected nonfiction issue or theme. The dossier includes close-reading annotations, a literary analysis, a rhetorical analysis, a research source matrix, an academic vocabulary record, and a final essay or presentation that synthesizes evidence from multiple texts.

## Assessment Ideas
- Close-reading checks graded on evidence quality and depth of commentary.
- Literary analysis tasks graded on character, theme, structure, and author craft.
- Rhetorical analysis tasks graded on audience, purpose, line of reasoning, and language choice.
- Research tasks graded on source credibility, synthesis, citation habits, and intellectual honesty.
- Capstone graded on analytical independence, cross-text evidence, academic vocabulary transfer, and clarity of written or spoken argument.
