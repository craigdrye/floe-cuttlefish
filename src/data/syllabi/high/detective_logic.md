# Detective Logic
Reason like a detective: separate evidence from interpretation, test competing theories, dodge fallacies, and build a case the clues actually support.

**ID**: `detectiveLogic` · **Discipline**: Logic / Critical Reasoning

## Course Aim
This high-school enrichment course uses detective stories, puzzles, and evidence labs to teach disciplined reasoning from clues. Students learn to tell what they actually know from what they are assuming, to generate more than one explanation for the same fact, and to keep score of which theory the evidence best supports. The atmosphere stays puzzle-rich and playful, but the underlying habits are the same ones used in science, journalism, law, and everyday decision-making.

The course is built around a single move repeated in harder and harder contexts: observe carefully, separate fact from inference, list competing explanations, and let evidence rather than emotion decide between them. Along the way students meet the formal vocabulary of reasoning - deduction, induction, abduction, validity, base rate, burden of proof - and the named traps that ambush careless reasoners, such as confirmation bias, base-rate neglect, and the red herring. Probability is introduced carefully so that students learn to treat "rare" and "impossible" as different things.

By the end, students can read a mystery or a real-world claim, build a structured case file, weigh sources and witnesses, update their beliefs as new clues arrive, and explain in writing why the best theory beats its rivals. The capstone asks them to design a fair-play mystery of their own, which is the strongest test that they understand how clues, misdirection, and honest deduction actually work.

## Course Design Notes
This is a reasoning course wearing a detective costume, not a forensic science survey. Every chapter pairs a puzzle or case with a named reasoning skill, then immediately stress-tests it: every suspect theory must be supported, every clue should have more than one possible reading at first, and every final explanation must answer the evidence better than its rivals. Recurring routines include the case file (observations vs. inferences vs. open questions), the evidence table (relevance, reliability, innocent explanation), and the theory chart (which explanation each clue favours and why). Assessment rewards fair treatment of alternative explanations, honest handling of uncertainty, and clear reasoning - not the most dramatic accusation. Useful anchors include the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entries on abduction and fallacies, Locard's exchange principle from forensic science, classic fair-play detective fiction, and basic conditional-probability reasoning.

## Chapter 1: The Detective's Question
- **Core questions**: What do we actually know for certain versus what are we guessing? Which detail is only a clue if some other claim is also true? What single question would narrow the case the most?
- **Key concepts**: observation, clue, fact, inference, assumption, hypothesis; motive, means, opportunity; uncertainty; the case file.
- **Applied skills**: building a one-page case file that separates observations, inferences, open questions, and early suspect theories; writing the one question whose answer would cut the suspect list fastest.
- **Common traps**: stating an inference as if it were an observation; locking onto a favourite suspect before the evidence is in; treating a vivid detail as proof rather than as a lead.

## Chapter 2: Deduction, Induction, and Abduction
- **Core questions**: Is this conclusion certain or merely probable? Which competing explanation also fits the same clue? What new evidence would decide between two explanations?
- **Key concepts**: deductive reasoning (premises guarantee the conclusion), inductive reasoning (evidence makes a conclusion more probable), abductive reasoning / inference to the best explanation; validity, soundness, strength; alternative explanation.
- **Applied skills**: building a reasoning map for a short scene that labels each move as deductive, inductive, or abductive, and lists at least one rival explanation per clue.
- **Common traps**: calling a probable conclusion "certain"; treating the first explanation that fits as the only explanation; confusing a valid argument (good structure) with a sound one (good structure plus true premises).

## Chapter 3: Evidence and Interpretation
- **Core questions**: What does this piece of evidence prove all by itself, and what does it merely suggest? Could it have been planted, transferred, or misread? What independent evidence corroborates it?
- **Key concepts**: direct vs. circumstantial evidence; trace evidence; testimony; alibi; chain of custody; contamination; corroboration; Locard's exchange principle ("every contact leaves a trace").
- **Applied skills**: building an evidence table that ranks each clue by reliability and relevance and names at least one innocent explanation for it.
- **Common traps**: treating circumstantial evidence as worthless or as conclusive; forgetting that a transferred fibre or fingerprint can have an innocent route; ignoring a broken chain of custody.

## Chapter 4: Fallacies, Bias, and False Leads
- **Core questions**: Which suspect are we favouring too early, and which evidence are we ignoring to keep favouring them? Is this clue genuinely relevant or just distracting? Did the argument attack a person instead of a claim?
- **Key concepts**: confirmation bias; tunnel vision; hasty generalization; false dilemma; ad hominem; red herring; circular reasoning; appeal to authority; base-rate neglect.
- **Applied skills**: running a fallacy-repair lab - diagnosing flawed detective reasoning, naming the specific fallacy, and rewriting it into a fairer argument.
- **Common traps**: collecting only evidence that confirms a hunch; mistaking a confident expert's say-so for proof; following a red herring because it is more interesting than the real clue.

## Chapter 5: Probability and Competing Theories
- **Core questions**: How surprising is this clue under each theory? Are we confusing "rare" with "impossible"? What should change about our ranking after a new clue appears, and which theory explains the most with the least strain?
- **Key concepts**: likelihood; base rate; conditional probability; coincidence vs. evidence; prior belief and updating; burden of proof; explanatory power; the simplest sufficient theory (parsimony).
- **Applied skills**: building a theory-comparison chart that re-ranks suspects after three new clues and explains each change; spotting base-rate neglect in a "the test is 99% accurate" style claim.
- **Common traps**: treating an unlikely coincidence as impossible; ignoring how common a trait is before treating it as incriminating; refusing to update a theory after evidence against it arrives.

## Chapter 6: Sources, Witnesses, and Reliability
- **Core questions**: Who is the source and how could they actually know this? What might they gain, fear, or misremember? Which independent source could verify the claim?
- **Key concepts**: source reliability; eyewitness memory and the misinformation effect; perception vs. inference; expertise; incentive and bias; primary vs. secondary source; rumour; cross-checking.
- **Applied skills**: building a witness-reliability profile that scores three statements for accuracy, motive, memory limits, and corroboration.
- **Common traps**: treating a confident witness as an accurate one; trusting a secondhand account as if it were firsthand; letting a leading question reshape what a witness "remembers."

## Chapter 7: Timelines, Constraints, and Logic Grids
- **Core questions**: Which events cannot both be true? What does the timeline make physically impossible? Which suspect is the only one left after every constraint is applied?
- **Key concepts**: timeline and sequence; contradiction; constraint; elimination; logic grid; cipher; substitution; frequency analysis; hidden message; proof by elimination.
- **Applied skills**: solving a logic-grid or cipher case and writing a short proof that explains why the answer is forced rather than merely guessed.
- **Common traps**: filling a grid from a guess instead of from a clue; missing that a clue rules out two cells at once; declaring a suspect guilty before all constraints are checked.

## Chapter 8: Building a Fair Mystery
- **Core questions**: Did the reader have enough information to solve it? Which clues point to the truth without being obvious? Which red herring is fair, and does the reveal explain every major clue?
- **Key concepts**: the fair-play mystery; clue placement; honest vs. unfair misdirection; the reveal; locked-room and least-likely-suspect structures; motive design; solution integrity.
- **Applied skills**: building an original mystery blueprint with a suspect list, clue trail, false leads, timeline, solution, and a fairness check confirming the reader could solve it.
- **Common traps**: hiding the decisive clue from the reader; relying on information only the detective could know; writing a red herring that is a lie rather than a misleading truth.

## Capstone
Students design, write, and present a playable detective case. The finished case must include a case brief, an evidence packet, witness statements, a timeline, at least one genuine probability or source-reliability issue, at least one fair false lead, and a solution that explains why the best theory beats every alternative. Classmates attempt to solve it, and the author defends the fairness of the puzzle: every clue accounted for, no information withheld, and the reveal earned by the evidence rather than asserted.

## Assessment Ideas
- Case files graded on clean separation of fact, inference, assumption, and open question.
- Evidence tables graded on relevance, reliability, corroboration, and named innocent explanations.
- Reasoning maps graded on clarity, fallacy awareness, and fair treatment of competing theories.
- Original mysteries graded on fairness, clue design, logical consistency, and the quality of the final explanation.
