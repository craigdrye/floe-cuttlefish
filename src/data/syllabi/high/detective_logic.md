# Detective Logic
**ID**: `detectiveLogic` - **Discipline**: Humanities/Extracurricular - **Year**: 10 (ages 14-15)

## Course Aim
Use detective stories, puzzles, and evidence labs to teach students how to reason from clues without jumping to conclusions. Students learn to distinguish evidence from interpretation, test competing explanations, notice fallacies, use probability carefully, judge source reliability, and construct fair mysteries of their own.

## Course Design Notes
This is a high-school enrichment course rather than a forensic science survey. Keep the atmosphere puzzle-rich and playful, but make the reasoning rigorous: every suspect theory should be supported, every clue should have more than one possible interpretation at first, and every final explanation should answer the evidence better than its rivals.

## Chapter 1: The Detective's Question
- **Key concepts**: observation, clue, fact, inference, assumption, hypothesis, motive, means, opportunity, uncertainty, and case file.
- **Practice questions**: What do we know for certain? What are we guessing? Which detail is a clue only if another claim is true? What question would narrow the case?
- **Student output**: A one-page case file that separates observations, inferences, open questions, and early suspect theories.

## Chapter 2: Deduction, Induction, and Abduction
- **Key concepts**: deductive reasoning, inductive reasoning, abductive reasoning, inference to the best explanation, validity, strength, and alternative explanation.
- **Practice questions**: Is this conclusion certain or probable? What would make the explanation stronger? Which other explanation fits the same clue? What evidence would decide between them?
- **Student output**: A reasoning map for a short mystery scene showing deductive claims, inductive patterns, and abductive hypotheses.

## Chapter 3: Evidence and Interpretation
- **Key concepts**: direct evidence, circumstantial evidence, trace evidence, testimony, alibi, chain of custody, contamination, corroboration, and Locard's exchange principle.
- **Practice questions**: What does this evidence prove by itself? What does it merely suggest? Could it have been planted, transferred, or misunderstood? What corroborates it?
- **Student output**: An evidence table that ranks clues by reliability, relevance, and possible innocent explanations.

## Chapter 4: Fallacies, Bias, and False Leads
- **Key concepts**: confirmation bias, hasty generalization, false dilemma, ad hominem, red herring, circular reasoning, appeal to authority, base-rate neglect, and tunnel vision.
- **Practice questions**: Which suspect are we favoring too early? What evidence are we ignoring? Is the clue relevant or distracting? Did the argument attack a person instead of a claim?
- **Student output**: A fallacy repair lab in which students diagnose flawed detective reasoning and rewrite it into a fairer argument.

## Chapter 5: Probability and Competing Theories
- **Key concepts**: likelihood, base rate, conditional probability, coincidence, prior belief, updating, burden of proof, explanatory power, and simplest sufficient theory.
- **Practice questions**: How surprising is this clue under each theory? Are we confusing rare with impossible? What should change after new evidence appears? Which theory explains the most with the least strain?
- **Student output**: A theory comparison chart that updates suspect rankings after three new clues and explains each change.

## Chapter 6: Sources, Witnesses, and Reliability
- **Key concepts**: source reliability, eyewitness memory, perception, expertise, incentive, bias, primary source, secondary source, rumor, and cross-checking.
- **Practice questions**: Who is the source, and how could they know? What might they gain or fear? What did they observe directly? Which independent source can verify the claim?
- **Student output**: A witness reliability profile that evaluates three statements for accuracy, motive, memory limits, and corroboration.

## Chapter 7: Codes, Timelines, and Logic Grids
- **Key concepts**: timeline, sequence, contradiction, constraint, cipher, substitution, frequency analysis, hidden message, elimination, and logic grid.
- **Practice questions**: Which events cannot both be true? What does the timeline make impossible? What pattern breaks the cipher? Which suspect remains after all constraints are applied?
- **Student output**: A solved logic-grid or cipher case with a short proof explaining why the answer is forced.

## Chapter 8: Building a Fair Mystery
- **Key concepts**: fair-play mystery, clue placement, red herring, reveal, misdirection, locked room, least likely suspect, motive design, and solution integrity.
- **Practice questions**: Did the reader have enough information to solve it? Which clues point to the truth without being obvious? Which red herring is fair? Does the reveal explain every major clue?
- **Student output**: An original mystery blueprint with suspect list, clue trail, false leads, timeline, solution, and fairness check.

## Capstone
Students design and present a playable detective case. The final case must include a case brief, evidence packet, witness statements, timeline, at least one probability or reliability issue, at least one false lead, and a solution that explains why the best theory beats the alternatives.

## Assessment Ideas
- Case files graded on separation of fact, inference, assumption, and question.
- Evidence tables graded on relevance, reliability, corroboration, and alternative explanations.
- Reasoning maps graded on clarity, fallacy awareness, and treatment of competing theories.
- Original mysteries graded on fairness, clue design, logical consistency, and quality of final explanation.
