# Chess Tactics
**ID**: `chessTactics` - **Discipline**: Extracurricular - **Year**: 10 (ages 14-15)

## Course Aim
Build students into thoughtful chess problem-solvers who can spot tactical patterns, calculate forcing lines, make practical plans, handle basic endgames, and reflect on their decisions after a game. The course treats chess as a laboratory for attention, patience, risk, and strategy.

## Course Design Notes
This enrichment course assumes students know the rules and basic notation but need a stronger tactical and strategic toolkit. Lessons should mix short puzzle sprints, slow calculation exercises, annotated master or club games, mini-games from key positions, and post-game reflection. Use real boards, lichess studies, US Chess learning resources, and puzzle databases when available.

## Chapter 1: Board Vision and Calculation Habits
- **Key concepts**: candidate moves, forcing moves, checks, captures, threats, legal move discipline, visualization, blunder check, loose pieces, and king safety.
- **Practice questions**: What are all the checks? Which captures are safe? What is the opponent threatening? Which pieces are undefended? What changes after one move in your head?
- **Student output**: A calculation log for five positions showing candidate moves, chosen line, rejected line, and final blunder check.

## Chapter 2: Tactical Pattern Recognition
- **Key concepts**: fork, pin, skewer, discovered attack, double attack, x-ray, trapped piece, overloaded defender, and pattern memory.
- **Practice questions**: Which piece is doing two jobs? Is this pin absolute or relative? What square would create a fork? What target is hidden behind the front piece?
- **Student output**: A labeled tactics notebook with diagrams for at least eight motifs and one original example of each.

## Chapter 3: Forcing Lines and Calculation Trees
- **Key concepts**: forcing sequence, candidate tree, branch, quiet move, zwischenzug, move order, refutation, evaluation, and calculation horizon.
- **Practice questions**: What is the most forcing first move? What is the opponent's best defense? Does move order matter? Where does the line stop being forced?
- **Student output**: A two-page analysis of three tactical positions with full candidate trees and a short explanation of the final evaluation.

## Chapter 4: Checkmating Patterns
- **Key concepts**: back rank mate, smothered mate, Arabian mate, Anastasia's mate, Boden's mate, mating net, escape square, defender removal, and king shelter.
- **Practice questions**: Which escape squares does the king have? What defender must be removed? Is the threat mate in one, two, or three? What piece delivers the final net?
- **Student output**: A checkmate pattern gallery with diagrams, annotations, and one student-composed mate-in-two puzzle.

## Chapter 5: Tactical Defense and Counterplay
- **Key concepts**: defensive resource, intermezzo, perpetual check, fortress idea, exchange sacrifice, counter-threat, simplification, and practical resistance.
- **Practice questions**: Can the threat be met with a stronger threat? Which piece can block, capture, or move away? Is perpetual check possible? When should you simplify?
- **Student output**: A defensive puzzle set in which each answer includes the opponent's threat, the defensive idea, and the reason it works.

## Chapter 6: Plans from Positions
- **Key concepts**: imbalances, pawn structure, open file, outpost, weak square, bad bishop, space, piece activity, initiative, and plan selection.
- **Practice questions**: Which side has the safer king? What is the worst-placed piece? Which pawn break changes the position? What plan matches the structure?
- **Student output**: A position planning memo that proposes two candidate plans, compares their risks, and selects one plan with a first move.

## Chapter 7: Endgame Thinking
- **Key concepts**: opposition, rule of the square, passed pawn, outside passer, zugzwang, triangulation, rook activity, king activity, and conversion.
- **Practice questions**: Can the king catch the pawn? Who has the opposition? Is this a win or a draw with best play? What is the simplest conversion plan?
- **Student output**: An endgame mini-portfolio with solved king-and-pawn positions, one rook activity example, and a written explanation of a zugzwang.

## Chapter 8: Game Review, Psychology, and Tournament Practice
- **Key concepts**: annotation, time management, tilt, opening principles, middlegame plan, endgame transition, mistake pattern, and reflective practice.
- **Practice questions**: Where did the evaluation change? Was the mistake tactical, strategic, or emotional? Did time pressure affect the choice? What would you train next?
- **Student output**: An annotated personal game or model game with three turning points, one missed tactic, one planning decision, and a training goal.

## Capstone
Students create a chess improvement dossier. It includes a curated puzzle set, one deeply analyzed calculation position, one planned middlegame position, one endgame explanation, an annotated game, and a reflection on how their thinking changed across the course.

## Assessment Ideas
- Puzzle work graded on accuracy, pattern naming, and explanation of candidate moves.
- Calculation logs graded on disciplined branching, attention to the opponent's best replies, and honest revision.
- Game annotations graded on tactical insight, strategic planning, endgame awareness, and reflective clarity.
- Capstone graded on range of positions, quality of analysis, and evidence of improved decision-making.
