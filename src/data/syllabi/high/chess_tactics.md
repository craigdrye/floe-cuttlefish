# Chess Tactics
A high-school enrichment course in tactical vision, calculation, planning, and endgame technique.

**ID**: `chessTactics` · **Discipline**: Games

## Course Aim
This course builds thoughtful chess problem-solvers who can spot tactical patterns, calculate forcing lines, form practical plans, convert basic endgames, and reflect honestly on their own decisions. It treats the chessboard as a laboratory for attention, patience, risk assessment, and strategy, where every move is a small argument the student must justify.

Students arrive knowing the rules, piece movement, and basic notation, but they often play on instinct and miss the patterns that decide most games at the club level. The course gives them a disciplined toolkit: a candidate-move process, a vocabulary of tactical motifs, a method for building calculation trees, a library of mating patterns, defensive resources, positional planning ideas, and the core king-and-pawn and rook endgames.

By the end, students should be able to look at an unfamiliar position, name the imbalances, list the forcing moves, calculate a short line accurately, and explain why their choice is sound. Just as importantly, they should leave able to review their own games, classify their mistakes as tactical, strategic, or emotional, and design a sensible plan for further improvement.

## Course Design Notes
This enrichment course assumes students know the rules and basic algebraic notation but need a stronger tactical and strategic foundation. Lessons should mix short puzzle sprints, slow calculation exercises, annotated master or club games, mini-games from key starting positions, and post-game reflection. Use real boards, online study tools, national-federation learning resources, and puzzle databases where available.

Material values used throughout are the standard rough scale (pawn 1, knight 3, bishop 3, rook 5, queen 9), with the understanding that activity and king safety frequently outweigh raw material. Questions are written at high-school level: they reward pattern recognition, careful reading of a position, and clear reasoning rather than memorized opening theory. Mate problems are kept to mate-in-one and mate-in-two ideas so the focus stays on the pattern, not on brute-force depth.

## Chapter 1: Board Vision and Calculation Habits
- **Core questions**: What are all the checks, captures, and threats in this position? Which of my pieces are undefended? What does the board look like after one move in my head?
- **Key concepts**: candidate moves, forcing moves (checks, captures, threats), legal-move discipline, visualization, blunder check, loose (undefended) pieces, king safety.
- **Applied skills**: generate a short candidate-move list, scan for forcing moves first, run a blunder check before committing, and track which pieces are loose.
- **Common traps**: playing the first move that looks good, forgetting the opponent gets to reply, leaving a piece hanging, and miscounting an exchange.

## Chapter 2: Tactical Pattern Recognition
- **Core questions**: Which piece is doing two jobs at once? Is this pin absolute or relative? What square would let one piece attack two targets? What target is hidden behind the front piece?
- **Key concepts**: fork (double attack), pin (absolute vs relative), skewer, discovered attack, x-ray, trapped piece, overloaded defender, removing the defender.
- **Applied skills**: name the motif present in a position, identify the overloaded or pinned piece, and find the square that creates a fork or discovery.
- **Common traps**: confusing a pin with a skewer, treating a relative pin as if it cannot be broken, and missing that a defender is overloaded.

## Chapter 3: Forcing Lines and Calculation Trees
- **Core questions**: What is the most forcing first move? What is the opponent's best defense? Does move order matter here? Where does the line stop being forced?
- **Key concepts**: forcing sequence, candidate tree, branching, quiet move, zwischenzug (in-between move), move order, refutation, evaluation, calculation horizon.
- **Applied skills**: build a small calculation tree, prune lines that fail, insert a zwischenzug when available, and evaluate the position at the end of a line.
- **Common traps**: assuming the opponent will cooperate, stopping calculation one move too early, and getting the move order wrong so a tactic fails.

## Chapter 4: Checkmating Patterns
- **Core questions**: Which escape squares does the enemy king have? What defender must be removed to deliver mate? Is this mate in one or mate in two? What piece delivers the final blow?
- **Key concepts**: back-rank mate, smothered mate, Anastasia's mate, mating net, escape squares, removing the defender, king shelter, support of the mating piece.
- **Applied skills**: count the king's escape squares, identify the supporting piece behind a mate, and find a mate-in-one or mate-in-two from a given position.
- **Common traps**: delivering a check that the king simply walks out of, leaving the mating piece undefended, and overlooking an interposing defensive move.

## Chapter 5: Tactical Defense and Counterplay
- **Core questions**: Can the threat be met with a stronger threat? Which piece can block, capture, or step away? Is perpetual check available? When is it right to simplify?
- **Key concepts**: defensive resource, counter-threat, perpetual check, fortress idea, exchange sacrifice, simplification, the in-between defensive move, practical resistance.
- **Applied skills**: meet a threat with a counter-threat, recognize a perpetual-check draw, and decide when trading into a defensible endgame is the safest path.
- **Common traps**: defending passively when counterplay exists, missing a saving perpetual check, and simplifying into a lost rather than a drawn ending.

## Chapter 6: Plans from Positions
- **Core questions**: Which side has the safer king? What is the worst-placed piece? Which pawn break changes the position? What plan matches this structure?
- **Key concepts**: imbalances, pawn structure, open file, outpost, weak square, good vs bad bishop, space, piece activity, initiative, plan selection.
- **Applied skills**: list the imbalances in a position, identify the worst piece and how to improve it, and choose a plan that fits the pawn structure.
- **Common traps**: making moves without a plan, trading off a good piece, ignoring an outpost, and pushing pawns that weaken your own king.

## Chapter 7: Endgame Thinking
- **Core questions**: Can the king catch the passed pawn? Who has the opposition? Is this a win or a draw with best play? What is the simplest conversion plan?
- **Key concepts**: opposition, rule of the square, passed pawn, outside passer, zugzwang, triangulation, rook activity, king activity, conversion technique.
- **Applied skills**: apply the rule of the square, take or keep the opposition, activate the king and the rook, and convert an extra pawn cleanly.
- **Common traps**: leaving the king passive, losing the opposition, placing the rook passively behind the wrong pawn, and rushing a conversion into a stalemate.

## Chapter 8: Game Review, Psychology, and Tournament Practice
- **Core questions**: Where did the evaluation change? Was the decisive mistake tactical, strategic, or emotional? Did time pressure affect the choice? What should you train next?
- **Key concepts**: annotation, time management, tilt, opening principles, middlegame plan, endgame transition, mistake patterns, reflective practice.
- **Applied skills**: annotate a game and mark turning points, classify each mistake by type, manage the clock, and set a concrete training goal.
- **Common traps**: blaming luck instead of finding the real error, flagging in time pressure, repeating the same opening mistake, and quitting after a loss instead of reviewing it.

## Capstone
Students assemble a chess improvement dossier. It includes a curated puzzle set with each motif named, one deeply analyzed calculation position with a full candidate tree, one planned middlegame position with two compared plans, one written endgame explanation, an annotated personal or model game with three turning points, and a reflection on how their thinking changed across the course.

## Assessment Ideas
- Puzzle work graded on accuracy, correct naming of the motif, and the explanation of candidate moves.
- Calculation logs graded on disciplined branching, attention to the opponent's best replies, and honest revision.
- Game annotations graded on tactical insight, strategic planning, endgame awareness, and reflective clarity.
- Capstone graded on the range of positions covered, the quality of the analysis, and the evidence of improved decision-making.
