# AP World History: Modern
*Global history from c. 1200 to the present, taught as argument: contextualize, compare, trace causation, and read sources like a historian.*

**ID**: `col-ap-world-history` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
AP World History: Modern asks students to make sense of the human past on a global scale, from roughly 1200 CE to the present. Rather than memorizing a parade of facts, the course trains students to think the way historians do: to situate events in context, to compare developments across regions, to trace causes and consequences, and to weigh continuity against change. The goal is a defensible, evidence-based argument, not a list.

This course follows the nine-unit College Board arc, beginning with the diverse and interconnected states of the thirteenth century and ending with the globalized, contested present. Along the way students examine state-building, trade networks, gunpowder empires, transoceanic contact, revolutions, industrialization, imperialism, world wars, the Cold War, decolonization, and globalization. Each period is studied both on its own terms and as part of larger Afro-Eurasian and Atlantic patterns.

Equally important is the writing. Every unit embeds the AP exam tasks: stimulus-based multiple choice, the short-answer question (SAQ), the document-based question (DBQ), and the long essay question (LEQ). Students learn to write a contextualized thesis, marshal specific evidence, source documents for point of view and purpose, and demonstrate historical complexity. By May, the course should feel less like coverage and more like a craft.

## Course Design Notes
The course is organized around the College Board's nine units and six reasoning practices (contextualization, comparison, causation, continuity and change over time, argumentation, and source analysis). The syllabus chapters below map onto those units, with two skills-focused chapters bracketing the chronology: an opening chapter on historical thinking and a closing exam studio.

Each content chapter pairs a region-by-region survey with a recurring writing task, so that document analysis and essay planning are practiced continuously rather than crammed before the exam. Stimulus questions deliberately use primary sources (travel accounts, treaties, speeches) so that students build the habit of reading evidence critically. Periodization is treated as an argument to be examined, not a fixed truth: students are regularly asked why a date or boundary is chosen and what it obscures.

## Chapter 1: Historical Thinking and Global Frames
- **Core questions**: What makes a development a *global* pattern rather than a local one? What counts as good historical evidence? How does a thesis become defensible?
- **Key concepts**: contextualization, comparison, causation, continuity and change over time, sourcing (HIPP/HAPP), thesis, evidence, complexity, periodization.
- **Applied skills**: drafting a contextualized thesis; sourcing a document for purpose, audience, and point of view; building SAQ, DBQ, and LEQ templates.
- **Common traps**: treating a single region as "the world"; describing instead of arguing; confusing correlation with causation; ignoring periodization as a choice.

## Chapter 2: The Global Tapestry, c. 1200-1450
- **Core questions**: How did states legitimize and exercise power? What roles did religion and bureaucracy play? How did regions differ in their political and economic forms?
- **Key concepts**: Song China and the civil-service exam, Dar al-Islam and the post-Abbasid order, South and Southeast Asian states, Mali and trans-Saharan wealth, Aztec and Inca administration, European feudalism and manorialism.
- **Applied skills**: comparing state-building across three regions; using a stimulus (e.g., Marco Polo) as evidence for urban and commercial scale.
- **Common traps**: equating cultural unity with political unity (Dar al-Islam); calling the manor a wage-labor market; assuming American states lacked complex administration.

## Chapter 3: Networks of Exchange, c. 1200-1450
- **Core questions**: What made long-distance exchange possible? What besides goods traveled along these networks? How did the Mongols reshape connectivity?
- **Key concepts**: Silk Roads, Indian Ocean monsoon trade, trans-Saharan caravan routes, Pax Mongolica, the camel saddle and dhow/junk technology, diasporic merchant communities, the Black Death, cultural and technological diffusion.
- **Applied skills**: mapping a trade network with its causes and consequences; reading a travel account (e.g., Ibn Battuta on Kilwa) as evidence of integration.
- **Common traps**: assuming European merchants dominated the Indian Ocean before 1498; thinking only luxuries moved by sea; treating the Black Death as proof that trade had collapsed.

## Chapter 4: Land-Based Empires and Maritime Exchange, c. 1450-1750
- **Core questions**: How did large empires consolidate and administer power? How did rulers manage religious diversity? Why did some states retreat from the sea while others pressed forward?
- **Key concepts**: Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal "gunpowder empires"; the Ottoman millet system and devshirme; Akbar's toleration and the mansabdari system; Qing and Ming China; Zheng He and Ming retrenchment; Romanov Siberian expansion; Tokugawa sakoku.
- **Applied skills**: DBQ-style source analysis on empire, exchange, or labor; explaining divergent maritime strategies.
- **Common traps**: calling the millet system forced conversion; confusing Akbar's toleration with Aurangzeb's orthodoxy; attributing Ming retrenchment to a lack of ship technology.

## Chapter 5: Transoceanic Interconnections, c. 1450-1750
- **Core questions**: What were the demographic and ecological effects of transatlantic contact? How did new financial and labor institutions reorganize the world economy?
- **Key concepts**: Columbian Exchange and the demographic catastrophe; joint-stock companies (VOC, EIC); the Atlantic triangular trade and Middle Passage; encomienda and coerced labor; the global silver trade (Potosi, Manila galleon, Ming demand).
- **Applied skills**: tracing causation from disease to labor systems; distinguishing encomienda from chattel slavery in legal form.
- **Common traps**: attributing American population collapse to deportation rather than disease; thinking joint-stock companies were crown-owned; reversing the legs of the triangular trade.

## Chapter 6: Revolutions and Industrialization, c. 1750-1900
- **Core questions**: What caused the Atlantic revolutions? Why did industrialization begin in Britain and spread unevenly? How did societies respond to industrial change?
- **Key concepts**: Enlightenment (Locke, Rousseau, Montesquieu), natural rights and consent; American, French, Haitian, and Latin American revolutions; creole leadership (Bolivar, San Martin); coal, capital, agriculture, and imperial markets; capitalism, socialism, reform, migration.
- **Applied skills**: outlining an LEQ on causation or comparison; reading an observer (e.g., Tocqueville) as evidence of civic life.
- **Common traps**: claiming the Haitian Revolution failed; assuming peninsulares led independence; explaining British industrialization by a single factor.

## Chapter 7: Imperialism and Global Conflict, c. 1750-1945
- **Core questions**: What motives drove the new imperialism? How did colonized peoples respond and resist? What made the world wars genuinely global and "total"?
- **Key concepts**: Berlin Conference and the partition of Africa; Opium Wars, unequal treaties, the "century of humiliation"; Meiji defensive modernization; the 1857 uprising and the British Raj; causes of WWI (MAIN); Treaty of Versailles and Article 231; total war and home fronts; fascism and the Holocaust.
- **Applied skills**: building a thematic timeline linking empire, migration, and conflict; sourcing a treaty (Berlin Act, Versailles) for purpose and effect.
- **Common traps**: dating Indian independence to 1858; calling the world wars purely European; reading Versailles as lenient toward Germany.

## Chapter 8: Cold War, Decolonization, and Globalization, c. 1900-Present
- **Core questions**: How did newly independent states navigate the bipolar Cold War order? What changed after 1991? What continuities shaped globalization?
- **Key concepts**: Partition of India; the African independence wave; Korean War and proxy conflict; Cuban Missile Crisis; Bandung and the Non-Aligned Movement; Nasser and Suez; collapse of the USSR; European integration; China's WTO accession; neoliberalism; BRICS; climate diplomacy.
- **Applied skills**: an SAQ set on decolonization, the Cold War, and global change; long-arc comparison (e.g., Mongol-era vs. post-1991 connectivity).
- **Common traps**: treating decolonization as purely ethical European choice; claiming the Cuban Missile Crisis ended the Cold War; flattening continuity claims into "everything was the same."

## Chapter 9: AP World Exam Studio
- **Core questions**: Which documents belong together, and why? What accurate outside evidence strengthens an argument? How can a thesis address change over time and complexity?
- **Key concepts**: stimulus MCQ strategy, SAQ precision, DBQ grouping and sourcing, LEQ planning, outside evidence, the complexity point.
- **Applied skills**: writing one timed DBQ and one LEQ; grouping documents and sourcing for HIPP/HAPP; keeping a skill-by-skill correction log.
- **Common traps**: summarizing documents instead of using them as evidence; forgetting to source; asserting complexity without demonstrating it; running out of time on the LEQ.

## Capstone
Build an AP World portfolio that demonstrates command of both content and craft: unit maps for all nine periods, two polished SAQ sets, one full LEQ, one timed DBQ with sourced documents, and a global-themes reflection that traces a single theme (such as networks of exchange, state power, or labor systems) across at least four periods. The portfolio should show explicit historical reasoning, accurate outside evidence, and a defensible argument about continuity and change over the whole arc.

## Assessment Ideas
- Source work graded on contextualization, sourcing (HIPP/HAPP), and use of evidence.
- Essays graded with the AP rubric: thesis, contextualization, evidence, reasoning, and complexity.
- Capstone graded on global scope, comparison across regions, and defensible periodization.
