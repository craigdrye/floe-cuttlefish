# AP Physics Collections
A combined AP Physics survey that builds physical intuition, mathematical modeling, and exam-ready reasoning across the algebra-based and calculus-based pathways.

**ID**: `col-ap-physics` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
AP Physics Collections gives students a single coherent route through the ideas shared by AP Physics 1, AP Physics 2, and AP Physics C. Rather than tracking one exam in isolation, it gathers the load-bearing concepts that recur across all of them: motion and forces, conservation laws, fields and circuits, waves and optics, and the foundations of modern physics. The goal is a student who can look at a new situation, choose a useful model, defend it with the right representation, and carry a calculation through to a checked answer.

The course treats physics as a modeling discipline. Every topic asks the same underlying questions: what system are we describing, what interactions cross its boundary, which quantities are conserved, and what does the data say. Students practice moving fluidly between words, diagrams, graphs, equations, and numbers, because the AP exams reward exactly this flexibility. Symbolic reasoning is emphasized before plugging in numbers so that relationships and limiting cases stay visible.

By the end of the course a student should be comfortable designing a simple experiment, analyzing motion and energy graphs, applying Newton's laws and conservation principles to multi-body systems, reasoning about electric and magnetic fields and circuits, and explaining wave, optical, and modern-physics phenomena in a clear claim-evidence-reasoning structure suited to AP free-response scoring.

## Course Design Notes
The College Board's AP Physics 1 framework is organized around algebra-based units and shared science practices: creating representations, applying mathematical routines, designing experiments, analyzing data, and constructing arguments. AP Physics 2 extends into fluids, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, optics, and modern physics, while AP Physics C (Mechanics and E&M) recasts the mechanics and electromagnetism content with calculus. This syllabus serves as a combined overview: teachers can use it as a survey or deepen either the algebra-based or calculus-based treatment of any chapter. Calculus links are flagged where they matter most (kinematics derivatives/integrals, work as a line integral, flux and induction) so the same chapters scale up for a C-level audience. Throughout, students should solve symbolically first, check units and limiting cases, and reserve numerical answers for the final step.

## Chapter 1: Kinematics and Motion Graphs
- **Core questions**: How are displacement, velocity, and acceleration related on a graph? How does 2D motion split into independent components? What stays constant during projectile and free-fall motion?
- **Key concepts**: displacement vs. distance, average vs. instantaneous velocity, constant-acceleration equations, slope and area interpretations, projectile and relative motion, calculus links (derivative = slope, integral = area).
- **Applied skills**: reading position/velocity/acceleration graphs, decomposing vectors, solving projectile problems with independent x and y, predicting from a data table.
- **Common traps**: confusing velocity with acceleration when a graph slopes; thinking horizontal projectile velocity changes; forgetting that "starts from rest" sets the 1/2 a t^2 form.

## Chapter 2: Forces and Newton's Laws
- **Core questions**: What forces act on this object, and which are interaction pairs? How does the net force set the acceleration? How does changing the system boundary change the analysis?
- **Key concepts**: Newton's three laws, free-body diagrams, weight vs. normal force, tension, static and kinetic friction, inclines, systems of connected masses.
- **Applied skills**: drawing and labeling free-body diagrams, summing forces along chosen axes, applying F_net = ma, identifying third-law pairs correctly.
- **Common traps**: treating the normal force as always equal to weight; pairing forces on the same object as action-reaction; ignoring that friction opposes relative motion, not motion in general.

## Chapter 3: Work, Energy, and Power
- **Core questions**: What energy transfers occur in this process? Is mechanical energy conserved, and if not, what force did the dissipating work? How fast is energy delivered?
- **Key concepts**: work as force times displacement (and as a line integral), kinetic and potential energy, the work-energy theorem, conservation of energy, nonconservative forces, power, energy bar charts.
- **Applied skills**: building energy bar charts, applying conservation to ramps and pendulums, computing work from a force-vs-position graph, relating power to force and velocity.
- **Common traps**: counting a force that is perpendicular to displacement as doing work; forgetting friction or drag when invoking conservation; confusing energy with force or power.

## Chapter 4: Momentum, Impulse, and Collisions
- **Core questions**: What quantity is conserved here, and why? What external impulse acts on the system? How do elastic and inelastic collisions differ in their energy accounting?
- **Key concepts**: linear momentum, impulse-momentum theorem, conservation of momentum, elastic vs. inelastic collisions, center of mass.
- **Applied skills**: choosing a system so external forces vanish, applying momentum conservation in 1D and 2D, distinguishing elastic from inelastic outcomes, using impulse from a force-vs-time graph.
- **Common traps**: assuming kinetic energy is conserved in every collision; forgetting momentum is a vector with direction; mixing up impulse (force times time) with work (force times distance).

## Chapter 5: Circular Motion, Gravitation, Torque, and Rotation
- **Core questions**: What provides the centripetal force here? How does Newton's law of gravitation set orbital motion? What external torque changes the rotation, and is angular momentum conserved?
- **Key concepts**: uniform circular motion, centripetal acceleration, universal gravitation and orbits, torque, moment of inertia, rotational kinematics and dynamics, angular momentum, rolling.
- **Applied skills**: identifying the real force acting as centripetal, relating orbital speed and radius, computing net torque, applying conservation of angular momentum.
- **Common traps**: invoking a fictitious "centrifugal force"; treating centripetal force as a new force rather than a role; ignoring the rotational kinetic energy of a rolling object.

## Chapter 6: Oscillations and Simple Harmonic Motion
- **Core questions**: What restoring force makes this motion oscillatory? What sets the period? How do energy and force vary through a cycle?
- **Key concepts**: simple harmonic motion, restoring force proportional to displacement, period of a mass-spring system and a simple pendulum, energy exchange between kinetic and potential.
- **Applied skills**: identifying SHM from a linear restoring force, computing period for springs and pendulums, locating where speed and acceleration are maximum or zero.
- **Common traps**: thinking pendulum period depends on mass or large amplitude; confusing maximum displacement (zero speed) with equilibrium (maximum speed); mixing up frequency and period.

## Chapter 7: Fluids and Thermodynamics
- **Core questions**: What pressure difference drives this flow or float? What work is represented on a PV diagram? How do heat, work, and internal energy balance?
- **Key concepts**: density and pressure, Pascal's and Archimedes' principles, buoyancy, continuity and Bernoulli's equation, ideal gas law, the first law of thermodynamics, PV diagrams, heat engines and efficiency.
- **Applied skills**: predicting whether an object floats, applying continuity and Bernoulli to flow, reading work as area on a PV diagram, applying ΔU = Q − W.
- **Common traps**: confusing pressure with force; sign errors on work done by vs. on a gas; assuming faster fluid means higher pressure (it is lower).

## Chapter 8: Electrostatics and DC Circuits
- **Core questions**: What field or potential exists at this point? Which charges interact, and how? What current flows along each path of the circuit?
- **Key concepts**: Coulomb's law, electric field and potential, field lines, conductors and insulators, capacitance, current, resistance, Ohm's law, series and parallel circuits, Kirchhoff's rules, power dissipation.
- **Applied skills**: computing field and potential from point charges, analyzing series/parallel resistor networks, applying Kirchhoff's loop and junction rules, finding power in a circuit element.
- **Common traps**: confusing electric field with potential; adding series and parallel resistances with the wrong rule; thinking current is "used up" around a loop.

## Chapter 9: Magnetism and Electromagnetic Induction
- **Core questions**: What magnetic force acts on this moving charge or current? How does a changing flux drive an EMF? Which way does the induced current flow?
- **Key concepts**: magnetic fields, force on moving charges and current-carrying wires, fields from wires and loops, magnetic flux, Faraday's law, Lenz's law, induced EMF (calculus link: flux as an integral, EMF as a rate of change).
- **Applied skills**: using the right-hand rule for force and field, computing flux through a loop, applying Faraday's and Lenz's laws to predict induced current direction.
- **Common traps**: confusing electric and magnetic forces on charges; forgetting that only the velocity component perpendicular to B contributes; reversing the induced-current direction from Lenz's law.

## Chapter 10: Waves, Sound, and Optics
- **Core questions**: What does the wave model predict here? How do interference and the Doppler effect arise? How do lenses and mirrors form images?
- **Key concepts**: transverse and longitudinal waves, wave speed/frequency/wavelength, superposition and standing waves, the Doppler effect, reflection and refraction, Snell's law, lenses and mirrors, ray diagrams, interference and diffraction.
- **Applied skills**: relating v, f, and λ; locating standing-wave nodes; applying Snell's law; drawing ray diagrams; predicting constructive vs. destructive interference.
- **Common traps**: confusing frequency and wavelength; forgetting wave speed is set by the medium, not the source; mixing up real vs. virtual images and the lens sign convention.

## Chapter 11: Modern Physics
- **Core questions**: Where does classical physics break down? How does the photon model explain the photoelectric effect? What governs nuclear stability and decay?
- **Key concepts**: photons and the photoelectric effect, wave-particle duality, atomic energy levels and spectra, mass-energy equivalence, nuclear processes (alpha, beta, gamma decay), half-life.
- **Applied skills**: applying E = hf and the photoelectric work-function relation, interpreting line spectra from energy-level transitions, balancing nuclear reactions, reasoning about half-life.
- **Common traps**: thinking brighter light raises photoelectron energy (it raises the number, not the energy); confusing energy levels with orbits; mishandling decay particle identities.

## Chapter 12: AP Exam Studio
- **Core questions**: What exactly does the prompt ask for? Which representation earns credit fastest? How should an explanation connect principle to result?
- **Key concepts**: multiple-choice strategy, symbolic derivation, experimental design, quantitative-qualitative translation, claim-evidence-reasoning, AP science practices and scoring guidelines.
- **Applied skills**: deriving results symbolically before substituting numbers, designing a controlled experiment with identified variables, writing concise free-response justifications, self-scoring against a rubric.
- **Common traps**: skipping straight to numbers and losing setup credit; vague explanations that restate the prompt; experimental designs with no controlled variable or measurement plan.

## Capstone
Assemble a physics portfolio with five pieces: (1) a mechanics lab analyzing motion or forces with a graph and uncertainty discussion; (2) an energy or momentum investigation that tests a conservation law with before/after representations; (3) a field, circuit, or wave model that makes a prediction and checks it against evidence; (4) an experimental-design free-response that names variables, controls, and a measurement procedure; and (5) a timed AP-style free-response set with a scoring-guideline correction log. Together these demonstrate modeling, data analysis, conservation reasoning, and exam-ready explanation across the AP Physics pathways.

## Assessment Ideas
- Lab reports graded on model, evidence, uncertainty, and reasoning.
- Problem sets graded on representations and symbolic setup before numerical answers.
- Capstone graded on AP science practices and the quality of the correction log.
