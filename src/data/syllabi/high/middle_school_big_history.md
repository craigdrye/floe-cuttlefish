# Middle School Big History
The biggest story you can responsibly tell: from the Big Bang to today, one evidence-labeled timeline at a time.

**ID**: `middleSchoolBigHistory` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
Big History asks the largest question a person can ask and still answer with evidence: how did the universe, Earth, life, humans, and modern societies come to be the way they are? This course invites students to investigate that story across thirteen-plus billion years, moving fluently between science and history and treating cosmology, geology, biology, and human history as one connected narrative rather than separate subjects.

The course is built around the idea of increasing complexity. At certain moments, called thresholds, new conditions allowed something more complex to appear: stars, chemical elements, planets, life, human culture, farming, and the modern industrial world. Students learn to recognise these thresholds, ask what made each one possible, and explain large patterns without losing sight of real people, places, and choices. Every claim is meant to be answerable with something a student can point to: an observation, a model, a fossil, an artifact, a map, a timeline, or a dataset.

Above all this is a course in evidence-first thinking. Students practise comparing sources, naming uncertainty honestly, and building explanations that are vivid, careful, and honest about what we do not yet know. The goal is not to memorise the universe but to reason about it like a historian and a scientist at the same time.

## Course Design Notes
This course is designed for curious students roughly ages 11-14 and follows the OER Project Big History threshold sequence. The tone keeps the wonder of Big History while insisting that every claim be backed by evidence. Vocabulary is introduced in context and revisited, scientific ideas are kept at a middle-school level of precision, and uncertainty is presented as a normal and respectable part of inquiry rather than a flaw. Each chapter pairs a scientific or historical idea with a thinking skill (scale, evidence evaluation, cause and effect, source comparison) so students leave able to reason, not just recite. Cross-disciplinary links are emphasised throughout, since the central payoff of Big History is seeing how stars, chemistry, biology, and human choices connect.

## Chapter 1: Big Questions, Big Time, and Evidence
- **Core questions**: How can a timeline show time too huge to picture? What makes one claim stronger than another? When should a historian or scientist say "we do not know yet"?
- **Key concepts**: scale, deep time, timeline, claim, evidence, model, uncertainty, origin story, chronology, perspective.
- **Applied skills**: place events at correct relative scale on a timeline; distinguish a claim from the evidence that supports it; rate the strength of a source.
- **Common traps**: treating human history as most of the timeline; confusing a vivid story with a well-evidenced one; mistaking "we are unsure" for "anything goes."

## Chapter 2: The Big Bang and the First Ingredients
- **Core questions**: What evidence suggests the universe is expanding? Why do scientists use models for events nobody watched happen? What did the early universe actually contain?
- **Key concepts**: Big Bang, expansion, redshift, cosmic microwave background, matter, energy, hydrogen, helium, gravity.
- **Applied skills**: explain redshift as wavelength stretching; describe the cosmic microwave background as leftover light; identify hydrogen and helium as the first abundant elements.
- **Common traps**: imagining the Big Bang as an explosion in pre-existing space; thinking all elements formed at once; treating "model" as a synonym for "guess."

## Chapter 3: Stars, Elements, and Cosmic Recycling
- **Core questions**: How do stars make new elements? Why are supernovae important for planets and living things? What does it mean to say atoms have histories?
- **Key concepts**: star, nebula, gravity, nuclear fusion, supernova, chemical element, periodic table, stellar life cycle, complexity.
- **Applied skills**: trace an element from fusion in a star through a supernova to Earth; connect heavy-element formation to the periodic table; explain stellar recycling.
- **Common traps**: thinking Earth's crust is the original source of elements; assuming living things create new elements; ignoring gravity's role in star formation.

## Chapter 4: Earth, the Moon, and Goldilocks Conditions
- **Core questions**: How did dust and rock become planets? Which conditions made early Earth unusual? How do scientists use rocks, craters, and simulations as evidence?
- **Key concepts**: solar system, accretion, orbit, Hadean Earth, Moon formation, habitable zone, atmosphere, liquid water, Goldilocks conditions.
- **Applied skills**: describe accretion of planets from a disk; compare Earth's conditions with another world; read craters and rocks as dated evidence.
- **Common traps**: assuming "Goldilocks" means hottest or biggest; treating every planet as identical; placing the Moon's formation after life began.

## Chapter 5: Life Begins and Changes the Planet
- **Core questions**: What counts as evidence for very early life? How did photosynthesis change the atmosphere? Why can extinction open space for new forms of life?
- **Key concepts**: life, cell, DNA, reproduction, adaptation, photosynthesis, oxygen, Great Oxidation Event, fossil, extinction, evolution.
- **Applied skills**: read fossils and rock layers as evidence; explain how cyanobacteria raised atmospheric oxygen; link extinction events to later diversification.
- **Common traps**: thinking microbes are too small to change a planet; confusing the order of major events; treating evolution as goal-directed.

## Chapter 6: Humans, Migration, and Collective Learning
- **Core questions**: What evidence helps us understand early humans? How did language and shared memory change survival? Why did humans live differently in different environments?
- **Key concepts**: hominin, Homo sapiens, migration, adaptation, tool use, control of fire, language, art, collective learning, culture.
- **Applied skills**: build an evidence-based migration map; explain collective learning as knowledge accumulating across generations; link tools and environments.
- **Common traps**: assuming knowledge is inherited at birth; claiming other animals learn identically to humans; treating "collective learning" as individual genius.

## Chapter 7: Farming, Cities, and Written Records
- **Core questions**: Why did some communities begin farming? What were the benefits and costs of settled life? How did writing change what later historians can know?
- **Key concepts**: agriculture, domestication, surplus, settlement, specialisation, hierarchy, trade, disease, writing, civilisation.
- **Applied skills**: weigh farming's benefits against its costs with at least three kinds of evidence; explain how surplus enabled cities and writing; date the rise of writing relative to farming.
- **Common traps**: telling a one-sided "farming made everything better" story; assuming dense settlements reduced disease; assuming writing began the moment farming did.

## Chapter 8: The Modern Revolution and the Anthropocene
- **Core questions**: Why did change speed up after about 1750? How are energy, machines, cities, and empires connected? What evidence shows humans now shape Earth systems?
- **Key concepts**: fossil fuel, industrialisation, population growth, exchange network, globalisation, great acceleration, Anthropocene, sustainability.
- **Applied skills**: read a graph or map to tell a data story about modern change; connect fossil-fuel energy to industrial and population growth; identify human impacts on Earth systems.
- **Common traps**: crediting one inventor for all modern technology; assuming humans became biologically smarter; ignoring the costs that came with the benefits.

## Capstone
Create a Big History museum exhibit called "How Did We Get Here?" The exhibit includes eight objects or panels, one from each chapter, and each panel must state a claim, present its evidence, include an image or diagram, and give a short explanation. The exhibit must show at least three cross-disciplinary links, such as stars to chemistry, farming to disease, or fossil fuels to climate, and the student must be able to answer audience questions about scale, evidence, and uncertainty for any panel.

## Assessment Ideas
- Evidence notebooks graded on clear claims, source labels, honest treatment of uncertainty, and revision after feedback.
- Short explanations graded for accurate sequence, cause and effect, correct sense of scale, and age-appropriate scientific vocabulary.
- Capstone exhibit graded on evidence quality, cross-disciplinary connections, visual clarity, and the student's ability to answer audience questions live.
