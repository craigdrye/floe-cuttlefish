# Practical Politics & Voting

## Course Aim
Equip high school students to understand how political power is organized, how public decisions are made, and how citizens can participate effectively in the United States, the United Kingdom, and the European Union. The course treats politics as a set of institutions, incentives, rights, responsibilities, arguments, and practical skills rather than as a spectator sport.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when students need practical civic literacy: how institutions work, how votes turn into power, how laws and budgets move, how media and money shape attention, and how citizens can act responsibly. Keep examples nonpartisan, comparative, and concrete enough for students who are preparing to vote and follow political news.

## Essential Questions
- Who has authority in a political system, and what limits that authority?
- How do constitutions, laws, courts, parties, elections, media, and organized groups shape public decisions?
- Why do different voting systems produce different kinds of representation?
- How can citizens evaluate political claims, follow a policy issue, and take responsible action?
- What can the U.S., U.K., and EU systems teach us by comparison?

## Unit 1: Citizenship, Democracy, and Political Power
**Key concepts:** democracy, representation, sovereignty, constitutionalism, rule of law, rights, responsibilities, legitimacy, accountability, civil society.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Distinguish direct, representative, parliamentary, presidential, federal, unitary, and supranational systems.
- Explain how rights, law, elections, courts, and a free press limit power.
- Build a "power map" for a current issue showing formal decision-makers and informal influencers.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- Short diagnostic: trace one public decision from problem to rule, budget, or court challenge.
- Comparative chart: where power lives in the U.S., U.K., and EU.

## Unit 2: Elections, Voting Systems, and Representation
**Key concepts:** first-past-the-post, proportional representation, ranked-choice voting, primaries, constituencies, districts, turnout, voter registration, referendums, gerrymandering, mandates, coalitions.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Compare how votes become seats under different electoral rules.
- Analyze who is represented, overrepresented, or discouraged from participating.
- Read an election result without confusing vote share, seat share, turnout, and governing power.
- Practice the mechanics of informed voting: eligibility, registration, ballot research, local election offices, and nonpartisan voter guides.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- Election simulation using the same class votes under first-past-the-post, ranked-choice, and proportional systems.
- Data brief on turnout or district design with a claim, evidence, and limitation.

## Unit 3: U.S. Government in Practice
**Key concepts:** Constitution, federalism, separation of powers, checks and balances, Congress, presidency, bureaucracy, Supreme Court, state and local government, committees, oversight, executive orders, budgets, judicial review.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Explain how a bill, regulation, executive action, budget choice, or lawsuit can change policy.
- Identify which level of government has authority over schools, elections, policing, environment, immigration, health, and taxes.
- Evaluate a U.S. policy claim using constitutional text, institutional incentives, data, and stakeholder interests.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- Committee hearing role-play on a proposed law.
- "Who can fix this?" memo assigning a problem to local, state, federal, court, agency, or private actors.

## Unit 4: U.K. Government in Practice
**Key concepts:** parliamentary sovereignty, constitutional monarchy, House of Commons, House of Lords, prime minister, cabinet, opposition, party discipline, whips, confidence, devolution, local councils, civil service, judicial independence.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Explain how governments form, survive, and fall in a parliamentary system.
- Describe how Parliament scrutinizes government through questions, committees, debates, amendments, and elections.
- Compare the role of MPs, ministers, judges, civil servants, devolved governments, and local councils.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- Prime Minister's Questions or select committee analysis: claim, evidence, evasion, accountability.
- Devolution case study comparing Westminster, Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland, and local authority roles.

## Unit 5: The European Union and Multi-Level Governance
**Key concepts:** European Commission, European Parliament, Council of the European Union, European Council, Court of Justice of the EU, treaties, regulations, directives, subsidiarity, member states, EU citizenship, rule of law, single market.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Explain how EU law is proposed, negotiated, approved, interpreted, and implemented.
- Distinguish the Council of the EU from the European Council and the Council of Europe.
- Analyze how national governments and EU institutions share responsibility for cross-border problems.
- Evaluate a claim about "Brussels" by identifying the actual institution, legal instrument, and member-state role.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- EU decision-making simulation on climate, digital privacy, migration, consumer safety, or trade.
- One-page explainer: regulation versus directive, with a real example.

## Unit 6: Political Ideas, Parties, and Public Opinion
**Key concepts:** liberalism, conservatism, socialism, social democracy, nationalism, populism, libertarianism, environmental politics, left-right spectrum, ideology, partisanship, political socialization, polling, polarization.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Separate values, empirical claims, policy tools, and trade-offs in political arguments.
- Compare party systems in the U.S., U.K., and EU without reducing them to caricatures.
- Read polls carefully: sample, margin of error, question wording, trend, and interpretation.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- Ideology and policy matrix: multiple political traditions responding to the same public problem.
- Poll critique: rewrite a weak headline into a more accurate one.

## Unit 7: Money, Media, Lobbying, and Influence
**Key concepts:** campaign finance, lobbying, interest groups, unions, think tanks, advocacy organizations, public consultations, political advertising, agenda-setting, framing, misinformation, disinformation, source evaluation.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Track who is trying to influence a decision and what tools they use.
- Evaluate news, social media, campaign materials, and advocacy claims for evidence, bias, missing context, and incentives.
- Distinguish legal influence, corruption, persuasion, mobilization, and manipulation.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- Influence audit for a live policy issue: donors, lobbyists, advocacy groups, media frames, public opinion, and decision points.
- Media comparison log across at least three outlets or platforms.

## Unit 8: Civic Action Lab
**Key concepts:** public problem, stakeholder, jurisdiction, theory of change, petition, public comment, meeting agenda, testimony, representative contact, volunteering, coalition, accountability.

**Practical skills and outcomes:**
- Convert a concern into a specific, lawful, evidence-based civic ask.
- Write a concise message to a representative, agency, school board, council, or public body.
- Practice deliberation: listen, question, revise, compromise, and justify a position.
- Reflect on ethical civic participation, including accuracy, respect, privacy, and consequences.

**Assessment and project ideas:**
- Capstone civic action portfolio: issue brief, power map, evidence packet, stakeholder interview or public meeting observation, action plan, and reflection.
- Public presentation judged on accuracy, feasibility, evidence, and understanding of opposing arguments.

## Course-Wide Assessment
- **Civic knowledge checks:** short quizzes on institutions, vocabulary, and processes.
- **Document and data analysis:** ballots, court excerpts, bills, regulations, speeches, budgets, maps, polls, and news reports.
- **Comparative essays:** explain how the same problem would move through U.S., U.K., and EU systems.
- **Simulations:** elections, legislative hearings, coalition negotiations, public consultations, and committee scrutiny.
- **Capstone portfolio:** a practical civic action project grounded in evidence and institutional reality.

## Capstone
Students complete a civic action portfolio on a real issue, including an issue brief, power map, evidence packet, stakeholder interview or public meeting observation, practical action plan, and reflection on accuracy, feasibility, and respectful disagreement.

## Assessment Ideas
Use institution checks, election simulations, bill tracing, media comparison logs, poll critiques, constitutional or legal document analysis, comparative essays, and the civic action portfolio. Assess whether students can identify who has authority, what process applies, and what responsible participation would look like.

## Final Outcomes
By the end of the course, students should be able to identify who has power, what process is being used, what evidence matters, whose interests are involved, how citizens can respond, and how democratic systems can be evaluated without cynicism or naivety.
