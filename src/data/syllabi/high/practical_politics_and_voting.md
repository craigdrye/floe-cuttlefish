# Practical Politics & Voting
Understand how political power actually works — institutions, votes, money, media, and the practical skills to participate as an informed citizen in the U.S., U.K., and EU.

**ID**: `practicalPolitics` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This course equips high school students to understand how political power is organized, how public decisions are made, and how citizens can participate effectively. It treats politics as a system of institutions, incentives, rights, responsibilities, arguments, and concrete skills rather than as a spectator sport or a clash of teams. Students learn to identify who holds authority, what process is being used, what evidence matters, and how a citizen can respond responsibly.

The course is deliberately comparative, built around three democratic systems — the United States, the United Kingdom, and the European Union — that make broadly similar promises through sharply different machinery: presidential federalism with strong judicial review; parliamentary government resting on parliamentary sovereignty and devolution; and supranational policy-making negotiated among member states. Comparison lets students see what institutions actually do, rather than confusing institutions with national culture or partisan reputation.

Throughout, examples are kept nonpartisan, current, and concrete enough for students who are preparing to vote, follow political news, and act on issues they care about. The goal is civic literacy without cynicism or naivety: students should be able to read an election result, trace a law, audit who is trying to influence a decision, and take a lawful, evidence-based civic action.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when students need practical civic literacy: how institutions work, how votes become power, how laws and budgets move, how media and money shape attention, and how citizens can act responsibly. Each chapter pairs institutional knowledge with a usable skill — building a power map, reading an election result, tracing a bill, critiquing a poll, running an influence audit, or drafting a message to a representative. Recurring routines include "who has authority here?", separating vote share from seat share from governing power, and distinguishing legal influence from persuasion, mobilization, and manipulation. Examples stay comparative across the U.S., U.K., and EU and are kept nonpartisan. Useful anchors include U.S. state civics standards, England's statutory citizenship curriculum and GCSE Citizenship Studies, the EU Learning Corner, and the Council of Europe Reference Framework of Competences for Democratic Culture.

## Chapter 1: Democracy, Power, and the Rule of Law
- **Core questions**: Who has authority in a political system, and what limits that authority? How is legitimate power different from raw power? Where does power live for a given issue, formally and informally?
- **Key concepts**: democracy, representation, sovereignty, constitutionalism, rule of law, separation of powers, rights and responsibilities, legitimacy, accountability, civil society.
- **Applied skills**: distinguishing direct, representative, parliamentary, presidential, federal, unitary, and supranational systems; building a "power map" for a current issue that shows formal decision-makers and informal influencers.
- **Common traps**: assuming whoever is loudest holds power; confusing legal authority with public popularity; treating "the government" as a single actor rather than competing institutions.

## Chapter 2: Elections, Voting Systems, and Representation
- **Core questions**: Why do different voting systems produce different kinds of representation? How do votes turn into seats, and seats into governing power? Who is represented, overrepresented, or discouraged from voting?
- **Key concepts**: first-past-the-post, proportional representation, ranked-choice voting, primaries, constituencies and districts, turnout, voter registration, referendums, gerrymandering, mandates, coalitions, vote share vs. seat share.
- **Applied skills**: comparing how identical votes convert to seats under different rules; reading an election result without confusing vote share, seat share, turnout, and governing power; the mechanics of informed voting (eligibility, registration, ballot research, nonpartisan voter guides).
- **Common traps**: equating the largest vote share with a majority of seats or a "mandate"; assuming low turnout always favors one side; mistaking a referendum result for a detailed policy instruction.

## Chapter 3: The U.S. System in Practice
- **Core questions**: How can a bill, regulation, executive action, budget choice, or lawsuit change policy? Which level of government has authority over a given problem? How do checks and balances actually constrain each branch?
- **Key concepts**: Constitution, federalism, separation of powers, checks and balances, Congress and committees, the presidency and executive orders, the bureaucracy, judicial review and the Supreme Court, state and local government, the budget and appropriations.
- **Applied skills**: tracing how a problem could be addressed through legislation, regulation, executive action, the courts, or state action; assigning a problem to the correct level of government; evaluating a policy claim using constitutional text, institutional incentives, data, and stakeholder interests.
- **Common traps**: thinking the president can make law alone; assuming federal power covers everything; confusing judicial review (deciding cases) with writing or vetoing legislation.

## Chapter 4: The U.K. System in Practice
- **Core questions**: How do governments form, survive, and fall in a parliamentary system? How does Parliament hold the government to account? How is power shared with devolved governments and local councils?
- **Key concepts**: parliamentary sovereignty, constitutional monarchy, House of Commons, House of Lords, prime minister and cabinet, the opposition, party discipline and whips, confidence and no-confidence, devolution, local councils, an independent civil service and judiciary.
- **Applied skills**: explaining government formation and collapse; describing scrutiny through questions, select committees, debates, and amendments; comparing the roles of MPs, ministers, judges, civil servants, devolved governments, and councils.
- **Common traps**: assuming the monarch governs; treating the prime minister as a U.S.-style president with separate powers; ignoring devolution and local government when assigning responsibility.

## Chapter 5: The European Union and Multi-Level Governance
- **Core questions**: How is EU law proposed, negotiated, approved, interpreted, and implemented? What is the difference between a regulation and a directive? How do member states and EU institutions share responsibility for cross-border problems?
- **Key concepts**: European Commission, European Parliament, Council of the EU, European Council, Court of Justice of the EU, treaties, regulations vs. directives, subsidiarity, member states, EU citizenship, the single market, the rule of law.
- **Applied skills**: explaining the ordinary legislative procedure; distinguishing the Council of the EU from the European Council and the Council of Europe; checking a claim about "Brussels" by naming the actual institution, legal instrument, and member-state role.
- **Common traps**: treating the EU as a single government rather than a negotiated system; confusing the three "Councils"; assuming a directive applies the same way in every member state as a regulation would.

## Chapter 6: Ideologies, Parties, and Public Opinion
- **Core questions**: How do values, empirical claims, policy tools, and trade-offs differ within a political argument? How do party systems compare across the U.S., U.K., and EU? How can a poll be read carefully?
- **Key concepts**: liberalism, conservatism, socialism and social democracy, nationalism, populism, libertarianism, environmental politics, the left-right spectrum, ideology, partisanship, political socialization, polling, polarization.
- **Applied skills**: separating values from facts from policy means in a real argument; comparing party systems without caricature; reading a poll for sample, margin of error, question wording, trend, and interpretation.
- **Common traps**: treating an ideology as a single fixed policy list; reading one poll as a prediction; confusing a value disagreement with a factual one.

## Chapter 7: Money, Media, Lobbying, and Influence
- **Core questions**: Who is trying to influence a decision, and with what tools? How can news, advertising, and advocacy claims be evaluated? Where is the line between legal influence and corruption?
- **Key concepts**: campaign finance, lobbying, interest groups, unions, think tanks, advocacy organizations, public consultations, political advertising, agenda-setting, framing, misinformation, disinformation, source evaluation.
- **Applied skills**: running an influence audit for a live issue (donors, lobbyists, advocacy groups, media frames, public opinion, decision points); evaluating sources for evidence, bias, missing context, and incentives; distinguishing legal influence, persuasion, mobilization, and manipulation.
- **Common traps**: assuming any spending or lobbying is automatically corruption; trusting a claim because it confirms a prior belief; mistaking emotional framing for evidence.

## Chapter 8: Civic Action Lab
- **Core questions**: How does a concern become a specific, lawful, evidence-based civic ask? Who is the right decision-maker, and what process applies? What does ethical, effective participation look like?
- **Key concepts**: public problem, stakeholder, jurisdiction, theory of change, petition, public comment and testimony, contacting a representative, volunteering, coalition-building, accountability.
- **Applied skills**: converting a concern into a concrete ask aimed at the right body; writing a concise message to a representative, agency, school board, or council; practicing deliberation — listen, question, revise, compromise, and justify a position.
- **Common traps**: aiming an ask at the wrong level of government; demanding action no institution actually has the power to take; mistaking volume of complaint for a workable plan.

## Capstone
Students complete a civic action portfolio on a real, current issue of their choosing. The portfolio includes an issue brief, a power map identifying formal decision-makers and informal influencers, an evidence packet with sources evaluated for bias and incentive, a stakeholder interview or public-meeting observation, a practical and lawful action plan aimed at the correct decision-maker, and a reflection on accuracy, feasibility, and respectful disagreement. Students present their work and are assessed on whether they can identify who has authority, what process applies, what evidence matters, whose interests are involved, and how a citizen could responsibly respond — while stating opposing arguments fairly.
