# AP Economics (Macro & Micro)
Graph-fluent, model-driven preparation for both AP Microeconomics and AP Macroeconomics in one combined year.

**ID**: `col-ap-economics` · **Discipline**: Economics

## Course Aim
This course prepares students for the AP Microeconomics and AP Macroeconomics exams by building the graph fluency, model reasoning, and quantitative analysis the College Board rewards. Students learn to label diagrams precisely, shift curves for a stated shock, read the new equilibrium, and explain the chain from cause to effect in clear AP-style prose. The two exams share an identical format — 60 multiple-choice questions worth about two-thirds of the score and three free-response questions (one long, two short) worth the remaining third — so the same disciplined habits transfer across both.

College Board treats Micro and Macro as two separate introductory college-level courses, but many schools teach them back-to-back across one year. This combined syllabus preserves both identities. It opens with the shared toolkit (scarcity, opportunity cost, the production possibilities curve, and comparative advantage), then works through the microeconomic story of how individual markets, firms, and resource owners make choices, and finally builds the macroeconomic story of how output, prices, employment, money, and policy interact for the economy as a whole.

Throughout, the emphasis is on transferable reasoning rather than memorized definitions. Students should leave able to choose the right model for a scenario, manipulate it correctly, distinguish movement along a curve from a shift of the curve, run the standard calculations (elasticity, multipliers, money creation, opportunity-cost ratios), and write explanations that connect a model change to a real-world outcome — exactly the four skill categories (principles and models, manipulation, interpretation, numerical analysis) the AP exams assess.

## Course Design Notes
The chapter order mirrors the College Board unit sequence so the syllabus can run alongside either AP course or both. Microeconomics is covered in Chapters 2-4 (supply and demand and welfare; production, costs, and competition; imperfect competition, factor markets, and market failure), and Macroeconomics in Chapters 5-7 (measurement and the business cycle; fiscal, money, and monetary policy; growth, the open economy, and cross-graph reasoning). Chapter 1 is the shared foundation and Chapter 8 is an exam studio.

Every chapter pairs conceptual understanding with at least one graph and one calculation, because AP points are won or lost on correctly labeled axes, correctly directed shifts, and correctly set-up arithmetic with units. "Common traps" sections target the specific errors AP readers see most often — confusing a shift with a movement, inverting a multiplier, reasoning from absolute rather than comparative advantage, or mixing up the directions of monetary and exchange-rate effects. The course is age-appropriate for the high-school tier: it assumes no calculus, builds quantitative skills gradually, and treats the graph as the primary thinking tool.

## Chapter 1: Economic Thinking and Graph Skills
- **Core questions**: What is the opportunity cost of a choice? Which decision is made at the margin? Which graph best models a given situation, and which axis carries which variable?
- **Key concepts**: scarcity, opportunity cost, incentives, marginal analysis, the production possibilities curve (PPC), increasing opportunity cost, comparative versus absolute advantage, gains from trade, positive versus normative claims.
- **Applied skills**: build and label a PPC; read opportunity cost as the slope between two points; compute opportunity-cost ratios to assign comparative advantage; separate a positive statement from a normative one.
- **Common traps**: treating opportunity cost as only the money spent; assigning advantage from absolute productivity instead of relative opportunity cost; forgetting that bowed-out PPCs imply increasing opportunity cost.

## Chapter 2: Supply, Demand, Elasticity, and Welfare
- **Core questions**: What shifts a curve versus what moves along it? How does elasticity determine the effect of a price change on total revenue? Who gains and who loses when the government intervenes?
- **Key concepts**: law of demand and supply, determinants of demand and supply, equilibrium, shortages and surpluses, price elasticity of demand and supply, income and cross-price elasticity, consumer and producer surplus, deadweight loss, taxes, subsidies, price ceilings, price floors.
- **Applied skills**: shift the correct curve for a stated determinant; compute price elasticity and predict the total-revenue effect; shade consumer surplus, producer surplus, and deadweight loss; show tax incidence on a diagram.
- **Common traps**: shifting demand when only quantity demanded changes (and vice versa); confusing elastic with inelastic when reading the revenue test; placing a binding price ceiling above equilibrium instead of below.

## Chapter 3: Production, Costs, and Perfect Competition
- **Core questions**: At what quantity does a firm maximize profit? Is the firm earning economic profit, breaking even, or taking a loss? Should it keep operating in the short run or shut down?
- **Key concepts**: total, marginal, and average product; diminishing marginal returns; fixed versus variable costs; marginal cost (MC), average total cost (ATC), average variable cost (AVC); the MR = MC rule; the shutdown rule (P < AVC); long-run entry and exit in perfect competition.
- **Applied skills**: draw the cost-curve family with MC cutting ATC and AVC at their minimums; locate the profit-maximizing quantity; identify profit, loss, or break-even by comparing price to ATC; apply the shutdown rule.
- **Common traps**: maximizing where MC is lowest rather than where MR = MC; using ATC instead of AVC for the shutdown decision; forgetting that economic profit attracts entry, driving long-run profit to zero in perfect competition.

## Chapter 4: Imperfect Competition, Factor Markets, and Market Failure
- **Core questions**: Which market structure fits a described firm, and where is its allocative or productive inefficiency? How are wages and resource prices determined? What policy could move the market toward efficiency?
- **Key concepts**: monopoly, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, game theory and the prisoner's dilemma, price discrimination; derived demand and marginal revenue product in factor markets; negative and positive externalities, marginal social versus private cost and benefit, public goods, the free-rider problem; Pigouvian taxes and subsidies.
- **Applied skills**: match a structure to its diagram (P > MR for price searchers); identify deadweight loss under monopoly; find a dominant strategy and Nash equilibrium in a payoff matrix; correct an externality with the right tax or subsidy.
- **Common traps**: drawing MR = demand for a monopolist; assuming any firm with profit is a monopoly; confusing private with social cost; treating a positive externality as something to tax rather than subsidize.

## Chapter 5: Macro Measurement and the Business Cycle
- **Core questions**: How is national output measured, and what is excluded? Which type of unemployment is present? How do real and nominal values differ, and what shock shifts aggregate demand or supply?
- **Key concepts**: GDP and the expenditure approach (C + I + G + Xn), nominal versus real GDP, the GDP deflator, the CPI and inflation rate, frictional/structural/cyclical unemployment, the natural rate and full employment, the business cycle, aggregate demand (AD), short-run and long-run aggregate supply (SRAS, LRAS).
- **Applied skills**: classify a transaction as counted or excluded from GDP; compute a real value or an inflation rate from index numbers; identify an unemployment type from a scenario; shift AD or SRAS for a demand or supply shock and read the new output and price level.
- **Common traps**: counting transfer payments or used-goods sales in GDP; confusing the CPI with the GDP deflator; labeling a recession-driven job loss as frictional; shifting AS when only AD has changed.

## Chapter 6: Fiscal Policy, Money, and Monetary Policy
- **Core questions**: Which policy closes a recessionary or inflationary gap, and by how much? How does the money market set the interest rate, and how does that affect investment? What does the central bank actually do?
- **Key concepts**: the spending and tax multipliers, marginal propensity to consume and save, automatic stabilizers, discretionary fiscal policy; the money market and money demand; fractional-reserve banking, the money multiplier, M1; central-bank tools (open market operations, the reserve requirement, the discount/policy rate); expansionary versus contractionary policy.
- **Applied skills**: compute the spending multiplier 1/(1 − MPC) and the tax multiplier −MPC/MPS; size a fiscal change needed to close a gap; trace open market operations through reserves, the federal funds rate, investment, and AD; calculate maximum money creation from a deposit.
- **Common traps**: inverting the multiplier (dividing by MPC instead of MPS); equating the tax and spending multipliers; thinking the Fed buys bonds to tighten; confusing the reserve requirement's direction of effect on the money supply.

## Chapter 7: Growth, the Open Economy, and Cross-Graph Reasoning
- **Core questions**: What raises long-run aggregate supply? How does government borrowing affect interest rates and private investment? How does an exchange-rate change ripple through net exports?
- **Key concepts**: productivity, capital deepening, technology, and long-run growth; the loanable funds market, real interest rates, and crowding out; the short-run Phillips curve and its vertical long-run form; the balance of payments (current and financial accounts); the foreign exchange market, currency appreciation and depreciation, and net exports.
- **Applied skills**: shift LRAS for a growth source; link a budget deficit to higher loanable-funds demand, higher real rates, and reduced investment; connect AD/AS, the money market, loanable funds, and forex on a single chain; predict the trade effect of an appreciation.
- **Common traps**: treating the long-run Phillips curve as a usable trade-off; reversing the crowding-out causal chain; assuming appreciation helps exports; mistaking a current-account deficit for an accounting error rather than an offsetting financial-account surplus.

## Chapter 8: AP Economics Exam Studio
- **Core questions**: Which graph earns the rubric point? What variable changes first, and what cascades from it? How should a free-response answer connect the model to the predicted outcome?
- **Key concepts**: command terms (identify, calculate, explain, show), graph labeling conventions, model manipulation, multi-part FRQ sequencing, time management across multiple-choice and free response, and the four AP skill categories.
- **Applied skills**: produce fully labeled diagrams under time pressure; show a shift and the resulting new equilibrium; set up calculations with correct units; write tight cause-and-effect explanations that earn each point.
- **Common traps**: leaving axes or curves unlabeled; explaining the wrong direction of change; skipping the "therefore" step that ties a graph to the outcome; spending FRQ time restating the prompt instead of answering it.

## Capstone
Analyze a fictional economy together with a single fictional product market. Submit, as one portfolio: a labeled supply-and-demand diagram with a welfare analysis of one intervention; a firm cost-curve diagram with a profit-and-shutdown decision; a market-structure classification with its efficiency loss; a macro indicators read-out (GDP, unemployment type, inflation) with a business-cycle diagnosis; a stabilization-policy chain from shock to AD/AS to outcome; an open-economy effect linking interest rates or the exchange rate to net exports; and AP-style written explanations for each. The portfolio should demonstrate correct model selection, accurate manipulation, sound numerical work, and clear interpretation across both Micro and Macro.

## Assessment Ideas
- Graph tasks graded on labels, correct shifts, equilibrium identification, and explanation.
- Calculation tasks graded on setup, formula choice, and units (elasticity, multipliers, money creation, opportunity-cost ratios).
- Capstone graded on model selection, policy reasoning, numerical accuracy, and AP-style clarity.
