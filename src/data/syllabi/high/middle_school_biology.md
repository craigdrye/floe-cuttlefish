# Middle School Biology
Living systems explained with evidence: how cells build bodies, how matter and energy flow through organisms and ecosystems, and how traits pass on and populations change over time.

**ID**: `middleSchoolBiology` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
Middle School Biology treats living things not as a list of facts to memorize but as systems that can be explained with evidence. The aim is for students to answer "how does this work?" for the phenomena they meet every day: why a cut heals, why a plant grows toward light, why energy runs out near the top of a food web, why siblings differ, and why a population of beetles can look different after many generations. Each explanation ties a visible result back to an underlying mechanism, and each mechanism is supported by observation rather than assertion.

A second aim is to build the reasoning habits of a biologist: distinguishing structure from function, tracing matter and energy as they move and transform, reading diagrams and data instead of relying on memory alone, and stating claims that match the evidence without overclaiming. Students practice the claim-evidence-reasoning move until it becomes automatic, so that a Punnett square, a food-web diagram, or a microscope image becomes something to reason from rather than just label.

The course keeps cells, body systems, energy flow, ecosystems, inheritance, and evolution connected rather than as isolated units, so the same crosscutting ideas recur all year: structure fits function, matter and energy are conserved as they move, systems interact and feed back, and small variation plus selection produces change over time. It is written for the high-school tier hosting middle-grade life science, and aligns with the NGSS Middle School Life Science (MS-LS) disciplinary core ideas and UK KS3 Biology, supported by OpenStax and HHMI BioInteractive framing.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they ask students to explain living systems: identifying cell structures and what they do; tracing food, oxygen, or nerve signals through body systems; comparing photosynthesis and cellular respiration; reading food chains, food webs, and energy pyramids; solving one-trait inheritance with Punnett squares; or reasoning about variation, selection, and evidence for change over time. The course threads a few ideas through every chapter. Structure-and-function reasoning connects cells, organelles, and body systems. Conservation of matter and energy links photosynthesis, respiration, and the flow of energy across trophic levels. Variation, inheritance, and selection connect genetics to evolution.

Questions favor reasoning over recall: "what would happen if," "which structure does this job," "what does this diagram show," "which explanation fits the evidence." Where a number matters (the roughly 10 percent of energy passed up a trophic level, the 3:1 phenotype ratio from a heterozygous cross) it is used to support an explanation, not as an end in itself. The final chapter is investigation-focused, drilling variables, fair tests, sample size, and the move from data to an evidence-based claim with honest uncertainty. Distractors target the common middle-grade traps: confusing photosynthesis with respiration, mixing genotype with phenotype, treating "survival of the fittest" as individual change, and reading a food web backwards.

## Chapter 1: Living Things and Cell Theory
- **Core questions**: What evidence shows something is alive, and how do we know all organisms are made of cells? How do the levels of organization build from a single cell up to a whole organism?
- **Key concepts**: characteristics of life; cell theory (all living things are made of cells, cells are the basic unit of life, cells come from other cells); unicellular and multicellular organisms; microscopes and scale; levels of organization (cell, tissue, organ, organ system, organism).
- **Applied skills**: sorting living from nonliving things by evidence; ordering biological levels from smallest to largest; estimating relative size using a microscope field of view; matching a microscope image to the structure it shows.
- **Common traps**: thinking large things (like a tree) are "too big" to be made of cells; skipping levels of organization or reversing their order; assuming unicellular organisms can't carry out all life processes; confusing magnification with actual size.

## Chapter 2: Cell Structures and Functions
- **Core questions**: Which organelle does each job, and why does its structure fit that function? How do plant and animal cells differ, and what does each difference let the cell do?
- **Key concepts**: cell membrane (controls what enters and leaves), cytoplasm, nucleus (stores genetic information), mitochondria (release energy from glucose), chloroplasts (capture light for photosynthesis), cell wall, vacuoles, ribosomes; structure-function reasoning.
- **Applied skills**: matching organelles to their jobs; comparing plant and animal cells from a diagram; predicting how a cell behaves if one organelle is missing; explaining why a structure suits its function.
- **Common traps**: assuming animal cells have chloroplasts or cell walls; thinking the nucleus makes energy (that is mitochondria); confusing the cell wall with the cell membrane; treating the vacuole as "empty" rather than storage.

## Chapter 3: Body Systems and Homeostasis
- **Core questions**: How do organ systems cooperate to keep the body working during an activity? How does the body sense a change and respond to stay within a safe range?
- **Key concepts**: digestive, respiratory, circulatory, nervous, muscular, and skeletal systems; enzymes; gas exchange in the lungs; transport in blood; nerve signals; feedback and homeostasis (body temperature, blood sugar at an introductory level).
- **Applied skills**: tracing food, oxygen, or a nerve signal through the body; identifying which systems interact in a given task (e.g., running); describing a simple negative-feedback loop that restores balance.
- **Common traps**: treating systems as independent rather than interacting; thinking digestion happens only in the stomach; confusing breathing (moving air) with cellular respiration (releasing energy); assuming homeostasis means "no change" rather than "kept within range."

## Chapter 4: Matter and Energy in Organisms
- **Core questions**: Where does the matter in a plant come from, and where does an organism's usable energy come from? How are photosynthesis and cellular respiration related, and how do their inputs and outputs compare?
- **Key concepts**: photosynthesis (carbon dioxide + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen); cellular respiration (glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + usable energy); glucose as stored chemical energy; ATP at an introductory level; producers, consumers, and decomposers.
- **Applied skills**: comparing the inputs and outputs of photosynthesis and respiration; tracing carbon atoms from air into a plant and on through a consumer; explaining how a plant gains mass mostly from carbon dioxide, not from soil.
- **Common traps**: swapping the inputs and outputs of photosynthesis and respiration; thinking only plants do cellular respiration (animals do too); believing plants gain most of their mass from soil; treating decomposers as unimportant in matter cycling.

## Chapter 5: Ecosystems and Interdependence
- **Core questions**: How do food chains and food webs show who eats whom, and why does available energy shrink at each higher level? How does a change in one population ripple through an ecosystem?
- **Key concepts**: food chains and food webs; trophic levels and energy pyramids; the roughly 10 percent rule (about 90 percent of energy is lost as heat and life processes at each level); populations, communities, limiting factors, carrying capacity, biodiversity, ecosystem stability.
- **Applied skills**: reading a food web to predict the effect of removing or adding a species; explaining why food chains rarely exceed four or five links; using an energy pyramid to compare amounts of energy across levels.
- **Common traps**: reading arrows in a food web backward (arrows point toward the eater, the energy receiver); assuming top predators have the most energy available; thinking energy "recycles" the way matter does (energy flows one way, matter cycles); confusing a population with a community.

## Chapter 6: Genetics and Inheritance
- **Core questions**: How do genes, written in DNA, lead to traits, and how do we predict a trait's chances in offspring? How do sexual and asexual reproduction differ in the variation they produce?
- **Key concepts**: DNA, genes, and chromosomes; traits, genotype, and phenotype; dominant and recessive alleles (capital letter dominant, lowercase recessive); homozygous and heterozygous; Punnett squares for one trait; sexual versus asexual reproduction.
- **Applied skills**: solving a one-trait Punnett square (a Bb × Bb cross gives a 3:1 phenotype ratio and 25% BB / 50% Bb / 25% bb); connecting a genotype to its phenotype; explaining why offspring of sexual reproduction vary while clones do not.
- **Common traps**: confusing genotype (the alleles) with phenotype (the visible trait); thinking a heterozygote (Bb) shows a blend rather than the dominant trait; reading Punnett-square probabilities as guarantees for a small family; assuming dominant means "more common."

## Chapter 7: Natural Selection and Change Over Time
- **Core questions**: What conditions cause a trait to become more or less common in a population over generations? What different kinds of evidence support that populations change over time?
- **Key concepts**: heritable variation; overproduction and competition; differential survival and reproduction (natural selection); adaptation; fossils, comparative anatomy, and embryology as evidence; artificial selection (selective breeding).
- **Applied skills**: identifying variation, selection pressure, and differential reproduction in a scenario; distinguishing change in a population over generations from change in one individual's lifetime; comparing types of evidence for common ancestry.
- **Common traps**: thinking individuals evolve or "choose" to change rather than populations changing across generations; believing organisms develop traits because they "need" them; treating "survival of the fittest" as strongest rather than best-suited to the environment; assuming acquired traits are inherited.

## Chapter 8: Biology Investigation Studio
- **Core questions**: What makes a biology investigation a fair test, and how do we move from data to a claim we can defend? When does the evidence support a claim, and when must we revise it?
- **Key concepts**: testable questions; independent, dependent, and controlled variables; fair tests and controls; observation logs; graphing; sample size; uncertainty and error; claim-evidence-reasoning.
- **Applied skills**: spotting the flaw in a poorly designed investigation; choosing the right graph for a data set; identifying which variable to change and which to hold constant; revising a claim so it matches the evidence and states its limits.
- **Common traps**: changing more than one variable at once; drawing strong conclusions from a tiny sample; confusing correlation with cause; stating a claim stronger than the data allows; forgetting a control group.

## Capstone
Students build a "living systems map" for one central question (for example, "How does a meadow stay healthy as seasons and populations change?") that connects cells, body systems, matter and energy flow, an ecosystem food web, inheritance, and evolution. The map must trace at least one path of matter and energy, use one piece of genetic or evolutionary reasoning, and end with an evidence-based claim and an honest statement of what is still uncertain. Students defend the map by answering questions about the mechanisms they drew.
