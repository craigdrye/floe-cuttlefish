# Middle School Biology
**ID**: `middleSchoolBiology` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: 8 (ages 12-13)
**Frameworks**: US NGSS Middle School Life Science, UK KS3 Biology

## Course Aim
Help students explain living systems with evidence: how cells work, how body systems coordinate, how matter and energy move through ecosystems, how traits are inherited, and how populations change over time.

## Course Design Notes
This course should feel concrete and visual. Route questions here when they ask students to label or explain cell structures, trace body-system interactions, interpret food webs, use Punnett squares, or reason about adaptation and variation from simple evidence.

## Chapter 1: Living Things and Cell Theory
- **Key concepts**: Characteristics of life, cell theory, unicellular and multicellular organisms, microscopes, scale, and levels of organization.
- **Practice questions**: Sort living and nonliving examples by evidence; order biological levels from cell to organism; interpret microscope images.
- **Student output**: A scale diagram showing how cells build tissues, organs, systems, and an organism.

## Chapter 2: Cell Structures and Functions
- **Key concepts**: Cell membrane, cytoplasm, nucleus, mitochondria, chloroplasts, cell wall, vacuoles, ribosomes, and structure-function reasoning.
- **Practice questions**: Match organelles to jobs; compare plant and animal cells; explain why structure matters for function.
- **Student output**: A labeled cell model with a short explanation for each major structure.

## Chapter 3: Body Systems and Homeostasis
- **Key concepts**: Digestive, respiratory, circulatory, nervous, muscular, and skeletal systems; enzymes; gas exchange; transport; feedback; and homeostasis.
- **Practice questions**: Trace food, oxygen, or signals through the body; identify system interactions; explain a simple homeostasis example.
- **Student output**: A body-systems flowchart explaining how at least three systems cooperate in one activity.

## Chapter 4: Matter and Energy in Organisms
- **Key concepts**: Photosynthesis, cellular respiration, glucose, oxygen, carbon dioxide, ATP at an introductory level, producers, consumers, and decomposers.
- **Practice questions**: Compare photosynthesis and respiration; identify inputs and outputs; trace matter through a living system.
- **Student output**: A matter-and-energy diagram for a familiar ecosystem or classroom-grown plant investigation.

## Chapter 5: Ecosystems and Interdependence
- **Key concepts**: Food chains, food webs, energy pyramids, populations, communities, limiting factors, carrying capacity, biodiversity, and ecosystem stability.
- **Practice questions**: Predict effects of population changes; read food-web diagrams; explain why energy decreases across trophic levels.
- **Student output**: An ecosystem stability brief using a food web, one disruption, and one evidence-based prediction.

## Chapter 6: Genetics and Inheritance
- **Key concepts**: DNA, genes, chromosomes, traits, genotype, phenotype, dominant and recessive alleles, sexual and asexual reproduction, and simple Punnett squares.
- **Practice questions**: Solve one-trait inheritance problems; connect genotype to phenotype; compare reproduction types.
- **Student output**: A genetics probability card that explains a trait outcome without overclaiming.

## Chapter 7: Natural Selection and Change Over Time
- **Key concepts**: Variation, inheritance, competition, differential survival and reproduction, adaptation, fossils, comparative anatomy, and artificial selection.
- **Practice questions**: Identify the parts of a natural selection scenario; distinguish individual change from population change; compare evidence types.
- **Student output**: A short evidence argument explaining how a population could change over generations.

## Chapter 8: Biology Investigation Studio
- **Key concepts**: Testable questions, variables, fair tests, observation logs, graphing, sample size, uncertainty, and claim-evidence-reasoning.
- **Practice questions**: Improve a flawed biology investigation; choose the best graph; revise a claim to match evidence.
- **Student output**: A mini investigation report using classroom, field, or provided biological data.

## Capstone
Students create a "living systems map" that connects cells, body systems, ecosystems, inheritance, and evolution around one central question.

## Assessment Ideas
- Diagram-based explanations rather than memorized definitions alone.
- Short claim-evidence-reasoning prompts from food webs, cells, or inheritance data.
- Investigation notebooks with variables, data, and revised conclusions.
- A final systems map defense with teacher or peer questions.
