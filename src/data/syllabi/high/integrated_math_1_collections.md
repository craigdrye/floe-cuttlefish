# Integrated Math 1 Collections
A coherent first-year integrated course where algebra, functions, geometry, and statistics reinforce one another: students model relationships, justify their methods, and read meaning back into context.

**ID**: `col-integrated-math-1` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
Integrated Math 1 is the first course in an integrated high-school pathway, designed so that students stop treating algebra, geometry, and statistics as separate subjects and start seeing the connections among them. The course builds on the Common Core high-school mathematics standards for Mathematics I, blending linear and exponential modeling, systems, transformational geometry, coordinate proof, and one-variable and two-variable statistics into a single coherent year. The aim is to balance procedural fluency with explanation, representation, and assessment-ready reasoning.

The throughline is the idea of a function: a rule that turns inputs into outputs. Students learn to move fluently among five representations of a relationship, equation, graph, table, diagram, and words, and to say what a symbol, slope, point, transformation, or statistic means in the situation it models. Arithmetic sequences are reframed as linear functions and geometric sequences as exponential functions, so the algebra of growth and the algebra of patterns become the same study. In geometry, congruence is defined through rigid motions rather than memorized rules, tying transformations directly to proof.

By the end of the year, students should write and solve linear equations, inequalities, and systems with justification; interpret and compare linear and exponential models; describe and perform rigid motions and decide congruence from them; use the coordinate plane to verify geometric claims; and summarize, display, and interpret data, including the difference between correlation and causation. Just as important, they should be able to judge whether an answer is reasonable, track units, and name the limits of a model.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve quantities and expression structure, one-variable linear equations and inequalities, functions and rate of change, linear functions and data models, systems of equations and inequalities, transformational and coordinate geometry, introductory coordinate proof, arithmetic and geometric sequences, linear-versus-exponential growth, and one- and two-variable descriptive statistics. Keep the work cumulative: function notation threads through every later chapter, slope reappears inside systems and coordinate geometry, and signed-number and distributive-property fluency underlie all of it.

Keep practice forward and error-aware. Every wrong answer should reflect a genuine misconception, forgetting to flip an inequality after dividing by a negative, reading slope as the y-intercept, taking the zeros of a sequence from its term number, confusing correlation with causation, with a specific reason it fails and a hint toward the right move. Favor relatable contexts (pricing, distance and time, savings, design constraints, school and sports data) so the mathematics stays grounded, and ask students throughout to interpret what a solution means. Mind the age boundary: quadratics, the quadratic formula, right-triangle trigonometry, and formal two-column proof belong to later courses; this course defines congruence through rigid motions and keeps proof at the level of coordinate verification and clear written justification.

## Chapter 1: Quantities, Expressions, and Algebraic Structure
**Core questions:** What are the parts of an expression, and how do I rewrite an expression, with correct units, so it reveals a useful quantity without changing its value?
**Key concepts:** variables, terms, factors, coefficients; order of operations; properties of operations; equivalent expressions; substitution and evaluation; units and the meaning of expression structure.
**Applied skills:** evaluating formulas for given inputs; combining like terms and distributing; checking equivalence by substitution; rewriting an expression to expose a rate or total; using units to test whether an answer is reasonable.
**Common traps:** distributing to only the first term inside parentheses; combining unlike terms (3x + 2 becoming 5x); ignoring order of operations; assuming two expressions are equal because one test value matches.

## Chapter 2: Linear Equations and Inequalities
**Core questions:** How do I solve a one-variable linear equation or inequality while justifying each move, and when must I reverse the inequality sign?
**Key concepts:** inverse operations; the distributive property in solving; variables on both sides; identities and no-solution cases; inequality symbols and solution sets; compound inequalities; the sign-flip rule.
**Applied skills:** solving multi-step equations and inequalities and checking the result; justifying each algebraic step; recognizing infinitely-many-solution and no-solution outcomes; graphing solution sets on a number line; translating real limits into inequalities.
**Common traps:** changing only one side of the equation; forgetting to reverse the inequality after dividing by a negative; concluding "no solution" when a variable cancels to a true statement (an identity); using the wrong open/closed endpoint.

## Chapter 3: Functions, Graphs, and Rate of Change
**Core questions:** What makes a relation a function, and how do I read and interpret f(x), domain, range, intercepts, and average rate of change?
**Key concepts:** function definition and the vertical-line idea; domain and range; function notation f(x); independent and dependent variables; intercepts; increasing and decreasing intervals; average rate of change over an interval.
**Applied skills:** deciding whether a relation is a function from a table, graph, or mapping; evaluating and interpreting f(x); matching tables, graphs, equations, and descriptions; computing average rate of change; stating a reasonable domain from context.
**Common traps:** reading f(x) as multiplication of f and x; swapping domain and range; calling a graph a function when one input maps to two outputs; reporting an unrealistic domain (negative or fractional counts) for a real context.

## Chapter 4: Linear Functions and Data Models
**Core questions:** How do I write a linear equation from a graph, points, table, or context, and how do I interpret and critique a linear model of data?
**Key concepts:** slope as constant rate of change; x- and y-intercepts; slope-intercept, point-slope, and standard forms; direct variation; scatterplots, line of best fit, residuals; the difference between correlation and causation; limits of linear models.
**Applied skills:** computing slope from two points; writing equations from graphs, points, tables, or stories; interpreting slope and intercept in context; fitting and judging a trend line; deciding when a linear model is inappropriate.
**Common traps:** confusing slope with the y-intercept or inverting rise/run; misreading the sign of slope; assuming correlation proves causation; extrapolating far beyond the data and overclaiming.

## Chapter 5: Systems and Constraints
**Core questions:** How do I solve a system of two linear equations exactly and approximately, and how do I represent and read a feasible region for a system of inequalities?
**Key concepts:** systems as simultaneous constraints; solving by graphing, substitution, and elimination; equivalent systems; one, none, or infinitely many solutions; linear inequalities in two variables and feasible regions.
**Applied skills:** solving systems by each method and choosing an efficient one; identifying the number of solutions from slopes and intercepts; graphing a system of inequalities and shading the feasible region; modeling break-even, mixture, and planning situations.
**Common traps:** stopping after finding one variable without back-substituting; adding equations to eliminate when subtraction is needed (sign error); confusing "no solution" with an identity; shading the wrong side of a boundary line.

## Chapter 6: Transformations and Congruence
**Core questions:** How do I describe and perform translations, rotations, and reflections, and how do rigid motions tell me whether two figures are congruent?
**Key concepts:** points, lines, segments, angles; parallel and perpendicular lines; precise definitions; translations, rotations, reflections; symmetry; rigid motions; congruence defined through rigid motions; transformation notation.
**Applied skills:** describing and performing transformations on and off the coordinate plane; writing transformation rules; identifying symmetries; deciding whether figures are congruent by finding a sequence of rigid motions; explaining why distance and angle are preserved.
**Common traps:** confusing the rules for reflection across the x-axis versus the y-axis; mixing up clockwise and counterclockwise rotation direction; thinking a figure changes size under a rigid motion; claiming congruence without naming a motion that maps one figure to the other.

## Chapter 7: Coordinate Geometry and Introductory Proof
**Core questions:** How do I use distance, midpoint, and slope on the coordinate plane to classify figures and justify a geometric claim?
**Key concepts:** coordinate plane; distance and midpoint; slope; parallel lines (equal slopes) and perpendicular lines (negative-reciprocal slopes); perimeter and area on the grid; coordinate verification and written justification.
**Applied skills:** finding distance and midpoint; classifying triangles and quadrilaterals from coordinates; computing perimeter or area; using slope and distance together to prove a claim such as "this quadrilateral is a parallelogram."
**Common traps:** dropping a square or square root in the distance formula; averaging incorrectly for the midpoint; using the reciprocal without the negative sign for perpendicular slopes; asserting a shape's type without checking both side lengths and slopes.

## Chapter 8: Sequences, Exponential Growth, and the Statistics Studio
**Core questions:** How do I tell an arithmetic (linear) pattern from a geometric (exponential) one, and how do I summarize, display, and interpret data honestly?
**Key concepts:** arithmetic sequences as linear functions and geometric sequences as exponential functions; common difference versus common ratio; exponential growth and decay y = a·b^x; dot plots, histograms, box plots; mean, median, IQR, standard deviation as spread; two-way tables and relative frequency; scatterplots and association; correlation versus causation.
**Applied skills:** writing a rule for a sequence and classifying it; distinguishing linear from exponential growth from a table or graph; comparing distributions; interpreting two-way tables; describing scatterplot association and judging causal claims.
**Common traps:** calling a pattern linear when each step multiplies rather than adds; using the term number as if it were the value; comparing distributions by center only while ignoring spread; reading association in a scatterplot as proof of cause.

## Capstone: Integrated Math 1 Modeling Portfolio
Build and defend an Integrated Math 1 modeling portfolio around one practical question, such as transportation, school scheduling, pricing, sports or environmental data, or a small design problem. The finished work should pull the whole course together:
- A linear equation or function written from data or a context, with slope and intercept interpreted in real terms.
- A system or two-variable inequality constraint, solved or graphed, with its solution point or feasible region explained.
- A geometric or coordinate representation that uses a transformation or a distance/slope argument to justify a claim.
- One data display with an appropriate summary statistic, plus an honest reflection on assumptions, units, precision, and the limits of the model.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short fluency checks on solving, graphing, function notation, slope, transformations, and summary statistics.
- Mixed-representation tasks requiring students to move among equations, graphs, tables, diagrams, and written explanations.
- Error-analysis prompts for invalid algebraic moves, misleading graphs, unsupported correlation claims, or unclear geometric reasoning.
- Modeling tasks scored on setup, method choice, interpretation, units, and reasonableness.
- Capstone graded on mathematical accuracy, representation quality, concept connections, communication, and reflection.
