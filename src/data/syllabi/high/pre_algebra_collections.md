# Pre-Algebra Collections
**Covers**: `col-illustrative-mathematics-6th-8th-grade`, `col-pre-algebra`, `col-algebra-basics`
**Discipline**: Maths - **Year**: 9 (ages 13-14)

## Course Aim
Build the bridge from arithmetic to Algebra 1 by making rational numbers, variables, equations, proportional reasoning, linear relationships, geometry, and data feel connected. Students should leave able to explain what symbols mean, solve accurately, check answers, and model simple real situations without panic.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve integers, fractions, decimals, order of operations, variables, expressions, one-variable equations, inequalities, ratios, proportions, percents, slope, coordinate graphs, functions, Pythagorean theorem, angle facts, area, volume, scatter plots, or Algebra 1 readiness. Keep the tone confidence-building: the course should surface misconceptions early and use multiple representations before formal shortcuts.

## Chapter 1: Number Sense, Integers, and Rational Numbers
**Key concepts**: Integers, opposites, absolute value, fractions, decimals, common denominators, fraction operations, negative numbers, number lines, estimation, and reasonableness.
**Practice questions**: Place rational numbers on a number line; add and subtract signed numbers; multiply and divide fractions; compare negative values; estimate before calculating; explain why a result is reasonable.
**Student output**: A rational-number toolkit with worked examples, visual models, and one common mistake corrected for each operation.

## Chapter 2: Properties, Order of Operations, and Expressions
**Key concepts**: Commutative, associative, distributive, identity, inverse, order of operations, exponents, substitution, variables, terms, coefficients, constants, and equivalent expressions.
**Practice questions**: Evaluate expressions with nested operations; substitute values for variables; identify terms and coefficients; use the distributive property; combine like terms; explain why two expressions are equivalent.
**Student output**: An expression-matching set that pairs verbal descriptions, algebraic expressions, and evaluated examples.

## Chapter 3: Equations and Inequalities
**Key concepts**: Equality, inverse operations, solution, one-step equations, two-step equations, multi-step equations, variables on both sides, no solution, infinitely many solutions, inequalities, and graphing on a number line.
**Practice questions**: Solve linear equations; check solutions by substitution; identify equation types; solve and graph inequalities; translate a word problem into an equation; explain each step without saying "move it over."
**Student output**: A solved-equation portfolio with error analysis, checks, and at least three student-created word problems.

## Chapter 4: Ratios, Rates, Proportions, and Percents
**Key concepts**: Ratio, unit rate, proportion, scale factor, percent, percent change, markup, markdown, tax, tip, simple interest, and proportional reasoning.
**Practice questions**: Find unit rates; solve proportions; convert among fractions, decimals, and percents; calculate discounts and tax; compare deals; identify whether a relationship is proportional.
**Student output**: A consumer-math mini-project comparing prices, discounts, interest, or scale models with clearly shown proportional reasoning.

## Chapter 5: Coordinate Plane, Slope, and Linear Relationships
**Key concepts**: Coordinate plane, quadrants, ordered pairs, input, output, function, table, graph, equation, slope, y-intercept, rate of change, and slope-intercept form.
**Practice questions**: Plot points; identify functions from tables and graphs; calculate slope from points, tables, and graphs; graph y = mx + b; write a simple linear equation from a context.
**Student output**: A linear relationship report using a table, graph, equation, and sentence interpretation for the same situation.

## Chapter 6: Geometry Foundations
**Key concepts**: Lines, angles, complementary angles, supplementary angles, vertical angles, parallel lines, transversals, triangles, quadrilaterals, perimeter, area, surface area, volume, and formula choice.
**Practice questions**: Solve missing-angle problems; classify shapes; find area and perimeter; compute surface area or volume of prisms and cylinders; use algebra with geometry facts.
**Student output**: A geometry reference sheet with diagrams, formulas, units, and one worked example for each major type.

## Chapter 7: Pythagorean Theorem, Exponents, and Scientific Notation
**Key concepts**: Squares, square roots, right triangles, hypotenuse, legs, Pythagorean theorem, distance, exponent rules, powers of ten, scientific notation, and scale.
**Practice questions**: Find missing side lengths; decide whether a triangle is right; use the distance formula informally; simplify exponent expressions; convert to and from scientific notation; compare very large or small quantities.
**Student output**: A right-triangle and scale investigation linking Pythagorean calculations to a map, screen, ramp, or classroom measurement.

## Chapter 8: Data, Review, and Algebra Readiness
**Key concepts**: Statistical question, data display, mean, median, range, variability, scatter plot, line of best fit, outlier, correlation, review planning, and algebra readiness.
**Practice questions**: Interpret dot plots, box plots, and scatter plots; compare centers and spreads; identify outliers; draw a trend line; choose a strategy for mixed review; explain which pre-algebra skill a problem is testing.
**Student output**: A readiness portfolio with a diagnostic reflection, corrected errors, and a personal plan for entering Algebra 1.

## Capstone
Students build a "math model of everyday life" project using at least four course strands: rational numbers, equations, proportions or percents, linear relationships, geometry, and data. Examples include planning a small event, comparing phone plans, designing a room, tracking practice progress, or analyzing a local data set.

## Assessment Ideas
Use short fluency checks, misconception quizzes, exit tickets, error analysis, mixed-topic spirals, graphing tasks, verbal explanations, and one practical modeling project. Assess not only speed and accuracy but also representation choice, checking habits, and the ability to explain why a method works.
