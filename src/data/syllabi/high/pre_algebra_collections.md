# Pre-Algebra Collections
The bridge from arithmetic to Algebra 1: making rational numbers, variables, equations, proportional reasoning, linear relationships, geometry, and data feel like one connected story.

**ID**: `col-illustrative-mathematics-6th-8th-grade` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
This course builds the conceptual and procedural bridge that carries a student from arithmetic into Algebra 1. Drawing on the Illustrative Mathematics 6-8 sequence and the Common Core middle-grades standards, it treats pre-algebra not as a grab-bag of skills but as a single developing idea: that numbers, symbols, tables, graphs, and real situations are different views of the same relationship. Students should leave able to explain what symbols mean, compute accurately with signed and rational numbers, solve and check equations, reason proportionally, and model simple everyday situations without panic.

Pre-algebra is where arithmetic begins to generalize. A student who has only ever found "the answer" learns to work with a variable that can stand for many values, an expression that has structure worth preserving, and an equation that is a balanced statement rather than a left-to-right command. The course stays concrete before it goes abstract: a context, a number line, a table, or a picture comes first, and the formal rule arrives as a shortcut the student can defend rather than a trick to memorize.

By the end, students should fluently order and operate on rational numbers including negatives and fractions, evaluate and rewrite expressions using the properties of operations, solve one- and multi-step equations and inequalities with justification, move among ratios, rates, proportions, and percents, read and write linear relationships across tables, graphs, and equations, apply core geometry and the Pythagorean theorem, and interpret simple data displays. Just as importantly, they should be able to estimate first, check whether an answer is reasonable, and explain why a method works.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve integers and signed-number arithmetic, fractions and decimals, order of operations, variables, expressions and equivalence, one-variable equations and inequalities, ratios, unit rates, proportions, percents, the coordinate plane, slope and rate of change, functions, the Pythagorean theorem, angle facts, area, surface area and volume, exponents and scientific notation, scatter plots, and general Algebra 1 readiness. Keep the work cumulative: signed-number and fraction fluency from Chapter 1 underlies every later computation, proportional reasoning from Chapter 4 becomes slope in Chapter 5, and the coordinate plane threads through functions, geometry, and data.

Keep the tone confidence-building and the practice forward and error-aware. Every distractor should reflect a genuine middle-grades misconception — adding numerators and denominators, dropping a negative sign, forgetting the 1/2 in a triangle's area, reading slope as the y-intercept, or treating a non-proportional table as proportional — paired with a specific reason it fails and a hint toward the right move. Favor relatable contexts such as recipes, prices and discounts, phone plans, ramps, and class data so the algebra stays grounded, and ask students throughout to say what an answer means and whether it is reasonable. Mind the tier boundary: this is pre-algebra readiness, so square roots, exponent rules, and scientific notation appear at an introductory level, while solving systems, factoring quadratics, and formal function families belong to Algebra 1 and beyond.

## Chapter 1: Number Sense, Integers, and Rational Numbers
**Core questions:** What does a negative number mean, and how do I add, subtract, multiply, and divide signed numbers and fractions reliably?
**Key concepts:** integers, opposites, and absolute value; the number line as distance and order; fractions, decimals, and common denominators; the four operations on signed numbers and on fractions; estimation and reasonableness.
**Applied skills:** placing rational numbers on a number line and comparing them; adding and subtracting signed numbers with consistent rules; multiplying and dividing fractions; converting among fractions and decimals; estimating before computing and judging whether a result is reasonable.
**Common traps:** adding numerators and denominators when adding fractions; dropping or mishandling a negative sign; treating absolute value as "make it positive" without understanding distance; thinking dividing by a fraction makes a number smaller.

## Chapter 2: Properties, Order of Operations, and Expressions
**Core questions:** How do the properties of operations let me rewrite an expression without changing its value, and how do I evaluate an expression correctly?
**Key concepts:** commutative, associative, distributive, identity, and inverse properties; order of operations including exponents; variables, terms, coefficients, and constants; substitution and evaluation; equivalent expressions.
**Applied skills:** evaluating expressions with nested operations and exponents; substituting values for variables; identifying terms and coefficients; applying the distributive property; combining like terms; justifying why two expressions are equivalent.
**Common traps:** distributing to only the first term inside parentheses; combining unlike terms (3x + 2 becoming 5x); ignoring order of operations and going strictly left to right; assuming two expressions are equal because one test value happens to match.

## Chapter 3: Equations and Inequalities
**Core questions:** How do I solve a one-variable equation while justifying each move, and how is solving an inequality different?
**Key concepts:** equality as balance and inverse operations; one-step, two-step, and multi-step equations; variables on both sides; no-solution and infinitely-many-solution cases; inequality symbols, solution sets, and graphing on a number line.
**Applied skills:** solving linear equations and checking by substitution; identifying whether an equation has one, no, or infinitely many solutions; solving and graphing inequalities; translating a word problem into an equation; explaining each step in terms of operations rather than "moving it over."
**Common traps:** applying an operation to only one side of the equation; sign errors when isolating the variable; forgetting to reverse the inequality when multiplying or dividing by a negative; reporting a single number instead of a solution set for an inequality.

## Chapter 4: Ratios, Rates, Proportions, and Percents
**Core questions:** How do I tell whether a relationship is proportional, and how do I move fluently among ratios, rates, fractions, decimals, and percents?
**Key concepts:** ratio, unit rate, and proportion; constant of proportionality; scale factor; percent as a rate per hundred; percent change, markup, markdown, tax, tip, and simple interest.
**Applied skills:** finding unit rates and solving proportions; converting among fractions, decimals, and percents; computing discounts, tax, and percent change; comparing deals; deciding whether a table or graph represents a proportional relationship.
**Common traps:** setting up a proportion with mismatched units across the two ratios; treating a percent change as a percent of the new amount instead of the original; assuming any relationship with constant addition is proportional; confusing percent off with the final price.

## Chapter 5: Coordinate Plane, Slope, and Linear Relationships
**Core questions:** How do I read a linear relationship across a table, graph, and equation, and what does slope tell me about a situation?
**Key concepts:** the coordinate plane, quadrants, and ordered pairs; input, output, and function; slope as rate of change; y-intercept; slope-intercept form y = mx + b; connecting tables, graphs, equations, and words.
**Applied skills:** plotting points and reading coordinates; deciding whether a table or graph is a function; computing slope from two points, a table, or a graph; graphing y = mx + b; writing a simple linear equation from a context and interpreting its parts.
**Common traps:** confusing slope with the y-intercept or inverting rise over run; misreading the sign of slope on a graph; thinking a graph is a function when one input gives two outputs; reading the y-intercept as a slope when interpreting a story.

## Chapter 6: Geometry Foundations
**Core questions:** How do I use angle facts and the right area or volume formula, and how does algebra help me find an unknown measurement?
**Key concepts:** lines and angles; complementary, supplementary, and vertical angles; parallel lines cut by a transversal; triangles and quadrilaterals; perimeter, area, surface area, and volume; choosing and using the correct formula with units.
**Applied skills:** solving missing-angle problems; classifying shapes; finding perimeter and area of polygons and circles; computing surface area and volume of prisms and cylinders; setting up and solving an equation from a geometric relationship.
**Common traps:** forgetting the 1/2 factor in a triangle's area; mixing up area and perimeter or area and volume; using the wrong units (squaring or cubing inconsistently); assuming angles look equal from a figure instead of using the stated relationship.

## Chapter 7: Pythagorean Theorem, Exponents, and Scientific Notation
**Core questions:** When and how do I use the Pythagorean theorem, and how do exponents and scientific notation let me handle very large or very small numbers?
**Key concepts:** squares and square roots; right triangles, legs, and hypotenuse; the Pythagorean theorem a² + b² = c²; distance on the coordinate plane; integer exponents and powers of ten; scientific notation and scale.
**Applied skills:** finding a missing side length in a right triangle; deciding whether a triangle is right; using right-triangle reasoning to find distance; simplifying simple exponent expressions; converting to and from scientific notation; comparing very large or small quantities.
**Common traps:** adding the legs instead of squaring them; treating the hypotenuse as a leg or solving for the wrong side; thinking a negative exponent makes a number negative; misplacing the decimal or sign of the power when writing scientific notation.

## Chapter 8: Data, Review, and Algebra Readiness
**Core questions:** How do I read and compare data displays, and how do I judge whether I am ready for Algebra 1?
**Key concepts:** statistical questions and variability; mean, median, and range; dot plots, box plots, and scatter plots; line of best fit, trend, and outliers; correlation versus causation; mixed review and self-assessment.
**Applied skills:** interpreting dot plots, box plots, and scatter plots; comparing centers and spreads of two data sets; identifying outliers; describing a trend and sketching a line of best fit; choosing a strategy for mixed-topic problems and naming which pre-algebra skill each one tests.
**Common traps:** confusing mean and median or treating one as always "the average"; reading a box plot's whiskers as containing equal counts; assuming a scatter-plot trend proves causation; extrapolating a trend far beyond the data.

## Capstone: A Math Model of Everyday Life
Students build and defend a "math model of everyday life" project that pulls together at least four course strands — rational numbers, equations, proportions or percents, linear relationships, geometry, and data. Examples include planning a small event on a budget, comparing phone or streaming plans, designing a room to scale, tracking practice or fitness progress, or analyzing a local data set. The finished work should include at least one computation justified with reasoning, one proportional or linear relationship shown as a table, graph, and equation, a clear statement of what each result means, a reasonableness check, and a short reflection on which pre-algebra skills the student is ready to carry into Algebra 1.
