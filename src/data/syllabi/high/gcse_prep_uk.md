# GCSE Prep (UK)
Build secure subject knowledge, tier-aware exam strategy, and a calm revision system for UK GCSE Mathematics and Combined Science.

**ID**: `col-gcse-maths-uk` · **Discipline**: Exam Prep

## Course Aim
This course prepares Year 11 students (ages 15-16) for the UK GCSE Mathematics and Combined Science examinations sat at the end of secondary school. It treats the specification itself as the spine: students learn what is actually assessed, how marks are awarded, and how to translate confident knowledge into the precise written answers examiners reward. The aim is not last-minute cramming but a durable, transferable system of subject mastery plus exam craft.

Mathematics and Combined Science are taught together here because the two papers share the same underlying habits: careful reading, correct units, shown working, sensible checking, and disciplined interpretation of graphs and data. The course is built to adapt to either Foundation tier (grades 1-5) or Higher tier (grades 4-9), so students can see which content and which command words belong to their target grades. It is aligned to the major English boards (AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR), with AQA used as the default reference where boards differ in detail.

A core principle is that revision should reduce anxiety rather than feed it. Students build a confidence-rated topic map, practise with past-paper-style questions, and convert every mistake into a specific, named next step. By the end, a learner should be able to choose efficient methods, quote or select the right formula, run a required-practical-style analysis, and write answers that match the exact demand of "describe", "explain", "calculate", "compare", or "evaluate".

## Course Design Notes
Each chapter pairs specification content with the exam behaviour that earns marks for it, so revision tactics are taught as core learning, not bolted on at the end. Command words, required practicals, working, units, significant figures, and error review recur deliberately across chapters. The maths chapters distinguish Foundation from Higher content and flag which formulae are given in the exam versus which must be memorised; the science chapters foreground "working scientifically", the 21 AQA-style required practicals, and the maths skills that appear inside science papers. Where boards diverge (for example on formula sheets or paper structure), the syllabus notes the difference rather than picking one silently. Throughout, the standard is correctness first, then concision, then exam-matched phrasing.

## Chapter 1: GCSE Exam Map, Tier Choice, and Revision System
- **Core questions**: Which tier is a given question written for? What does "describe", "explain", "calculate", "compare", or "evaluate" demand? Which mistake should be revised first?
- **Key concepts**: specification mapping; Foundation (grades 1-5) vs Higher (grades 4-9) tiers; paper and mark structure; assessment objectives; command words; the role of shown working; retrieval practice; spaced revision; error logs.
- **Applied skills**: build a personal revision dashboard with tier decisions, topic confidence ratings, command-word reminders, and a weekly practice cycle; turn a marked paper into a ranked list of fixes.
- **Common traps**: choosing Higher tier when grade 4-5 security on Foundation would score higher; answering "explain" with only a description; rereading notes (familiarity) instead of self-testing (retrieval).

## Chapter 2: Mathematics - Number, Ratio, and Algebra Fluency
- **Core questions**: Which is the most efficient method here? How do you keep units and bounds straight in a ratio or proportion problem? When is an answer not reasonable?
- **Key concepts**: fractions, percentages (including reverse percentage and compound change), ratio and proportion, standard form, upper and lower bounds, indices and surds (Higher), expanding and factorising, algebraic fractions, rearranging formulae, linear and quadratic equations, simultaneous equations, inequalities, and sequences.
- **Applied skills**: solve multi-step ratio and percentage problems with correct units; rearrange and substitute into formulae; choose between factorising, the quadratic formula, and completing the square; use bounds to sanity-check results.
- **Common traps**: adding fractions by adding numerators and denominators; treating a reverse percentage as a forward one; dropping a sign when rearranging; forgetting that the quadratic formula is given on the AQA sheet but Pythagoras and trig ratios are not.

## Chapter 3: Mathematics - Geometry, Graphs, Statistics, and Probability
- **Core questions**: Which theorem or rule justifies this step? What does a graph's gradient or area represent? Is this probability model independent or conditional?
- **Key concepts**: angle rules, circle theorems, constructions, similarity and congruence, Pythagoras, trigonometry (including SOH-CAH-TOA and, on Higher, sine/cosine rules), vectors, transformations, straight-line and quadratic graphs, rates of change, averages and spread, cumulative frequency, histograms with frequency density, probability trees, Venn diagrams, and conditional probability (Higher).
- **Applied skills**: justify each geometric step with a named reason; read frequency density off a histogram; build and use a tree diagram; interpret gradient and area under a graph in context.
- **Common traps**: confusing frequency with frequency density; reading a histogram bar height as the frequency; assuming events are independent when a tree shows "without replacement"; quoting a circle theorem without naming it.

## Chapter 4: Combined Science Skills and Required Practicals
- **Core questions**: Which variable is independent, dependent, or controlled? How would you improve this method? What does this experimental graph actually show?
- **Key concepts**: working scientifically; variables and controls; apparatus and technique; accuracy, precision, resolution, and uncertainty; repeats and anomalies; risk and safety; graphing and gradients; units, significant figures, and the maths skills embedded in science papers; the 21 AQA-style required practicals (7 biology, 6 chemistry, 8 physics).
- **Applied skills**: identify and classify variables; evaluate and improve a practical method; calculate means excluding anomalies; draw lines of best fit and read gradients; answer required-practical exam questions on method, data, and evaluation.
- **Common traps**: calling a repeat a "control"; confusing precision with accuracy; including an anomaly in a mean; quoting a result without its unit or to the wrong number of significant figures.

## Chapter 5: Biology - Cells, Organisation, Infection, and Bioenergetics
- **Core questions**: How do you complete a microscopy magnification calculation? How does active transport differ from diffusion? What do photosynthesis or respiration data reveal?
- **Key concepts**: cell structure and microscopy (magnification = image size / actual size); transport (diffusion, osmosis, active transport); mitosis; enzymes and digestion; the heart and circulation; transport in plants; pathogens, immunity, and vaccination; photosynthesis and respiration; the effect of exercise.
- **Applied skills**: rearrange and apply the magnification equation with consistent units; compare transport mechanisms by energy and gradient; interpret limiting-factor graphs; link structure to function (e.g. alveoli, villi, root hairs).
- **Common traps**: mixing up units (mm, µm) in microscopy; saying osmosis moves "water to high concentration of water"; calling aerobic respiration "breathing"; confusing the reactants and products of photosynthesis and respiration.

## Chapter 6: Biology - Homeostasis, Inheritance, Evolution, and Ecology
- **Core questions**: How do you complete and interpret a genetic cross? How does a negative feedback loop keep a variable stable? Does the evidence support the ecological conclusion?
- **Key concepts**: the nervous system and reflex arc; hormones and the endocrine system; blood glucose regulation and diabetes; reproduction and DNA; monohybrid genetic crosses and Punnett squares; variation; natural selection, selective breeding, and genetic engineering; food chains and trophic levels; carbon and water cycles; biodiversity, sampling, and human impact.
- **Applied skills**: build a Punnett square and state genotype/phenotype ratios; describe a negative feedback loop in correct sequence; use quadrats or transects to estimate population; evaluate evidence for and against an ecological claim.
- **Common traps**: confusing genotype with phenotype; muddling dominant and recessive alleles; describing selection as organisms "choosing" to adapt; reporting correlation in ecology as if it proved causation.

## Chapter 7: Chemistry - Matter, Reactions, Quantitative Work, and Resources
- **Core questions**: How do bonding and structure explain a property? How do you complete a reacting-mass or concentration calculation? Which method best makes or tests this substance?
- **Key concepts**: atomic structure and the periodic table; ionic, covalent, and metallic bonding and the structures that follow; conservation of mass; relative formula mass (Mr = sum of Ar); moles (n = mass / Mr); concentration (g/dm³ and mol/dm³); acids, bases, and neutralisation; electrolysis; energy changes; rates and reversible reactions; organic chemistry basics; chemical analysis; the atmosphere and using Earth's resources.
- **Applied skills**: predict properties from bonding and structure; balance equations and apply conservation of mass; calculate Mr, moles, and concentration with units; describe required-practical methods for salts, electrolysis, and chromatography.
- **Common traps**: forgetting that gases escaping make mass appear lost in an open system; confusing mass and molar concentration; mis-reading Ar versus Mr; saying ionic compounds conduct when solid (they only conduct molten or in solution).

## Chapter 8: Physics - Energy, Electricity, Particles, Forces, Waves, and Magnetism
- **Core questions**: Which equation applies and how do you rearrange it? What does a motion or decay graph show? How do you explain a required-practical result using physics vocabulary?
- **Key concepts**: energy stores, transfers, and efficiency; circuits, resistance, and mains supply; density and the particle model; specific heat capacity (ΔE = mcΔθ); radioactivity and half-life; forces, motion, and momentum; kinetic energy (Eₖ = ½mv²) and gravitational PE (Eₚ = mgh); waves and wave speed (v = fλ); the electromagnetic spectrum; magnetism, electromagnets, and the motor effect.
- **Applied skills**: select, rearrange, and substitute into the correct equation (knowing which are on the equation sheet); read gradient and area from motion graphs; use half-life to find remaining activity; explain practical results with correct units and terms.
- **Common traps**: forgetting to square the speed in kinetic energy; mixing up speed/velocity (scalar vs vector); reading the area under a velocity-time graph as the gradient; dropping or mismatching units (J, W, N, V, A, Ω).

## Capstone
Assemble a GCSE readiness portfolio that demonstrates exam-ready competence across both subjects: (1) a mixed maths paper review with shown working, units, and a Foundation/Higher tier judgement; (2) a biology required-practical-linked response interpreting real data; (3) a chemistry calculation-and-explanation set (Mr, moles, concentration, and a bonding-to-property explanation); (4) a physics equations-and-graphs set with correct rearrangement and unit checks; and (5) a two-week revision plan built from an error taxonomy rather than from anxiety, naming the top fixes and the retrieval schedule to lock them in.
