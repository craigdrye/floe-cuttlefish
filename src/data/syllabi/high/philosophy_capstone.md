# Philosophy Capstone
**ID**: `philosophyCapstone` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: 12 / senior high school

## Course Aim
Treat philosophy as disciplined public thinking: asking better questions, testing reasons, listening without surrendering judgment, and revising a position when the argument requires it. Students revisit ethics, knowledge, mind, identity, politics, technology, and meaning while building a substantial capstone argument for a real audience.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they ask senior students to synthesize philosophical fields, conduct independent inquiry, write extended arguments, run seminars, defend a thesis, or connect philosophy to public life. The course should feel rigorous but usable: students are practicing the habits needed when slogans and quick takes are not enough.

## Chapter 1: Philosophical Method and Seminar Practice
**Key concepts**: Argument, premise, conclusion, validity, soundness, counterexample, conceptual analysis, thought experiment, charity, steelmanning, burden of proof, fallacy, dialectic, and intellectual humility.
**Practice questions**: What is the difference between an opinion and a reasoned position? When does a counterexample defeat a claim? Can conviction and humility coexist?
**Student output**: An argument anatomy portfolio that reconstructs short philosophical passages and current public claims in standard form before evaluating them.

## Chapter 2: Knowledge Under Pressure
**Key concepts**: Truth, belief, justification, skepticism, testimony, expertise, disagreement, Gettier cases, epistemic luck, intellectual virtue, bias, misinformation, and responsible doubt.
**Practice questions**: What turns true belief into knowledge? When experts disagree, what should non-experts do? Is it morally wrong to believe something on weak evidence?
**Student output**: An epistemic responsibility brief on a contested public claim, explaining what a reasonable person may assert, doubt, investigate, or withhold judgment about.

## Chapter 3: Mind, Personhood, and Identity
**Key concepts**: Consciousness, physicalism, dualism, functionalism, qualia, memory, embodiment, personal identity, moral status, artificial intelligence, disability, neurodiversity, and personhood.
**Practice questions**: What would count as evidence that another being is conscious? Is personal identity based on body, memory, psychology, narrative, relationships, or something else? Could an AI system deserve moral consideration?
**Student output**: A theory-comparison matrix testing at least three views of mind or identity against difficult cases such as memory loss, teleportation, fission, nonhuman minds, or AI systems.

## Chapter 4: Moral Theories in Collision
**Key concepts**: Utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, care ethics, rights, duties, consequences, character, flourishing, moral luck, complicity, integrity, supererogation, and moral distress.
**Practice questions**: Should actions be judged by outcomes, intentions, rules, character, relationships, or a combination? When does compromise become complicity? What do we owe strangers and future people?
**Student output**: An Ethics Bowl-style case analysis with stakeholders, competing principles, at least three frameworks, objections, replies, and questions for discussion.

## Chapter 5: Justice, Power, and Public Reason
**Key concepts**: Social contract, legitimacy, liberty, equality, fairness, rights, punishment, democracy, civil disobedience, pluralism, domination, institutional trust, and public reason.
**Practice questions**: What makes a law legitimate when citizens disagree? Should justice prioritize equal rights, opportunity, outcomes, capabilities, or freedom from domination? When is civil disobedience justified?
**Student output**: A public reason memo on a live policy dilemma, including principle, evidence, institutional constraints, reasonable disagreement, and a reply to the strongest objection.

## Chapter 6: Meaning, Freedom, and the Examined Life
**Key concepts**: Existentialism, absurdity, authenticity, freedom, responsibility, bad faith, alienation, mortality, happiness, friendship, work, attention, technology, and the good life.
**Practice questions**: Are meaning and happiness the same goal? How free are people under social, economic, psychological, and technological pressure? Is authenticity private or social?
**Student output**: A philosophical life audit that connects one existential concept to a concrete choice, habit, institution, or relationship while maintaining analytic precision.

## Chapter 7: Technology, Science, and Future Persons
**Key concepts**: AI ethics, algorithmic bias, privacy, automation, science and values, models, uncertainty, future generations, climate risk, bioethics, and responsibility gaps.
**Practice questions**: Who is responsible when automated systems cause harm? Can science be value-free? What do present people owe future people? How should uncertainty shape public action?
**Student output**: A technology or science ethics review that traces stakeholders, evidence, assumptions, risks, duties, and governance options.

## Chapter 8: Capstone Argument Studio
**Key concepts**: Inquiry question, thesis, literature map, annotated bibliography, synthesis, objection, reply, revision, oral defense, public audience, and research ethics.
**Practice questions**: Is the question genuinely philosophical? What must be defined before the argument can begin? Which source changes the shape of the thesis? What objection is hardest to answer?
**Student output**: A complete capstone portfolio: proposal, annotated bibliography, argument map, draft, peer commentary, revision memo, final product, oral defense, and process reflection.

## Capstone
Students complete one substantial work: a 2500-3500 word research thesis, ethics case dossier, public philosophy project, policy philosophy brief, podcast, video essay, exhibition, website, or facilitated dialogue. Every capstone must include a clear philosophical question, source base, thesis, objection-and-reply structure, revision record, and oral defense.

## Assessment Ideas
Use seminar logs, argument drills, source evaluations, short essays, case analyses, peer commentary, draft conferences, revision memos, and final defenses. Assess conceptual clarity, fair reconstruction, quality of reasoning, evidence use, objection handling, seminar citizenship, and audience-aware communication.
