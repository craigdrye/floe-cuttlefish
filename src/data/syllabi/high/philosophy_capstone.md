# Philosophy Capstone
Senior-level philosophy as disciplined public thinking: ask better questions, test reasons across mind, morality, knowledge, and justice, and defend a substantial argument for a real audience.

**ID**: `philosophyCapstone` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This capstone treats philosophy as disciplined public thinking rather than opinion exchange. Students revisit ethics, knowledge, mind, identity, politics, technology, and meaning while learning to ask sharper questions, reconstruct arguments charitably, test reasons against cases, and revise a position when the argument requires it. The goal is not to win debates but to make reasoning more precise, more honest, and more answerable to evidence and objection.

The course is synthetic by design. Where earlier philosophy courses introduce one field at a time, the capstone asks students to bring several fields to bear on the same hard problem, to compare frameworks before reaching judgement, and to notice when a dispute is really about facts, concepts, values, or the meaning of the words being used. Theory is always paired with application so that abstract ideas such as justification, duty, personhood, and legitimacy become live tools for real decisions.

Above all, the course builds toward independent inquiry. Students learn the habits needed when slogans and quick takes are not enough: stating an opposing view fairly, locating the exact point of disagreement, raising a serious objection, answering it, and producing an extended, well-sourced argument that they can defend out loud before a real audience.

## Course Design Notes
The course is seminar-based and case-driven, and it scaffolds toward a major independent capstone. Each chapter introduces a field or problem, then immediately stress-tests it against contested cases drawn from medicine, technology, justice, and everyday life. Recurring routines include argument reconstruction in standard form (premises, conclusion, objection, reply), charitable summaries of views a student does not hold, theory-comparison matrices, and short written defences. Assessment rewards accuracy about what each view actually claims, fairness to opposing positions, factual care in applied cases, and the quality of objection-and-reply rather than the popularity of a conclusion. Useful anchors include the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the National High School Ethics Bowl model, and the argument-analysis practices of IB Theory of Knowledge and AP Seminar.

## Chapter 1: Philosophical Method and Seminar Practice
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between an opinion and a reasoned position? When does a counterexample defeat a claim? Can conviction and intellectual humility coexist?
- **Key concepts**: argument, premise, conclusion; validity vs. soundness; counterexample; conceptual analysis; thought experiment; charity and steelmanning; burden of proof; common fallacies; dialectic; intellectual humility.
- **Applied skills**: reconstructing short philosophical passages and current public claims into standard form before evaluating them; building an argument-anatomy portfolio.
- **Common traps**: treating a strong feeling as a strong argument; calling an argument "true" rather than valid or sound; attacking a weak version of a view instead of its strongest form.

## Chapter 2: Knowledge Under Pressure
- **Core questions**: What turns a true belief into knowledge? When experts disagree, what should a non-expert do? Is it ever wrong to believe something on weak evidence?
- **Key concepts**: truth, belief, justification; the Gettier problem; skepticism; testimony and expertise; epistemic luck; intellectual virtue; cognitive bias; misinformation; responsible doubt and suspension of judgement.
- **Applied skills**: writing an epistemic-responsibility brief on a contested public claim, stating what a reasonable person may assert, doubt, investigate, or withhold judgement about.
- **Common traps**: assuming knowledge is just confident belief; treating any disagreement as proof that no one can know; confusing "I am unsure" with "no one can tell."

## Chapter 3: Mind, Personhood, and Identity
- **Core questions**: What would count as evidence that another being is conscious? Is personal identity grounded in the body, memory, psychology, narrative, relationships, or none of these? Could an AI system deserve moral consideration?
- **Key concepts**: consciousness; physicalism, dualism, and functionalism; qualia; the hard problem; personal identity over time; the teletransportation and fission puzzles; moral status and personhood; minds and machines.
- **Applied skills**: building a theory-comparison matrix that tests at least three views of mind or identity against hard cases such as memory loss, teleportation, fission, nonhuman minds, or AI systems.
- **Common traps**: assuming a being must be human to have a mind; treating "we cannot fully explain consciousness" as proof of dualism; equating passing a behavioural test with proven inner experience.

## Chapter 4: Moral Theories in Collision
- **Core questions**: Should actions be judged by outcomes, intentions, rules, character, relationships, or some combination? When does compromise become complicity? What do we owe strangers and future people?
- **Key concepts**: utilitarianism (act vs. rule); deontology and the categorical imperative; virtue ethics and eudaimonia; care ethics; rights and duties; moral luck; integrity and complicity; supererogation; the is-ought gap.
- **Applied skills**: an Ethics Bowl–style case analysis identifying stakeholders, competing principles, at least three frameworks, objections, replies, and questions for discussion.
- **Common traps**: thinking utilitarianism counts only the agent's happiness; reading "using someone as a means" (often fine) as "merely as a means" (the violation); treating virtue ethics as offering no action guidance.

## Chapter 5: Justice, Power, and Public Reason
- **Core questions**: What makes a law legitimate when citizens disagree? Should justice prioritise equal rights, opportunity, outcomes, capabilities, or freedom from domination? When, if ever, is civil disobedience justified?
- **Key concepts**: social contract and legitimacy; liberty and the harm principle; equality, equity, and the difference principle; Rawls's veil of ignorance; punishment; democracy and pluralism; domination; public reason.
- **Applied skills**: a public-reason memo on a live policy dilemma that states a principle, weighs evidence and institutional constraints, acknowledges reasonable disagreement, and answers the strongest objection.
- **Common traps**: equating any inequality with injustice (or denying any can be unjust); stretching the harm principle so that "offence" counts as "harm"; smuggling a contested premise in as if it were neutral.

## Chapter 6: Meaning, Freedom, and the Examined Life
- **Core questions**: Are meaning and happiness the same goal? How free are people under social, economic, psychological, and technological pressure? Is authenticity a private achievement or a social one?
- **Key concepts**: existentialism and the absurd; authenticity and bad faith; freedom and responsibility; alienation; mortality; the relationship of pleasure, happiness, and meaning; attention and the good life; compatibilism vs. hard determinism.
- **Applied skills**: a philosophical "life audit" that connects one existential concept to a concrete choice, habit, institution, or relationship while maintaining analytic precision.
- **Common traps**: collapsing meaning into momentary pleasure; treating "bad faith" as ordinary dishonesty rather than self-deception about one's freedom; assuming freedom requires that nothing causes our choices.

## Chapter 7: Technology, Science, and Future Persons
- **Core questions**: Who is responsible when an automated system causes harm? Can science be value-free? What do present people owe future people? How should deep uncertainty shape public action?
- **Key concepts**: AI ethics and algorithmic bias; privacy and surveillance; automation and labour; the fact-value distinction in science; models and uncertainty; the responsibility gap; obligations to future generations; bioethics; the precautionary principle.
- **Applied skills**: a technology- or science-ethics review tracing stakeholders, evidence, assumptions, risks, duties, and governance options for an applied case.
- **Common traps**: treating "natural" as automatically "good"; blaming a tool while ignoring the humans who designed, deployed, and profited from it; assuming that uncertainty means no action is justified.

## Chapter 8: Capstone Argument Studio
- **Core questions**: Is the question genuinely philosophical? What must be defined before the argument can begin? Which source changes the shape of the thesis? What is the single hardest objection to answer?
- **Key concepts**: inquiry question and thesis; literature map and annotated bibliography; synthesis across fields; objection and reply; revision; research ethics and citation; oral defence; audience and public philosophy.
- **Applied skills**: assembling a full capstone portfolio — proposal, annotated bibliography, argument map, draft, peer commentary, revision memo, final product, oral defence, and process reflection.
- **Common traps**: choosing a question that is empirical or merely verbal rather than philosophical; arguing before defining key terms; collecting sources that confirm the thesis while ignoring the strongest dissent.

## Capstone
Students complete one substantial work in a form of their choosing: a 2,500–3,500 word research thesis, an ethics case dossier, a public-philosophy project, a policy-philosophy brief, a podcast, a video essay, an exhibition, a website, or a facilitated dialogue. Every capstone must include a clearly stated philosophical question, an annotated source base, a defensible thesis, an objection-and-reply structure, a revision record, and a live oral defence. Grading rewards conceptual clarity, fair reconstruction of opposing views, quality of reasoning and evidence, the strength of the objection-and-reply, audience-aware communication, and the quality of revision after challenge.
