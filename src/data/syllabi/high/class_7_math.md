# Class 7 Math
A calm, well-structured first year of secondary mathematics - extending arithmetic into rational numbers, proportion, early algebra, geometry, and data, with reasoning and accurate communication at the centre.

**ID**: `col-class-7-math` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
Class 7 (Year 7, ages 11-12) is the gateway from primary arithmetic into secondary mathematics. This course extends whole-number work into the full world of rational numbers - positive and negative, fractional and decimal - and uses that foundation to build proportional reasoning, the language of algebra, the angle and measurement facts of geometry, and the basics of statistics and probability. It is aligned across the major Year 7 frameworks: UK KS3 Year 7, US Common Core Grade 7, ACARA v9 Year 7, and India's NCERT Class 7, so the same core ideas surface whatever curriculum a student follows.

The emphasis throughout is on understanding why methods work, not just executing them. A student should be able to calculate accurately, choose an efficient strategy, and then explain the result using a number line, a ratio table, a graph, an equation, or words. Signed-number arithmetic is treated as movement and direction rather than a list of sign rules; proportion is treated as a single multiplicative relationship that shows up as unit rates, scale factors, and the equation y = kx; and algebra is introduced as a way of recording and generalising the arithmetic students already do.

By the end of the course a student can order and compute with rational numbers, solve proportion and percent problems in real contexts, write and solve one- and two-step equations and inequalities, find missing angles and the areas and volumes of common shapes, summarise and display a data set, and reason about the probability of simple events. Just as importantly, they routinely estimate first, label units, and check whether an answer is reasonable.

## Course Design Notes
Use this syllabus for Year 7 / Grade 7 / Class 7 mathematics support across UK KS3, US Common Core Grade 7, ACARA v9, and NCERT. Route a question here when it involves rational numbers, fractions, decimals, percentages, ratio and proportion, simple expressions and one- or two-step equations and inequalities, angle facts, perimeter, area, volume of prisms, statistics, probability, or multi-step applied problem solving. Keep practice cumulative: students should regularly estimate before computing, label units, explain operations with signed numbers in terms of direction on a number line, simplify and convert between fraction-decimal-percent forms, and connect numerical work to algebraic structure. Worked examples should foreground reasoning vocabulary ("explain why", "is this reasonable", "which method is more efficient") and the move from a numerical answer to a justified one. The number line is the unifying model for ordering, signed arithmetic, and inequalities; the ratio table and double number line unify proportional reasoning across rates, scaling, and percent.

## Chapter 1: Rational Numbers and the Number Line
- **Core questions**: What kinds of numbers live on the number line, and how do we compare them? What does a negative quantity mean in context? Why is -2 greater than -5 even though 5 is greater than 2? What is absolute value?
- **Key concepts**: integers; positive and negative fractions and decimals; the number line as an ordering model; opposites and additive inverses; absolute value as distance from zero; comparing and ordering mixed rational numbers; interpreting negatives as temperatures, elevations, and account balances.
- **Applied skills**: locate fractions and decimals on a number line; order a mixed set of rational numbers from least to greatest; compare two negative values and justify which is larger; read and write the absolute value of a number; interpret a signed quantity in a real context.
- **Common traps**: assuming a "bigger-looking" negative (-7) is greater than a "smaller-looking" one (-3); confusing absolute value with making a number negative; treating a negative fraction as positive when ordering; placing a fraction on the wrong side of zero.

## Chapter 2: Operations With Fractions, Decimals, and Integers
- **Core questions**: How do the four operations work when negatives are involved? How do we add fractions with different denominators? How do order-of-operations rules decide what to compute first? How do factors and multiples help simplify?
- **Key concepts**: adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing integers, fractions, decimals, and mixed numbers; equivalent forms and conversion between fractions and decimals; lowest terms; common denominators; order of operations with brackets and powers; factors, multiples, prime factorisation, HCF and LCM.
- **Applied skills**: evaluate a multi-step signed expression using order of operations; add and subtract fractions by finding a common denominator; multiply and divide fractions (including by reciprocals); convert a fraction to a decimal and back; estimate to check a calculated answer.
- **Common traps**: adding fractions by adding numerators and denominators; flipping the wrong fraction when dividing; dropping or mis-applying the sign on a negative term; doing operations left-to-right instead of following order of operations; forgetting to simplify the final fraction.

## Chapter 3: Ratio, Proportion, and Unit Rates
- **Core questions**: What does a ratio compare, and when are two ratios equivalent? What is a unit rate and why is it useful for comparison? What does it mean for two quantities to be proportional, and how does y = kx capture that?
- **Key concepts**: ratio language and notation; equivalent ratios; sharing a quantity in a given ratio; unit rates and "best buy" comparisons; scale factors for recipes and drawings; the constant of proportionality; ratio tables, double number lines, and graphs of proportional relationships; the equation y = kx.
- **Applied skills**: divide a quantity in a given ratio; compute and compare unit rates to find the better value; scale a recipe or a drawing up or down; decide from a table or graph whether a relationship is proportional; find k and write y = kx, and explain what k means in context.
- **Common traps**: adding the same amount to both parts instead of multiplying when forming equivalent ratios; inverting a unit rate (writing dollars-per-item as items-per-dollar); assuming any increasing relationship is proportional; reading a graph as proportional when it does not pass through the origin.

## Chapter 4: Percent and Financial Applications
- **Core questions**: What does percent mean, and how does it relate to fractions and decimals? How do we find a percent of a quantity, or a percent increase or decrease? How do discounts, tax, markup, and simple interest combine in a real purchase?
- **Key concepts**: percent as a rate per 100; fraction-decimal-percent equivalence; finding a percent of a quantity; percent increase and decrease; discounts, sales tax, and markup; simple interest; estimation as a reasonableness check on percent answers.
- **Applied skills**: find 15%, 37.5%, or 120% of a quantity; compute a sale price after a discount and then add tax; find a percent change between two amounts; calculate simple interest over a period; estimate a percent result to judge whether a calculated answer is sensible.
- **Common traps**: treating a percent increase then an equal percent decrease as returning to the start; adding percents that are taken of different bases; confusing "percent of" with "percent off"; misplacing the decimal when converting a percent to a decimal.

## Chapter 5: Expressions, Equations, and Inequalities
- **Core questions**: How do we use a letter to stand for a number, and what is the difference between an expression and an equation? How do we simplify and evaluate expressions? How do we solve a two-step equation, and how is an inequality different from an equation?
- **Key concepts**: variables, constants, terms, and coefficients; substitution and evaluation; equivalent expressions; collecting like terms; the distributive property; one- and two-step equations; inequalities and their solution sets; checking a solution by substitution; representing inequality solutions on a number line.
- **Applied skills**: evaluate an expression for given values; simplify expressions such as 3x + 2x - 4; expand a single bracket; translate a word problem into an equation; solve one- and two-step equations and check the answer; solve and graph a simple inequality.
- **Common traps**: combining unlike terms (e.g. 3x + 2 = 5x); only distributing to the first term inside a bracket; performing an operation on one side of an equation but not the other; forgetting to reverse the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative.

## Chapter 6: Geometry, Angles, and Measurement
- **Core questions**: Which angle facts let us find a missing angle without measuring? How are the angles in triangles, around a point, and on parallel lines related? How do we find the perimeter, area, and volume of common shapes?
- **Key concepts**: angles on a straight line, around a point, and vertically opposite; the angle sum of a triangle and of a quadrilateral; parallel-line angle relationships (corresponding, alternate, co-interior); properties of triangles and quadrilaterals; circles (radius, diameter, circumference); perimeter and area of triangles, parallelograms, trapeziums, and composite shapes; volume of cuboids and triangular prisms.
- **Applied skills**: find a missing angle using one or more angle facts and state the reason; calculate the area and perimeter of a composite shape; find the volume of a cuboid or triangular prism with correct cubic units; construct an accurate labelled diagram.
- **Common traps**: assuming angles are equal because they "look" equal rather than using a rule; using the slant height instead of the perpendicular height for triangle or parallelogram area; mixing up area (square units) and perimeter (linear units); forgetting that volume needs cubic units.

## Chapter 7: Statistics and Probability
- **Core questions**: What makes a question a statistical question? Which average best summarises a data set, and how does an outlier affect it? How do we display data well? How likely is a simple event, and how does experiment compare with theory?
- **Key concepts**: statistical questions and sampling; dot plots, bar charts, line plots, and (where appropriate) histograms; mean, median, mode, and range; outliers and their effect on measures of centre; the 0-to-1 probability scale; theoretical probability of a simple event; experimental probability from collected data.
- **Applied skills**: choose and calculate an appropriate measure of centre for a data set; build and read a suitable data display; identify an outlier and describe its effect; compute the probability of a simple event as a fraction; compare a predicted (theoretical) probability with an observed (experimental) one.
- **Common traps**: using the mean when an outlier makes the median more representative; confusing median with mode, or range with a measure of centre; writing a probability greater than 1; assuming the experimental result must exactly match the theoretical one over a few trials.

## Chapter 8: Mathematical Applications and Reasoning Studio
- **Core questions**: How do we break a multi-step problem into stages and choose the right tool for each? What information is missing or assumed? How can two correct methods reach the same answer, and how do we spot where an incorrect solution goes wrong?
- **Key concepts**: multi-step problem solving across percent, ratio, equations, geometry, and data; choosing and switching between representations (table, graph, diagram, equation, words); units and estimation; constraints and assumptions; critiquing and comparing solution methods.
- **Applied skills**: solve a mixed problem that combines two or more strands (e.g. a discount that feeds into a ratio split); identify what information a problem is missing; find and explain the error in a flawed worked solution; compare two methods and say which is more efficient and why; write a clear answer sentence with units.
- **Common traps**: starting to calculate before deciding what the question is actually asking; losing track of units across a multi-step problem; rounding too early and distorting the final answer; presenting an answer without checking it against an estimate.

## Capstone: Year 7 Applied Mathematics Portfolio
Build an applied mathematics portfolio around a practical question - planning a small event, designing a scaled room layout, comparing subscriptions or prices, analysing class data, or modelling a simple trip. The finished portfolio should pull the whole course together and include:
- One rational-number calculation that uses signed numbers, fractions, or decimals, shown with reasoning and a reasonableness check.
- One proportional-reasoning task (a unit-rate comparison, a scaling, or a y = kx relationship) presented with a table, graph, or equation.
- One percent application that models a discount, tax, markup, or simple-interest scenario and explains each step.
- One algebraic component: an equation or inequality written from the context, solved, and checked.
- One geometry component: a labelled diagram or a measurement calculation with correct units.
- One data display with a short interpretation, plus a brief reflection on the assumptions made and how accurate the result is likely to be.
