# A-Level Maths
Pure, statistics, and mechanics built for fluent, proof-aware, exam-ready sixth-form mathematics.

**ID**: `alevelMaths` · **Discipline**: Mathematics

## Course Aim
A-Level Mathematics is the UK's flagship sixth-form qualification in pure and applied mathematics, taken at ages 16-18 across the Edexcel (9MA0), AQA (7357), and OCR (H240) specifications. All three routes share a common core: roughly two-thirds pure mathematics (proof, algebra, functions, coordinate geometry, sequences, trigonometry, exponentials and logarithms, calculus, vectors, and numerical methods) and one-third applied mathematics split between statistics and mechanics. This course prepares students to sit those papers with confidence.

The emphasis is on fluency joined to reasoning. Students should not only execute procedures but justify them: choosing the right proof method, stating modelling assumptions, deciding when an answer must be exact, and interpreting results in context. Calculator and graphing technology is treated as a checking and exploration tool, never a replacement for method, because most marks reward correct working and clear argument.

By the end of the course a student can move fluidly between symbolic algebra, graphs, and worded contexts; differentiate and integrate confidently; analyse data and run a hypothesis test with a properly worded conclusion; and model a mechanics situation with a labelled force diagram, consistent units, and a sense check. The course is examined entirely by terminal written papers, so written communication and time management are mathematics, not extras.

## Course Design Notes
Use this syllabus for UK A-Level Mathematics support across Edexcel, AQA, and OCR. Keep pure, statistics, and mechanics connected through modelling and interpretation rather than teaching them as isolated silos. Require clear notation, explicit assumptions, diagrams, technology checks, and post-question review throughout. The chapter order broadly follows the pure-then-applied structure most centres teach, but topics are deliberately cross-linked (e.g. calculus appears in kinematics; logarithms appear in exponential modelling). Worked examples should foreground exam command words ("prove", "show that", "hence", "state assumptions") and the difference between method marks and accuracy marks.

## Chapter 1: Proof, Notation, and Mathematical Argument
- **Core questions**: Which proof method fits a given statement? When is a counterexample a complete disproof? How should set and function notation be read?
- **Key concepts**: proof by deduction, proof by exhaustion, proof by contradiction, disproof by counterexample, conditional statements, necessary and sufficient conditions, set-builder and interval notation, quantifiers.
- **Applied skills**: select and lay out a valid proof; show that irrationality of root 2 follows by contradiction; convert between worded claims and symbolic logic.
- **Common traps**: confirming a few cases and calling it a proof; reversing a conditional; assuming what is to be proved; treating an illustration as a deductive argument.

## Chapter 2: Algebra, Functions, and Coordinate Geometry
- **Core questions**: How does completing the square reveal a vertex or a circle's centre? What transformation maps one graph to another? How are composite and inverse functions formed?
- **Key concepts**: indices and surds, quadratics and the discriminant, simultaneous equations, inequalities, polynomial division, factor and remainder theorems, algebraic fractions, functions, domain and range, transformations, modulus, straight lines, and circles.
- **Applied skills**: complete the square to find the centre and radius of a circle; apply graph transformations and state the new domain and range; use the discriminant to determine the number of roots.
- **Common traps**: dropping the ± when square-rooting; mishandling inequality direction when multiplying by a negative; confusing f(x)+a with f(x+a); forgetting domain restrictions on inverses.

## Chapter 3: Sequences, Series, and the Binomial Expansion
- **Core questions**: When does a geometric series converge? How is sigma notation evaluated? How does the binomial expansion approximate a value?
- **Key concepts**: arithmetic and geometric sequences, nth term and sum formulae, sum to infinity for |r| < 1, sigma notation, recurrence relations, binomial expansion for positive integer n and for fractional or negative n with its validity condition.
- **Applied skills**: find the sum of an arithmetic series; determine the sum to infinity of a convergent geometric series; expand (1+x)^n for non-integer n and state the range of x for which it is valid.
- **Common traps**: applying the sum-to-infinity formula when |r| >= 1; off-by-one errors in sigma limits; forgetting the validity interval |x| < 1 (suitably scaled) for general binomial expansions.

## Chapter 4: Trigonometry
- **Core questions**: How are equations solved over a restricted interval? Which identity simplifies a given expression? When are small-angle approximations valid?
- **Key concepts**: radians, arc length and sector area, exact values, sine and cosine rules, the graphs of sin, cos, tan, the reciprocal functions sec, cosec, cot, Pythagorean identities, compound and double-angle formulae, R cos(x ± a) form, and small-angle approximations.
- **Applied skills**: solve a trigonometric equation on a stated interval and report all solutions; prove an identity; rewrite a sin + b cos in R-form to find a maximum.
- **Common traps**: working in degrees when radians are required; losing solutions by dividing through by a trig factor; mixing up the small-angle approximations sin θ ≈ θ and cos θ ≈ 1 − θ²/2.

## Chapter 5: Exponentials, Logarithms, and Modelling
- **Core questions**: Why is e the natural base for growth? How do logarithms linearise a power or exponential model? What does a model parameter mean in context?
- **Key concepts**: exponential functions, the number e, natural logarithms, the laws of logarithms, solving exponential equations, exponential growth and decay models, linearising y = ax^n and y = ab^x using logs, and the limitations of models.
- **Applied skills**: solve an equation involving e^x or ln x; use a log-log or log-linear plot to estimate model constants; interpret a decay constant as a half-life or rate.
- **Common traps**: treating log(a+b) as log a + log b; sign errors when taking logs of both sides; forgetting the domain x > 0 for ln x; over-extrapolating an exponential model.

## Chapter 6: Differentiation and Its Applications
- **Core questions**: Which rule applies to a given function? How is a stationary point classified? How is an optimisation problem set up from words?
- **Key concepts**: derivative as a rate of change, differentiation from first principles, standard derivatives, product, quotient, and chain rules, implicit and parametric differentiation, tangents and normals, increasing/decreasing functions, stationary points, second derivative test, optimisation, and connected rates of change.
- **Applied skills**: differentiate a product or quotient correctly; classify a stationary point using the second derivative; build and solve an optimisation model with a stated constraint.
- **Common traps**: using the power rule on a product; sign slips in the quotient rule; forgetting the chain-rule inner derivative; declaring a maximum without checking the second derivative or endpoints.

## Chapter 7: Integration and Numerical Methods
- **Core questions**: How is a definite integral interpreted as an area? Which integration technique fits the integrand? When is an iterative root estimate reliable?
- **Key concepts**: antiderivatives, the constant of integration, definite integrals and area, area between curves, integration by substitution, integration by parts, partial fractions, separable differential equations with initial conditions, the trapezium rule, and the Newton-Raphson iteration.
- **Applied skills**: evaluate an area between two curves; solve a separable differential equation given a condition; apply the trapezium rule and state whether it over- or under-estimates; iterate Newton-Raphson and check convergence.
- **Common traps**: omitting +c on indefinite integrals; subtracting areas in the wrong order; using the wrong sign in by-parts; assuming an iteration converges without testing the sign change or stationary points.

## Chapter 8: Vectors
- **Core questions**: How is the magnitude and direction of a vector found? When are two vectors parallel? How do position vectors describe geometry?
- **Key concepts**: vectors in two (and three) dimensions, column and i/j notation, magnitude, unit vectors, vector addition and scalar multiples, position vectors, parallel and collinear conditions, and geometric problem solving.
- **Applied skills**: find the magnitude and direction of a vector; show three points are collinear; use position vectors to find a point that divides a line in a given ratio.
- **Common traps**: confusing position and displacement vectors; forgetting that parallel means scalar multiple, not equal; sign errors when subtracting position vectors.

## Chapter 9: Statistics - Data, Probability, and Distributions
- **Core questions**: Is a sampling method sound? Which distribution models a situation? How is a probability computed from a distribution?
- **Key concepts**: sampling methods and the large data set, measures of location and spread, outliers and cleaning, histograms, box plots, scatter diagrams, correlation and the regression line, probability rules, conditional probability, the binomial distribution, and the normal distribution.
- **Applied skills**: critique a sampling method and suggest an improvement; calculate a binomial probability; use the normal distribution (via a calculator) to find a probability or a quantile.
- **Common traps**: confusing P(X = x) with P(X <= x); using the binomial when trials are not independent or n is not fixed; reading the normal table tail in the wrong direction; treating correlation as causation.

## Chapter 10: Statistics - Hypothesis Testing
- **Core questions**: What are the null and alternative hypotheses? How is the significance level used? How should a conclusion be worded in context?
- **Key concepts**: null and alternative hypotheses, one- and two-tailed tests, test statistic, significance level, critical region, p-value, hypothesis tests for a binomial proportion, and tests for a correlation coefficient.
- **Applied skills**: set up appropriate hypotheses; decide whether the result falls in the critical region; write a conclusion that refers back to the original context, not just "reject H0".
- **Common traps**: stating hypotheses about a sample rather than a population parameter; halving (or not halving) the significance level incorrectly for one-tailed vs two-tailed tests; concluding the alternative is "proved" rather than "supported".

## Chapter 11: Mechanics - Kinematics and Projectiles
- **Core questions**: When do the SUVAT equations apply? How is calculus used when acceleration is not constant? How is projectile motion split into components?
- **Key concepts**: scalars and vectors, displacement, velocity, acceleration, displacement-time and velocity-time graphs, the constant-acceleration (SUVAT) equations, vertical motion under gravity, variable acceleration via differentiation and integration, and projectile motion resolved into horizontal and vertical components.
- **Applied skills**: choose and apply the correct SUVAT equation; differentiate or integrate a displacement function to find velocity or acceleration; solve a projectile problem by linking horizontal and vertical motion through time.
- **Common traps**: applying SUVAT when acceleration is not constant; sign errors with downward gravity; forgetting that horizontal velocity is constant for a projectile; mixing up displacement and distance.

## Chapter 12: Mechanics - Forces, Newton's Laws, and Moments
- **Core questions**: What forces act, and which way? When is friction limiting? How is equilibrium of a rigid body analysed?
- **Key concepts**: force as a vector, weight, normal reaction, tension, thrust, friction with F <= μR and limiting F = μR, Newton's three laws, F = ma, connected particles and pulleys, resolving on inclined planes, moments, and equilibrium of a rigid body.
- **Applied skills**: draw a complete labelled force diagram before calculating; apply Newton's second law to each particle in a connected system; take moments about a point to find an unknown force or distance.
- **Common traps**: omitting a force from the diagram; using F = μR when the body is not on the point of moving; inconsistent positive directions across connected particles; forgetting that moments depend on perpendicular distance.

## Capstone
Build an A-Level Maths readiness portfolio containing: one rigorous proof (deduction, contradiction, or exhaustion); one pure problem combining algebra, trigonometry, or logarithms with full exact working; one calculus application (an optimisation or an area/differential-equation task) with a written conclusion; one statistics investigation using the large data set that runs and correctly interprets a hypothesis test in context; one mechanics model with a labelled force diagram, stated assumptions, consistent units, and a physical sense check; and one timed mixed-paper correction log showing how each error was diagnosed against the mark scheme and a plan for targeted retrieval practice.
