# High School Grammar
Mechanics, usage, and sentence style for confident editing and deliberate rhetorical choices.

**ID**: `col-grammar` · **Discipline**: English

## Course Aim
This course builds practical command of English grammar, punctuation, usage, and sentence style so students can edit their own writing, recognize standardized-test conventions, and make deliberate stylistic choices. It treats grammar not as a list of rules to memorize but as a toolkit for diagnosing and fixing problems in real sentences.

Students learn to name a structure only when the name helps them see or fix something: identifying a dependent clause matters because it explains why a fragment fails; spotting a passive construction matters because it reveals a missing actor. Each chapter pairs a body of terminology with the editing move it unlocks, moving from sentence foundations through clause variety, boundary errors, verb and pronoun control, punctuation, and finally the stylistic editing choices skilled writers make on purpose.

By the end, students should be able to read a draft, name what is wrong in grammatical terms, choose the correct fix, and explain why an alternative fix would be wrong. This explanatory ability — knowing why a comma splice is not the same error as a fused sentence — is what transfers to test performance and to genuinely cleaner writing.

## Course Design Notes
This is not grammar for grammar's sake. Each chapter connects rules to editing, sentence clarity, and rhetorical control. The progression is diagnostic: students first learn to identify structures, then to spot the errors those structures make possible, and finally to weigh competing correct revisions for tone and emphasis. Examples are concrete and often playful so that the grammar, not the subject matter, stays in focus. The course aligns with Common Core Language Standards for grades 9-10 and with conventions tested on the SAT and ACT, but it frames every rule around the question a working editor actually asks.

## Chapter 1: Sentence Foundations
- **Core questions**: What is the main subject? Is this group of words a complete sentence? Which words form a phrase rather than a clause?
- **Key concepts**: subject, predicate, phrase, clause, independent clause, dependent clause, simple sentence, direct object, indirect object.
- **Applied skills**: mapping a sentence into subject, verb, and modifiers; distinguishing a clause from a phrase; testing whether a word group can stand alone.
- **Common traps**: calling the verb the subject; treating a prepositional phrase as a clause; assuming any long string of words is a sentence.

## Chapter 2: Parts of Speech in Context
- **Core questions**: What job does this word do in this sentence? Which modifier is in the wrong place? What changes when a word shifts function?
- **Key concepts**: nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, prepositions, conjunctions, interjections, function shift.
- **Applied skills**: identifying part of speech by role rather than by word; annotating a sentence; revising modifier placement.
- **Common traps**: labeling words by their usual category instead of their function here; confusing adjectives with adverbs; mistaking a preposition for a conjunction.

## Chapter 3: Clause Types and Sentence Variety
- **Core questions**: Which idea should be the main clause? Which clause adds supporting detail? How can sentence variety improve rhythm and emphasis?
- **Key concepts**: noun clauses, adjective clauses, adverb clauses, simple, compound, complex, compound-complex sentences, coordination, subordination.
- **Applied skills**: classifying sentences by structure; combining short sentences through subordination; rebalancing a paragraph for variety.
- **Common traps**: stacking coordinate clauses where subordination would clarify emphasis; misnaming a complex sentence as compound; burying the main idea in a dependent clause.

## Chapter 4: Fragments, Run-Ons, and Boundaries
- **Core questions**: Where does one complete thought end? Which punctuation correctly joins these clauses? Is the colon introducing an explanation?
- **Key concepts**: fragment, fused sentence, comma splice, coordinating conjunction, semicolon, colon, dash.
- **Applied skills**: locating clause boundaries; choosing among period, semicolon, and comma-plus-conjunction; labeling each correction with its rule.
- **Common traps**: confusing a comma splice with a fused sentence; joining independent clauses with a comma alone; using a colon after an incomplete clause.

## Chapter 5: Verbs, Agreement, Voice, and Mood
- **Core questions**: Does the verb agree with the true subject? Is the passive voice hiding the actor? Is the tense sequence logical?
- **Key concepts**: tense, aspect, active voice, passive voice, subject-verb agreement, indicative, imperative, interrogative, conditional, subjunctive mood.
- **Applied skills**: finding the real subject behind intervening phrases; converting between active and passive; using the subjunctive for hypotheticals.
- **Common traps**: matching the verb to a noun inside a prepositional phrase; assuming passive voice is always wrong; using indicative "was" where the subjunctive "were" is required.

## Chapter 6: Pronouns, Modifiers, and Parallelism
- **Core questions**: What noun does this pronoun refer to? What is this modifier actually modifying? Are the items in this list grammatically parallel?
- **Key concepts**: antecedent, pronoun-antecedent agreement, vague reference, misplaced modifier, dangling modifier, parallel structure.
- **Applied skills**: tracing each pronoun to one clear antecedent; reattaching dangling modifiers to the right subject; rewriting lists into parallel form.
- **Common traps**: leaving a pronoun with two possible referents; placing a modifier next to the wrong noun; mixing gerunds and infinitives in one series.

## Chapter 7: Punctuation and Style
- **Core questions**: Is this element essential to the meaning? Is the possession singular or plural? Which punctuation best supports the sentence's rhythm?
- **Key concepts**: commas, apostrophes, quotation marks, parentheses, hyphens, dashes, appositives, restrictive and nonrestrictive elements.
- **Applied skills**: setting off nonrestrictive elements with commas; forming singular and plural possessives; choosing dashes, parentheses, or commas for asides.
- **Common traps**: surrounding a restrictive clause with commas; putting the apostrophe on the wrong side of the s; using a hyphen where a dash is needed.

## Chapter 8: Grammar for Editing and Rhetoric
- **Core questions**: What can be cut without losing meaning? Which sentence should be longer or shorter? Which rule is being bent on purpose for effect?
- **Key concepts**: concision, emphasis, rhythm, syntax, intentional fragments, repetition, proofreading, style guides.
- **Applied skills**: trimming wordiness; varying sentence length for emphasis; recognizing when a "broken" rule is a deliberate stylistic choice.
- **Common traps**: cutting words that carry meaning; mistaking every fragment for an error; over-correcting natural rhythm into stiff uniformity.

## Capstone
Edit a multi-paragraph piece of writing through three passes: correctness, clarity, and style. Submit the marked-up drafts, a rule checklist showing which conventions were applied, and a short reflection naming the highest-impact revisions and explaining why each improved the writing.

## Assessment Ideas
- Corrections graded on accurate rule recognition and clear explanation.
- Revision tasks graded on clarity and sentence control.
- Capstone graded on transfer of grammar knowledge to authentic writing.
