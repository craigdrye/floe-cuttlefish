# Algebra 2 Collections
A full Algebra 2 course that extends Algebra 1 into the major function families and the modeling habits that connect them.

**ID**: `col-algebra-2` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
Algebra 2 is where the separate skills of Algebra 1 become a connected system. Students learn to recognize a function family from any representation, transform it deliberately, solve the equations it generates, and check that their answers respect the constraints of the situation. The course spans polynomial, rational, radical, exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric-adjacent reasoning, alongside complex numbers, sequences and series, conic sections, probability, and statistics.

The through-line is the modeling cycle: notice structure, choose a representation, transform or solve, check reasonableness, and interpret in context. Students should leave able to move fluently among equations, graphs, tables, and words, and to explain why a method works rather than only that it produces an answer.

This course targets the Year 11 high-school tier (ages 15-16) and aligns to Common Core high-school algebra, functions, and statistics standards. It is region-tagged US and draws on the public Eureka Math / EngageNY Algebra II sequence and Illustrative Mathematics tasks, but the reasoning is curriculum-neutral and transfers to any rigorous Algebra 2 course.

## Course Design Notes
Keep the course organized around function families and the modeling cycle rather than isolated procedures. Route a question here when it involves advanced algebraic manipulation, comparing or transforming functions, solving equations with domain or extraneous-solution constraints, the complex number system, sequences and series, conic sections, or selecting and interpreting a statistical model. Earlier linear and basic-quadratic work belongs in Algebra 1; calculus-level limits and derivatives belong in Precalculus or Calculus.

Every chapter should demand multiple representations and a reasonableness check. Favor questions that ask students to justify a step, identify a hidden restriction, or detect a wrong answer over questions that reward only mechanical execution. Common traps below name the specific misconceptions to surface deliberately, since most Algebra 2 errors are predictable: dropped negative signs, forgotten domain restrictions, extraneous solutions, and misapplied logarithm or exponent rules.

## Chapter 1: Function Structure and Transformation
- **Core questions**: How do function notation, composition, and inverses let us build and read complex functions? How do shifts, stretches, and reflections relate the parent graph to its transformed form?
- **Key concepts**: Function notation, domain and range, average rate of change, composition, inverse functions, parent graphs (linear, quadratic, absolute value, square root, cube root), transformations, and piecewise definitions.
- **Applied skills**: Evaluate and compose functions; find an inverse with an appropriate domain restriction; describe a transformation from an equation; match graphs, tables, equations, and verbal descriptions of the same function.
- **Common traps**: Treating f(g(x)) as multiplication; assuming every function has an inverse; confusing the effect of changes inside versus outside the function on the graph; ignoring domain when inverting.

## Chapter 2: Quadratics, Complex Numbers, and Algebraic Fluency
- **Core questions**: When is each quadratic-solving method most efficient, and what does the discriminant predict? How does the complex number system complete the set of solutions?
- **Key concepts**: Completing the square, the quadratic formula, the discriminant, the imaginary unit i, operations with complex numbers, complex conjugates, and structure in expressions.
- **Applied skills**: Solve quadratics by factoring, completing the square, and the formula; classify roots from the discriminant; add, subtract, multiply, and simplify complex expressions including powers of i.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting the plus-or-minus when taking a square root; sign errors while completing the square; treating i^2 as 1 instead of -1; misreading a negative discriminant as "no solutions" rather than two complex solutions.

## Chapter 3: Polynomial Functions and Identities
- **Core questions**: How do a polynomial's factored form, zeros, and degree determine its graph? How do the Remainder and Factor Theorems link division to roots?
- **Key concepts**: Polynomial operations, long and synthetic division, the Remainder and Factor Theorems, zeros and multiplicity, end behavior, turning points, polynomial identities, and the Binomial Theorem.
- **Applied skills**: Divide polynomials; use a known factor to find remaining zeros; sketch end behavior and multiplicity from factored form; expand binomials with the Binomial Theorem or Pascal's triangle.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting placeholder zero coefficients in division; assuming the number of real zeros always equals the degree; ignoring how even versus odd multiplicity changes whether the graph crosses or touches the x-axis.

## Chapter 4: Rational and Radical Expressions
- **Core questions**: Why do rational expressions have excluded values, and how do those produce asymptotes or holes? Why must radical and rational equations be checked for extraneous solutions?
- **Key concepts**: Rational expressions and restrictions, equivalent forms, vertical and horizontal asymptotes, holes, rational equations, radical equations, rational exponents, and extraneous solutions.
- **Applied skills**: Simplify and combine rational expressions; identify excluded values, asymptotes, and holes; solve rational and radical equations; rewrite radicals using rational exponents and back again.
- **Common traps**: Canceling terms instead of factors; losing the domain restriction after simplifying; squaring both sides without checking for extraneous roots; mishandling fractional exponents.

## Chapter 5: Exponential and Logarithmic Models
- **Core questions**: How are logarithms the inverse of exponentials, and how do their properties let us solve equations where the variable is an exponent? What do growth and decay parameters mean in context?
- **Key concepts**: Exponential growth and decay, logarithms as inverses, product, quotient, and power log properties, common and natural logarithms, continuous compounding, half-life, and doubling time.
- **Applied skills**: Convert between exponential and logarithmic form; solve exponential equations with logarithms; apply log properties to expand or condense; fit and interpret a growth or decay model with a reasonableness check.
- **Common traps**: Writing log(a + b) as log a + log b; confusing the base; mishandling the change-of-base step; misreading the growth rate versus the growth factor in a model.

## Chapter 6: Sequences, Series, and Recursive Reasoning
- **Core questions**: How do recursive and explicit forms describe the same pattern? When does a finite geometric series have a compact closed form, and how does that model finance?
- **Key concepts**: Arithmetic and geometric sequences, recursive and explicit formulas, sigma notation, finite arithmetic and geometric series, and repeated-percent-change applications.
- **Applied skills**: Translate between recursive and explicit forms; find a specified term; evaluate a finite series; model savings, loan payments, or repeated percent change.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the common difference with the common ratio; off-by-one errors in the term index; misusing the series sum formula for the nth term; forgetting that geometric growth compounds rather than adds.

## Chapter 7: Conics and Coordinate Geometry
- **Core questions**: How does completing the square reveal the center and key features of a conic? How do the defining distances (foci, directrix) generate each conic's equation?
- **Key concepts**: Distance and midpoint formulas, completing the square for conics, circles, parabolas, ellipses, hyperbolas, foci, directrices, axes, and asymptotes.
- **Applied skills**: Write and graph conic equations; identify center, vertices, foci, and asymptotes; derive a parabola from focus and directrix; model a geometric design constraint.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the ellipse and hyperbola standard forms (sum versus difference); forgetting to balance both sides when completing the square; mistaking the radius for the radius-squared in a circle equation.

## Chapter 8: Probability, Statistics, and Function Modeling Studio
- **Core questions**: How do conditional probability and independence shape predictions from data? How do we choose between linear, quadratic, and exponential models, and what can a model honestly claim?
- **Key concepts**: Conditional probability, independence, two-way tables, normal distributions, z-scores, study design, correlation, regression, residuals, and model selection.
- **Applied skills**: Compute conditional probabilities from two-way tables; interpret normal-model estimates with z-scores; critique a survey or experiment; compare model fits using residual evidence.
- **Common traps**: Confusing P(A and B) with P(A or B); assuming correlation implies causation; reading independence from a table incorrectly; over-trusting a model outside the range of its data.

## Capstone
Students complete a modeling project that begins with a measurable question and ends with a defensible algebraic model. The final product includes the data or stated assumptions, at least two representations of the relationship, a solved or optimized question, an explicit check of constraints (domain, extraneous solutions, or reasonableness), and a clear interpretation written for a non-specialist audience. Strong projects name the limits of the model: what it can predict and where it breaks down.

## Assessment Ideas
- Mixed-representation quizzes that require moving among equations, graphs, tables, and written descriptions of the same function.
- Error-analysis prompts focused on domain restrictions, extraneous solutions, logarithm properties, and inverse functions.
- Modeling tasks scored on representation choice, algebraic accuracy, interpretation, and stated limits of the model.
- Chapter portfolios in which students revise one solved problem after feedback and explain the correction.
- A capstone rubric covering question quality, model fit, mathematical justification, technology use, and communication.
