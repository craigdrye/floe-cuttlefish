# Life Skills & Adulting
Money, work, housing, health, and home — the practical playbook for leaving high school ready to run your own life.

**ID**: `lifeSkillsAndAdulting` · **Discipline**: Life Skills

## Course Aim
Life Skills & Adulting prepares students for the transition from high-school academics to independent living. It treats "adulting" as a set of learnable, repeatable systems rather than a personality trait you either have or lack. Across eight chapters, students build fluency in money and banking, credit and debt, paychecks and taxes, housing and roommates, transportation, careers and professional communication, health care and civic life, and the domestic and emotional routines that keep a household running.

The driving goal is fewer costly surprises. Most adult money pain does not come from rare disasters; it comes from predictable, avoidable mistakes — an overdraft fee, a minimum-payment debt spiral, a misread lease, a lapsed insurance policy, an unfiled form. Students learn the vocabulary and the arithmetic, but the real outcome is judgment: spotting the hidden fee, choosing the cheaper-over-time option, knowing which problem needs a professional, and knowing where to get trustworthy help.

Because rules, prices, and benefits vary by place and time, the course teaches students to localize and verify rather than memorize a single answer. Dollar figures and tax thresholds are taught as examples that change yearly; the durable skill is reading the actual document, doing the math on real numbers, and asking the right next question.

## Course Design Notes
- **Concrete and scenario-driven.** Every concept is anchored to a realistic artifact: a bank statement, a paystub, a lease clause, a loan offer, an insurance quote, a job posting. Questions ask students to find the fee, balance the budget, or decode the form — not to recite definitions.
- **Localizable and current.** US federal facts (FICA rates, FDIC limits, standard deduction) are presented as worked examples; students are reminded that figures update yearly and that state and local rules differ. Verification beats memorization.
- **Judgment over jargon.** Distractors target the real traps: confusing gross with net, treating minimum payments as the cost of credit, assuming a deposit is non-refundable, or buying coverage that does not match the risk.
- **High-school appropriate.** No tax-prep professional depth; the aim is competent first-time independence and knowing when and where to seek expert help (CFPB, IRS, Healthcare.gov, local tenant resources).

## Chapter 1: Money Basics and Banking
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between a checking and a savings account? Which fee is being charged, and how do you avoid it? How big should a first emergency fund be?
- **Key concepts**: checking vs. savings, APY vs. interest rate, overdraft and NSF fees, debit vs. credit, direct deposit, FDIC insurance ($250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category), emergency fund, the 50/30/20 budgeting guideline.
- **Applied skills**: build a monthly starter budget from take-home pay; read a bank statement and identify each fee; calculate interest earned at a given APY; decide what to fund first.
- **Common traps**: confusing APY with a one-time bonus; assuming debit purchases can never overdraw; thinking FDIC insures stocks or crypto; budgeting before naming an emergency fund.

## Chapter 2: Credit, Debt, and Financial Decisions
- **Core questions**: What does this loan actually cost over its life? What happens if you pay only the minimum? Is this offer legitimate or a scam?
- **Key concepts**: credit score (FICO 300–850; payment history 35%, amounts owed/utilization 30%), APR vs. interest rate, minimum payment, compound interest, credit utilization, debt-to-income ratio, secured vs. unsecured debt, predatory lending and consumer protections (CFPB).
- **Applied skills**: compare two credit cards or loans by total cost; estimate payoff time and interest under minimum payments; calculate utilization; flag red flags in a too-good-to-be-true offer.
- **Common traps**: believing the minimum payment is the price of credit; thinking a higher credit limit improves a score by itself; confusing APR with the monthly rate; assuming closing old cards always helps.

## Chapter 3: Paychecks, Taxes, and Benefits
- **Core questions**: Why is take-home pay so much less than the salary? Is this worker an employee or an independent contractor? Which benefit changes total compensation the most?
- **Key concepts**: gross vs. net pay, W-4 (tells employer how much to withhold), W-2 vs. 1099, FICA (Social Security 6.2%, Medicare 1.45% = 7.65% employee share), federal/state withholding, standard deduction, employer health insurance, 401(k) match, PTO, total compensation.
- **Applied skills**: decode a paystub line by line; separate FICA from income-tax withholding; classify employee vs. contractor; value a 401(k) match and benefits as part of pay.
- **Common traps**: budgeting off gross pay; thinking a refund means you "earned" money rather than over-withheld; ignoring the self-employment tax burden on 1099 work; passing up a 401(k) match.

## Chapter 4: Housing and Roommate Life
- **Core questions**: What does this lease actually obligate you to? Which housing costs recur every month versus once? What should a roommate agreement put in writing before move-in?
- **Key concepts**: lease terms, security deposit (and conditions for its return), renter's insurance, utilities and who pays them, tenant rights and habitability, move-in/move-out inspection, maintenance requests, roommate agreements, subletting and breaking a lease.
- **Applied skills**: read a lease and list obligations and penalties; total true monthly housing cost; document a unit's condition at move-in; draft a roommate agreement covering rent, bills, guests, and chores.
- **Common traps**: assuming a security deposit is non-refundable or, conversely, always returned; skipping renter's insurance because "the landlord's covers me"; not documenting move-in condition; relying on verbal roommate deals.

## Chapter 5: Transportation and Basic Maintenance
- **Core questions**: What is the true monthly cost of owning a car versus using transit? Which insurance coverage actually matters for your situation? When is a car problem urgent versus routine?
- **Key concepts**: total cost of ownership (payment, insurance, fuel, maintenance, registration, depreciation), buying vs. leasing vs. financing, liability vs. collision vs. comprehensive coverage, deductible, routine maintenance (oil, tires, brakes), reading a repair estimate, an emergency kit.
- **Applied skills**: compare transit, used car, and lease by monthly all-in cost; match insurance coverages to scenarios; check tire pressure and read an oil-change interval; triage which repairs can wait.
- **Common traps**: comparing only sticker price or monthly payment instead of total cost; thinking liability covers your own car; ignoring depreciation; deferring brake or tire warnings.

## Chapter 6: Career Navigation and Professional Communication
- **Core questions**: Which of your experiences fit this specific job? How should a professional message be phrased? What story best answers a behavioral interview question?
- **Key concepts**: resume and cover letter, tailoring to a job description, professional vs. casual email, the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result), interview etiquette, references, workplace norms, giving and receiving feedback, networking.
- **Applied skills**: match resume bullets to a posting's requirements; write a clear, professional email with a useful subject line; build a STAR answer bank; prepare and send a follow-up note.
- **Common traps**: using one generic resume for every job; informal tone or vague subject lines in work email; listing duties instead of accomplishments; rambling, unstructured interview answers.

## Chapter 7: Health Care, Civic Life, and Personal Documents
- **Core questions**: What will this doctor's visit actually cost? Which election or office affects the issue you care about? Where should critical documents live so you can find them under stress?
- **Key concepts**: premium, deductible, copay, coinsurance, out-of-pocket maximum, in-network vs. out-of-network, primary care vs. urgent care vs. ER, voter registration, levels of government and what each does, IDs and vital records (Social Security card, birth certificate, passport), privacy and document storage.
- **Applied skills**: estimate the cost of a visit before and after the deductible; choose the right care setting for a symptom; register to vote and find a ballot's local races; build a secure document checklist.
- **Common traps**: assuming a low premium means low total cost; confusing deductible with copay; going to the ER for non-emergencies; storing every important document in one unsecured place.

## Chapter 8: Domestic Systems and Emotional Survival
- **Core questions**: What can you cook cheaply and safely on a busy week? Which problems should you handle yourself versus call a professional for? How do you address conflict before it grows?
- **Key concepts**: meal planning and grocery budgeting, food safety (danger zone 40–140°F, safe cooking temperatures, leftovers), laundry care symbols, basic cleaning and minor repairs, time management and calendars, boundaries, conflict resolution, and asking for help (including mental-health support).
- **Applied skills**: plan a week of cheap, safe meals; sort and run laundry without ruining clothes; fix or escalate a household problem appropriately; build a weekly life system (meals, chores, calendar, money check, support plan).
- **Common traps**: leaving perishables in the danger zone; mixing cleaning chemicals; deferring all maintenance until it becomes expensive; letting small conflicts fester instead of naming them early.

## Capstone
Build a first-apartment-or-first-job survival packet that proves readiness across the course. It should include: a monthly budget built from take-home pay with a banking plan; a credit and debt strategy; a decoded sample paystub; a lease and renter's checklist plus a roommate agreement draft; a transportation cost comparison; a tailored resume and a STAR answer bank; a health-care cost and care-setting guide; a meal plan with food-safety notes; and an organized list of personal documents and emergency contacts. The packet should be localized to the student's state where rules differ and should explicitly flag where to verify current figures and where to seek professional help.

## Assessment Ideas
- Calculations graded on realistic assumptions, correct arithmetic, and use of net rather than gross figures.
- Checklists and drafts graded on practical completeness and risk awareness (fees, deadlines, deposits, coverage).
- Capstone graded on readiness, local adaptation, and the ability to identify hidden costs and verification steps.
