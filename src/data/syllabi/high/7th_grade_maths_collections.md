# 7th Grade Maths Collections
Confident middle-grade mathematics: rational numbers, proportional reasoning, percent, equations, geometry, and chance, building a clean bridge into algebra.

**ID**: `col-7th-grade-math` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
This course builds confident, flexible middle-grade mathematicians who can calculate with rational numbers, reason with ratios and percents, use expressions and equations to solve problems, and make sense of geometry, data, and chance. It follows the Common Core Grade 7 sequence: ratios and proportional relationships, the number system, expressions and equations, geometry, and statistics and probability. The aim is not just correct answers but durable number sense and clear reasoning.

Students arrive from different sixth-grade pathways, so the course stays concrete before it goes abstract. Every topic starts from a model students can picture, a number line, a tape diagram, a proportional table, a scaled drawing, and then moves to a rule they can defend. The recurring habit is explanation: students should be able to say why a method works and whether an answer is reasonable, not only produce a number.

By the end of the course students should compute fluently with positive and negative rationals, recognize and use proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, and words, solve percent and scale problems in real contexts, solve two-step equations and simple inequalities, work with angles, circles, area, surface area, and volume, and reason about samples, distributions, and probability. They should also leave with a clear bridge into 8th-grade algebra and functions.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve rational-number operations, proportional relationships, unit rates and constants of proportionality, percent increase and decrease, simple interest, scale drawings, linear expressions, one- and two-step equations or inequalities, angle and circle problems, area, surface area, and volume, sampling and inference, probability, or multistep word problems at the Grade 7 level. Keep the work cumulative: percent leans on ratios, equations reappear in geometry and statistics, and signed numbers thread through everything.

Keep practice forward and error-aware. Every wrong answer should reflect a real student misconception, a sign slip, a percent-of-the-wrong-base error, a unit-rate inversion, a confusion of area with circumference, with a specific reason it fails and a forward hint toward the right move. Favor concrete, relatable contexts (recipes, maps, sales, sports, surveys, games) so the mathematics feels grounded. Watch the age-appropriate boundary: this is Grade 7, so y = kx and px + q = r belong here, but slope-intercept lines, systems, and the Pythagorean theorem belong to 8th grade.

## Chapter 1: Rational Number Foundations
**Core questions:** How do I compare and order positive and negative rational numbers, and what does absolute value tell me about distance?
**Key concepts:** integers, fractions, and decimals as rational numbers; opposites and additive inverses; absolute value as distance from zero; ordering on the number line; signed numbers in real contexts.
**Applied skills:** placing positives and negatives on a number line; comparing rationals written in different forms; interpreting absolute value as distance; reading signs in context (temperature, elevation, balances).
**Common traps:** thinking a "bigger" negative is larger (−8 > −3); treating absolute value as "make it positive" without the distance idea; comparing fractions and decimals without a common form.

## Chapter 2: Operations With Rational Numbers
**Core questions:** How do I add, subtract, multiply, and divide positive and negative rationals, and how can I check that an answer is reasonable?
**Key concepts:** sign rules for the four operations; subtraction as adding the opposite; the distributive property with signed numbers; converting fractions to terminating or repeating decimals; estimation and reasonableness checks.
**Applied skills:** computing with negative fractions and decimals; solving four-operation word problems; converting between fractions and decimals; estimating before computing.
**Common traps:** "two negatives make a positive" applied to addition; dividing by a fraction instead of multiplying by its reciprocal; dropping a sign mid-problem; reporting an answer that fails an estimate.

## Chapter 3: Ratios, Rates, and Proportional Relationships
**Core questions:** How do I find a unit rate, and how do I decide whether a table, graph, equation, or story is proportional?
**Key concepts:** equivalent ratios; unit rates including complex-fraction rates; proportional tables; graphs that are straight lines through the origin; the constant of proportionality k; equations of the form y = kx.
**Applied skills:** computing unit rates (including rates like 1/2 cup per 1/4 minute); testing tables and graphs for proportionality; identifying and interpreting k in a context; writing y = kx from a situation.
**Common traps:** assuming any straight-line graph is proportional even when it misses the origin; inverting a unit rate (miles per hour vs. hours per mile); reading k as just one ordered pair rather than the constant ratio.

## Chapter 4: Percent and Proportion Problem Solving
**Core questions:** How do I solve percent increase, decrease, tax, tip, discount, markup, and simple-interest problems, and how do I use scale and proportion in real contexts?
**Key concepts:** percent as a rate per 100; percent increase and decrease; tax, tip, discount, markup, markdown; simple interest I = Prt; percent error; scale factor; proportion setups for multistep problems.
**Applied skills:** finding a sale price or final price after tax; comparing two percent changes; computing simple interest; using proportions in recipe, map, and scale problems.
**Common traps:** taking the percent of the wrong base (discounting the original after tax, or vice versa); adding percents that should compound; treating a 20% increase then 20% decrease as returning to the start.

## Chapter 5: Expressions, Equations, and Inequalities
**Core questions:** How do I write equivalent expressions and solve two-step equations and inequalities, and what does the solution mean in context?
**Key concepts:** variables, terms, coefficients, like terms; properties of operations; expanding and factoring linear expressions; equations of the form px + q = r and p(x + q) = r; one- and two-step inequalities and their graphs.
**Applied skills:** combining like terms; expanding and factoring; solving two-step equations and checking the solution; solving and graphing inequalities, including when to reverse the sign.
**Common traps:** distributing to only the first term inside parentheses; doing operations in the wrong order when undoing; forgetting to flip the inequality when multiplying or dividing by a negative.

## Chapter 6: Geometry, Scale, and Measurement
**Core questions:** How do I use scale drawings, angle relationships, and circle, area, and volume formulas to solve practical problems?
**Key concepts:** scale drawings and scale factor; angle relationships (complementary, supplementary, vertical, adjacent); circumference C = πd and area A = πr² of circles; area of composite figures; surface area and volume of prisms; cross-sections of solids.
**Applied skills:** solving scale-drawing and map problems; finding unknown angles from relationships; using circle formulas; computing area, surface area, and volume in context.
**Common traps:** swapping circumference and area formulas (using r vs. d incorrectly); confusing surface area with volume; assuming all angle pairs are supplementary; mishandling units (cm vs. cm² vs. cm³).

## Chapter 7: Statistics, Sampling, and Probability
**Core questions:** When is a sample fair, how do I compare two data sets, and how do I find the probability of simple and compound events?
**Key concepts:** populations vs. samples; random sampling and bias; measures of center and variability; comparing distributions; probability from 0 to 1; experimental vs. theoretical probability; compound events using lists, tables, tree diagrams, and simulations.
**Applied skills:** judging whether a sampling method is fair; comparing two data sets using center and spread; estimating probability from experiments; counting outcomes for compound events.
**Common traps:** trusting a biased (convenience) sample; comparing data sets by a single value while ignoring spread; treating experimental probability as exact theoretical probability; double counting or missing outcomes in compound events.

## Chapter 8: Mathematical Problem-Solving Studio
**Core questions:** How do I read a multistep problem carefully, choose a representation, and revise work when something goes wrong?
**Key concepts:** identifying given and missing information; choosing a representation (diagram, table, equation); working backward; checking units and reasonableness; connecting arithmetic, algebra, geometry, and data.
**Applied skills:** solving mixed multistep problems; spotting missing information; comparing two solution strategies; diagnosing and correcting an error with a clear explanation.
**Common traps:** answering a different question than the one asked; abandoning units until the end; accepting an unreasonable answer because the arithmetic "looked right"; not checking the solution back in the original problem.

## Capstone: Plan and Defend a Real Project
Design and fully solve a realistic planning problem, such as a school event, classroom redesign, fundraiser, game analysis, or local travel plan. The finished work should pull the whole course together:
- Rational-number calculations and at least one proportional-reasoning step (a unit rate, table, or y = kx relationship).
- A percent or scale component (a discount, tax, simple-interest, or scale-drawing calculation) shown with its setup.
- A table or graph that supports a claim, plus a clear written explanation of how the answer was checked for reasonableness.
