# High School Physics
**Covers**: `col-high-school-physics`, `col-high-school-physics---ngss`
**Discipline**: Science · **Year**: 11 (ages 15-16)

## Course Aim
Build a physics course that makes students comfortable moving between the real world, a diagram, a graph, an equation, and a verbal explanation. The goal is conceptual command first, with quantitative fluency layered in through repeated modeling and lab work.

## Course Design Notes
This course should be AP-adjacent without turning into AP Physics. Route questions here when they involve interpreting motion graphs, drawing force diagrams, reasoning about energy or momentum, reading wave and circuit diagrams, or designing simple physical investigations.

## Chapter 1: Physics Practices, Measurement, and Models
- **Key concepts**: Units, vectors, scalars, uncertainty, proportional reasoning, graph interpretation, system definition, and the purpose of idealized models.
- **Practice questions**: Convert and estimate physical quantities; identify independent and dependent variables; match a physical situation to a graph.
- **Student output**: A measurement and modeling notebook page that explains where a simplified model works and where it breaks.

## Chapter 2: Motion in One and Two Dimensions
- **Key concepts**: Position, displacement, velocity, acceleration, motion graphs, constant acceleration equations, projectile motion, and vector components.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret position-time and velocity-time graphs; solve constant-acceleration scenarios; explain projectile motion qualitatively and quantitatively.
- **Student output**: A motion-analysis lab using video, sensor data, or a provided dataset.

## Chapter 3: Forces and Newton's Laws
- **Key concepts**: Free-body diagrams, net force, inertia, mass and weight, friction, tension, normal force, Newton's laws, and equilibrium.
- **Practice questions**: Draw and interpret force diagrams; calculate net force and acceleration; explain why common force misconceptions fail.
- **Student output**: A force-modeling sheet for at least three physical scenarios, with equations matched to diagrams.

## Chapter 4: Work, Energy, and Power
- **Key concepts**: Work, kinetic energy, gravitational potential energy, elastic energy, conservation of energy, energy transfer, power, and efficiency.
- **Practice questions**: Track energy transformations; calculate work or power; choose when energy methods are better than force methods.
- **Student output**: An energy audit of a physical system, including an energy bar chart or flow diagram.

## Chapter 5: Momentum, Impulse, and Collisions
- **Key concepts**: Momentum, impulse, conservation of momentum, elastic and inelastic collisions, explosions, center of mass at an introductory level, and safety applications.
- **Practice questions**: Use impulse-momentum reasoning; solve one-dimensional collision problems; interpret collision data.
- **Student output**: A collision investigation report using carts, video, simulation, or teacher-provided data.

## Chapter 6: Waves, Sound, and Light
- **Key concepts**: Wave speed, frequency, wavelength, amplitude, superposition, sound, resonance, reflection, refraction, lenses, and color at an introductory level.
- **Practice questions**: Calculate wave quantities; read ray diagrams; explain resonance or interference from evidence.
- **Student output**: A wave-model explainer that connects a diagram, equation, and observed phenomenon.

## Chapter 7: Electricity and Magnetism
- **Key concepts**: Charge, electric force, current, voltage, resistance, Ohm's law, series and parallel circuits, power, magnetic fields, and induction qualitatively.
- **Practice questions**: Analyze simple circuits; compare series and parallel arrangements; reason about energy transfer in electrical systems.
- **Student output**: A circuit-design lab with a schematic, predicted measurements, observed measurements, and revision notes.

## Chapter 8: Engineering Physics Studio
- **Key concepts**: Design constraints, tradeoffs, testing, iteration, data-driven redesign, and communicating physical reasoning to non-specialists.
- **Practice questions**: Diagnose a flawed design; choose a measurement plan; defend a tradeoff with physics evidence.
- **Student output**: A final device, model, or analysis project with a test plan and short technical defense.

## Capstone
Students choose a familiar technology, sport, transport system, musical object, or household device and explain it through at least three physics ideas from the course.

## Assessment Ideas
- Whiteboard checks where diagrams, graphs, equations, and words must agree.
- Short lab practicals focused on measurement choices and uncertainty.
- Mixed conceptual and quantitative questions with explanations required.
- A final project defense where students answer "what would change if..." prompts.
