# High School Physics
Move fluently between the real world, a diagram, a graph, an equation, and a clear explanation — and back again.

**ID**: `col-high-school-physics` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
High School Physics builds conceptual command of the mechanical and electromagnetic world, then layers quantitative fluency on top of it. The organizing idea is representational fluency: a student who truly understands a phenomenon can describe it in words, sketch it as a diagram, read it off a graph, capture it in an equation, and predict what changes when a condition changes. Every chapter pushes students to keep those representations in agreement.

The course is mathematically honest but not AP. Algebra and basic trigonometry are the working tools; calculus is not required. Newtonian mechanics forms the spine — kinematics, dynamics, energy, and momentum — and the second half opens out into waves, optics, electricity, and magnetism. Throughout, the emphasis is on modeling: deciding what to keep, what to ignore, and where a simplified model stops being trustworthy.

Lab and investigation work runs alongside the theory. Students collect or interpret real data, reason about measurement uncertainty, and defend conclusions with evidence rather than recalled formulas. The aim is a graduate who can look at an unfamiliar physical situation, choose a useful representation, and explain not just the answer but why a tempting wrong answer is wrong.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve interpreting motion graphs, drawing or reading free-body diagrams, reasoning about energy or momentum conservation, analyzing wave and circuit diagrams, applying Ohm's law and series/parallel rules, or designing and critiquing simple physical investigations. This course is AP-adjacent without being AP: prefer algebra-and-trig solutions, conceptual reasoning, and graph/diagram interpretation over calculus. It covers both the standard high-school physics sequence and NGSS physical-science physics expectations (HS-PS2 forces and interactions, HS-PS3 energy, HS-PS4 waves). Questions should reward students who can explain why a misconception fails, not just identify the correct number.

## Chapter 1: Physics Practices, Measurement, and Models
- **Core questions**: What makes a measurement trustworthy? When is a simplified model good enough, and when does it break? How do units, significant figures, and uncertainty constrain a claim?
- **Key concepts**: SI units, scalars vs. vectors, vector components, dimensional analysis, significant figures, measurement uncertainty, proportional reasoning, idealized models (point mass, frictionless surface), graph interpretation.
- **Applied skills**: Convert and estimate physical quantities; identify independent and dependent variables; match a physical situation to the correct graph shape; state the limits of a model.
- **Common traps**: Treating a vector like a scalar; reporting more precision than the data supports; assuming an idealized model (no friction, no air resistance) describes the real world without saying so.

## Chapter 2: Motion in One Dimension
- **Core questions**: How do position, velocity, and acceleration differ, and how are they linked? What information is hidden in the slope and the area of a motion graph?
- **Key concepts**: Position, displacement, distance, average vs. instantaneous velocity, acceleration, constant-acceleration (kinematic) equations, slope as rate, area under a velocity-time graph as displacement, free fall.
- **Applied skills**: Read position-time and velocity-time graphs; select and apply the right kinematic equation; analyze free-fall problems with g = 9.8 m/s^2.
- **Common traps**: Confusing displacement with distance; thinking zero velocity means zero acceleration (top of a toss); reading the value of a graph when the slope is what matters.

## Chapter 3: Motion in Two Dimensions and Projectiles
- **Core questions**: Why can two-dimensional motion be split into independent horizontal and vertical parts? What stays constant and what changes during a projectile's flight?
- **Key concepts**: Vector addition and resolution, independence of perpendicular components, projectile motion, constant horizontal velocity, vertical free-fall acceleration, range and time of flight, relative velocity (introductory).
- **Applied skills**: Resolve a velocity into components; predict time of flight from the vertical problem alone; explain why a dropped and a horizontally launched ball land together.
- **Common traps**: Believing horizontal motion affects fall time; assuming a projectile's horizontal velocity changes in the absence of air resistance; mixing up which component gravity acts on.

## Chapter 4: Forces and Newton's Laws
- **Core questions**: What does a force actually do? How do free-body diagrams encode the forces on an object, and how does the net force set the acceleration?
- **Key concepts**: Free-body diagrams, net force, inertia, mass vs. weight, Newton's three laws, friction (static and kinetic), tension, normal force, equilibrium, action-reaction pairs.
- **Applied skills**: Draw and interpret free-body diagrams; compute net force and acceleration with F = ma; identify the partner in a Newton's-third-law pair.
- **Common traps**: Thinking motion requires a continuous net force; pairing forces that act on the same object as third-law partners; confusing mass (kg) with weight (N).

## Chapter 5: Work, Energy, and Power
- **Core questions**: When is energy reasoning more powerful than force reasoning? How does energy move and change form without ever being created or destroyed?
- **Key concepts**: Work (W = Fd cos θ), kinetic energy, gravitational potential energy, elastic potential energy, conservation of mechanical energy, energy transfer and dissipation, power, efficiency.
- **Applied skills**: Track energy transformations with bar charts; compute work, kinetic energy, and power; choose energy methods when forces or paths are complicated.
- **Common traps**: Counting work done by a force perpendicular to motion (it is zero); forgetting that friction dissipates energy as heat; treating power and energy as the same quantity.

## Chapter 6: Momentum, Impulse, and Collisions
- **Core questions**: Why is momentum conserved when energy sometimes is not? How does extending the time of a collision reduce the force felt?
- **Key concepts**: Momentum, impulse (J = FΔt = Δp), impulse-momentum theorem, conservation of momentum, elastic vs. inelastic collisions, explosions, safety applications (crumple zones, airbags).
- **Applied skills**: Apply impulse-momentum reasoning; solve one-dimensional collision and explosion problems; explain safety devices in momentum terms.
- **Common traps**: Assuming kinetic energy is conserved in every collision; forgetting momentum is a vector with direction; confusing impulse (a change) with momentum (a state).

## Chapter 7: Waves, Sound, and Light
- **Core questions**: What does a wave transport, and what does it not? How do frequency, wavelength, and speed lock together, and how do waves combine?
- **Key concepts**: Transverse vs. longitudinal waves, amplitude, frequency, wavelength, v = fλ, the wave-speed-depends-on-medium idea, superposition and interference, reflection, refraction, the electromagnetic spectrum, basic optics.
- **Applied skills**: Compute wave quantities with v = fλ; distinguish mechanical from electromagnetic waves; read simple ray diagrams; reason about resonance and interference from evidence.
- **Common traps**: Thinking a wave carries matter rather than energy; assuming changing frequency changes wave speed in a fixed medium; conflating amplitude (loudness/brightness) with frequency (pitch/color).

## Chapter 8: Electricity and Magnetism
- **Core questions**: What drives charge around a circuit, and what resists it? How do series and parallel arrangements differ, and how is electricity tied to magnetism?
- **Key concepts**: Electric charge, Coulomb's law qualitatively, current, voltage, resistance, Ohm's law (V = IR), electrical power (P = IV), series vs. parallel circuits, magnetic fields, electromagnetic induction (qualitative).
- **Applied skills**: Analyze simple circuits with Ohm's law; compare current and voltage in series vs. parallel; reason about electrical power and energy use; describe how a changing magnetic field induces current.
- **Common traps**: Believing current is "used up" as it passes through components; swapping the roles of voltage and current; assuming adding parallel resistors increases total resistance.

## Chapter 9: Engineering Physics Studio
- **Core questions**: How do physics principles turn into a working design? How do you decide which tradeoff is worth making, and how do you back it with evidence?
- **Key concepts**: Design constraints and criteria, tradeoffs, prototyping and testing, iteration, data-driven redesign, communicating physical reasoning to non-specialists.
- **Applied skills**: Diagnose a flawed design from physics; choose a measurement plan; defend a design tradeoff using physics evidence rather than preference.
- **Common traps**: Optimizing one variable while ignoring a coupled one; calling an untested idea a "solution"; presenting a result without quantifying its uncertainty.

## Capstone
Students choose a familiar technology, sport, transport system, musical instrument, or household device and explain how it works through at least three physics ideas drawn from across the course — for example, kinematics, energy conservation, and circuit behavior. The deliverable connects diagrams, graphs, and equations into one coherent explanation, names the simplifying assumptions the analysis relies on, and answers a panel of "what would change if..." questions to demonstrate genuine command rather than recall.

## Assessment Ideas
- Whiteboard checks where diagrams, graphs, equations, and words must all agree before an answer counts.
- Short lab practicals focused on measurement choices, uncertainty, and evidence-based conclusions.
- Mixed conceptual and quantitative questions that require a written justification, not just a number.
- A final project defense where students field "what would change if..." prompts on the spot.
