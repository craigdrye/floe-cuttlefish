# Advanced Ethics & Moral Philosophy
Reason carefully about right and wrong: build, test, and defend moral arguments across the great ethical theories and today's hardest dilemmas.

**ID**: `advancedEthicsMoralPhilosophy` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This senior course develops the ability to reason carefully about moral theory, moral disagreement, and applied ethical problems. Students study the major approaches in normative ethics, metaethics, and public philosophy while practising argument reconstruction, case comparison, principled disagreement, and the written defence of a position. The aim is not to settle which side is correct, but to make moral thinking more precise, more honest, and more answerable to reasons.

Ethics is treated here as disciplined inquiry rather than opinion exchange. Students learn to separate descriptive, normative, conceptual, and empirical claims; to test intuitions against principles and against cases; to compare several frameworks before reaching judgement; and to argue in ways that could be understood by people who do not share all of their starting assumptions. Theory is always paired with application, so that abstract ideas like utility, duty, virtue, and justice are felt as live tools for real decisions.

Applied topics are framed with deliberate balance. The goal is not to manufacture a single class view, but to equip students to state opposing positions charitably, locate the exact point of disagreement, and recognise when a dispute is really about facts, concepts, values, or the meaning of moral language itself. By the end, students can read a moral controversy, reconstruct its strongest arguments, raise a serious objection, answer it, and defend a considered conclusion.

## Course Design Notes
The course is roughly seminar-based and case-driven. Each chapter introduces a framework or domain, then immediately stress-tests it against dilemmas drawn from medicine, technology, justice, and everyday life. Students are expected to apply more than one theory to the same case, because disagreement between frameworks is where the real learning happens. Recurring routines include argument reconstruction (premises, conclusion, objection, reply), charitable summaries of views a student does not hold, and short written defences. Assessment rewards accuracy about what each theory actually claims, fair treatment of opposing views, factual care in applied cases, and the quality of objection-and-reply, not the popularity of a conclusion. Useful anchors include the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, the National High School Ethics Bowl model, and IB Philosophy / AP Seminar argument-analysis practices.

## Chapter 1: Ethical Method and Moral Argument
- **Core questions**: What is the conclusion of this argument, and which premise carries the most weight? When should an intuition be revised rather than defended? How do we tell a moral claim from a factual one?
- **Key concepts**: moral, descriptive, normative, and metaethical claims; validity vs. soundness; premise, conclusion, counterexample; reflective equilibrium; charity in interpretation.
- **Applied skills**: reconstructing a short editorial, speech, or case response into premises, conclusion, objection, and reply; spotting equivocation and question-begging.
- **Common traps**: confusing a strong feeling with a strong argument; treating "is" claims as "ought" claims; calling an argument "true" rather than valid or sound.

## Chapter 2: Consequentialism and Utilitarian Reasoning
- **Core questions**: Which outcomes count, and for whom? Does the theory protect individuals from being sacrificed for aggregate benefit? How should uncertainty change a moral calculation?
- **Key concepts**: consequentialism; act vs. rule utilitarianism; hedonic vs. preference utility; aggregation; impartiality; demandingness objection; expected value.
- **Applied skills**: applying act and rule utilitarian reasoning to two dilemmas (e.g., hospital triage and platform content moderation) and comparing the verdicts.
- **Common traps**: assuming utilitarianism only counts the agent's happiness; ignoring distribution while maximising the total; forgetting that rule utilitarians judge rules, not single acts.

## Chapter 3: Deontology, Rights, and Respect for Persons
- **Core questions**: Could this maxim be universalised without contradiction? Does the action use someone merely as a means? What happens when duties or rights conflict?
- **Key concepts**: deontology; the categorical imperative; Formula of Universal Law; Formula of Humanity; autonomy and dignity; perfect vs. imperfect duties; rights and consent; side constraints.
- **Applied skills**: a structured analysis of a case involving lying, consent, surveillance, academic integrity, or professional confidentiality.
- **Common traps**: confusing the categorical with a merely hypothetical imperative; reading "as a means" (often fine) as "merely as a means" (the violation); treating deontology as the rejection of all consequences.

## Chapter 4: Virtue Ethics, Care Ethics, and Moral Character
- **Core questions**: What would practical wisdom notice that a rule or calculation might miss? When are special obligations to family, friends, or community appropriate? Can character guide action in genuinely hard cases?
- **Key concepts**: virtue and vice; eudaimonia / flourishing; the doctrine of the mean; phronesis (practical wisdom); habituation; moral exemplars; care ethics; relationship, dependency, and partiality.
- **Applied skills**: a comparative response showing how virtue ethics and care ethics reinterpret a dilemma first framed through consequences and rights.
- **Common traps**: treating virtue ethics as having no action guidance; reducing a virtue to a single rule; assuming impartiality is always the moral ideal.

## Chapter 5: Metaethics, Relativism, and Moral Knowledge
- **Core questions**: Does cultural disagreement show that moral truth is relative? Are moral claims more like facts, preferences, commands, or commitments? What could count as evidence in ethics?
- **Key concepts**: moral realism and anti-realism; cognitivism vs. non-cognitivism; subjectivism; cultural relativism; emotivism; error theory; objectivity; moral progress; first-order vs. second-order claims.
- **Applied skills**: a short metaethical essay distinguishing a first-order moral claim from a claim about the status, meaning, or justification of moral claims.
- **Common traps**: inferring relativism directly from disagreement; confusing "people disagree" with "there is no fact"; mixing up describing a culture's values with endorsing them.

## Chapter 6: Bioethics, Personhood, and Medical Decision-Making
- **Core questions**: When may a clinician or family override a patient's choice? What makes a being a person or a rights-holder? How should scarce medical resources be allocated when every option has moral costs?
- **Key concepts**: the four principles (respect for autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice); informed consent and decision-making capacity; paternalism; confidentiality; personhood and moral status; the doctrine of double effect.
- **Applied skills**: a policy memo on a case such as organ allocation, treatment refusal, end-of-life care, reproductive ethics, genetic selection, or disability ethics.
- **Common traps**: collapsing beneficence and non-maleficence; treating autonomy as the only principle that matters; misusing double effect to excuse a harm that was actually intended.

## Chapter 7: Justice, Power, and Public Ethics
- **Core questions**: When does inequality become injustice? What do citizens owe strangers, future people, or those outside their nation? Can a policy be justified to people with different moral or political commitments?
- **Key concepts**: distributive justice; equality, equity, sufficiency, desert; liberty and the harm principle; Rawls's veil of ignorance and difference principle; punishment and civil disobedience; global justice; public reason.
- **Applied skills**: a public-reason brief evaluating a school, city, national, or global policy using at least three principles of justice plus one serious objection.
- **Common traps**: equating any inequality with injustice (or denying any can be unjust); using the harm principle so loosely that "offence" counts as "harm"; smuggling in a contested premise as if it were neutral.

## Chapter 8: Environmental, Animal, Technology, and Media Ethics
- **Core questions**: Do animals, ecosystems, or future people have claims on us? Who is responsible when an automated system causes harm? How should freedom, safety, truth, and dignity be balanced online?
- **Key concepts**: moral standing and sentience; speciesism and animal welfare; anthropocentrism; climate justice and obligations to future generations; privacy; algorithmic bias; the responsibility gap; misinformation.
- **Applied skills**: an ethics-review report for an applied case such as geoengineering, school AI policy, autonomous weapons, risk-scoring algorithms, plant-based defaults, or recommendation systems.
- **Common traps**: treating "natural" as automatically "good"; assuming only humans can have interests; blaming a tool while ignoring the humans who designed, deployed, and profited from it.

## Capstone
Students complete an original ethics inquiry in one of three forms: an Ethics Bowl case packet, a philosophical position paper, or a policy recommendation memo. Each capstone must include a clearly stated question, the relevant background facts, at least two normative theories, at least one metaethical or conceptual issue where relevant, a serious objection, a reply, and an annotated source list. Students present their work in a seminar defence and revise after peer questioning. Grading rewards research quality, philosophical depth, the strength of the objection-and-reply, factual and framework accuracy, fairness to opposing views, and the quality of revision after challenge.
