# Advanced Ethics & Moral Philosophy
**ID**: `advancedEthicsMoralPhilosophy` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 12 (ages 16–18)

## Course Philosophy
Building upon introductory ethics, this course examines complex meta-ethics, applied bioethics, and contemporary moral dilemmas. It challenges students to rigorously defend their moral intuitions using formal philosophical frameworks.

## Module 1: Meta-Ethics (20%)
- What is the nature of moral language?
- Moral Realism/Cognitivism: Moral statements express propositions that can be true or false (Naturalism vs Non-Naturalism/Intuitionism)
- Moral Anti-Realism/Non-Cognitivism: Moral statements do not express objective truths (Emotivism "Boo/Hurrah theory", Prescriptivism, Error Theory)
- G.E. Moore's "Open Question Argument" and the Naturalistic Fallacy
- **Questions**: Defending moral realism against emotivist critiques, explaining the Naturalistic Fallacy

## Module 2: Advanced Normative Ethics (20%)
- Kantian Deontology: The second formulation of the Categorical Imperative (humanity as an end in itself); perfect vs imperfect duties
- Utilitarianism: Act vs Rule Utilitarianism (Bentham vs Mill); Preference Utilitarianism (Peter Singer)
- Virtue Ethics: Neo-Aristotelian approaches (Philippa Foot, Rosalind Hursthouse); the problem of action-guidance
- Contractarianism: Thomas Hobbes and David Gauthier (morality as mutual self-interest)
- **Questions**: Comparing Act and Rule utilitarian outcomes in the Trolley Problem, evaluating the action-guidance critique of Virtue Ethics

## Module 3: Bioethics & Medical Ethics (20%)
- The Four Principles of Biomedical Ethics: Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence, Justice
- Euthanasia and Physician-Assisted Suicide: Active vs Passive, Voluntary vs Non-voluntary; the Doctrine of Double Effect
- Abortion: The moral status of the fetus, bodily autonomy (Judith Jarvis Thomson's Violinist thought experiment), the "Future Like Ours" argument (Don Marquis)
- Resource allocation (triage) and the ethics of organ transplantation
- **Questions**: Applying the Doctrine of Double Effect to end-of-life care, evaluating Thomson's violinist analogy

## Module 4: Environmental and Animal Ethics (20%)
- Animal Rights: Peter Singer's *Animal Liberation* (Speciesism, Utilitarian calculus applied to animals), Tom Regan's Rights approach (subjects-of-a-life)
- Anthropocentrism vs Biocentrism vs Ecocentrism (Aldo Leopold's Land Ethic)
- Climate Change Ethics: Intergenerational justice, historical responsibility vs capacity to pay
- **Questions**: Arguing for/against vegetarianism using Singer's utilitarianism, evaluating the moral obligations to future generations

## Module 5: Justice, Tech, and Modern Dilemmas (20%)
- Global Justice: Peter Singer's "Famine, Affluence, and Morality" (the drowning child analogy); Cosmopolitanism vs Communitarianism
- Just War Theory: *Jus ad bellum* (justice in resorting to war), *jus in bello* (justice in the conduct of war), *jus post bellum*
- Ethics of AI and Technology: Algorithmic bias, data privacy, autonomous weapons systems, and the "Alignment Problem"
- **Questions**: Applying Just War Theory to modern asymmetrical warfare, evaluating the ethical framework for programming a self-driving car

## Stretch Zone
- Feminist Ethics and the Ethics of Care (Carol Gilligan, Nel Noddings)
- Moral Luck and Determinism (Do we have free will, and does it matter for moral responsibility?)
