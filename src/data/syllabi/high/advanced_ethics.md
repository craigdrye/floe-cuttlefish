# Advanced Ethics & Moral Philosophy
**ID**: `advancedEthicsMoralPhilosophy` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: 12 (ages 16-18)

## Course Aim
Develop senior students' ability to reason carefully about moral theory, moral disagreement, and applied ethical problems. Students study major approaches in normative ethics, metaethics, and public philosophy while practicing argument reconstruction, case comparison, principled disagreement, and written defense of a position.

## Course Design Notes
This course treats ethics as disciplined inquiry rather than opinion exchange. Students learn to separate descriptive, normative, conceptual, and empirical claims; test intuitions against principles and cases; compare multiple frameworks before reaching judgment; and argue in ways that could be understood by people who do not share all of their assumptions. Applied topics are framed with political balance: the aim is not to produce one class view, but to make disagreements more precise, informed, and fair.

## Chapter 1: Ethical Method and Moral Argument
- **Key concepts**: moral claim, descriptive claim, normative claim, metaethical claim, validity, soundness, premise, conclusion, counterexample, reflective equilibrium.
- **Practice questions**: What is the argument's conclusion? Which premise carries the most weight? When should an intuition be revised rather than defended?
- **Student output**: an argument reconstruction that turns a short editorial, speech, or case response into premises, conclusion, objection, and reply.

## Chapter 2: Consequentialism and Utilitarian Reasoning
- **Key concepts**: consequentialism, act utilitarianism, rule utilitarianism, preference satisfaction, utility, aggregation, demandingness, side constraint, expected value.
- **Practice questions**: Which outcomes count, and for whom? Does the theory protect individuals from being sacrificed for aggregate benefit? How should uncertainty affect moral calculation?
- **Student output**: a case comparison applying act and rule utilitarian reasoning to two dilemmas, such as hospital triage and platform content moderation.

## Chapter 3: Deontology, Rights, and Respect for Persons
- **Key concepts**: deontology, duty, categorical imperative, universalizability, humanity formulation, autonomy, dignity, right, consent, perfect and imperfect duty.
- **Practice questions**: Could the proposed rule be universalized without contradiction? Does the action use someone merely as a means? What happens when rights or duties conflict?
- **Student output**: a structured analysis of a case involving lying, consent, surveillance, academic integrity, or professional confidentiality.

## Chapter 4: Virtue Ethics, Care Ethics, and Moral Character
- **Key concepts**: virtue, vice, flourishing, practical wisdom, moral exemplar, habituation, care ethics, dependency, attentiveness, relationship, partiality.
- **Practice questions**: What would practical wisdom notice that a rule or calculation might miss? When are special obligations to family, friends, or community morally appropriate? Can character guide action in hard cases?
- **Student output**: a comparative response explaining how virtue ethics and care ethics reinterpret a dilemma first framed through consequences and rights.

## Chapter 5: Metaethics, Relativism, and Moral Knowledge
- **Key concepts**: moral realism, anti-realism, subjectivism, relativism, error theory, cognitivism, non-cognitivism, moral disagreement, objectivity, moral progress.
- **Practice questions**: Does cultural disagreement show that moral truth is relative? Are moral claims more like facts, preferences, commands, or commitments? What would count as evidence in ethics?
- **Student output**: a short metaethical essay that distinguishes a first-order moral claim from a claim about the status, meaning, or justification of moral claims.

## Chapter 6: Bioethics, Personhood, and Medical Decision-Making
- **Key concepts**: autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, informed consent, capacity, paternalism, confidentiality, personhood, moral status, double effect.
- **Practice questions**: When may a doctor or family override a patient's choice? What makes a being a person or a rights-holder? How should scarce medical resources be allocated when every option has moral costs?
- **Student output**: a policy memo on a medical case, such as organ allocation, treatment refusal for a minor, end-of-life care, reproductive ethics, genetic selection, or disability ethics.

## Chapter 7: Justice, Power, and Public Ethics
- **Key concepts**: distributive justice, equality, equity, sufficiency, desert, liberty, harm principle, paternalism, punishment, civil disobedience, global justice, public reason.
- **Practice questions**: When does inequality become injustice? What should citizens owe strangers, future citizens, or people outside their nation? Can a policy be justified to people with different moral or political commitments?
- **Student output**: a public reason brief that evaluates a school, city, national, or global policy using at least three principles of justice and one serious objection.

## Chapter 8: Environmental, Animal, Technology, and Media Ethics
- **Key concepts**: moral standing, sentience, speciesism, animal welfare, anthropocentrism, climate justice, future generations, privacy, algorithmic bias, responsibility gap, misinformation.
- **Practice questions**: Do animals, ecosystems, or future people have claims on us? Who is responsible when an automated system causes harm? How should freedom, safety, truth, and dignity be balanced in digital spaces?
- **Student output**: an ethics review report for an applied case, such as geoengineering research, school AI policies, autonomous weapons, risk assessment algorithms, plant-based defaults, or platform recommendation systems.

## Capstone
Students complete an original ethics inquiry in one of three forms: an Ethics Bowl case packet, a philosophical position paper, or a policy recommendation memo. Each capstone must include a clear question, relevant background facts, at least two normative theories, at least one metaethical or conceptual issue where relevant, a serious objection, a reply, and an annotated source list. Students present their work in a seminar defense and revise after peer questioning.

## Assessment Ideas
- Argument reconstruction quizzes on premises, conclusions, assumptions, counterexamples, and objections.
- Seminar preparation notes that include a charitable summary of a view the student does not initially accept.
- Theory comparison papers applying consequentialist, deontological, virtue, care, and justice-based reasoning to the same case.
- Applied ethics memos graded for stakeholder analysis, factual clarity, framework accuracy, political balance, and practical recommendation.
- Metaethics reflection assessing whether a disagreement is factual, conceptual, normative, or about moral language itself.
- Capstone assessment based on research quality, philosophical depth, objection and reply, oral defense, and revision.

## Research Notes
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Consequentialism": background on outcome-based moral theories, aggregation, and objections. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consequentialism/
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Deontological Ethics": background on duty-based ethics, constraints, permissions, and rights. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ethics-deontological/
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Virtue Ethics": background on character, flourishing, practical wisdom, and action guidance. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ethics-virtue/
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Feminist Ethics": includes care ethics, relational responsibility, and critiques of overly abstract impartiality. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/feminism-ethics/
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Moral Realism": supports the metaethics chapter on truth, objectivity, and disagreement. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/moral-realism/
- Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Ethics": broad overview of metaethics, normative ethics, and applied ethics for course framing. https://iep.utm.edu/ethics/
- Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Biomedical Ethics": useful overview of autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice, and major medical cases. https://iep.utm.edu/bioethic/
- PLATO, "Teaching High School Philosophy": supports seminar-based high-school philosophy, argument evaluation, and inquiry-centered classroom practice. https://plato-apa.org/getting-started/teaching-high-school-philosophy/
- National High School Ethics Bowl, "How Ethics Bowl Works": useful model for collaborative case analysis and respectful moral disagreement. https://nhseb.org/how
- International Baccalaureate Diploma Programme Philosophy Guide: relevant for ethics, applied ethics, and philosophy assessment models at the upper-secondary level. https://dl.ibdocs.re/IB%20SUBJECT%20GUIDES/Group%203%20-%20Individuals%20and%20Societies/Philosophy/Philosophy%20Guide%202016%20-%20English.pdf
- College Board, AP Seminar course page: useful for argument analysis, evidence evaluation, and presentation skills adjacent to advanced ethics work. https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/courses/ap-seminar
