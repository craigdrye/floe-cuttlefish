# Finance and Capital Markets
**ID**: `financeAndCapitalMarkets` - **Discipline**: Economics/Business - **Year**: 11 (ages 15-16)

## Course Aim
Build practical financial literacy by helping students understand how saving, borrowing, investing, markets, institutions, risk, and regulation fit together. Students learn to ask informed questions, read basic financial information, evaluate tradeoffs, and avoid hype, scams, and oversimplified promises.

## Course Design Notes
Keep the course balanced, evidence-based, and non-promotional. The goal is not to recommend securities or simulate get-rich-quick trading, but to help students understand financial decisions, consumer protection, market structure, and the role of capital markets in households, businesses, and the wider economy. Route questions here when they involve interest, inflation, credit, bonds, stocks, funds, diversification, retirement accounts, financial statements, market indexes, regulation, behavioral finance, or investor education.

## Chapter 1: Money, Goals, Budgets, and Financial Choices
- **Key concepts**: income, expense, saving, spending plan, emergency fund, opportunity cost, tradeoff, needs and wants, liquidity, financial goal, and net worth.
- **Practice questions**: What tradeoff is being made in this budget? How much should be set aside for a short-term goal? Which choice improves flexibility if income changes?
- **Student output**: A personal finance scenario budget with goals, constraints, tradeoffs, and a short reflection on what information is missing.

## Chapter 2: Interest, Inflation, and the Time Value of Money
- **Key concepts**: simple interest, compound interest, annual percentage yield, annual percentage rate, present value, future value, inflation, purchasing power, real return, nominal return, and Rule of 72.
- **Practice questions**: How does compounding change over time? What is the difference between nominal and real growth? How long might money take to double at a given rate?
- **Student output**: A time-value comparison showing two savings or borrowing scenarios with calculations, graphs, and an explanation in everyday language.

## Chapter 3: Credit, Debt, and Consumer Protection
- **Key concepts**: credit score, credit report, loan, principal, interest rate, minimum payment, amortization, collateral, delinquency, default, fee, fraud, disclosure, and consumer rights.
- **Practice questions**: What is the true cost of paying only the minimum? Which loan term is more expensive over time? What warning signs suggest a financial product or offer may be unsafe?
- **Student output**: A credit decision memo comparing two realistic borrowing options and identifying risks, protections, and questions to ask before signing.

## Chapter 4: Banks, the Federal Reserve, and the Financial System
- **Key concepts**: bank, credit union, deposit, FDIC or NCUA insurance, central bank, Federal Reserve, money supply, interest rate target, monetary policy, inflation, recession, and financial stability.
- **Practice questions**: How do banks connect savers and borrowers? Why might the Federal Reserve raise or lower interest rates? How can a rate change affect households and businesses differently?
- **Student output**: A financial system map showing households, firms, banks, markets, regulators, and the Federal Reserve, with arrows for money, credit, and risk.

## Chapter 5: Bonds and Fixed Income
- **Key concepts**: bond, issuer, principal, coupon, maturity, yield, price, Treasury bond, municipal bond, corporate bond, credit rating, default risk, interest rate risk, and current yield.
- **Practice questions**: Why do bond prices usually fall when interest rates rise? How does credit risk affect yield? What is different about lending to a government, city, or company?
- **Student output**: A bond fact sheet comparing three bond types by issuer, risk, yield, maturity, and possible use in a diversified plan.

## Chapter 6: Stocks, Ownership, and Public Markets
- **Key concepts**: stock, share, ownership, dividend, capital gain, initial public offering, secondary market, exchange, market capitalization, earnings per share, price-to-earnings ratio, index, bull market, and bear market.
- **Practice questions**: What does a shareholder own? How is an IPO different from trading shares later? What can and cannot be learned from a P/E ratio or market index?
- **Student output**: A company and index explainer that interprets basic public information without making a buy, sell, or hold recommendation.

## Chapter 7: Funds, Diversification, and Long-Term Planning
- **Key concepts**: diversification, risk and return, volatility, mutual fund, exchange-traded fund, index fund, expense ratio, asset allocation, rebalancing, tax-advantaged account, 401(k), traditional IRA, and Roth IRA.
- **Practice questions**: Why can owning many securities reduce some risks but not all risks? How do fees affect long-term outcomes? How do traditional and Roth accounts differ in tax timing?
- **Student output**: A diversified portfolio concept for a fictional person, including goals, time horizon, risk tolerance, fees, and reasons for not relying on one investment.

## Chapter 8: Behavioral Finance, Crises, Scams, and Market Ethics
- **Key concepts**: loss aversion, confirmation bias, anchoring, herd behavior, overconfidence, bubble, leverage, liquidity crisis, systemic risk, disclosure, fiduciary duty, suitability, pump and dump, and affinity fraud.
- **Practice questions**: Which bias is shaping this decision? What separates normal investment risk from misleading promotion? What lessons can be drawn from the dot-com bubble or the 2008 financial crisis without pretending history repeats exactly?
- **Student output**: An investor warning brief that analyzes a realistic financial claim, identifies red flags, checks reliable sources, and recommends questions a cautious consumer should ask.

## Capstone
Students complete a financial literacy case study for a fictional household, young worker, club, or small business. The final product should include a budget or cash-flow view, a credit or borrowing decision, an explanation of interest and inflation, a comparison of saving and investment options, a risk discussion, and a clear statement of what the analysis can and cannot conclude.

## Assessment Ideas
- Calculation checks on interest, compounding, inflation, yields, fees, and basic ratios.
- Scenario responses that require students to explain tradeoffs, risk, consumer protections, and missing information.
- Source-quality tasks using regulator and investor education websites rather than social media claims.
- Capstone rubric covering accuracy, practicality, balanced reasoning, clarity of communication, and avoidance of unsupported recommendations.
