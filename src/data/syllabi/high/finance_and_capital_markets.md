# Finance and Capital Markets
How saving, borrowing, investing, markets, and regulation fit together — and how to make informed financial decisions without falling for hype.

**ID**: `financeAndCapitalMarkets` · **Discipline**: Economics/Business

## Course Aim
This course builds practical financial literacy for high-school students by showing how households, businesses, banks, markets, and regulators are connected through the flow of money, credit, and risk. Students learn to read basic financial information, work through interest and inflation calculations, weigh tradeoffs, and ask the kinds of questions a careful consumer or citizen should ask before making a financial decision.

The emphasis is on understanding, not speculation. The course does not recommend securities, simulate get-rich-quick trading, or promise outcomes. Instead it explains why bonds, stocks, and funds behave the way they do, what diversification can and cannot accomplish, how the Federal Reserve and deposit insurance support financial stability, and why disclosure, fiduciary duty, and consumer protection exist. Throughout, students practice separating evidence from marketing.

By the end, students should be able to budget toward a goal, compare borrowing options, estimate the real value of money over time, interpret a P/E ratio or market index without overreading it, distinguish ordinary investment risk from fraud, and explain what their own analysis can and cannot conclude. These habits transfer to college, work, and adult financial life.

## Course Design Notes
Keep the course balanced, evidence-based, and non-promotional. Route questions here when they involve interest, inflation, credit, bonds, stocks, funds, diversification, retirement accounts, financial statements, market indexes, regulation, behavioral finance, or investor education. Anchor factual claims in regulator and investor-education sources (SEC Investor.gov, the FINRA Investor Education Foundation, the Federal Reserve, and Federal Reserve Bank education programs) rather than social media or promotional material.

Calculations stay at a high-school level: simple and compound interest, the Rule of 72, present and future value reasoning, real versus nominal returns, current yield, and basic ratios such as price-to-earnings. Conceptual questions reward students for naming the missing information and stating tradeoffs. The course deliberately reuses US institutions (FDIC, the Federal Reserve, the SEC, IRA and 401(k) accounts) as concrete examples while keeping the underlying ideas portable. Assessment favors scenario reasoning, calculation checks, and source-quality tasks over rote definitions, and every applied task ends with a clear statement of what the analysis cannot prove.

## Chapter 1: Money, Goals, Budgets, and Financial Choices
- **Core questions**: What tradeoff is a given budget making? How much belongs in a short-term goal versus an emergency cushion? Which choice protects flexibility if income changes?
- **Key concepts**: income, expense, saving, spending plan, emergency fund, opportunity cost, tradeoff, needs versus wants, liquidity, financial goal, net worth.
- **Applied skills**: Build a scenario budget with explicit goals and constraints; calculate net worth from assets and liabilities; identify the opportunity cost of a spending choice.
- **Common traps**: Treating wants as needs; ignoring opportunity cost; confusing a high income with high net worth; planning with no buffer for income shocks.

## Chapter 2: Interest, Inflation, and the Time Value of Money
- **Core questions**: How does compounding change outcomes over time? What separates nominal from real growth? How long does money take to double at a given rate?
- **Key concepts**: simple interest, compound interest, APY, APR, present value, future value, inflation, purchasing power, real return, nominal return, Rule of 72.
- **Applied skills**: Apply the Rule of 72 to estimate doubling time; compute future value with annual compounding; convert a nominal return to a real return by subtracting inflation.
- **Common traps**: Adding interest linearly when it compounds; confusing APR with APY; forgetting that inflation erodes purchasing power; reading nominal gains as real gains.

## Chapter 3: Credit, Debt, and Consumer Protection
- **Core questions**: What is the true cost of paying only the minimum? Which loan term costs more over its life? What warning signs suggest a product or offer is unsafe?
- **Key concepts**: credit score, credit report, principal, interest rate, minimum payment, amortization, collateral, delinquency, default, fee, disclosure, consumer rights.
- **Applied skills**: Compare two loan offers by total interest paid; read the difference between a credit report and a credit score; spot required disclosures and red flags before signing.
- **Common traps**: Equating the minimum payment with the cheapest path; assuming a lower monthly payment means less total cost; confusing report with score; ignoring fees buried in the APR.

## Chapter 4: Banks, the Federal Reserve, and the Financial System
- **Core questions**: How do banks connect savers and borrowers? Why might the Federal Reserve raise or lower interest rates? How can one rate change affect households and businesses differently?
- **Key concepts**: bank, credit union, deposit, FDIC and NCUA insurance, central bank, Federal Reserve, money supply, interest-rate target, monetary policy, recession, financial stability.
- **Applied skills**: Map the flow of money, credit, and risk among households, firms, banks, markets, and regulators; explain how a rate change transmits to borrowing and saving; describe what deposit insurance does and does not cover.
- **Common traps**: Thinking deposit insurance covers investments like stocks; assuming the Fed sets every interest rate directly; confusing fiscal with monetary policy; treating a rate cut as automatically good for everyone.

## Chapter 5: Bonds and Fixed Income
- **Core questions**: Why do bond prices usually fall when interest rates rise? How does credit risk shape yield? What differs about lending to a government, a city, or a company?
- **Key concepts**: bond, issuer, principal, coupon, maturity, yield, price, Treasury bond, municipal bond, corporate bond, credit rating, default risk, interest-rate risk, current yield.
- **Applied skills**: Compute current yield from coupon and price; rank three bond types by risk and yield; explain the inverse relationship between price and yield in plain language.
- **Common traps**: Assuming bonds cannot lose value; thinking a higher yield is always better without weighing default risk; ignoring interest-rate risk for long maturities; treating a credit rating as a guarantee.

## Chapter 6: Stocks, Ownership, and Public Markets
- **Core questions**: What does a shareholder actually own? How is an IPO different from later trading? What can — and cannot — be learned from a P/E ratio or a market index?
- **Key concepts**: stock, share, ownership, dividend, capital gain, IPO, secondary market, exchange, market capitalization, earnings per share, price-to-earnings ratio, index, bull and bear markets.
- **Applied skills**: Compute market capitalization and a P/E ratio; distinguish primary issuance from secondary trading; interpret an index level without inferring a recommendation.
- **Common traps**: Believing a high P/E always means overvalued; thinking the company gets cash every time its shares trade; confusing share price with company size; reading a rising index as proof every stock rose.

## Chapter 7: Funds, Diversification, and Long-Term Planning
- **Core questions**: Why can owning many securities reduce some risk but not all of it? How do fees compound against long-term outcomes? How do traditional and Roth accounts differ in tax timing?
- **Key concepts**: diversification, risk and return, volatility, mutual fund, ETF, index fund, expense ratio, asset allocation, rebalancing, tax-advantaged account, 401(k), traditional IRA, Roth IRA.
- **Applied skills**: Explain diversifiable versus market risk; estimate how an expense ratio erodes returns over time; compare the tax timing of traditional and Roth accounts.
- **Common traps**: Believing diversification removes all risk; ignoring expense ratios because they look small; assuming an index fund is risk-free; thinking Roth and traditional are taxed the same way.

## Chapter 8: Behavioral Finance, Crises, Scams, and Market Ethics
- **Core questions**: Which bias is shaping a given decision? What separates ordinary investment risk from misleading promotion? What lessons hold from the dot-com bubble or the 2008 crisis without pretending history repeats exactly?
- **Key concepts**: loss aversion, confirmation bias, anchoring, herd behavior, overconfidence, bubble, leverage, liquidity crisis, systemic risk, disclosure, fiduciary duty, suitability, pump and dump, affinity fraud.
- **Applied skills**: Name the bias at work in a scenario; flag red flags such as guaranteed high returns or pressure to act fast; check a claim against a regulator source like SEC Investor.gov.
- **Common traps**: Mistaking confidence for evidence; assuming high past returns guarantee future ones; trusting a pitch because it comes from inside a community; confusing a registered, risky product with an outright scam.

## Capstone
Students complete a financial-literacy case study for a fictional household, young worker, club, or small business. The final product should include a budget or cash-flow view, a credit or borrowing decision, an explanation of interest and inflation, a comparison of saving and investing options, a risk discussion, and a clear statement of what the analysis can and cannot conclude. Strong capstones cite at least one regulator or investor-education source, show at least one worked calculation (interest, real return, yield, or a basic ratio), and make no buy, sell, or hold recommendation.
