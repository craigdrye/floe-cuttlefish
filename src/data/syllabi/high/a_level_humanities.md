# A-Level Humanities & Computing
Source evaluation, comparative argument, and extended essay craft for advanced UK pre-university study across history, literature, philosophy, religion, and public ideas.

**ID**: `alevelHistory` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
Prepare advanced pre-university students for humanities study through rigorous reading, source evaluation, comparative argument, extended essay writing, and disciplined independent research. Students learn to move confidently between history, literature, religious studies, philosophy, and public reasoning while building the analytical habits expected in UK A-level work across AQA, OCR, and Pearson Edexcel specifications.

This syllabus is a humanities refit of an older combined A-level file. It serves the family of UK A-level humanities tracks — History, English Literature, Economics, and Computer Science argumentation — but its learning spine is the transferable skill set those subjects share: weighing evidence, judging provenance, comparing interpretations, and constructing precise, well-supported written responses under both coursework and timed conditions.

Throughout, students handle unfamiliar material rather than memorised summaries. They practise the moves examiners reward: stating a sharp thesis, selecting evidence that actually proves the claim, engaging genuinely with counterargument and scholarly interpretation, and sustaining analytical control across a long answer. The aim is not coverage of one syllabus but mastery of the reasoning that makes any humanities essay strong.

## Course Design Notes
The course is skills-first and discipline-spanning. Each chapter foregrounds a method — argument, source work, periodisation, close reading, comparison, ethical reasoning, public argument, research — and grounds it in concrete material a 16-to-18-year-old can analyse without specialist prior study. Worked exemplars, annotation codes, and timed-writing drills run alongside conceptual teaching, mirroring the AQA/OCR/Edexcel emphasis on evaluating interpretations, comparing texts or arguments, and writing under pressure. Where the older file blurred subjects together, this version keeps the disciplinary distinctions sharp while teaching the common analytical engine. It is age-appropriate for the high-school tier: demanding in reasoning, but built on accessible sources rather than university-level reading lists.

## Chapter 1: Humanities Methods and Academic Argument
- **Core questions**: What is the precise question being asked? Which evidence actually proves the claim? How should a strong counterargument change the line of reasoning?
- **Key concepts**: claim, evidence, warrant, thesis, interpretation, context, comparison, evaluation, counterargument, scholarly convention.
- **Applied skills**: write a single-sentence thesis that takes a defensible position; build a paragraph as claim-evidence-explanation-link; integrate and rebut a counterargument; annotate a source for relevance, not just content.
- **Common traps**: describing instead of arguing; listing evidence without a warrant connecting it to the claim; treating a counterargument as a concession rather than something to answer; a thesis so broad it cannot be proved.

## Chapter 2: Historical Sources, Context, and Interpretation
- **Core questions**: How useful is this source for the enquiry? What does the source reveal unintentionally? How far does one historian's interpretation explain the evidence?
- **Key concepts**: primary source, secondary interpretation, provenance, utility, reliability, representativeness, corroboration, nature-origin-purpose.
- **Applied skills**: weigh utility against reliability for a stated enquiry; read provenance (who, when, why) before content; corroborate or challenge one source with another; judge how convincingly a historian's interpretation fits the evidence.
- **Common traps**: confusing reliability with usefulness; dismissing a biased source as worthless when bias can itself be evidence; evaluating a source in the abstract rather than against the specific enquiry; summarising a historian instead of testing them.

## Chapter 3: Breadth, Periodisation, and Comparative History
- **Core questions**: Where should the period begin and end, and why? Which change was most significant? How do two societies reveal similar pressures but different outcomes?
- **Key concepts**: periodisation, turning point, long-term cause, short-term trigger, continuity, change, significance, comparison.
- **Applied skills**: justify the boundaries of a period; distinguish long-term conditions from immediate triggers; rank causes by significance with criteria; structure a comparison around shared themes rather than parallel narration.
- **Common traps**: assuming a famous date is automatically the turning point; confusing what happened first with what mattered most; comparing two cases side by side without ever connecting them; mistaking continuity for the absence of change.

## Chapter 4: Literary Form, Genre, and Close Reading
- **Core questions**: How does form shape meaning? What changes when a text is staged, spoken, or read silently? Which critical interpretation best accounts for the language?
- **Key concepts**: genre, form, structure, narrative voice, dramatic method, imagery, metre, ambiguity, critical lens, reception.
- **Applied skills**: link a specific formal choice to an effect on meaning; analyse how structure or voice positions the reader; quote precisely and embed evidence; weigh a critical reading against the text rather than accepting it.
- **Common traps**: feature-spotting (naming a device without explaining its effect); paraphrasing the plot instead of analysing the writing; treating one critical interpretation as the single correct answer; ignoring how form, not just content, carries meaning.

## Chapter 5: Comparative Literature and Context
- **Core questions**: How do two texts treat the same human problem differently? When does context illuminate rather than replace textual analysis? What makes a comparison genuinely analytical?
- **Key concepts**: intertextuality, theme, context, canon, adaptation, gender, class, power, critical debate.
- **Applied skills**: build a comparison around a shared idea, not a shared topic; use context to deepen a reading rather than as biography or background filler; trace how two writers' methods produce different meanings; weave critical views into a comparative argument.
- **Common traps**: writing two separate essays joined by "similarly"; letting historical context crowd out analysis of the writing; comparing surface similarities while missing the more revealing differences; name-dropping critics without using them.

## Chapter 6: Religion, Ethics, and Philosophical Reasoning
- **Core questions**: Is the argument valid, persuasive, or both? What assumptions about human nature are being made? How would two ethical theories judge the same case?
- **Key concepts**: argument, validity, soundness, premise, conclusion, theodicy, virtue ethics, deontology, utilitarianism, applied ethics.
- **Applied skills**: separate validity (logical form) from soundness (true premises); reconstruct an argument's hidden assumptions; apply rival ethical theories to one concrete dilemma; evaluate the strengths and limits of a position rather than just stating it.
- **Common traps**: confusing a valid argument with a true conclusion; calling an argument "wrong" without identifying the faulty premise or step; treating ethical theories as opinions rather than reasoned frameworks; asserting a view without testing the strongest objection.

## Chapter 7: Politics, Economics, and Public Ideas
- **Core questions**: How does a thinker define freedom or justice? What evidence supports a policy claim? How do economic assumptions shape a political argument?
- **Key concepts**: ideology, liberalism, conservatism, socialism, rights, market, opportunity cost, inequality, public policy, rhetoric.
- **Applied skills**: identify the value a public argument rests on; test a policy claim against its evidence; expose the economic assumptions inside a political position; distinguish persuasive rhetoric from sound reasoning.
- **Common traps**: mistaking a confident tone for strong evidence; treating contested terms like "freedom" or "fair" as if their meaning were obvious; accepting a statistic without asking what it measures; conflating what is popular with what is justified.

## Chapter 8: Independent Research and Exam Studio
- **Core questions**: Is the research question narrow enough to answer? Which sources deserve most weight? How can a timed essay stay analytical under pressure?
- **Key concepts**: research question, bibliography, historiography, methodology, quotation, citation, synthesis, timed writing, reflection.
- **Applied skills**: narrow a broad topic into an answerable question; build and annotate a bibliography; cite primary and secondary material accurately; plan and write an analytical essay to time without drifting into narrative.
- **Common traps**: choosing a research question too broad to answer in the word count; collecting sources without weighing their authority; quoting at length instead of analysing; spending so long planning that the timed essay is rushed and unsupported.

## Capstone
Produce a pre-university humanities portfolio built around one broad problem — such as revolution, freedom, belief, identity, empire, technology, justice, or memory. The portfolio should include one historical source analysis (with provenance and corroboration), one literary comparison, one philosophy or religious-studies argument that tests a counterposition, one public-ideas briefing that audits the evidence behind a claim, and a 2,500-3,500 word research essay using properly cited primary and secondary material. Students present one strand to peers and revise it after challenge.

## Assessment Ideas
- Source responses graded on provenance, context, utility, limitation, corroboration, and judgement.
- Essays graded on thesis precision, conceptual control, evidence selection, comparative structure, critical engagement, and written fluency.
- Seminars graded on preparation, listening, questioning, and the ability to refine a claim after challenge.
- Capstone graded on research design, source range, disciplinary comparison, citation practice, and quality of revision.
