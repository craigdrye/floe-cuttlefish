# College Admissions
Build the judgment, the list, and the paperwork to apply to college with less panic and more control.

**ID**: `collegeAdmissions` · **Discipline**: College Prep

## Course Aim
College admissions rewards students who treat it as a project they manage, not a verdict they wait for. This course helps high-school juniors and seniors move from anxiety to usable judgment: deciding what they actually need from a college, building a list that is balanced rather than aspirational, and executing the application without missing the deadlines and money that quietly decide outcomes.

The course is U.S.-centered and grounded in current official guidance from the Common App and Federal Student Aid, with comparison notes for transfer, international, portfolio, and athletic paths and a short look at the U.K. UCAS system. It treats the essay as evidence about a person rather than a performance, treats financial aid as math rather than mystery, and treats "fit" as a set of testable claims rather than a vibe.

By the end, a student should be able to assemble a complete, realistic admissions packet: criteria, a balanced list, a working calendar, an honest academic and activities profile, an essay plan, a recommendation strategy, a financial-aid checklist, and a decision matrix they can defend to a parent.

## Course Design Notes
Every requirement, deadline, and policy in this course changes by college and by year. The course teaches the system and the reasoning; students must always verify each college's own deadlines, testing policy, and required materials on that college's admissions and financial-aid pages. Where a specific number appears (essay word counts, activity limits, the FAFSA tax year), it reflects the 2025-2026 cycle and should be re-checked. The goal is durable judgment, not memorized trivia, so questions emphasize what is binding, what controls money, what must come from someone else, and what a reader can actually verify.

## Chapter 1: Admissions Landscape and Personal Goals
- **Core questions**: What does this student actually need from college, and which of their constraints are non-negotiable? What does holistic review mean for someone who is not at the top of every metric?
- **Key concepts**: fit, selectivity vs. quality, holistic review, yield and demonstrated interest, major exploration, outcomes data, financial/academic/geographic/personal constraints.
- **Applied skills**: write a personal criteria brief separating must-haves, nice-to-haves, and deal-breakers; read a college's published outcomes and Common Data Set instead of its marketing.
- **Common traps**: confusing prestige with fit; assuming low acceptance rate means high quality; treating "holistic" as "they will overlook my grades."

## Chapter 2: Building a Balanced College List
- **Core questions**: Is each school academically and financially plausible for this student, and what evidence supports that category? What is missing from the list?
- **Key concepts**: reach/target/likely (safety), admit-rate and GPA/test ranges, public vs. private, liberal arts college vs. research university, community college and transfer pathways, financial safety.
- **Applied skills**: classify schools using the middle 50% range and net price, not gut feeling; ensure at least one academic AND financial safety; document risk notes per school.
- **Common traps**: a list of all reaches; calling a school a "safety" on academics while ignoring cost; ignoring transfer as a legitimate strategic path.

## Chapter 3: Application Systems and Timelines
- **Core questions**: Which deadline is binding, and which one controls scholarships and aid? Who else has to do something before this application is complete?
- **Key concepts**: Common App, Coalition/Scoir, institutional portals, Early Decision (binding), Early Action (non-binding), Restrictive/Single-Choice Early Action, Regular Decision, rolling and direct admission, priority deadlines.
- **Applied skills**: build a master calendar with tasks, owners, and dates; identify which materials (transcript, counselor letter, school report) come from the school, not the student.
- **Common traps**: assuming applying early always helps; missing that scholarship deadlines often precede admission deadlines; applying ED without running the net price first.

## Chapter 4: Academic Profile, Testing, and Course Rigor
- **Core questions**: What story does the transcript tell over four years, and should a test score be submitted? What context deserves explanation?
- **Key concepts**: GPA in context, course rigor, AP/IB/dual enrollment, SAT/ACT scoring, test-optional vs. test-blind, score-choice and superscoring, English-proficiency tests for international applicants.
- **Applied skills**: decide whether to submit a score by comparing it to a college's middle 50%; plan a senior-year course load that signals rigor; use Additional Information to explain a dip without excuses.
- **Common traps**: submitting a below-median score at a test-optional school; assuming test-blind means scores help; treating an upward grade trend as worthless.

## Chapter 5: Activities, Honors, and Applicant Narrative
- **Core questions**: What did the student actually do, what changed because of it, and which activities reveal a value or strength rather than just filling slots?
- **Key concepts**: the Common App activities list (up to 10, tight character limits), impact vs. participation, leadership and initiative, intellectual vitality, family responsibilities and paid work as legitimate activities, narrative coherence.
- **Applied skills**: write activity descriptions that lead with a strong verb and a concrete result inside the character limit; order activities by importance; surface unpaid family and work contributions.
- **Common traps**: padding with low-impact memberships; describing duties instead of outcomes; hiding real responsibilities (caregiving, jobs) that strengthen the application.

## Chapter 6: Personal Statement and Supplemental Essays
- **Core questions**: What does this essay reveal that the rest of the application cannot? Which concrete detail proves the claim instead of asserting it?
- **Key concepts**: the 650-word personal statement, the seven Common App prompts, voice and specificity, reflection, the "Why us" supplement, community and intellectual-curiosity prompts, revision, avoiding résumé rehash.
- **Applied skills**: draft one personal statement under 650 words and outline two supplements; cut generic sentences; replace claims with scenes and evidence; research a college so "Why us" names real programs.
- **Common traps**: a "Why us" essay that could be copy-pasted for any school; restating the activities list in prose; a dramatic topic with no reflection.

## Chapter 7: Recommendations, Interviews, Portfolios, and Special Paths
- **Core questions**: Who can write a letter with specific evidence, and what early planning do special paths require?
- **Key concepts**: recommendation requests and the brag sheet, FERPA waiver, interviews and demonstrated interest, arts/portfolio submissions, NCAA athletic recruiting and eligibility, transfer applications, UCAS personal statement comparison.
- **Applied skills**: assemble a recommendation packet (who, why, what to give them, by when); prepare interview answers tied to evidence; map the earlier timeline that portfolios and athletic recruiting demand.
- **Common traps**: asking a teacher who likes you but cannot cite specifics; treating an optional interview as unimportant where demonstrated interest matters; missing the much earlier deadlines for portfolios and recruiting.

## Chapter 8: Financial Aid, Offers, and Final Decision
- **Core questions**: What is the actual net cost of each offer, which aid is renewable, and what debt level is realistic for this student?
- **Key concepts**: FAFSA and the Student Aid Index (SAI), CSS Profile, cost of attendance vs. net price, need-based vs. merit aid, gift aid vs. loans, net price calculators, aid appeals, the May 1 decision deadline.
- **Applied skills**: build an offer-comparison table (sticker price, gift aid, loans, net cost, renewability, fit); read an award letter critically; write a reasoned aid-appeal request; estimate four-year debt.
- **Common traps**: comparing sticker prices instead of net prices; counting loans as "aid"; assuming a one-year merit award renews automatically; choosing on prestige after the numbers say otherwise.

## Capstone
Build a complete admissions planning packet for one real student profile: a personal criteria brief, a balanced college list with category evidence and risk notes, a master application calendar, an academic-profile review with a testing decision, a polished activities-and-honors section, a personal-statement draft plus two supplement outlines, a recommendation packet and interview prep sheet, a financial-aid checklist, and a final offer-comparison decision matrix with a defended recommendation.

## Assessment Ideas
- College list graded on balance, fit evidence, and affordability awareness (must include an academic and a financial safety).
- Essays graded on specificity, reflection, and revision quality rather than topic drama.
- Capstone graded on completeness, realism, and student-owned decision making.
