# AP Art History
Read works of art across the globe and across 30,000 years, and argue from what you see to what it meant.

**ID**: `col-ap-art-history` · **Discipline**: Arts/Humanities

## Course Aim
AP Art History asks students to look closely, place what they see in context, and build evidence-based arguments about the global history of art. The College Board frames the work around 250 required works drawn from ten content areas and six art-historical skills: visual analysis, contextual analysis, comparison, attribution, interpretation of artistic decisions, and argumentation. This course turns those skills into weekly habits rather than end-of-year cramming.

The syllabus is organized to match the College Board's ten content areas, from Global Prehistory through Global Contemporary, bracketed by an opening chapter on art-historical thinking and a closing chapter on exam strategy. Each content area is treated both on its own terms and as material for cross-cultural comparison, because the AP exam rewards students who can connect a Mexica featherwork to a Hawaiian feather cloak, or read a Persian manuscript folio against an Italian altarpiece, using accurate evidence rather than vague impressions.

The goal is not memorization of captions but fluency: a student finishing this course should be able to confront an unknown work, describe its form, propose a defensible attribution, name relevant context, and choose the strongest comparison. Accuracy and respect for cultural meaning matter throughout, especially for works whose function depends on ritual, performance, place, or community use that a museum display cannot show.

## Course Design Notes
The course is global and skills-first. Visual analysis is practiced every week, not saved for a unit, and the same vocabulary (form, function, content, context, patronage, materials, technique, iconography) recurs across every culture so that comparison becomes natural. Required works anchor each chapter, but the emphasis is on what those works teach about looking, not on trivia.

Two design commitments run through the chapters. First, technique and material are treated as evidence: knowing that the Bayeux Tapestry is embroidery, not tapestry, or that Leonardo's Last Supper failed because it was not buon fresco, changes what students can argue. Second, context is treated as something that can be stated accurately or gotten wrong: works from Indigenous American, African, and Pacific traditions are presented with attention to ritual and community use, and students are taught to flag what a museum object cannot, on its own, reveal.

Assessment mirrors the AP exam: weekly visual-analysis drills, comparison writing graded on specific evidence and meaningful contrast, attribution practice on unknown works, and timed long-response planning. The capstone is a curated digital exhibition that forces global range and an argued central idea.

## Chapter 1: Art Historical Thinking Skills
- **Core questions**: What do you actually see before you interpret? What does a work do, and for whom? How do materials and context shape meaning? What makes one comparison stronger than another?
- **Key concepts**: form, function, content, context; patronage; materials and technique; iconography and attribution; the six AP skills; visual vocabulary (line, color, composition, scale, texture).
- **Applied skills**: write a tight formal description before interpreting; build comparison stems; distinguish description from analysis; cite visible evidence for every claim.
- **Common traps**: jumping to meaning before describing; treating personal taste as analysis; confusing provenance (ownership) with composition; assuming an artist's biography is the starting point.

## Chapter 2: Global Prehistory (c. 30,000-500 BCE)
- **Core questions**: How can art communicate power, ritual, or cosmology without surviving texts? How do scale, site, and material carry meaning?
- **Key concepts**: representational image-making (Apollo 11 Stones); solar alignment and ritual architecture (Stonehenge); use of natural cave surfaces (Hall of Bulls, Lascaux); schematic frontality (anthropomorphic stele); ritual geometry (Liangzhu jade cong).
- **Applied skills**: infer function from form and find-context; compare two prehistoric works across regions using shared formal strategies rather than shared cultures.
- **Common traps**: assuming Europe holds the oldest images; calling cave painting buon fresco; reading schematic abstraction as a failure of skill rather than a choice.

## Chapter 3: Ancient Mediterranean (3500 BCE-300 CE)
- **Core questions**: How did Greek and Roman art turn the human body and civic space into instruments of ideology? How does idealization differ from verism?
- **Key concepts**: contrapposto and the canon of proportion (Doryphoros); civic and imperial program (Parthenon); Roman concrete engineering (Pantheon dome); imperial portraiture (Augustus of Prima Porta); Greek naturalism vs. Egyptian frontality (Riace Warriors).
- **Applied skills**: read sculptural pose and costume as political message; explain structural engineering in plain terms; contrast idealized and veristic portrait conventions.
- **Common traps**: confusing Gothic devices (pointed arches, flying buttresses) with Roman vaulting; treating frontality as primitive; assuming a temple is a tomb.

## Chapter 4: Early Europe and Colonial Americas (200-1750 CE)
- **Core questions**: How do sacred spaces guide movement and devotion? How do patronage and technique shape the Renaissance and Baroque?
- **Key concepts**: pendentives and the domed church (Hagia Sophia); enamel and goldsmith work (Pala d'Oro); embroidery vs. tapestry (Bayeux Tapestry); Gothic structure and stained glass (Chartres); fresco technique and its failures (Last Supper); papal patronage (Sistine ceiling); Renaissance vs. Baroque theatricality (the two Davids); painting about looking (Las Meninas).
- **Applied skills**: distinguish Romanesque, Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque markers; use medium and technique as evidence; trace a single patron's influence.
- **Common traps**: calling the Bayeux Tapestry a tapestry; assuming the Sistine ceiling postdates the Council of Trent; mixing up the calm and the active David.

## Chapter 5: Later Europe and Americas (1750-1980 CE)
- **Core questions**: How did revolution, industry, and photography change subject matter and technique? How did artists challenge academic convention?
- **Key concepts**: Romantic political painting (Liberty Leading the People); the confrontational modern nude (Olympia); Post-Impressionist surface and feeling (The Starry Night); history painting that indicts rather than celebrates (Third of May 1808); the sources of Cubism (Demoiselles); narrative sequence in tempera (Lawrence's Migration Series).
- **Applied skills**: link social change and technology to artistic experiment; read brushwork and surface as content; place a work precisely in its historical moment.
- **Common traps**: dating Liberty Leading the People to 1789; assuming Olympia was the first Salon nude; reading impasto as careless rather than expressive.

## Chapter 6: Indigenous Americas (1000 BCE-1980 CE)
- **Core questions**: How does meaning change when a work is used, worn, or sited within a community? What can a museum display fail to show?
- **Key concepts**: paired cosmology in state architecture (Templo Mayor); diplomatic-grade featherwork (Ruler's Feather Headdress); trade materials and continuity (Lenape bandolier bag); material record-keeping (Inka maize cob and khipu); royal women's ritual labor (Yaxchilán lintels).
- **Applied skills**: write context briefs that respect ritual and community use; read non-alphabetic record-keeping; distinguish pre-contact and post-contact materials.
- **Common traps**: assuming trade beads make a work inauthentic; reading administrative or ritual objects as commercial currency; treating monumental art as anonymous decoration.

## Chapter 7: Africa (1100-1980 CE)
- **Core questions**: How do African court, ritual, and performance objects mean differently in motion than on a museum shelf?
- **Key concepts**: earthen architecture and community maintenance (Great Mosque of Djenné); sophisticated lost-wax casting (Ife heads); masquerade as activated performance (Bwa/Aka mask); court metalwork recording hierarchy (Benin plaques); activated ritual power objects (Kongo nkisi nkondi).
- **Applied skills**: explain how maintenance, dance, and activation are part of an object's meaning; correct outdated assumptions about African metallurgy.
- **Common traps**: reading masks as static sculpture; assuming Benin plaques were imported; treating a museum nkisi as complete without its history of activation.

## Chapter 8: West and Central Asia (500 BCE-1980 CE)
- **Core questions**: How does ornament, calligraphy, and patterned space organize meaning differently from optical illusion?
- **Key concepts**: imperial ideology in relief (Persepolis apadana); the qibla and pilgrimage (Kaaba); Persian manuscript painting in opaque watercolor and gold (Shahnama, Khamsa folios); water and stucco muqarnas (Court of the Lions, Alhambra).
- **Applied skills**: read patterned, stacked space rather than single-point perspective; explain how architecture anchors a global geometry of prayer.
- **Common traps**: forcing Italian perspective onto Persian painting; confusing Nasrid or Mughal vocabulary with Romanesque or Gothic; treating tribute reliefs as bookkeeping.

## Chapter 9: South, East, and Southeast Asia (300 BCE-1980 CE)
- **Core questions**: How do monuments and images structure devotion through movement, axis, and presence?
- **Key concepts**: relic enshrinement and circumambulation (Great Stupa at Sanchi); terraced cosmology as a walk (Borobudur); axial imperial order (Forbidden City); sacred image as relic-presence (Jowo Rinpoche); collaborative woodblock printing (Hokusai's Great Wave, nishiki-e).
- **Applied skills**: describe how a building choreographs the body; treat a devotional image as presence, not only object; explain ukiyo-e as team production.
- **Common traps**: reading a stupa as a building to enter rather than to circle; calling a woodblock print a unique painting; treating an axial plan as radial.

## Chapter 10: The Pacific (700-1980 CE)
- **Core questions**: How do material, labor, and rank fuse in Pacific objects? When is an object meant to be temporary?
- **Key concepts**: mana and chiefly rank (Hawaiian 'ahu 'ula feather cloak); ancestral architecture (Māori wharenui); ancestral figures on ahu platforms (moai of Rapa Nui); temporary funerary commemoration (New Ireland malagan).
- **Applied skills**: connect rare materials and labor to spiritual and social status; explain works designed to be used once and released.
- **Common traps**: assuming moai face the sea; importing Roman or extraterrestrial explanations; treating a wharenui as a secular hall or a malagan as a permanent monument.

## Chapter 11: Global Contemporary Art (1980-present)
- **Core questions**: How does contemporary art use site, audience, identity, and time as part of its meaning? What historical question is the artist revisiting?
- **Key concepts**: photography that exposes constructed femininity (Sherman, Untitled Film Stills); site, material, and duration as content (earthworks and installation such as Spiral Jetty and Salcedo's interventions); postcolonial critique, memory, and public art.
- **Applied skills**: write a curatorial label with evidence and interpretation; explain how site and elapsed time become content; identify the earlier tradition a contemporary work answers.
- **Common traps**: assuming staged photographs are documentary; expecting easel painting where the medium is installation or earthwork; ignoring the political and geographic context of a site-specific work.

## Chapter 12: AP Exam Studio
- **Core questions**: What evidence supports an attribution of an unknown work? Which comparison is strongest? What context can be stated accurately rather than guessed?
- **Key concepts**: unknown-work analysis; attribution from formal and contextual clues; image-set recall; short-response precision; long-essay planning; the two 30-minute and four 15-minute essay tasks; the 80-question multiple-choice section.
- **Applied skills**: structure a thesis-driven long response; choose and defend the best comparison; pace timed writing; separate confident claims from speculation.
- **Common traps**: padding answers with unverifiable context; choosing a weak comparison because it is familiar; misallocating time across the six free-response questions.

## Capstone
Curate a small digital exhibition of six works drawn from at least four different cultures or content areas. Write a label for each that analyzes form, function, content, and context, then write a final comparison essay that states the exhibition's central argument and defends it with specific visual and contextual evidence. The exhibition is graded on global range, accuracy of context, strength of comparison, and the clarity of its curatorial argument.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly visual-analysis drills graded on observation and precise art vocabulary.
- Comparison writing graded on specific evidence and meaningful contrast across cultures.
- Attribution practice on unknown works, defended from formal and contextual clues.
- Timed long-response planning mirroring the AP free-response structure.
- Capstone graded on curatorial argument, global range, and accurate context.
