# Microeconomics
How individuals, firms, and markets make choices under scarcity - and how to read the graphs, run the marginal arithmetic, and judge when markets work well or fail.

**ID**: `microeconomics` · **Discipline**: Economics

## Course Aim
This course teaches microeconomics as a disciplined way of reasoning about choice under scarcity rather than a catalog of definitions. Aimed at students around ages 15-16 (roughly Year 11 / Grade 10-11), it can serve as AP Microeconomics preparation or as a standalone non-AP economics elective. The recurring habit is marginal thinking: every decision is evaluated one more unit at a time, weighing the additional benefit against the additional cost, and every market outcome is read off a labeled graph rather than asserted.

Students begin with scarcity, opportunity cost, the production possibilities frontier, and comparative advantage, then build the supply-and-demand model and use elasticity to predict how quantities, prices, and revenue respond to shocks. They learn to measure efficiency with consumer and producer surplus, to locate deadweight loss when price controls or taxes distort a market, and to trace consumer choice and a firm's cost structure from the underlying production function. The second half works through market structures - perfect competition, monopoly, monopolistic competition, and oligopoly with simple game theory - before turning to factor markets, externalities, public goods, and the information problems that cause markets to fail.

By the end, a student can identify which curve shifts in response to an event, compute and interpret an elasticity, find a firm's profit-maximizing output and decide whether it should produce at all, distinguish efficient from inefficient market outcomes, and write a short policy analysis that names the winners, the losers, and the unintended consequences. The emphasis throughout is on honest reasoning: choosing the right model, labeling the graph correctly, and explaining the outcome without hand-waving.

## Course Design Notes
Sequence the course so the tools accumulate: Chapter 1 establishes scarcity and marginal reasoning; Chapter 2 builds the supply-demand-elasticity engine that every later chapter uses; Chapter 3 adds the welfare lens (surplus and deadweight loss) and applies it to government intervention; Chapter 4 derives consumer choice and the firm's cost curves; Chapters 5 and 6 analyze the four market structures; Chapter 7 covers factor markets and market failure; Chapter 8 is a synthesis studio. Route questions here when they involve individual consumers, single firms, or specific markets - macro topics such as GDP, inflation, unemployment, and monetary or fiscal policy belong to the companion Macroeconomics course.

Every chapter pairs a model with its interpretation in context, because at this tier the diagram and the marginal logic matter more than memorized terms. "Common traps" sections target the errors that most distort microeconomic reasoning - confusing a shift of a curve with a movement along it, treating sunk and fixed costs as relevant to a marginal decision, applying the shutdown rule with the wrong cost, calling a movement up the demand curve a "decrease in demand," and confusing absolute with comparative advantage. The course assumes no calculus; it relies on graphs, ratios, and simple per-unit arithmetic, and it treats the carefully labeled graph and the payoff matrix as primary thinking tools.

## Chapter 1: Economic Choice and Market Basics
- **Core questions**: What is the true cost of a choice once forgone alternatives are counted? Who should produce what when people differ in productivity? Why does specialization and trade leave everyone better off?
- **Key concepts**: scarcity and tradeoffs; opportunity cost; marginal benefit vs. marginal cost; incentives; the production possibilities curve (PPC) and its shape; productive vs. allocative efficiency; absolute vs. comparative advantage; gains from specialization and trade.
- **Applied skills**: compute opportunity cost from a PPC or a trade-off table; identify points that are efficient, inefficient, or unattainable; determine comparative advantage by comparing opportunity costs; find the terms of trade that benefit both parties.
- **Common traps**: counting only out-of-pocket money and ignoring forgone alternatives; confusing absolute advantage (more output) with comparative advantage (lower opportunity cost); reading the PPC's bowed shape as a mistake rather than increasing opportunity cost; letting sunk costs influence a forward-looking choice.

## Chapter 2: Supply, Demand, and Elasticity
- **Core questions**: Which curve shifts when an event occurs, and which way? How does the market move to a new equilibrium after a shock? How responsive is quantity to a change in price or income, and what does that do to revenue?
- **Key concepts**: law of demand and law of supply; the determinants that shift each curve (income, related goods, expectations, number of buyers/sellers, input costs, technology); equilibrium, shortages, and surpluses; price elasticity of demand and supply; income and cross-price elasticity; the total-revenue test.
- **Applied skills**: decide whether an event shifts demand, shifts supply, or causes a movement along a curve, and predict the new price and quantity; compute price elasticity and classify demand as elastic, inelastic, or unit-elastic; use the total-revenue test; classify goods as normal/inferior and substitutes/complements from elasticity signs.
- **Common traps**: calling a price-driven movement along a curve a "shift" in demand or supply; flipping the direction of a shift; treating the slope of a curve as its elasticity; forgetting that revenue rises when raising price on inelastic demand but falls when demand is elastic.

## Chapter 3: Welfare and Government Intervention
- **Core questions**: How do you measure the gains a market creates and who captures them? Where does deadweight loss come from? When a government sets a price control, levies a tax, or pays a subsidy, who wins, who loses, and what goes wrong?
- **Key concepts**: consumer surplus and producer surplus; total surplus and allocative efficiency; deadweight loss; binding price ceilings (and shortages) vs. price floors (and surpluses); per-unit taxes, tax incidence, and the role of elasticity; subsidies and quotas.
- **Applied skills**: shade consumer surplus, producer surplus, and deadweight loss on a graph; predict the shortage or surplus a binding control creates; determine who bears more of a tax from relative elasticities; explain the unintended consequence of a specific intervention.
- **Common traps**: assuming a tax burden falls only on whoever legally pays it; thinking a price ceiling above equilibrium (or a floor below it) changes anything; forgetting that deadweight loss is the value of mutually beneficial trades that no longer happen; equating "the government collects revenue" with "the policy is efficient."

## Chapter 4: Consumer Choice and Production Costs
- **Core questions**: How does a rational consumer spend a limited budget to get the most satisfaction? Why does marginal cost eventually rise? How are a firm's short-run cost curves related to one another?
- **Key concepts**: total and marginal utility; diminishing marginal utility; the budget constraint and the utility-maximizing rule (equal marginal utility per dollar); the production function and diminishing marginal returns; fixed vs. variable costs; marginal cost (MC), average total cost (ATC), average variable cost (AVC), and average fixed cost.
- **Applied skills**: find the utility-maximizing bundle using the MU-per-dollar rule; trace how diminishing returns drive the shape of the MC curve; compute and graph MC, ATC, and AVC from a cost table; locate the output where ATC is minimized (where MC crosses ATC).
- **Common traps**: maximizing total utility instead of marginal utility per dollar; confusing diminishing marginal utility with negative utility; thinking ATC always falls; forgetting that the MC curve intersects ATC and AVC at their minimum points.

## Chapter 5: Perfect Competition
- **Core questions**: How much should a price-taking firm produce? When should it keep producing at a loss, and when should it shut down? How do entry and exit drive the long-run outcome?
- **Key concepts**: price taker and a horizontal (perfectly elastic) firm demand curve; marginal revenue equals price; the profit-maximizing rule MR = MC; short-run profit, loss, and break-even; the shutdown rule (price below AVC); long-run zero-economic-profit equilibrium; productive and allocative efficiency.
- **Applied skills**: find profit-maximizing quantity where MR = MC and compute profit or loss using ATC; apply the shutdown rule by comparing price to AVC; explain how entry erodes profits (and exit erases losses) until P = minimum ATC; show why perfect competition is efficient.
- **Common traps**: shutting down whenever the firm has a loss rather than when price falls below AVC; using ATC instead of AVC for the shutdown decision; thinking long-run zero profit means the firm earns nothing (it earns a normal return); assuming a competitive firm can raise its price.

## Chapter 6: Imperfect Competition
- **Core questions**: What gives a firm market power, and what does it do with it? Why is a single-price monopoly inefficient even when it is profitable? How do a few interdependent firms decide what to do?
- **Key concepts**: monopoly and barriers to entry; marginal revenue below price for a price searcher; the monopoly's deadweight loss; natural monopoly and regulation; price discrimination; monopolistic competition, product differentiation, and long-run zero profit; oligopoly, game theory, dominant strategies, and Nash equilibrium.
- **Applied skills**: find a monopolist's quantity (MR = MC) and price (off the demand curve) and shade its deadweight loss; explain why MR lies below demand for a price searcher; build and solve a simple payoff matrix for a dominant strategy and a Nash equilibrium; compare the four market structures on price, output, and efficiency.
- **Common traps**: setting the monopoly price where MR = MC instead of reading the price up on the demand curve; assuming a monopoly can charge any price it likes regardless of demand; expecting monopolistic competitors to earn long-run profits; confusing a Nash equilibrium with the jointly best (cooperative) outcome.

## Chapter 7: Factor Markets, Externalities, and Public Goods
- **Core questions**: How does a firm decide how many workers to hire? What happens when a market ignores costs or benefits that fall on third parties? Why do some valuable goods get under-provided by markets?
- **Key concepts**: derived demand and marginal revenue product (MRP); the hiring rule MRP = wage in a competitive labor market; monopsony and below-competitive wages; negative and positive externalities and the gap between private and social cost/benefit; Pigouvian taxes and subsidies; the Coase theorem; public goods (non-rival, non-excludable) and the free-rider problem; asymmetric information and adverse selection.
- **Applied skills**: determine the profit-maximizing number of workers by comparing MRP to the wage; identify whether an externality is positive or negative and find the socially optimal quantity; choose a policy (tax, subsidy, property rights, regulation) that internalizes a given externality; explain why public goods and free riding cause market under-provision.
- **Common traps**: forgetting that labor demand is derived from the value of output; assuming a monopsony pays the competitive wage; confusing the private optimum with the social optimum; treating a positive externality as a reason to tax rather than subsidize; calling a merely expensive good a public good when it is actually excludable.

## Chapter 8: Microeconomics Studio
- **Core questions**: Which model fits the situation in front of you? When several things change at once, what moves first and what follows? How do you explain a market outcome rigorously instead of asserting it?
- **Key concepts**: model selection across the full course; precise graph construction and labeling; chaining shifts through to a new equilibrium; integrating welfare, cost, structure, and externality reasoning; written economic explanation; self-correction from feedback.
- **Applied skills**: pick the correct model and diagram for a novel scenario; work a multi-step problem where a shock propagates from one market to another; defend a conclusion with marginal reasoning and a labeled graph; keep an error log and revise mistaken analyses.
- **Common traps**: forcing a familiar model onto a situation it does not fit; drawing unlabeled or mislabeled axes and curves; stopping after the first shift instead of following the chain to the new equilibrium; asserting an outcome without the marginal logic that produces it.

## Capstone
Students analyze a fictional but internally consistent market from end to end. Starting from consumer demand and a stated supply situation, they (1) build and shift the supply-and-demand model in response to one or more events, (2) compute and interpret a relevant elasticity, (3) measure efficiency and deadweight loss before and after a government intervention of their choice, (4) examine a representative firm's costs and profit-maximizing output under a specified market structure, and (5) identify an externality or information problem and propose a policy that internalizes it. The deliverable can be a written report, a slide briefing, or an annotated graph packet, but it must make every model choice explicit, present correctly labeled diagrams, name the winners and losers of each policy, and close with a clear, marginal-reasoning-based answer together with a candid statement of the analysis's limitations.
