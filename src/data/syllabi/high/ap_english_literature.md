# AP English Literature

College-level close reading and argument: interpret poetry, short fiction, longer fiction, and drama, then defend that interpretation in evidence-based analytic writing.

**ID**: `col-ap-english-literature` · **Discipline**: English

## Course Aim
This course prepares students for the AP English Literature and Composition exam and for first-year college literary study by treating reading as active interpretation rather than passive reception. The College Board frames the course around how writers make meaning through deliberate choices of language, structure, and perspective, and around how readers build defensible interpretations from textual evidence. Students learn to notice the small choices that carry meaning, diction, syntax, imagery, point of view, structure, figurative language, and to explain how those choices produce an effect on the reader.

The exam assesses three free-response tasks, poetry analysis, prose fiction analysis, and a literary argument drawn from a work the student selects, alongside a multiple-choice section that pairs short literary passages with close-reading questions. This syllabus spirals the major genres so students practice the same analytic moves, claim, textual evidence, and commentary that explains how the evidence supports the claim, across poetry, short fiction, longer fiction, and drama all year. The aim is interpretive flexibility: a student should be able to read an unfamiliar passage cold and say something specific and arguable about how it works.

By the end of the year students should write a defensible thesis, sustain a line of reasoning, integrate precise evidence, and move commentary beyond paraphrase toward genuine analysis of effect and complexity. They should recognize and name literary devices not as a vocabulary quiz but as tools for explaining meaning, and they should carry a small set of well-known works in memory, ready to deploy as evidence in the literary-argument essay.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve close reading of poetry, prose fiction, or drama; identification and interpretive use of literary devices; character, setting, structure, point of view, tone, theme, and motif; or the craft of analytic writing, thesis, evidence, commentary, line of reasoning, and sophistication. Keep device questions interpretive rather than definitional wherever possible: the exam rarely asks "what is an apostrophe" and far more often asks "what effect does this device produce in this line." Quote the writer when a passage appears so students analyze the text in front of them rather than guess its source.

Keep practice forward and error-aware. The most common AP confusions are predictable and should drive distractors: conflating adjacent devices (caesura vs. enjambment, metonymy vs. synecdoche, dramatic vs. verbal vs. situational irony, metaphor vs. personification), mistaking plot summary or paraphrase for analytic commentary, treating tone as the reader's feeling rather than the speaker's attitude, and asserting a theme without grounding it in evidence. Every wrong answer should name a real misconception, explain briefly why it fails, and hint toward the correct interpretive move. Favor prompts that ask students to interpret a short quoted passage, justify a reading, or distinguish two effects, since that mirrors how the exam measures understanding. Useful anchors include the College Board AP English Literature Course and Exam Description, released free-response questions and scoring guidelines, and the Purdue OWL guide to literary theory and close reading.

## Chapter 1: Close Reading and Literary Argument
**Core questions:** What pattern do you notice in the language, and what does it mean? Which textual details can support an arguable claim, and how does commentary explain that support rather than restate it?
**Key concepts:** diction; syntax; imagery; tone; figurative language; theme; defensible thesis; textual evidence; commentary; the difference between summary, paraphrase, and analysis; line of reasoning.
**Applied skills:** annotating a short passage for patterns; turning an observation into an arguable claim; writing a claim-evidence-commentary paragraph in which the commentary explains how the evidence creates meaning.
**Common traps:** retelling the plot instead of analyzing it; quoting evidence without explaining its effect; making a claim too broad to defend from the passage; treating a feeling about the text as if it were a textual argument.

## Chapter 2: Short Fiction I
**Core questions:** How does setting shape character and conflict? What does the point of view reveal, and what does it hide? Where does the story shift, and why does that turn matter?
**Key concepts:** character; setting; plot and structure; conflict; point of view (first person, third limited, third omniscient); narrator reliability; exposition, rising action, climax, resolution; in medias res.
**Applied skills:** tracing how a single narrative choice (a limited narrator, a flash-forward, a withheld detail) controls what the reader knows; writing a focused paragraph on one narrative technique and its effect.
**Common traps:** confusing the narrator with the author; assuming a first-person narrator is reliable; describing what happens instead of how the telling shapes meaning; ignoring the effect of where the story chooses to begin and end.

## Chapter 3: Poetry I
**Core questions:** Who is speaking, in what situation? What tension drives the poem? How do form, sound, and line breaks affect the way the poem is read and understood?
**Key concepts:** speaker vs. poet; dramatic situation; imagery; metaphor and simile; sound devices (alliteration, assonance, consonance); lineation, enjambment, end-stop, caesura; stanza; rhyme and meter; the volta or shift.
**Applied skills:** writing a short explication that connects a formal choice (an enjambment, a meter break, a stanza turn) to the poem's meaning; identifying the shift and explaining what changes around it.
**Common traps:** confusing enjambment (no pause at line end) with caesura (pause inside a line); assuming the speaker is the poet; listing devices without interpreting their effect; mistaking a poem's subject for its theme.

## Chapter 4: Longer Fiction or Drama I
**Core questions:** How does a motif develop across a whole work? What conflict deepens over time, and how does scene structure reveal it? What does a dramatized scene show that summary cannot?
**Key concepts:** character arc; motif; symbol; dramatic tension; scene and act structure; dialogue; soliloquy and aside; stage directions; theme as it accumulates across a work.
**Applied skills:** keeping a reading journal that tracks one motif, one character arc, and one developing theme across a novel or play; analyzing how a single scene advances character or theme.
**Common traps:** treating a symbol as a fixed code with one meaning; confusing a recurring image (motif) with a theme (the idea it builds toward); reducing a character to a label; analyzing a scene in isolation from the work's larger pattern.

## Chapter 5: Short Fiction and Poetry II
**Core questions:** Where does the text resist a single, simple reading? How do two techniques work together to create a complex effect? What is the interpretive payoff of contrast, irony, or allusion?
**Key concepts:** complexity and ambiguity; irony (verbal, dramatic, situational); contrast and juxtaposition; allusion; tonal shift; narrative compression; paradox; the interaction of multiple devices.
**Applied skills:** writing a comparative analysis of two short texts or paired passages; explaining how two devices combine to produce a meaning neither would alone; defending a reading that accounts for, rather than erases, the text's ambiguity.
**Common traps:** confusing the three kinds of irony; flattening ambiguity into a single tidy message; treating an allusion as decoration rather than evidence; analyzing two texts side by side without ever putting them in genuine relationship.

## Chapter 6: Longer Fiction or Drama II
**Core questions:** How does the ending revise or reframe the beginning? What purpose does a minor character serve in the whole? How do social and historical context shape the central conflict?
**Key concepts:** structure across a complete work; foil and secondary characters; setting as pattern; social and historical context; tragic and comic form; tragic flaw and reversal; resolution and its meaning.
**Applied skills:** outlining a literary argument about a whole work recalled from memory, the core skill for the Q3 literary-argument essay; explaining how a minor character or recurring setting illuminates theme.
**Common traps:** building the literary-argument essay on plot retelling rather than a thesis; ignoring how the ending reinterprets earlier events; treating context as background trivia instead of an interpretive lever; choosing a work too thin to sustain a college-level argument.

## Chapter 7: AP Essay Studio
**Core questions:** What is the prompt actually asking you to argue? Which evidence is most specific and most relevant? How does commentary move past paraphrase to explain effect, complexity, and the writer's choices?
**Key concepts:** the three free-response types (poetry analysis, prose fiction analysis, literary argument); defensible thesis; line of reasoning; evidence selection; commentary; sophistication; the AP analytic rubric (thesis, evidence and commentary, sophistication).
**Applied skills:** writing one timed essay of each free-response type and revising it against the rubric; planning an essay in the first minutes by converting the prompt into a thesis and a sequence of evidence.
**Common traps:** writing a thesis that merely restates the prompt or is not arguable; front-loading evidence without commentary; chasing "sophistication" with inflated vocabulary instead of genuine interpretive complexity; running out of time because the essay was never planned.

## Chapter 8: Independent Reading and Exam Readiness
**Core questions:** Which work in your repertoire fits this prompt? What details can you recall accurately enough to quote or paraphrase as evidence? What pattern do your missed multiple-choice questions share?
**Key concepts:** text selection for the literary-argument essay; recallable evidence; thematic range across one's reading; timed planning; multiple-choice strategy (reading the passage, the question stem, and the line-reference); error analysis and revision.
**Applied skills:** building an independent-reading evidence deck of works, themes, and quotable details; keeping an exam-correction log that classifies errors by type and adjusts strategy accordingly.
**Common traps:** preparing one all-purpose work that fits no prompt well; relying on a movie or summary rather than the text; rushing the multiple-choice passage and missing the line-reference; repeating the same error because misses were never analyzed.

## Capstone
Students assemble an AP Literature portfolio that demonstrates growth across the year: close-reading annotations of at least one poem and one prose passage; two polished analytic essays revised against the AP rubric; three timed free-response drafts, one of each exam type, with revision notes; an independent-reading evidence deck for the literary-argument essay; and a short reflection that traces how the student's commentary moved from paraphrase toward genuine analysis. The portfolio is assessed on textual specificity, defensibility and complexity of interpretation, quality of revision, and readiness for the timed multiple-choice and free-response sections of the exam.
