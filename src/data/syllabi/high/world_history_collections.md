# World History Collections
*From the first villages to the digital present — global history read as evidence, comparison, and change over time across every inhabited region.*

**ID**: `col-oer-project-world-history` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
World History Collections builds a global, chronological, and source-driven understanding of the human past, from the earliest farming villages to the contemporary, interconnected world. Rather than marching through one region's story, the course treats world history as a set of comparisons and connections: how different societies organized power, belief, work, and family; how networks of trade, migration, and disease tied distant places together; and how the same broad forces — agriculture, empire, religion, industry, revolution, globalization — produced very different outcomes in different places.

The course is built for the standard high-school world history classroom while drawing on AP World History, the OER Project's World History Project, and other open resources. Students learn to do history, not just recall it: to read primary sources critically, to compare societies fairly without flattening their differences, to trace causes and consequences across time, and to argue from evidence. Every chapter pairs regional knowledge with a recurring historical-thinking skill, so source analysis and argument are practiced continuously rather than crammed at the end.

A guiding idea, borrowed from the World History Project, is that the past can be viewed through recurring frames — communities (how people structure society, state, and belief), networks (how people, goods, ideas, and pathogens move and connect), and production and distribution (how people make and move the things they use). Students return to these frames across eras so that history feels like a set of patterns to analyze rather than a list of dates to memorize.

## Course Design Notes
The eight content chapters move chronologically from early human societies to the present, but each is also organized around themes that recur across the whole course: state power, belief systems, economic life, migration, technology, social hierarchy, resistance, and environmental change. This lets students compare, for example, how the Roman Empire and Han China governed diverse populations, or how the Silk Roads and the Atlantic system both moved goods, ideas, and disease — even though they are studied in different chapters.

Periodization is treated as an argument, not a fact handed down from above. Students are regularly asked why a date or boundary is chosen, what it highlights, and what it hides. Likewise, the course avoids a single-region storyline: Afro-Eurasia, the Americas, and Africa are kept in view together, and "the West" is studied as one region among many rather than as the default center of the story. Source work uses the historian's habits of sourcing, contextualizing, and corroborating, with attention to whose voice a document carries and whose it leaves out.

## Chapter 1: Human Origins, Agriculture, and Early States, to c. 600 BCE
- **Core questions**: Why did agriculture emerge in some regions and not others? What advantages and new problems came with cities and states? How do historians combine artifacts, maps, and fragmentary writing into an argument?
- **Key concepts**: human migration out of Africa, foraging, the Neolithic Revolution, domestication, pastoralism, river-valley societies (Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus, Yellow River), cities, the invention of writing, law codes (Hammurabi), social hierarchy, patriarchy, polytheism, early long-distance trade.
- **Applied skills**: building a comparative evidence chart for two early societies using one archaeological and one written source; distinguishing a primary source from a later interpretation.
- **Common traps**: treating agriculture as automatic "progress" that improved every life; assuming writing began as literature rather than record-keeping; confusing the Neolithic Revolution with the Industrial Revolution.

## Chapter 2: Classical Civilizations and Belief Systems, c. 600 BCE-600 CE
- **Core questions**: How did classical empires govern large, diverse populations? Why did major belief systems spread and change as they moved? How can Rome, Han China, and South Asian states be compared without treating them as identical?
- **Key concepts**: empire, citizenship, bureaucracy, the civil-service ideal, slavery and social class, Confucianism, Daoism, Legalism, Hinduism, Buddhism, Judaism, early Christianity, Greek and Roman political thought, Maurya and Gupta India, Han China, Achaemenid Persia, Rome.
- **Applied skills**: writing a comparison paragraph stating one genuine similarity and one genuine difference between two empires or belief systems; identifying a thesis a reader could dispute.
- **Common traps**: equating Buddhism's spread with conquest; calling Roman and Han governance "the same" because both were empires; assuming every classical society had the same view of citizenship.

## Chapter 3: Post-Classical Networks, States, and Religions, c. 600-1200
- **Core questions**: How did trade networks move goods, ideas, technologies, and disease at once? How did religious and political authority reinforce or challenge each other? What made the post-classical world more connected than before?
- **Key concepts**: the rise of Islam and the caliphates, Dar al-Islam, the Byzantine Empire, Tang and Song China, the civil-service exam, South and Southeast Asian kingdoms, West African states (Ghana, early Mali), European feudalism and manorialism, the Silk Roads, Indian Ocean monsoon trade, trans-Saharan caravan routes, trade diasporas.
- **Applied skills**: building an annotated network map that traces one commodity, technology, or belief across at least three regions; reading a travel account as evidence of connection.
- **Common traps**: assuming cultural unity (Dar al-Islam) meant political unity; describing the manor as a wage-labor market; thinking only luxury goods moved along the networks.

## Chapter 4: Global Convergence and Empire, c. 1200-1450
- **Core questions**: Did the Mongol Empire mainly destroy, connect, or transform Afro-Eurasia? How did American states develop in relation to their environments? How should historians weigh travelers' accounts as evidence?
- **Key concepts**: the Mongol Empire and Pax Mongolica, Afro-Eurasian exchange, Mali and Mansa Musa's 1324 hajj, the Aztec (Mexica) and Inca states and their administration, maritime technologies (dhow, junk, compass), the spread of the Black Death, travel accounts (Ibn Battuta, Marco Polo).
- **Applied skills**: building a sourced argument that evaluates one empire or network from more than one perspective; sourcing a travel account for point of view and purpose.
- **Common traps**: assuming the Mongols only destroyed and never connected; thinking American states lacked complex administration; reading the Black Death as proof that all trade had stopped.

## Chapter 5: Maritime Worlds, Gunpowder Empires, and Exchange, c. 1450-1750
- **Core questions**: How did land-based and maritime empires consolidate power and manage diversity? What were the demographic, ecological, and economic effects of the Columbian Exchange? How did enslaved and colonized peoples resist?
- **Key concepts**: the Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal, Qing, and Russian land empires, the millet system, Akbar's toleration, Songhai and Askia Muhammad, Tokugawa Japan, European maritime expansion, the Columbian Exchange, the Atlantic slave trade and Middle Passage, encomienda and coerced labor, the global silver trade (Potosi, the Manila galleon), mercantilism, the Scientific Revolution.
- **Applied skills**: a document-based source-set analysis on empire, exchange, labor, or resistance; tracing causation from disease to new labor systems.
- **Common traps**: blaming American population collapse on deportation rather than disease; calling the millet system forced conversion; treating the legs of the triangular trade in the wrong order.

## Chapter 6: Revolutions, Industrialization, and Imperialism, c. 1750-1900
- **Core questions**: Why did revolutions share ideas yet produce different outcomes? Why did industrialization begin and then spread unevenly? How did imperialism reshape economies, borders, and cultural identities?
- **Key concepts**: the Enlightenment (Locke, Rousseau, Montesquieu), natural rights and consent, the American, French, Haitian, and Latin American revolutions, the Haitian Revolution as the only successful large-scale slave revolt to found a state (1804), creole leadership (Bolivar, San Martin), industrialization (coal, capital, labor, markets), capitalism and socialism, labor movements, mass migration, the "new imperialism," the Berlin Conference, the Opium Wars, Meiji modernization.
- **Applied skills**: outlining a thesis-driven essay comparing two revolutions or evaluating the causes of imperial expansion; sourcing a treaty or observer account.
- **Common traps**: claiming the Haitian Revolution failed; explaining British industrialization by a single cause; assuming colonized peoples never resisted.

## Chapter 7: Global Conflict, Ideology, and Decolonization, c. 1900-1991
- **Core questions**: What made twentieth-century wars global and total? How did ideologies compete for legitimacy? Why did decolonization follow different paths across South Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia?
- **Key concepts**: World War I and the MAIN causes, total war and home fronts, the Treaty of Versailles and Article 231, the Russian Revolution, the Great Depression, fascism, communism, liberal democracy, World War II, the Holocaust, atomic weapons, the United Nations, the Cold War and proxy wars, decolonization (India 1947 and Partition, Ghana 1957), the Bandung Conference (1955), and the Non-Aligned Movement.
- **Applied skills**: building a thematic timeline that connects war, ideology, empire, and independence across at least four regions; corroborating two sources on a contested event.
- **Common traps**: treating the world wars as purely European; reading Versailles as lenient toward Germany; describing decolonization as a purely ethical European gift rather than the result of resistance and pressure.

## Chapter 8: Globalization, Memory, and the Contemporary World, 1991-Present
- **Core questions**: What changed after the Cold War, and what older patterns continued? How have globalization and technology connected people unevenly? How do nations and communities remember traumatic or contested pasts?
- **Key concepts**: the post-Cold War order, globalization, trade and supply chains, mass migration, terrorism and 9/11, the digital and communications revolution, human-rights regimes, global public health, climate change, economic inequality, China's rise and WTO accession, contested historical memory and monuments.
- **Applied skills**: writing a contemporary-history brief that explains one present-day issue through long-term historical causes and multiple regional perspectives; evaluating a source for bias in a present-day debate.
- **Common traps**: assuming 1991 erased all earlier patterns; treating globalization as benefiting everyone equally; confusing the end of the Cold War with the end of all conflict.

## Capstone
Build a world history inquiry portfolio on a theme such as migration, empire, belief, technology, environment, labor, or rights, traced across at least four periods. The portfolio includes a focused research question, primary sources from at least three regions, a periodized timeline, a map or visual-source analysis, a thesis with counterevidence honestly addressed, and a reflection on how looking at global scale changes the interpretation. The finished portfolio should demonstrate sourcing, contextualization, comparison across regions, and a defensible argument about continuity and change over the whole arc of the course.

## Assessment Ideas
- Source work graded on sourcing, context, corroboration, and attention to whose perspective a document carries.
- Comparative writing graded for accurate regional evidence, genuine similarity and difference, and fair treatment of complexity.
- Timeline and map tasks graded for chronology, spatial reasoning, and explanation of connections across regions.
- Capstone graded on global scope, source range, argument, counterevidence, and historical-thinking reflection.
