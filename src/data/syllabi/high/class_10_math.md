# Class 10 Math
Turn a year's worth of algebra, geometry, and trigonometry into exam-ready reasoning for the most decisive board year in many systems.

**ID**: `col-class-10-math` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
Class 10 is a pivotal examination year — India's CBSE/NCERT board year, the run-up to UK GCSE, and a consolidation point for Common Core high-school algebra, functions, geometry, statistics, and probability. This course treats the year as both concept-building and assessment preparation: students move from fluency with single skills to strategy across mixed, multi-step problems. The spine is the connection between algebra and geometry — quadratics and their parabolas, systems and their intersection points, coordinates and the shapes they describe, trigonometric ratios and the triangles they measure.

Students learn to select an efficient method rather than defaulting to one, to write complete reasoning that earns method marks, to interpret graphs and diagrams rather than only producing them, and to check whether a final answer is reasonable in context. Proof and explanation are first-class skills here: a correct number with no justification is treated as an incomplete answer, and a clean two-column or paragraph proof is rewarded as much as a numerical result.

By the end, a student should be able to read an unfamiliar exam-style problem, identify which topic and representation it calls for, carry out the work with disciplined notation and units, and communicate the conclusion clearly — whether that means solving a quadratic three ways, proving two triangles similar, modelling a height with trigonometry, or defending a statistical claim with its limitations.

## Course Design Notes
Build the course around retrieval and mixed practice rather than topic isolation: students need regular spaced review, proof routines, modelling tasks, and timed exam sets. Route questions here when they involve real numbers and irrationality proofs, algebraic identities and rearrangement, quadratic equations and polynomials, pairs of linear equations, coordinate geometry, trigonometric ratios and identities, similarity and circle theorems, mensuration of combined solids and sectors, grouped statistics, and probability of simple events. Keep items at the high-school tier: one core idea per question, numbers chosen so the reasoning (not the arithmetic) is the challenge, and distractors that mirror the specific exam mistakes students actually make — sign errors, forgotten factors, inverted ratios, and confusing a quantity for its method. Emphasise command words (solve, prove, show that, hence) and reward correct method even when the final value slips, because that mirrors how board and GCSE mark schemes are written. Where useful, ask students to translate among equation, graph, table, and diagram, and to state assumptions before computing.

## Chapter 1: Real Numbers, Algebra Review, and Exam Habits
- **Core questions**: Find the HCF and LCM of two numbers using prime factorisation; prove that a given surd is irrational; simplify or expand an algebraic expression using a standard identity; rearrange a formula to make a chosen variable the subject; identify the avoidable error in a worked solution.
- **Key concepts**: The fundamental theorem of arithmetic and prime factorisation, the relationship HCF × LCM = product of the two numbers, proof by contradiction for irrationality, the algebraic identities (a ± b)² and a² − b², rearranging formulas, and disciplined notation under exam conditions.
- **Applied skills**: Choosing prime factorisation over listing for HCF/LCM, structuring a short irrationality proof, and checking each line of working for sign and notation errors before moving on.
- **Common traps**: Assuming HCF × LCM equals the sum rather than the product; "proving" irrationality without the assume-rational-then-contradict structure; expanding (a + b)² as a² + b² and dropping the middle term; sign slips when rearranging across the equals sign.

## Chapter 2: Quadratic Equations and Polynomials
- **Core questions**: Solve a quadratic by factorising, completing the square, and the formula, and say which is most efficient; use the discriminant to count and classify roots; connect the zeros of a polynomial to where its graph crosses the x-axis; form a quadratic from an area, motion, or number context and solve it.
- **Key concepts**: Standard form ax² + bx + c = 0, factorisation, completing the square, the quadratic formula, the discriminant b² − 4ac, the sum and product of roots (−b/a and c/a), and the parabola as the graph of a quadratic.
- **Applied skills**: Selecting a solution method from the structure of the equation, reading the number of real roots from the discriminant before solving, and translating a word problem into a solvable quadratic with a sensible domain.
- **Common traps**: Setting factors equal to the constant instead of zero; dropping a solution when taking a square root (forgetting ±); misreading a negative discriminant as "no answer" rather than "no real roots"; keeping a negative or non-integer root that the context rules out.

## Chapter 3: Systems of Equations and Algebraic Modeling
- **Core questions**: Solve a pair of linear equations by substitution and by elimination; solve the same pair graphically and interpret the intersection; classify a system as consistent, inconsistent, or dependent; write a two-equation model from a word problem and interpret the solution in context.
- **Key concepts**: Pairs of linear equations in two variables, substitution and elimination, graphical solution as the point of intersection, the three cases (unique solution, no solution from parallel lines, infinitely many from identical lines), and modelling constraints algebraically.
- **Applied skills**: Choosing elimination vs. substitution based on the coefficients, recognising from slopes whether a system has a solution before solving, and explaining what an intersection point means for pricing, mixtures, travel, or planning.
- **Common traps**: Sign errors when subtracting one equation from another; checking a candidate solution in only one equation; reading the intersection coordinates in the wrong order; declaring "no solution" when the lines are actually identical.

## Chapter 4: Coordinate Geometry and Functions
- **Core questions**: Find the distance between two points and the midpoint of a segment; use the section formula to divide a segment in a given ratio; find the area of a triangle from its vertices; classify a quadrilateral using coordinates; compare a linear and a quadratic model on the same axes.
- **Key concepts**: The distance and midpoint formulas, the section formula, the area-of-a-triangle formula from coordinates, gradient and intercepts, linear vs. quadratic behaviour, and using coordinates to justify a geometric claim (coordinate proof).
- **Applied skills**: Setting up a distance or area calculation cleanly from a diagram, using coordinates to prove a shape is (say) a parallelogram, and interpreting gradient and intercept in a real context.
- **Common traps**: Subtracting coordinates in the wrong order or forgetting to square inside the distance formula; averaging only the x-values for a midpoint; getting a negative area and not taking the absolute value; confusing the section ratio m:n order.

## Chapter 5: Trigonometry and Right-Triangle Modeling
- **Core questions**: Evaluate sine, cosine, and tangent (and reciprocal ratios where required) for a given right triangle; state exact values for 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 90°; prove a simple trigonometric identity; solve an angle-of-elevation or angle-of-depression problem from a diagram or field data.
- **Key concepts**: The SOH-CAH-TOA ratios, reciprocal ratios (cosec, sec, cot), exact values for standard angles, the identity sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 and its rearrangements, and angles of elevation and depression in applied measurement.
- **Applied skills**: Labelling opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse correctly before choosing a ratio, building a right triangle from a height-and-distance scenario, and reporting an answer with appropriate units and precision.
- **Common traps**: Inverting a ratio (writing hypotenuse over opposite for sine); mixing up opposite and adjacent relative to the chosen angle; confusing the angle of elevation with the angle of depression; rounding too early and losing accuracy in a multi-step problem.

## Chapter 6: Triangles, Circles, and Geometric Proof
- **Core questions**: Prove two triangles are similar and use the ratio to find a missing length; apply the Pythagorean theorem and its converse; use the tangent–radius perpendicularity and the equal-tangents-from-an-external-point theorems; write the steps of a standard construction; justify each step of a short geometric proof.
- **Key concepts**: Similarity and the AA/SSS/SAS criteria, proportional reasoning between similar figures, the Pythagorean theorem and converse, tangent and chord properties of circles, the relationship between length scale factor and area scale factor, and the structure of a logical proof.
- **Applied skills**: Identifying corresponding sides in similar figures, selecting the right circle theorem for a configuration, and presenting reasoning as an ordered, justified sequence rather than a list of numbers.
- **Common traps**: Pairing non-corresponding sides when setting up a similarity ratio; squaring the side scale factor for length (it stays linear) but forgetting to square it for area; treating a tangent as a chord; asserting a step in a proof without naming the theorem that justifies it.

## Chapter 7: Mensuration, Statistics, and Probability
- **Core questions**: Find the area of a sector or segment and the perimeter of a composite figure; compute surface area and volume of a combined solid (and a frustum where required); calculate the mean, median, and mode of grouped data; find the probability of a simple event for cards, dice, spinners, or survey outcomes.
- **Key concepts**: Areas related to circles (sectors and segments), surface area and volume of cones, cylinders, spheres, and their combinations, the frustum, grouped-data measures of centre and spread, and probability of single and simple compound events.
- **Applied skills**: Decomposing a composite solid into known shapes, choosing the right grouped-data formula and reading class midpoints correctly, and listing a sample space to compute a probability.
- **Common traps**: Using diameter in place of radius, or forgetting the 1/3 factor for a cone; double-counting a shared face when combining solids; treating the class boundary as the class midpoint; giving a probability greater than 1 or forgetting that the outcomes must total the whole sample space.

## Chapter 8: Modeling Studio and Exam Readiness
- **Core questions**: Choose a method and representation for an unfamiliar problem and justify the choice; explain why a chosen model fits a situation and where it breaks down; complete a mixed, timed exam set; revise a flawed solution against a mark scheme or rubric.
- **Key concepts**: Selecting representations and assumptions, units and precision, multi-step problem solving across topics, command words (solve, prove, show that, hence, estimate), calculator vs. non-calculator strategy, and final-answer checking.
- **Applied skills**: Reading a command word to decide how much working is expected, allocating time across a mixed paper, and using a mark scheme to diagnose exactly where method marks were lost.
- **Common traps**: Spending too long on one high-value question and leaving easy marks unclaimed; "showing" an answer the question told you to derive without actual reasoning; abandoning method marks by writing only the final number; not checking units, sign, or reasonableness at the end.

## Capstone
Complete a mathematical modelling and exam-defence project. Students investigate a realistic scenario using at least two of quadratics, systems, trigonometry, coordinate geometry, circle geometry, statistics, or probability. The final product includes the calculations, a diagram or graph, a clear statement of assumptions and units, a final recommendation, and a short oral defence in which the student answers exam-style follow-up questions about method choice and limitations. The project is assessed for mathematical range, correctness, quality of reasoning and notation, clarity of representation, and the strength of the response under questioning — mirroring how a board or GCSE examiner weighs method, accuracy, and communication together.
