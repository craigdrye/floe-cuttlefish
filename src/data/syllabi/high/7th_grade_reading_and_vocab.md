# 7th Grade Reading and Vocab
**ID**: `col-7th-grade-reading-and-vocab` | **Discipline**: English | **Year**: 7 (ages 11-12)
**Frameworks**: US Common Core ELA Grade 7

## Course Aim
Develop independent, analytical readers who can infer, interpret, compare, and write about increasingly complex texts. Students learn to track theme, point of view, rhetoric, text structure, vocabulary in context, and cross-text relationships while building the habits needed for confident discussion and evidence-based writing.

## Course Design Notes
Grade 7 shifts more responsibility to students. Teachers still model close reading, vocabulary reasoning, and paragraph craft, but students increasingly choose evidence, test interpretations, compare sources, and revise their own claims. The course moves between literature, poetry, speeches, essays, articles, and multimedia texts so students see reading as a disciplined way to investigate voice, structure, argument, and meaning.

## Chapter 1: Independent Reading Habits and Inference
**Key concepts**: stamina, annotation, inference, evidence trail, ambiguity, objective summary, reading goal, metacognition.

**Essential questions**
- How do strong readers monitor understanding when a text gets difficult?
- What makes an inference reasonable, risky, or unsupported?
- How can several pieces of evidence work together?

**Skills**
- Set reading goals and use annotation systems independently.
- Cite several pieces of evidence to support analysis and inference.
- Separate what the text says from what the reader concludes.
- Summarize accurately without adding opinion.

**Assessment idea**
- Evidence trail response: students answer an inference question using several quoted or paraphrased details and a clear explanation of how the evidence connects.

## Chapter 2: Theme, Character, and Literary Development
**Key concepts**: theme, central idea, motif, conflict, setting, character interaction, motivation, subplot, development, turning point.

**Essential questions**
- How does a theme develop rather than simply appear?
- How do setting, conflict, and character choices shape one another?
- What patterns help readers move from topic to interpretation?

**Skills**
- Track theme development across a story, drama, or novel excerpt.
- Analyze how story elements interact, including setting, plot, and character.
- Identify turning points and explain their effect on meaning.
- Write evidence-based claims about theme and character.

**Assessment idea**
- Theme tracker: students collect evidence across several scenes or chapters and write a paragraph explaining how a theme changes or deepens.

## Chapter 3: Point of View, Voice, and Perspective
**Key concepts**: narrator, speaker, point of view, perspective, reliability, bias, contrast, voice, tone, interiority.

**Essential questions**
- How does point of view control what readers know, feel, or question?
- What happens when characters or narrators see the same event differently?
- How can voice reveal attitude and bias?

**Skills**
- Analyze how authors develop and contrast points of view.
- Notice gaps, bias, reliability, and limits in narration.
- Compare a written text with an audio, staged, filmed, or multimedia version.
- Explain how performance choices affect interpretation.

**Assessment idea**
- Perspective comparison: students compare two viewpoints or two versions of a scene, explaining how voice, emphasis, and evidence change the reader's understanding.

## Chapter 4: Poetry, Form, and Figurative Language
**Key concepts**: form, line break, stanza, repetition, rhyme, sound pattern, allusion, imagery, figurative language, connotation.

**Essential questions**
- How does form shape meaning in a poem or drama?
- What do sound and repetition make readers notice?
- How do allusions and figurative language expand meaning?

**Skills**
- Interpret figurative language, literary allusions, and layered word meanings.
- Analyze how structure contributes to meaning in poems and drama.
- Explain the impact of word choice, repetition, rhyme, and sound.
- Use vocabulary from poetry analysis in discussion and writing.

**Assessment idea**
- Form-and-meaning commentary: students annotate a poem or dramatic excerpt and explain how form, sound, and figurative language work together.

## Chapter 5: Informational Text Structures and Idea Relationships
**Key concepts**: central idea, interaction, text structure, chronology, comparison, cause, problem, solution, section, transition, technical vocabulary.

**Essential questions**
- How do authors organize complex information so readers can follow it?
- How do people, events, and ideas influence one another in nonfiction?
- When does structure make an explanation clearer or more persuasive?

**Skills**
- Determine two or more central ideas and analyze their development.
- Analyze interactions among individuals, events, and ideas.
- Explain how major sections contribute to the whole text.
- Use context and structure to understand technical and academic vocabulary.

**Assessment idea**
- Nonfiction structure map: students diagram a text's sections, central ideas, key interactions, and vocabulary, then explain how the organization supports understanding.

## Chapter 6: Rhetoric, Argument, and Evidence Quality
**Key concepts**: claim, counterclaim, reason, evidence, relevance, sufficiency, sound reasoning, rhetoric, ethos, pathos, logos, audience, purpose.

**Essential questions**
- How does a writer persuade a particular audience?
- When is evidence relevant and sufficient?
- How can readers tell the difference between emotional force and sound reasoning?

**Skills**
- Trace and evaluate arguments and specific claims.
- Assess whether reasoning is sound and evidence is relevant and sufficient.
- Analyze word choice, tone, and rhetorical appeals.
- Identify how an author distinguishes a position from opposing views.

**Assessment idea**
- Argument evaluation memo: students assess a speech, editorial, or article for claim, evidence, reasoning, audience, and rhetorical choices.

## Chapter 7: Vocabulary in Context and Word Precision
**Key concepts**: context, morphology, root, affix, analogy, nuance, connotation, domain vocabulary, register, word choice, allusion.

**Essential questions**
- How do context, word parts, and reference tools work together?
- Why does one precise word often do more work than several vague ones?
- How do writers use allusion and connotation to add layers of meaning?

**Skills**
- Determine meanings of unfamiliar words using context, morphology, and references.
- Analyze connotations among near-synonyms.
- Interpret literary, mythological, historical, and biblical allusions when appropriate to the text.
- Revise written responses for more precise vocabulary.

**Assessment idea**
- Vocabulary-in-action quiz: students explain unfamiliar words from current readings, justify meanings with context, and revise sentences to improve precision and tone.

## Chapter 8: Cross-Text Synthesis and Writing About Reading
**Key concepts**: synthesis, comparison, contrast, source, quotation, paraphrase, claim, evidence, explanation, transition, revision, commentary.

**Essential questions**
- What changes when readers put texts in conversation with each other?
- How can a writer combine evidence without losing a clear claim?
- What makes commentary explain rather than repeat evidence?

**Skills**
- Compare literary and informational texts on a shared theme, issue, or question.
- Select, introduce, and explain evidence from multiple sources.
- Write analytical paragraphs and short essays with clear claims and commentary.
- Revise for organization, transitions, precision, and evidence integration.

**Assessment idea**
- Cross-text response: students write a short essay comparing how two or three texts develop a theme, idea, perspective, or argument.

## Capstone
Create an "Independent Reading and Inquiry Dossier" on a debatable question or enduring theme. Each student selects a literary text, a nonfiction text, and one additional source such as a speech, poem, visual text, or article. The dossier includes an annotation sample, vocabulary-in-context page, theme or point-of-view analysis, argument evaluation, cross-text synthesis essay, and brief oral presentation with questions from peers.

## Assessment Ideas
- Use reading conferences and annotation checks to monitor independence, stamina, inference, and evidence selection.
- Assess literary analysis through theme development, character and setting interactions, point of view, form, word choice, and comparison.
- Assess informational reading through central ideas, text structures, idea relationships, vocabulary, rhetoric, and argument evaluation.
- Review writing about reading for clear claims, relevant evidence, commentary, organization, synthesis, and revision.
- Grade discussions and presentations on preparation, textual grounding, listening, questioning, response to challenge, and academic vocabulary use.
