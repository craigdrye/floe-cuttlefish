# 7th Grade Reading and Vocab
Become an independent, analytical reader who can infer, interpret, compare texts, and write about meaning with evidence.

**ID**: `col-7th-grade-reading-and-vocab` · **Discipline**: English

## Course Aim
This course develops Grade 7 readers into independent, analytical thinkers who can infer, interpret, compare, and write about increasingly complex texts. Aligned to US Common Core ELA Grade 7, it asks students to cite several pieces of textual evidence, track how theme and central ideas develop, analyze point of view, and evaluate arguments across literature, poetry, speeches, essays, articles, and multimedia.

Grade 7 marks a shift in responsibility. Students no longer just locate the answer; they select the best evidence, test interpretations against the whole text, weigh whether reasoning is sound, and revise their own claims. Vocabulary is treated as a reasoning tool: learners use context, word parts, and reference materials to unlock unfamiliar words, then choose precise language in their own writing.

By the end of the course students should read with stamina and metacognition, write clear evidence-based paragraphs and short essays, and put multiple texts in conversation on a shared theme, issue, or question. The aim is durable habits of careful reading and disciplined writing rather than coverage of any single book.

## Course Design Notes
The course moves deliberately between literary and informational texts so students see reading as a single discipline applied to many forms. Teachers model close reading, vocabulary reasoning, and paragraph craft early, then steadily transfer the work to students through annotation, evidence selection, comparison, and revision. Questions emphasize *why* an answer is best, not just which one is correct, so distractors target common misreadings.

Assessment favors short, frequent, evidence-grounded responses over high-stakes recall. Reading conferences and annotation checks monitor independence and stamina; written responses are scored for clear claims, relevant evidence, and commentary that explains rather than repeats. The capstone integrates literature, nonfiction, and a third source into an inquiry dossier so students rehearse the full reading-to-writing cycle.

## Chapter 1: Independent Reading Habits and Inference
**Core questions**
- How do strong readers monitor understanding when a text gets difficult?
- What makes an inference reasonable, risky, or unsupported?
- How can several pieces of evidence work together to support one analysis?

**Key concepts**: stamina, annotation, inference, textual evidence, evidence trail, ambiguity, objective summary, reading goal, metacognition.

**Applied skills**
- Set reading goals and use an annotation system independently.
- Cite several pieces of evidence to support analysis and inference (RL.7.1 / RI.7.1).
- Separate what the text states explicitly from what the reader concludes.
- Summarize accurately without adding opinion.

**Common traps**
- Treating a single quotation as enough when the standard asks for several.
- Confusing a personal opinion with a supported inference.
- Letting a summary drift into evaluation or feelings about the text.

## Chapter 2: Theme, Character, and Literary Development
**Core questions**
- How does a theme develop over a text rather than simply appearing?
- How do setting, conflict, and character choices shape one another?
- What patterns help a reader move from topic to interpretation?

**Key concepts**: theme, central idea, motif, conflict, setting, character interaction, motivation, subplot, turning point, development.

**Applied skills**
- Track theme development across a story, drama, or novel excerpt (RL.7.2).
- Analyze how plot, setting, and character elements interact (RL.7.3).
- Identify turning points and explain their effect on meaning.
- Write evidence-based claims about theme and character.

**Common traps**
- Stating a topic ("friendship") as if it were a theme (a full claim).
- Picking the first scene's mood as the whole theme and missing how it changes.
- Ignoring how setting or a minor choice drives a character's decision.

## Chapter 3: Point of View, Voice, and Perspective
**Core questions**
- How does point of view control what readers know, feel, or question?
- What happens when two characters or narrators see the same event differently?
- How can voice reveal attitude and bias?

**Key concepts**: narrator, speaker, point of view, perspective, reliability, bias, contrast, voice, tone, interiority.

**Applied skills**
- Analyze how an author develops and contrasts points of view (RL.7.6).
- Notice gaps, bias, reliability, and limits in narration.
- Compare a written text with an audio, staged, filmed, or multimedia version (RL.7.7).
- Explain how performance or format choices affect interpretation.

**Common traps**
- Confusing the narrator's view with the author's own position.
- Assuming a first-person narrator is fully reliable.
- Comparing two versions by plot only, ignoring emphasis and tone.

## Chapter 4: Poetry, Form, and Figurative Language
**Core questions**
- How does form shape meaning in a poem or drama?
- What do sound and repetition make a reader notice?
- How do allusions and figurative language expand meaning?

**Key concepts**: form, line break, stanza, repetition, rhyme, sound pattern, alliteration, allusion, imagery, figurative language, connotation.

**Applied skills**
- Interpret figurative and connotative meanings and literary allusions (RL.7.4).
- Analyze how structure contributes to meaning in poems and drama (RL.7.5).
- Explain the impact of word choice, repetition, rhyme, and sound.
- Use poetry-analysis vocabulary in discussion and writing.

**Common traps**
- Reading a metaphor literally instead of for its figurative meaning.
- Treating rhyme or line breaks as decoration with no effect on meaning.
- Missing an allusion and so missing the layer of meaning it adds.

## Chapter 5: Informational Text Structures and Idea Relationships
**Core questions**
- How do authors organize complex information so readers can follow it?
- How do people, events, and ideas influence one another in nonfiction?
- When does structure make an explanation clearer or more persuasive?

**Key concepts**: central idea, interaction, text structure, chronology, comparison, cause-effect, problem-solution, section, transition, technical vocabulary.

**Applied skills**
- Determine two or more central ideas and analyze their development (RI.7.2).
- Analyze interactions among individuals, events, and ideas (RI.7.3).
- Explain how major sections contribute to the whole text (RI.7.5).
- Use context and structure to understand technical and academic vocabulary.

**Common traps**
- Confusing a supporting detail with one of the central ideas.
- Naming a structure (cause-effect) without explaining what it accomplishes.
- Reading sections in isolation instead of how they build the whole.

## Chapter 6: Rhetoric, Argument, and Evidence Quality
**Core questions**
- How does a writer persuade a particular audience?
- When is evidence relevant and sufficient?
- How can readers tell emotional force from sound reasoning?

**Key concepts**: claim, counterclaim, reason, evidence, relevance, sufficiency, sound reasoning, rhetoric, ethos, pathos, logos, audience, purpose.

**Applied skills**
- Trace and evaluate an argument and its specific claims (RI.7.8).
- Assess whether reasoning is sound and evidence is relevant and sufficient.
- Determine an author's point of view or purpose and how it differs from others (RI.7.6).
- Analyze word choice, tone, and rhetorical appeals.

**Common traps**
- Counting the *amount* of evidence rather than judging its relevance.
- Mistaking a strong emotional appeal for a logically sound argument.
- Confusing the author's purpose with a mere summary of the topic.

## Chapter 7: Vocabulary in Context and Word Precision
**Core questions**
- How do context, word parts, and reference tools work together?
- Why does one precise word often do more work than several vague ones?
- How do writers use allusion and connotation to add layers of meaning?

**Key concepts**: context, morphology, root, affix, analogy, nuance, connotation, denotation, domain vocabulary, register, word choice, allusion.

**Applied skills**
- Determine meanings of unfamiliar words using context, morphology, and references (L.7.4).
- Analyze connotations and relationships among near-synonyms (L.7.5).
- Interpret literary, mythological, historical, and biblical allusions in context.
- Revise written responses for more precise vocabulary.

**Common traps**
- Picking a dictionary sense that does not fit the context.
- Treating synonyms as interchangeable, ignoring connotation.
- Misreading a familiar root because the affix changes the meaning.

## Chapter 8: Cross-Text Synthesis and Writing About Reading
**Core questions**
- What changes when readers put texts in conversation with each other?
- How can a writer combine evidence without losing a clear claim?
- What makes commentary explain rather than repeat the evidence?

**Key concepts**: synthesis, comparison, contrast, source, quotation, paraphrase, claim, evidence, explanation, transition, revision, commentary.

**Applied skills**
- Compare literary and informational texts on a shared theme, issue, or question (RI.7.9).
- Select, introduce, and explain evidence from multiple sources.
- Write analytical paragraphs and short essays with clear claims and commentary.
- Revise for organization, transitions, precision, and evidence integration.

**Common traps**
- Summarizing each text separately instead of synthesizing across them.
- Dropping a quotation in without introducing or explaining it.
- Writing commentary that just restates the quotation in new words.

## Capstone
Create an "Independent Reading and Inquiry Dossier" on a debatable question or enduring theme. Each student selects a literary text, a nonfiction text, and one additional source such as a speech, poem, visual text, or article. The dossier includes an annotation sample, a vocabulary-in-context page, a theme or point-of-view analysis, an argument evaluation, a cross-text synthesis essay, and a brief oral presentation that fields questions from peers. The dossier is scored on independence and stamina, quality of evidence selection, soundness of reasoning, vocabulary precision, and the clarity of claims and commentary.
