# Precalculus Collections
The bridge from Algebra 2 to calculus - functions, trigonometry, vectors, matrices, analytic geometry, and a first look at limits, taught so that students understand why each method works.

**ID**: `col-get-ready-for-precalculus` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
Precalculus consolidates and extends the algebra and geometry of earlier high-school years into the toolkit a student needs for calculus and other quantitative study. It deepens command of functions and their transformations, develops trigonometry from the unit circle through identities and oblique triangles, and introduces vectors, complex numbers in polar form, matrices, and the polar and parametric ways of describing curves. The course closes with an informal treatment of limits and rates of change so that the central ideas of calculus arrive as a natural next step rather than a sudden break.

The emphasis throughout is on structure, representation, and justification. Students learn to recognise which family a function belongs to, to predict end behaviour and asymptotes before computing, and to choose the representation - rectangular, polar, parametric, vector, or matrix - that makes a problem easiest to reason about. Periodicity, rate, dimension, and approximation recur as organising themes, and every technique is connected to a reason it works rather than memorised as an isolated rule. Modelling is treated as a core skill: students fit and interpret growth, decay, and periodic models and judge which candidate function best fits a context.

By the end of the course a student can analyse and transform functions across families; convert fluently between degrees and radians and evaluate exact trigonometric values; verify identities and solve trigonometric equations on stated intervals; solve triangles with the Laws of Sines and Cosines including the ambiguous case; work with vectors, complex numbers, and matrices; describe a curve in more than one coordinate system; and estimate a limit or an instantaneous rate of change graphically, numerically, and algebraically. These are exactly the foundations on which a first calculus course is built.

## Course Design Notes
Treat precalculus as a bridge course rather than a fresh start. Algebra 2 fluency (factoring, exponent and logarithm rules, function notation, solving and graphing) is assumed and maintained through retrieval practice embedded in new contexts. Route questions here when they involve advanced function analysis, trigonometric identities or equations, the unit circle, vectors, complex numbers in polar form, polar or parametric representations, matrices and systems, conic sections, or an informal first encounter with limits and rates of change. Demand precise notation - domain restrictions, the distinction between an equation in x and one in y for asymptotes, exact versus approximate trigonometric values, and correct interval notation - and ask students to state restrictions and justify each transformation rather than only producing a final value. Worked examples should foreground "explain why", "state the restriction", and "which representation is better here" prompts so that reasoning is assessed alongside computation. Chapter titles below match the labels used in the question bank so that practice and study stay aligned.

## Chapter 1: Function Families and Transformation Review
- **Core questions**: Which parent function underlies a given graph or equation, and how has it been transformed? When does a function have an inverse, and how is it verified? What restrictions on the domain does a composition introduce?
- **Key concepts**: parent functions and their graphs; vertical and horizontal shifts, stretches, and reflections; composition of functions; one-to-one functions and inverses; polynomial and rational behaviour; exponential and logarithmic functions; domain and range; precise function notation.
- **Applied skills**: describe a transformed function from its equation and sketch it; find an inverse and confirm it with composition; compare function families from graphs, tables, and equations; solve equations while tracking domain restrictions.
- **Common traps**: applying horizontal transformations in the intuitive but wrong direction (inside changes act oppositely); assuming every function has an inverse without checking one-to-one; forgetting the domain restriction created by a composition; confusing f(x)+k with f(x+k).

## Chapter 2: Polynomial, Rational, Exponential, and Logarithmic Models
- **Core questions**: What do the zeros and their multiplicities tell you about a graph's shape? Where are the asymptotes and holes of a rational function? Which model - linear, exponential, or logistic - best fits a data set?
- **Key concepts**: zeros and multiplicity; end behaviour from degree and leading coefficient; vertical, horizontal, and slant asymptotes; removable discontinuities (holes); sign charts and polynomial/rational inequalities; exponential and logarithmic models; parameter interpretation; model selection.
- **Applied skills**: sketch a polynomial or rational function from its key features; solve an inequality with a sign chart; fit and interpret a growth, decay, or logistic model with technology; read the meaning of a model's parameters in context.
- **Common traps**: reading a vertical asymptote off a numerator zero or vice versa; ignoring multiplicity when deciding whether a graph crosses or touches an axis; flipping the leading-coefficient ratio when finding a horizontal asymptote; mistaking a hole for an asymptote.

## Chapter 3: Trigonometric Foundations and Periodic Functions
- **Core questions**: How are radian measure and the unit circle connected? What are the exact values of the trigonometric functions at the standard angles? How do amplitude, period, phase shift, and midline change a sinusoidal graph?
- **Key concepts**: radian and degree measure; the unit circle; sine, cosine, tangent and the reciprocal functions; amplitude, period, phase shift, and midline; graphs of transformed trigonometric functions; circular and periodic motion; periodic modelling.
- **Applied skills**: convert between degrees and radians; evaluate exact trigonometric values from the unit circle; graph y = A sin(Bx + C) + D and read its parameters; model seasonal, wave, or rotational behaviour and make a prediction.
- **Common traps**: confusing the roles of B (period) and A (amplitude) in a sinusoid; mixing degree and radian modes; mis-signing a phase shift; reporting an approximate decimal when an exact value is expected.

## Chapter 4: Analytic Trigonometry and Oblique Triangles
- **Core questions**: Which identity turns one side of an equation into the other? How many solutions does a trigonometric equation have on a given interval? Which law solves a non-right triangle, and when is the answer ambiguous?
- **Key concepts**: Pythagorean, reciprocal, quotient, and even-odd identities; sum, difference, double-angle, and half-angle identities; solving trigonometric equations on restricted and general intervals; the Law of Sines; the Law of Cosines; the ambiguous (SSA) case.
- **Applied skills**: verify an identity by transforming one side; solve a trigonometric equation and report all solutions on a stated interval; choose and apply the correct law to solve a triangle; detect and resolve the ambiguous case.
- **Common traps**: dividing both sides by a trigonometric factor and losing solutions; forgetting the full period when listing general solutions; using the Law of Sines blindly in the ambiguous case; sign errors in double- and half-angle formulas.

## Chapter 5: Vectors, Complex Numbers, and Polar Form
- **Core questions**: How are a vector's magnitude and direction recovered from its components? What does the dot product measure? How is a complex number written in polar form, and what does DeMoivre's Theorem do?
- **Key concepts**: vector magnitude and direction; component form; vector addition and scalar multiplication; the dot product, the angle between vectors, and projections; applications to force and velocity; the complex plane; polar (trigonometric) form of a complex number; DeMoivre's Theorem.
- **Applied skills**: add, subtract, and scale vectors and resolve a vector into components; compute a dot product and the angle between two vectors; convert a complex number between rectangular and polar form; use DeMoivre's Theorem to find a power or root.
- **Common traps**: treating the dot product as a vector instead of a scalar; dropping a sign when summing component products; placing a complex number in the wrong quadrant when finding its argument; confusing magnitude with the squared magnitude.

## Chapter 6: Polar, Parametric, and Analytic Geometry
- **Core questions**: How do you move between rectangular, parametric, and polar descriptions of the same curve? What does a parameter add that a rectangular equation hides? How are a conic's features read from its equation?
- **Key concepts**: parametric equations and eliminating the parameter; polar coordinates and polar graphs; conic sections (parabola, ellipse, hyperbola); focus-directrix definitions; standard forms and key features; choosing a representation.
- **Applied skills**: convert among rectangular, parametric, and polar forms; graph a parametric path and a polar curve; identify a conic and its centre, foci, and axes from its equation; argue which representation suits a given problem.
- **Common traps**: losing direction or domain information when eliminating a parameter; mismatching r-theta points with x-y intuition; reading a hyperbola as an ellipse because of a sign; misplacing a conic's centre after an incomplete square.

## Chapter 7: Matrices, Systems, and Discrete Structure
- **Core questions**: When can two matrices be multiplied, and what does the product mean? How do matrices solve a linear system? What does a determinant tell you about invertibility?
- **Key concepts**: matrix operations and dimensions; the identity and inverse matrices; determinants; systems of equations; row operations and reduced row-echelon form; linear transformations; transition matrices and applications.
- **Applied skills**: multiply conformable matrices and explain why dimensions must match; solve a system with an inverse matrix or row reduction; interpret a determinant (including a zero determinant); model a network or repeated process with a matrix.
- **Common traps**: assuming matrix multiplication is commutative; multiplying matrices whose inner dimensions do not match; treating a zero determinant as a unique solution; swapping rows and columns when stating dimensions.

## Chapter 8: Limits, Rates, and Calculus Readiness
- **Core questions**: What value does a function approach near a point, and is that the same as its value there? How does the average rate of change become an instantaneous rate? What does a difference quotient have to do with a tangent line?
- **Key concepts**: informal limits; one-sided behaviour; continuity and types of discontinuity; average rate of change; the difference quotient; secant and tangent lines; instantaneous rate as a limit; an early preview of area accumulation.
- **Applied skills**: estimate a limit from a graph and from a table; simplify an expression to evaluate a removable discontinuity; compute average rates over shrinking intervals and interpret the trend; connect a difference quotient to the slope of a tangent line.
- **Common traps**: assuming a limit equals the function value when there is a hole or jump; concluding a limit exists when one-sided limits disagree; confusing average rate of change with instantaneous rate; algebra slips when simplifying a difference quotient.

## Capstone
Students build a multi-representation model of a complex situation - for example motion, periodic change, navigation, signal behaviour, or a constrained design. The final product should include a justified choice of functions or representations (rectangular, polar, parametric, vector, or matrix), use of technology, analytic checks of the work, interpretation of the model's parameters in context, and a short reflection on what calculus would add to the analysis. The capstone is assessed on mathematical accuracy, fluency in moving between representations, the quality of modelling decisions, clarity of communication, and readiness for the central ideas of calculus.
