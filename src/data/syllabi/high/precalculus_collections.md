# Precalculus Collections
**Covers**: `col-get-ready-for-precalculus`, `col-precalculus`, `col-get-ready-for-ap-calculus`
**Discipline**: Maths | **Year**: 12 (ages 16-18)

## Course Aim
Prepare students for calculus and advanced quantitative study by deepening their command of functions, trigonometry, analytic geometry, vectors, matrices, and introductory limits. Students learn to analyze behavior, choose efficient representations, model change, and explain why a method works before relying on it.

## Course Design Notes
Treat precalculus as a bridge course: Algebra 2 skills are maintained, but every unit asks students to reason about structure, rate, periodicity, dimension, and approximation. Route questions here when they involve advanced function analysis, trigonometric identities or equations, vectors, polar or parametric representations, matrices, limits, or technology-supported modeling.

## Chapter 1: Function Families and Transformation Review
- **Key concepts**: Parent functions, transformations, composition, inverse functions, polynomial and rational behavior, exponential and logarithmic functions, domain restrictions, and notation precision.
- **Practice questions**: Analyze transformed functions; find and verify inverses; compare function families from graphs and equations; solve equations while checking domains.
- **Student output**: A function-analysis dossier for one selected function family, including transformations, intercepts, end behavior, inverse relationships, and model contexts.

## Chapter 2: Polynomial, Rational, Exponential, and Logarithmic Models
- **Key concepts**: Zeros and multiplicity, asymptotes, holes, end behavior, sign charts, exponential and logarithmic models, parameter interpretation, and model selection.
- **Practice questions**: Sketch rational and polynomial functions; solve inequalities using sign analysis; fit growth, decay, and logistic models with technology.
- **Student output**: A model-comparison memo that evaluates at least two candidate function types for the same dataset or scenario.

## Chapter 3: Trigonometric Foundations and Periodic Functions
- **Key concepts**: Radian measure, unit circle, sine, cosine, tangent, reciprocal functions, amplitude, period, phase shift, midline, circular motion, and periodic modeling.
- **Practice questions**: Convert between degrees and radians; evaluate exact trig values; graph transformed trig functions; model seasonal, wave, or rotational behavior.
- **Student output**: A periodic-model poster or notebook page connecting a graph, equation, parameter interpretation, and one prediction.

## Chapter 4: Analytic Trigonometry and Oblique Triangles
- **Key concepts**: Pythagorean, reciprocal, quotient, even-odd, sum, difference, double-angle, and half-angle identities; trigonometric equations; Law of Sines; Law of Cosines; ambiguous case.
- **Practice questions**: Verify identities; solve trig equations on restricted intervals and in general form; solve triangles from real measurements.
- **Student output**: A trig-proof and triangle-solving portfolio with annotated steps, restrictions, and diagrams where appropriate.

## Chapter 5: Vectors, Complex Numbers, and Polar Form
- **Key concepts**: Vector magnitude and direction, component form, dot product, projections, applications to force and velocity, complex plane, polar form, and DeMoivre's Theorem.
- **Practice questions**: Add and scale vectors; compute dot products and angles; resolve vectors into components; convert complex numbers between rectangular and polar forms.
- **Student output**: A vector application brief using diagrams and calculations to analyze motion, navigation, force, or another two-dimensional context.

## Chapter 6: Polar, Parametric, and Analytic Geometry
- **Key concepts**: Parametric equations, eliminating parameters, polar coordinates, polar graphs, conic sections, focus-directrix definitions, and representation choice.
- **Practice questions**: Convert among rectangular, parametric, and polar forms; graph parametric paths and polar curves; identify conic features from equations.
- **Student output**: A representation study comparing rectangular, parametric, and polar descriptions of a path or curve, with advantages and limitations of each.

## Chapter 7: Matrices, Systems, and Discrete Structure
- **Key concepts**: Matrix operations, identity and inverse matrices, determinants, systems of equations, row operations, linear transformations, transition matrices, and applications.
- **Practice questions**: Multiply matrices; solve systems using matrices; interpret determinants; use matrices to represent networks, transformations, or repeated processes.
- **Student output**: A systems-solving lab that compares substitution, elimination, and matrix methods for a multi-variable problem.

## Chapter 8: Limits, Rates, and Calculus Readiness
- **Key concepts**: Informal limits, one-sided behavior, continuity, average rate of change, instantaneous rate intuition, difference quotients, secant lines, tangent lines, and area accumulation previews.
- **Practice questions**: Estimate limits graphically and numerically; simplify expressions to evaluate removable discontinuities; interpret average rates over shrinking intervals.
- **Student output**: A calculus-readiness notebook entry explaining a limit or rate question with a table, graph, algebraic work, and plain-language interpretation.

## Capstone
Students build a multi-representation model of a complex situation such as motion, periodic change, navigation, signal behavior, or constrained design. The final product should include a justified choice of functions or representations, use of technology, analytic checks, interpretation of parameters, and a brief reflection on what calculus would add.

## Assessment Ideas
- Frequent retrieval checks on Algebra 2 prerequisites embedded in new precalculus contexts.
- Identity and equation quizzes that require students to state restrictions and justify transformations.
- Technology-supported modeling tasks scored on representation choice, parameter interpretation, and evidence from residuals or graphical behavior.
- Oral or written explanations of why rectangular, polar, parametric, vector, or matrix representations are useful for a given problem.
- A capstone rubric covering mathematical accuracy, representation fluency, modeling decisions, communication, and readiness for calculus concepts.
