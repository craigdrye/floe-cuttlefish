# Macroeconomics
A graph-driven high-school course on how the whole economy behaves — output, jobs, prices, money, and policy.

**ID**: `macroeconomics` · **Discipline**: Economics

## Course Aim
This course teaches students to analyze the economy as a whole rather than a single market. Students learn to measure national output and the price level, diagnose unemployment and the business cycle, model the economy with aggregate demand and aggregate supply, and trace how fiscal and monetary policy ripple through interest rates, investment, output, and prices. The throughline is reasoning: choosing the right model for a scenario, shifting the correct curve, reading the new equilibrium, and explaining the cause-and-effect chain in clear prose.

The course is built so it can support AP Macroeconomics preparation or serve as a standalone, non-AP economics sequence. It mirrors the College Board content sequence — measurement, the financial sector, stabilization policy, long-run growth, and the open economy — and emphasizes the same skills the AP exam rewards: precise graph labeling, correct curve manipulation, sound quantitative work (multipliers, real values, money creation), and tight written interpretation. The AP exam pairs 60 multiple-choice questions (about two-thirds of the score) with three free-response questions (one long, two short), so the disciplined habits this course builds transfer directly.

Throughout, the emphasis is on transferable analysis over memorized definitions. By the end, students should be able to read a set of macro indicators, place an economy in the business cycle, model a shock on AD/AS and the money market, recommend a policy, and predict its short-run and long-run consequences — including effects on the exchange rate and net exports.

## Course Design Notes
The chapter order moves from measurement to models to policy to the long run, so it can run alongside an AP Macroeconomics course or stand on its own. Chapters 1-2 establish economic thinking and the core indicators (GDP, CPI, unemployment, the business cycle). Chapter 3 introduces the AD/AS model and the Phillips curve. Chapters 4-6 develop policy: fiscal policy and multipliers, money and banking, and monetary policy. Chapter 7 covers long-run growth and the open economy, and Chapter 8 is a policy studio that integrates everything across multiple linked graphs.

Every chapter pairs a concept with at least one graph and, where relevant, one calculation, because exam points are won or lost on correctly labeled axes, correctly directed shifts, and correctly set-up arithmetic with units. The "Common traps" sections target the errors graders see most often — confusing a shift with a movement along a curve, inverting a multiplier, reasoning from absolute rather than comparative advantage, or reversing the direction of monetary and exchange-rate effects. The course is age-appropriate for the high-school tier: it assumes no calculus, builds quantitative skill gradually, and treats the graph as the primary thinking tool.

## Chapter 1: Economic Thinking and Measurement
- **Core questions**: What is the opportunity cost of a choice? What counts in GDP and what is excluded? How does the difference between real and nominal values change the story the data tells?
- **Key concepts**: scarcity, opportunity cost, the production possibilities curve (PPC), comparative versus absolute advantage, gains from trade, the circular-flow model, GDP and the expenditure approach (Y = C + I + G + NX), real versus nominal GDP, the GDP deflator, GDP per capita.
- **Applied skills**: build and label a PPC and read opportunity cost from it; classify a transaction as counted or excluded from GDP; assign comparative advantage from opportunity-cost ratios; convert nominal to real values using a price index.
- **Common traps**: treating opportunity cost as only money spent; counting transfer payments, financial transactions, or used-goods sales in GDP; assigning advantage from absolute productivity rather than relative opportunity cost.

## Chapter 2: Inflation, Unemployment, and Business Cycles
- **Core questions**: How is the price level measured and how do we compute inflation? Which type of unemployment is present in a scenario? Where in the business cycle is the economy, and what does that imply?
- **Key concepts**: the Consumer Price Index (CPI), inflation rate, the GDP deflator, purchasing power and real income, the labor force and labor-force participation rate, the unemployment rate, frictional/structural/cyclical unemployment, the natural rate and full employment, the business cycle (peak, recession, trough, expansion).
- **Applied skills**: compute an inflation rate from index numbers; calculate and interpret the unemployment and participation rates; classify an unemployment type from a description; locate the economy on the business cycle from indicator data.
- **Common traps**: confusing the CPI with the GDP deflator; labeling recession-driven job loss as frictional; counting discouraged workers inside the labor force; treating a one-item price jump as economy-wide inflation.

## Chapter 3: Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply
- **Core questions**: What shifts aggregate demand versus aggregate supply? After a shock, what happens to real output and the price level? How does the economy self-correct over time?
- **Key concepts**: aggregate demand (AD), short-run aggregate supply (SRAS), long-run aggregate supply (LRAS), recessionary and inflationary gaps, demand shocks, supply shocks, self-correction toward full employment, the short-run and long-run Phillips curve, stagflation.
- **Applied skills**: shift the correct curve for a stated shock and read the new equilibrium; identify a recessionary or inflationary gap relative to LRAS; map an AD/AS outcome onto the Phillips curve; show how SRAS adjusts in the long run.
- **Common traps**: shifting AS when only AD has changed (and vice versa); treating the long-run Phillips curve as a usable trade-off; drawing self-correction in the wrong direction; mislabeling a supply shock as a demand shock.

## Chapter 4: Fiscal Policy and Multipliers
- **Core questions**: Which fiscal policy closes a recessionary or inflationary gap, and by how much? How large is the multiplier effect? What long-run tradeoff does deficit-financed policy create?
- **Key concepts**: government spending, taxes, and transfers; the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) and to save (MPS); the spending multiplier 1/(1 − MPC); the tax multiplier −MPC/MPS; discretionary fiscal policy versus automatic stabilizers; budget deficits, debt, and crowding out.
- **Applied skills**: compute the spending and tax multipliers; size the fiscal change needed to close a stated gap; distinguish discretionary action from an automatic stabilizer; trace a deficit through the loanable funds market to higher real rates and reduced investment.
- **Common traps**: inverting the multiplier (dividing by MPC instead of MPS); treating the tax and spending multipliers as equal; calling an automatic stabilizer a discretionary policy; ignoring the crowding-out cost of debt-financed spending.

## Chapter 5: Money, Banking, and Financial Markets
- **Core questions**: How do banks create money, and how much? What sets the nominal interest rate in the money market? How does saving fund investment in the loanable funds market?
- **Key concepts**: functions of money, fractional-reserve banking, required and excess reserves, the money multiplier and cash drains, M1, money demand and the money market, the loanable funds market, nominal versus real interest rates, the Fisher relation.
- **Applied skills**: compute maximum money creation from a new deposit and reserve ratio; graph the money market and find the equilibrium interest rate; link the money market to loanable funds and investment; convert between nominal and real interest rates.
- **Common traps**: using the simple multiplier when leakages or excess reserves exist; confusing the money market with the loanable funds market; mixing up nominal and real rates; assuming more money raises rather than lowers the nominal interest rate.

## Chapter 6: Monetary Policy
- **Core questions**: What should the central bank do to address a gap? How does open market operations transmit to interest rates, investment, AD, and output? Why is money neutral in the long run?
- **Key concepts**: the central bank, open market operations, the discount/policy rate, the reserve requirement, interest on reserve balances, expansionary versus contractionary policy, the policy transmission chain, the quantity theory of money (MV = PY), long-run monetary neutrality.
- **Applied skills**: choose the correct tool and direction for a stated goal; build the transmission chain from a policy action to output and the price level; show the money-market and AD/AS effects together; apply the quantity theory to a money-supply change.
- **Common traps**: thinking the central bank buys bonds to tighten; reversing the reserve-requirement effect on the money supply; expecting monetary policy to change real output in the long run; skipping the interest-rate step between the tool and AD.

## Chapter 7: Long-Run Growth and International Economics
- **Core questions**: What raises long-run aggregate supply? Why does a currency appreciate or depreciate? How does an exchange-rate change affect net exports and the trade balance?
- **Key concepts**: productivity, physical capital, human capital, technology, diminishing returns, long-run growth policy; the balance of payments (current and financial accounts); the foreign exchange market, currency appreciation and depreciation, net exports, capital mobility.
- **Applied skills**: shift LRAS for a stated growth source; connect a real-interest-rate change to capital flows and the exchange rate; predict the net-export effect of an appreciation; read a balance-of-payments identity (current and financial accounts offset).
- **Common traps**: confusing physical with human capital; assuming appreciation helps exports; reversing the capital-flow direction after a rate change; mistaking a current-account deficit for an error rather than an offsetting financial-account surplus.

## Chapter 8: Macro Policy Studio
- **Core questions**: Which policy mix fits the diagnosed problem? What unintended consequence might appear? How do multiple graphs (AD/AS, money market, loanable funds, forex) connect in one chain?
- **Key concepts**: the fiscal-monetary policy mix, crowding out, inflation expectations, graphing precision, data interpretation, multi-graph causal reasoning, written explanation under time pressure.
- **Applied skills**: produce fully labeled diagrams quickly; chain a shock across AD/AS, the money market, loanable funds, and the exchange rate; set up calculations with correct units; write tight cause-and-effect explanations that earn each point.
- **Common traps**: leaving axes or curves unlabeled; explaining the wrong direction of change; skipping the "therefore" step that ties a graph to the outcome; ignoring the long-run consequence of a short-run fix.

## Capstone
Analyze a fictional national economy facing a shock. Submit, as one portfolio: a read-out of the indicators (GDP, unemployment type, inflation) with a business-cycle diagnosis; a labeled AD/AS diagram showing the shock and the resulting gap; a money-market diagram with the recommended monetary response and its transmission chain to output; a fiscal-policy recommendation with the relevant multiplier calculation and a note on crowding out; an open-economy analysis linking interest rates or the exchange rate to net exports; and AP-style written explanations for each. The portfolio should demonstrate correct model selection, accurate manipulation, sound numerical work, and clear interpretation of short-run and long-run consequences.

## Assessment Ideas
- Graphs graded on labels, correct shifts, and equilibrium identification.
- Calculation tasks graded on setup, formula choice, and units (multipliers, real values, money creation, inflation rate).
- Policy memos graded on model-to-outcome reasoning and tradeoff awareness.
- Capstone graded on indicator interpretation, policy chains, and short-run versus long-run analysis.
