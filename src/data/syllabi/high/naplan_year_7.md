# NAPLAN Prep (Year 7)
Calm, flexible test readiness for the Australian national assessment across reading, writing, language conventions, and numeracy.

**ID**: `naplanYear7` · **Discipline**: Exam Prep

## Course Aim
This course prepares Year 7 students for NAPLAN by building durable, transferable skills rather than test tricks. Students learn to diagnose what a question is really asking across the four assessment domains — reading, writing, conventions of language, and numeracy — and to back their answers with evidence and clean working. The aim is calm, flexible test readiness: students who can read a stem carefully, eliminate wrong options, plan a piece of writing under time, and check whether a numerical answer is reasonable.

NAPLAN sits at the bridge between primary and lower-secondary expectations, and the skills it samples map directly onto the Australian Curriculum (Version 9.0) English and Mathematics achievement standards for Year 7. So the work here doubles as foundation-building: comparing texts, controlling sentence and paragraph structure, applying spelling and grammar rules, operating with integers, fractions, decimals and percentages, solving simple linear equations, and reading data displays. Students leave with stronger secondary literacy and numeracy, not just a higher band on one assessment.

Because NAPLAN is delivered online and is partly adaptive, the course also treats digital-test behaviour as a learnable skill. Students rehearse reading the screen carefully, using scratch working, flagging uncertain items, watching units, and recovering after a hard question instead of spiralling. The throughline is confidence built from competence: short diagnostic checks, explicit error review, model answers, timed rehearsals, and reflection on the next three targets.

## Course Design Notes
Keep practice diagnostic and confidence-building. Each cycle should pair a short skill check with explicit error review so students see exactly which mistake type cost them marks. Use ACARA NAPLAN-style reading, writing, conventions, and numeracy tasks, and connect every skill back to the Australian Curriculum Year 7 standards so the learning outlasts the test. Teach the online format as learning behaviour, not anxiety management: read the screen, use scratch working, flag and return, check units, and manage stamina across a 40–65 minute session. Marking should reward method and reasoning (evidence selection, answer elimination, clear working, reasonableness checks), not just the final letter or number. Age-appropriateness matters: keep contexts concrete and familiar, and keep numeracy demand at genuine Year 7 level — two-step equations, composite areas, simple probability — rather than drifting upward.

## Chapter 1: NAPLAN Map, Mindset, and Digital Strategy
- **Core questions**: What domain and skill is this item testing? Which details on the screen matter before you answer? When should a student estimate, skip, flag, check, or move on?
- **Key concepts**: the four domains; the online and partly adaptive format; question stems; time awareness; flagging and returning; scratch working; calculator vs non-calculator sections; stamina; error logs.
- **Applied skills**: classifying an item by domain and skill; planning a per-section time budget; building personal digital-test routines; keeping a running error log.
- **Common traps**: rushing into the answers before reading the stem; never flagging, so one hard item drains the whole section; treating the calculator section as a licence to skip estimation.

## Chapter 2: Reading for Main Ideas and Evidence
- **Core questions**: Which option is directly supported by the text? What is the best summary of the passage? Which detail actually proves the inference rather than merely sounding related?
- **Key concepts**: literal meaning vs inference; main idea vs supporting detail; summarising; evidence selection; context clues; text purpose; answer elimination.
- **Applied skills**: locating the line that supports a claim; writing a one-sentence main idea; distinguishing a proof detail from a plausible-but-unsupported distractor.
- **Common traps**: choosing the option that repeats a word from the text without matching its meaning; confusing a true-but-irrelevant statement with the answer; over-inferring beyond what the text states.

## Chapter 3: Reading Across Text Types
- **Core questions**: How does the author's tone shape the reader's response? What does an image, heading, or graph add to the written text? How do two texts treat the same issue differently?
- **Key concepts**: narrative, poem, article, report, review, and visual text; audience and purpose; tone; bias; figurative language; layout features; cross-text comparison.
- **Applied skills**: identifying purpose and audience from features; reading information from a caption, heading, or graph; comparing how two texts frame one topic.
- **Common traps**: judging tone from a single emotive word; ignoring visual and layout information; assuming two texts agree just because they share a subject.

## Chapter 4: Writing Planning and Text Structure
- **Core questions**: What does this writing prompt ask you to create? Which plan has the strongest structure for the audience? Where would a new paragraph improve control?
- **Key concepts**: persuasive vs narrative purpose; unpacking a prompt; audience; thesis or controlling idea; orientation–complication–resolution; paragraphing; cohesion; planning under time; conclusion.
- **Applied skills**: turning a prompt into a clear position or storyline; sketching a timed plan with topic sentences or key events; choosing paragraph breaks that aid the reader.
- **Common traps**: starting to write with no plan and losing structure; ignoring the named genre or audience; one undifferentiated block of text with no paragraphing.

## Chapter 5: Writing Craft, Voice, and Revision
- **Core questions**: Which word choice best suits the audience and purpose? How can a sentence be varied for emphasis? Which revision makes the argument or narrative clearer?
- **Key concepts**: vocabulary precision; sentence variety; cohesion; imagery; characterisation; tension; persuasive devices; modality; counterargument; editing and proofreading.
- **Applied skills**: replacing a vague word with a precise one; varying sentence length and openers; revising a paragraph for clarity and acting on feedback.
- **Common traps**: using big words inaccurately; repetitive sentence openings; editing only for spelling while ignoring structure and cohesion.

## Chapter 6: Language Conventions in Context
- **Core questions**: Which spelling follows the pattern and meaning? Which sentence is grammatically correct? Where should punctuation be added to clarify the idea?
- **Key concepts**: spelling patterns, prefixes and suffixes; homophones; subject–verb agreement; tense consistency; pronoun reference; commas, apostrophes, quotation marks, colons, and semicolons.
- **Applied skills**: applying a spelling rule to an unfamiliar word; fixing agreement and tense errors; placing apostrophes and commas to remove ambiguity.
- **Common traps**: confusing common homophones (their/there/they're, its/it's); adding apostrophes to plurals; comma splices joining two independent clauses.

## Chapter 7: Numeracy — Number, Algebra, and Measurement
- **Core questions**: How do you solve a two-step equation? How do percentage change and ratio compare? What is the area of a composite shape, and is the answer reasonable?
- **Key concepts**: integers; equivalence and operations with fractions, decimals, and percentages; ratios and rates; order of operations; index notation; variables and expressions; simple linear equations; perimeter, area, and volume; angles on parallel lines; units.
- **Applied skills**: solving two-step equations and substituting into expressions; converting between fractions, decimals, and percentages; finding areas of composite figures and volumes of rectangular prisms; checking reasonableness by estimation.
- **Common traps**: breaking order of operations; treating a percentage of a percentage additively; forgetting or mismatching units; misreading angle relationships on parallel lines cut by a transversal.

## Chapter 8: Numeracy — Data, Probability, and Mixed Problem Solving
- **Core questions**: What does this data display tell you? How do two data sets compare? What is the probability from this table, and which information do you actually need for a multistep problem?
- **Key concepts**: tables, column graphs, line graphs, and stem-and-leaf plots; mean, median, and range; outliers; likelihood; theoretical vs experimental probability; multistep reasoning.
- **Applied skills**: reading and comparing data displays; computing mean, median, and range; finding a probability from a sample space or table; selecting the relevant numbers in a multistep word problem.
- **Common traps**: confusing mean and median; misreading a graph's scale or axis; assuming experimental results must equal the theoretical probability; using every number in a problem instead of only the relevant ones.

## Capstone
Students assemble a Year 7 NAPLAN readiness portfolio. It includes a reading response set (with one corrected multiple-choice explanation that names the elimination reasoning), a persuasive or narrative writing task with a plan and revision notes, a language conventions error log with rules in the student's own words, a mixed numeracy set with clear working and reasonableness checks, and a digital-test strategy reflection naming the student's strongest habits and next three targets. The portfolio is graded on skill growth and correction quality rather than a single mock-test score, so students leave able to explain not just what they got wrong but how they will fix it.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short diagnostic checks across reading, writing, conventions, and numeracy before and after each cycle.
- Reading responses marked for evidence, inference, text-type awareness, and answer-elimination reasoning.
- Writing tasks assessed with NAPLAN-style criteria: audience, text structure, ideas, persuasive devices or narrative craft, vocabulary, cohesion, paragraphing, sentence structure, punctuation, and spelling.
- Numeracy tasks marked for method, accuracy, representation, units, and reasonableness checks.
- Digital practice reflections focused on time use, flagging, rereading, scratch working, and recovery from uncertainty.
- Capstone graded on skill growth, correction quality, confidence-building habits, and readiness to explain next steps.
