# Geometry Collections
Reason, construct, prove, and model space — from rigid motions and congruence to circles, coordinate proof, trigonometry, and 3D measurement.

**ID**: `col-geometry` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
This course builds a high-school geometry that treats reasoning, construction, calculation, and modeling as connected tools rather than separate skills. Students learn when a task calls for a precise diagram, when it calls for a proof, when it calls for a measurement, and how to communicate geometric thinking so another person can follow it. The aim is calm fluency: confidence with definitions and theorems, and the judgment to choose the right tool for a problem.

The sequence follows the Common Core high-school geometry strand, opening with the logical foundations of proof and the rigid-motion definition of congruence, then moving through formal triangle and parallel-line proof, similarity and dilations, right-triangle trigonometry, circles, coordinate (analytic) geometry, and three-dimensional measurement and modeling. Algebra 1 skills — solving equations, working with coordinates, manipulating expressions — are revisited continuously, because coordinate proof, circle equations, and trigonometric solving all depend on them.

By the end, students should be able to state and apply the major theorems of plane geometry, justify claims with valid two-column, paragraph, or coordinate proofs, solve right triangles and circle problems, and use geometric modeling to estimate and critique real designs. Throughout, assessment language stays explicit so students always know whether they are being asked to construct, calculate, prove, model, or explain.

## Course Design Notes
Use the Common Core high-school geometry standards as the spine, with repeated retrieval of Algebra 1 for coordinates, equations, and modeling. Route questions here when they involve proof, congruence, transformations, similarity, circles, coordinate geometry, measurement, right-triangle trigonometry, or spatial modeling. Keep the demand explicit: every task should signal whether it wants a construction, a calculation, a proof, a model, or an explanation. Diagrams are first-class — students should habitually mark given information and identify what theorem the markings unlock. The course covers `col-geometry`, `col-eureka-math-geometry`, and `col-get-ready-for-geometry`.

## Chapter 1: Geometry Foundations and Proof Habits
- **Core questions**: What makes a definition precise? When does a single counterexample settle a claim? How do conditional statements and their converses differ?
- **Key concepts**: Points, lines, planes, segments, rays, angles; parallel and perpendicular lines; definitions, conjectures, counterexamples, conditional statements (and converse, inverse, contrapositive); proof structure; vertical, complementary, and supplementary angle relationships.
- **Applied skills**: Name and classify geometric objects, write converse statements, find counterexamples, and supply missing reasons in short proofs.
- **Common traps**: Treating a few confirming examples as proof; confusing a statement with its converse; assuming a diagram "looks" perpendicular without given marks.

## Chapter 2: Transformations and Congruence
- **Core questions**: Which transformations preserve distance and angle? How can a sequence of rigid motions show two figures are congruent?
- **Key concepts**: Translations, rotations, reflections; rigid motions; composition of transformations; symmetry; the rigid-motion definition of congruence; corresponding parts; transformation notation and coordinate rules.
- **Applied skills**: Draw images under given transformations, describe a sequence of rigid motions mapping one figure to another, and decide congruence from a diagram or coordinates.
- **Common traps**: Confusing clockwise and counterclockwise rotation; mixing up reflection lines; thinking a dilation is a rigid motion.

## Chapter 3: Triangles, Parallels, and Formal Proof
- **Core questions**: How do transversal angle relationships drive proofs? Which combinations of parts guarantee triangle congruence — and which do not?
- **Key concepts**: Angle relationships with transversals; triangle angle sum and exterior angle; isosceles triangle theorem; triangle congruence criteria (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, HL); perpendicular bisectors, angle bisectors, medians; parallelogram properties.
- **Applied skills**: Prove line and angle theorems, select and apply a valid congruence criterion, and justify triangle and quadrilateral relationships in two-column, paragraph, or flow form.
- **Common traps**: Using "SSA" or "AAA" as congruence shortcuts; assuming all quadrilateral properties hold for every parallelogram; skipping the reason column.

## Chapter 4: Similarity, Dilations, and Scale
- **Core questions**: What does a dilation do to lengths, angles, perimeter, and area? When are two triangles similar?
- **Key concepts**: Dilations and scale factor; similarity transformations; proportional sides with congruent corresponding angles; triangle similarity criteria (AA, SSS~, SAS~); side-splitter theorem; indirect measurement; scale drawings.
- **Applied skills**: Find missing lengths with proportions, prove triangles similar, apply scale factors to maps and blueprints, and compare how perimeter (×k) and area (×k²) change under scaling.
- **Common traps**: Scaling area by k instead of k²; setting up proportions with mismatched corresponding sides; confusing similarity with congruence.

## Chapter 5: Right Triangles and Trigonometry Basics
- **Core questions**: Why are side ratios in right triangles fixed by the angles? How do you choose the right trigonometric ratio for an applied problem?
- **Key concepts**: Pythagorean Theorem and its converse; special right triangles (45-45-90 and 30-60-90); geometric mean relationships (enrichment); sine, cosine, tangent; the sine-cosine complementary relationship; angles of elevation and depression.
- **Applied skills**: Solve right triangles, choose the correct ratio, estimate plausibility of an answer, and model height or distance from a labeled diagram.
- **Common traps**: Mislabeling opposite vs. adjacent; using sine when the problem needs tangent; forgetting the hypotenuse is the longest side.

## Chapter 6: Circles and Circular Measurement
- **Core questions**: How do central, inscribed, and tangent relationships constrain a circle diagram? How do arc length and sector area scale with the central angle?
- **Key concepts**: Radius, chord, diameter, tangent, secant; arcs and central angles; the inscribed angle theorem (and inscribed angle on a diameter is right); tangent-radius perpendicularity; cyclic quadrilaterals; arc length and sector area; radian measure (enrichment).
- **Applied skills**: Apply circle angle relationships, compute arc length and sector area, use tangent-radius and chord relationships, and solve multi-step circle diagrams.
- **Common traps**: Setting an inscribed angle equal to its arc instead of half; forgetting the n/360 fraction in arc/sector formulas; treating a secant like a tangent.

## Chapter 7: Coordinate Geometry and Algebraic Proof
- **Core questions**: How does algebra verify a geometric claim? What does the equation of a circle reveal about its center and radius?
- **Key concepts**: Distance and midpoint formulas; slope; parallel (equal slope) and perpendicular (negative-reciprocal slope) lines; coordinate proofs; equation of a circle and completing the square; partitioning a segment in a given ratio; area on the coordinate plane.
- **Applied skills**: Classify figures from coordinates, prove a quadrilateral property using slope or distance, and find a circle's center and radius from its equation.
- **Common traps**: Using equal slopes for perpendicular lines; sign errors in the distance formula; mishandling the constant when completing the square.

## Chapter 8: Area, Volume, and Geometry Modeling Studio
- **Core questions**: How do you decompose a composite figure for measurement? Which measurements does a real design problem actually require?
- **Key concepts**: Area, perimeter, circumference; surface area and volume of prisms, cylinders, pyramids, cones, and spheres; composite figures; cross-sections and solids of revolution; density; units; optimization and model limits.
- **Applied skills**: Compute area and volume for composite shapes, compare solids, identify cross-sections, and choose the measurements needed for a design or capacity problem.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting the 1/3 factor for pyramids and cones; mixing linear, square, and cubic units; ignoring whether a problem wants surface area or volume.

## Capstone
Students complete a geometry modeling-and-proof portfolio organized around one design or investigation problem. The final product must include a precise, marked diagram; at least one valid proof or justification; a transformation or similarity argument; a coordinate or measurement calculation; and a reflection on assumptions, units, and sources of error. Work is judged on geometric reasoning, accuracy, proof communication, quality of representation, and assessment-ready explanation.

## Assessment Ideas
- Diagram-reading checks: identify given information, needed information, and the theorem that connects them.
- Proof revisions scored on claim, reason, logical sequence, and correct use of definitions.
- Mixed calculation tasks requiring units, a diagram, and contextual interpretation.
- Short modeling labs using scale, trigonometry, circle measurement, area, or volume.
- Capstone graded on reasoning, accuracy, proof communication, representation, and explanation.
