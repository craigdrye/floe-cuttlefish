# AP Chemistry
Particle-level reasoning, quantitative problem solving, and laboratory evidence for the college-level AP Chemistry exam.

**ID**: `col-ap-chemistry` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
AP Chemistry is a college-level introductory course that asks students to explain the macroscopic world through the behavior of atoms, ions, and molecules. The aim is to build fluency in moving between three representations of every chemical situation: the macroscopic (what you observe and measure), the particulate (the atoms and molecules responsible), and the symbolic (formulas, equations, and mathematical models). Students who succeed treat chemistry less as a list of facts and more as a system of connected models.

Across the year, students develop quantitative problem solving anchored in the mole concept and dimensional analysis, and they learn to support claims with laboratory data and uncertainty. The course progresses from the structure of matter (atoms, periodicity, bonding, and intermolecular forces) through how matter changes (reactions and stoichiometry, kinetics, thermodynamics) and finally to systems at equilibrium (chemical equilibrium, acids and bases, and electrochemistry).

The course follows the College Board AP Chemistry Course and Exam Description (effective Fall 2024), which is organized around nine units and a set of science practices: models and representations, question and method, representing data and phenomena, model analysis, mathematical routines, and argumentation. This syllabus groups those units into eight study chapters and repeatedly asks students to justify answers with reasoning, not just compute them.

## Course Design Notes
Each chapter combines conceptual explanation with calculation and a recurring demand to connect observation to the particle level. Questions are written in the AP idiom: "Which model best explains...", "Justify your answer using...", and "Predict and explain...". Mathematical routines (weighted averages, stoichiometric ratios, rate laws, ICE tables, Gibbs free energy, Nernst-style reasoning) are practiced as tools that serve an explanation, never as ends in themselves. Laboratory thinking is woven throughout: students interpret data tables, particulate diagrams, titration and kinetics curves, and they discuss sources of error and the limits of a model. The final chapter is exam-focused, drilling the representation choices and justification structure that earn credit on multiple-choice and free-response questions.

## Chapter 1: Chemistry Practices and Atomic Structure
- **Core questions**: What does a measurement actually tell you, and with what uncertainty? How do mass spectra and photoelectron spectra reveal isotopes and electron configuration?
- **Key concepts**: measurement and significant figures, the mole and molar mass, isotopes and average atomic mass, mass spectrometry, Coulomb's law, photoelectron spectroscopy (PES), electron configuration, periodic trends in atomic radius and ionization energy.
- **Applied skills**: compute weighted average atomic mass from spectral peaks; read a PES spectrum to identify an element; rank Coulombic attractions by charge and radius.
- **Common traps**: using an unweighted mean for isotopic mass; assuming effective nuclear charge decreases across a period; ignoring half-filled and filled-subshell stability when predicting ionization energy.

## Chapter 2: Periodicity, Bonding, and Molecular Structure
- **Core questions**: Why do periodic trends occur at the particle level? Which Lewis structure and geometry best fit the evidence, and how does shape control polarity?
- **Key concepts**: periodic trends and effective nuclear charge, ionic versus covalent bonding, lattice energy, Lewis structures, formal charge, resonance, VSEPR geometry, hybridization, bond polarity and molecular polarity.
- **Applied skills**: draw and evaluate Lewis structures using formal charge; assign electron and molecular geometry with VSEPR; predict net dipole from geometry and bond polarity.
- **Common traps**: confusing electron geometry with molecular shape; declaring a molecule polar from polar bonds alone without checking symmetry; treating resonance structures as physically alternating rather than an average.

## Chapter 3: Intermolecular Forces and Properties
- **Core questions**: Which intermolecular force dominates, and how does that explain physical properties? How do mixtures separate?
- **Key concepts**: London dispersion forces, dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonding, ion-dipole, polarizability, properties of gases (ideal gas law, kinetic molecular theory), liquids, solids, solutions, and chromatography.
- **Applied skills**: rank substances by boiling point or vapor pressure from IMF strength; apply PV = nRT and deviations from ideality; explain a separation by relative attractions.
- **Common traps**: calling hydrogen bonding an actual covalent bond; assuming larger molar mass always means a higher boiling point regardless of polarity; forgetting that real gases deviate at high pressure and low temperature.

## Chapter 4: Chemical Reactions and Stoichiometry
- **Core questions**: What species actually react in solution? Which reactant limits the product, and what does the evidence show that a reaction occurred?
- **Key concepts**: balancing equations, net ionic equations, precipitation and solubility rules, acid-base neutralization, oxidation-reduction and oxidation numbers, mole ratios, limiting reactant, percent yield, molarity and titration basics.
- **Applied skills**: write net ionic equations; determine the limiting reactant and theoretical yield; carry out and interpret a titration calculation.
- **Common traps**: including spectator ions in a net ionic equation; using mass directly instead of moles for ratios; mismatching the stoichiometric ratio at the equivalence point in a titration.

## Chapter 5: Kinetics
- **Core questions**: How fast does a reaction go and why? What is the rate law, which step is rate-determining, and how do catalysts work?
- **Key concepts**: reaction rate, rate laws and reaction order, method of initial rates, integrated rate laws and half-life, collision theory, activation energy, reaction mechanisms and intermediates, catalysts, energy profile diagrams.
- **Applied skills**: determine a rate law from initial-rate data; identify reaction order from a linear integrated-rate plot; connect a proposed mechanism to the observed rate law.
- **Common traps**: reading stoichiometric coefficients as reaction orders; confusing intermediates with catalysts; assuming a catalyst changes the overall enthalpy rather than the activation energy.

## Chapter 6: Thermochemistry and Thermodynamics
- **Core questions**: Where does energy go in a reaction, and what makes a process thermodynamically favorable?
- **Key concepts**: heat and temperature, specific heat and calorimetry, enthalpy and bond energies, Hess's law and standard enthalpies of formation, entropy, Gibbs free energy (delta G = delta H minus T delta S), and the temperature dependence of favorability.
- **Applied skills**: solve calorimetry problems with q = mc(delta T); apply Hess's law and formation enthalpies; predict favorability from the signs of delta H and delta S across temperature.
- **Common traps**: equating exothermic with spontaneous; dropping or mishandling units when combining delta H and T delta S; forgetting that bond breaking absorbs energy while bond forming releases it.

## Chapter 7: Equilibrium, Acids and Bases, and Electrochemistry
- **Core questions**: Which way will a system at equilibrium shift, and why? Which acid-base model applies, and is a redox reaction spontaneous?
- **Key concepts**: dynamic equilibrium, the equilibrium constant K, the reaction quotient Q, Le Chatelier's principle, solubility product Ksp, pH and pOH, Ka and Kb, weak-acid and buffer calculations, titration curves, galvanic and electrolytic cells, standard cell potential and its link to delta G.
- **Applied skills**: build and solve ICE tables; compare Q and K to predict shift direction; calculate pH of strong and weak systems and buffers; determine cell potential and spontaneity.
- **Common traps**: treating weak acids as fully dissociated; confusing the equivalence point with a neutral pH 7; reversing the sign relationship between cell potential and free energy.

## Chapter 8: AP Chemistry Exam Studio
- **Core questions**: What representation earns credit, which assumption is hidden, and how should a calculation or claim be justified?
- **Key concepts**: particulate diagrams, data-table and graph interpretation, experimental design, multi-step calculations, claim-evidence-reasoning explanations, error and uncertainty analysis, and free-response strategy.
- **Applied skills**: translate between macroscopic, particulate, and symbolic representations; structure a justified free-response answer; audit work for units, significant figures, and stated assumptions.
- **Common traps**: stating a claim without connecting it to particle-level evidence; skipping units or sig-fig discipline under time pressure; answering a "justify" prompt with only a number.

## Capstone
Build an AP Chemistry evidence portfolio that demonstrates integration across the course: (1) a particulate-modeling task linking macroscopic observation to a particle diagram and symbolic equation; (2) a stoichiometry or kinetics lab analysis with balanced equations, a rate-law or yield calculation, and an error discussion; (3) a thermodynamics analysis with an energy diagram and a favorability argument from delta G; (4) an equilibrium or acid-base task with an ICE table or titration curve and interpretation; and (5) a timed AP-style free-response set with a scoring-guideline correction log. The portfolio is assessed on the consistent integration of particulate, symbolic, and macroscopic reasoning.

## Assessment Ideas
- Problem sets graded on setup, units, significant figures, and chemical reasoning rather than the final number alone.
- Labs graded on data quality, the connection between data and a particle-level model, and treatment of uncertainty.
- Capstone graded on particulate-symbolic-macroscopic integration and the quality of justification.
