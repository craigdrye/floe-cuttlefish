# Game Design Basics
**ID**: `gameDesignBasics` · **Discipline**: Computing/Arts · **Year**: 10 (ages 14-15)

## Course Aim
Introduce students to game design as a creative systems discipline: mechanics, player experience, prototyping, playtesting, iteration, level design, balance, narrative, and design ethics.

## Course Design Notes
This course draws from the MDA framework, playcentric design traditions, Unity Learn level-design materials, and game education curriculum frameworks. It is not a programming-heavy course; students can prototype with paper, slides, block coding, simple engines, or tabletop materials.

## Chapter 1: What Game Designers Design
- **Key concepts**: game, goal, rules, constraints, feedback, player, system, prototype.
- **Practice questions**: What is the player trying to do? What rule creates the challenge? What feedback tells the player what happened?
- **Design output**: a one-page analysis of a familiar game's goal, rules, actions, and feedback.

## Chapter 2: Mechanics, Dynamics, and Aesthetics
- **Key concepts**: MDA, mechanics, dynamics, aesthetics as player experience, designer perspective versus player perspective.
- **Practice questions**: Which mechanic creates this behavior? What player feeling is the design aiming for? What dynamic emerged unexpectedly?
- **Design output**: an MDA breakdown of two contrasting games.

## Chapter 3: Core Loops and Meaningful Choice
- **Key concepts**: core loop, short-term and long-term goals, agency, risk/reward, resource tradeoffs, dominant strategy.
- **Practice questions**: What does the player do repeatedly? Which choice has real consequence? What strategy makes the game stale?
- **Design output**: a core-loop diagram and choice map for an original game concept.

## Chapter 4: Prototyping and Playtesting
- **Key concepts**: paper prototype, minimum playable version, observation, feedback questions, iteration, design hypothesis.
- **Practice questions**: What are we testing? What did players actually do? Which change should be tried next?
- **Design output**: a playable paper or digital prototype with a playtest notes sheet.

## Chapter 5: Level Design, Pacing, and Onboarding
- **Key concepts**: signposting, affordance, tutorialization, challenge curve, flow, pacing, safe practice, surprise.
- **Practice questions**: How does the player learn without a lecture? Where does difficulty spike? What visual cue guides action?
- **Design output**: a level sketch or encounter plan with teaching moments and pacing notes.

## Chapter 6: Systems, Economy, and Balance
- **Key concepts**: sources, sinks, feedback loops, progression, randomness, fairness, asymmetry, power creep.
- **Practice questions**: Where does the resource enter and leave? Who has an advantage? What tuning change improves fairness?
- **Design output**: a balance table with variables, expected effects, and test plan.

## Chapter 7: Narrative, World, and Theme
- **Key concepts**: embedded narrative, emergent narrative, environmental storytelling, tone, character goals, theme, dissonance.
- **Practice questions**: What story comes from the player's actions? What does the world imply without dialogue? Does the mechanic support the theme?
- **Design output**: a short narrative design brief for a level, quest, or tabletop scenario.

## Chapter 8: Ethics, Accessibility, and Final Pitch
- **Key concepts**: accessibility, inclusive design, dark patterns, monetization, privacy, community safety, pitch structure.
- **Practice questions**: Who can play comfortably? What design choice manipulates rather than delights? How should the game be explained in one minute?
- **Design output**: a final pitch deck or poster with prototype evidence, playtest changes, accessibility notes, and next steps.

## Capstone
Create a small original game or substantial redesign of an existing game. Submit the design brief, prototype, playtest notes, tuning changes, accessibility/ethics review, and final pitch.

## Assessment Ideas
- Analysis tasks graded on design vocabulary and evidence.
- Prototypes graded on playability, focus, and iteration.
- Capstone graded on player experience, playtest learning, and clarity of pitch.
