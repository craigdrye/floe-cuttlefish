# AP European History
Trace Europe from the Renaissance to the present, and learn to argue about the past the way historians do: with sources, context, and evidence-backed claims.

**ID**: `apEuropeanHistory` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This course prepares students for AP European History by building durable historical knowledge from roughly 1450 to the present and by developing the historical reasoning the AP exam rewards. Students learn not only what happened — the Renaissance, Reformation, Scientific Revolution, Enlightenment, the age of revolutions, industrialisation, the world wars, the Cold War, and European integration — but how to use that knowledge to construct and defend arguments about change, continuity, causation, and comparison across four centuries.

The course treats history as an evidence-based discipline rather than a list of names and dates. Every chapter pairs content with skill: students practise sourcing (asking who made a document, when, why, and for whom), contextualisation (placing events in their broader moment), and corroboration (testing one source against others). They learn to read primary sources as arguments, to weigh competing historical interpretations, and to recognise when a single-cause story is weaker than a well-stacked multi-causal one. These habits map directly onto the exam's stimulus multiple-choice questions, short-answer questions (SAQ), the document-based question (DBQ), and the long essay question (LEQ).

Writing is central. Students draft and revise defensible theses, organise evidence into clear lines of reasoning, integrate document analysis with outside knowledge, and demonstrate complexity by qualifying claims, acknowledging counter-evidence, or tracing connections across periods. The aim is a student who can walk into the exam able to periodise confidently, compare across regions and eras, and write a historically grounded argument under time pressure.

## Course Design Notes
The College Board organises AP European History into nine units and seven recurring themes — interaction of Europe and the world; economic and commercial developments; cultural and intellectual developments; states and other institutions of power; social organisation and development; national and European identity; and technology and innovation. This syllabus condenses the nine units into eight teachable chapters that follow the four AP periods (1450–1648, 1648–1815, 1815–1914, 1914–present) while preserving sustained writing practice and a dedicated exam-skills studio. Each chapter is built around the same routines: close reading and sourcing of primary documents, contextual placement, thesis-and-evidence drafting, and timed practice. Assessment rewards accurate content, fair handling of competing interpretations, the quality of source analysis, and argument structure (thesis, evidence, reasoning, complexity) rather than memorised opinions. Useful anchors include the College Board AP European History Course and Exam Description, released DBQ/LEQ/SAQ prompts and scoring guidelines, and reputable primary-source collections.

## Chapter 1: Historical Thinking and Early Modern Europe
- **Core questions**: What changed around 1450, and what stayed the same? How did Italian and Northern humanism reshape culture and education? How do historians turn a document into evidence for a claim?
- **Key concepts**: contextualisation, sourcing, corroboration, argumentation; Renaissance humanism and the studia humanitatis; civic humanism; the printing press and vernacular literacy; voyages of exploration; the new monarchies and early state formation.
- **Applied skills**: building a source-analysis routine (sourcing, context, claim, evidence) on Renaissance documents; comparing Italian (Petrarch, Castiglione) and Northern (Erasmus, More) humanism.
- **Common traps**: equating humanism with atheism or anti-Christian secularism; reading the printing press as replacing rather than scaling text production; confusing textual recovery with experimental science.

## Chapter 2: Reformation, Religious Conflict, and State Power
- **Core questions**: Why did the Reformation spread so fast and so far? How did religion and politics reinforce each other? What did sovereignty mean after the Peace of Westphalia?
- **Key concepts**: Luther and the 95 Theses; sola fide and sola scriptura; Calvinism and predestination; the Catholic (Counter-)Reformation and the Council of Trent; confessional conflict; the French Wars of Religion; the Thirty Years' War and the 1648 Peace of Westphalia; the rise of new monarchies.
- **Applied skills**: a DBQ-style document grouping on a Reformation or religious-conflict prompt; tracing how confessional choice tracked political authority.
- **Common traps**: treating the Thirty Years' War as purely religious (Catholic France fought the Catholic Habsburgs); mistaking Westphalia for an edict of toleration rather than territorial sovereignty; confusing Trent's reaffirmation with compromise.

## Chapter 3: Absolutism, Constitutionalism, and the Scientific Revolution
- **Core questions**: How did absolutist and constitutional paths diverge after 1648? How did new science challenge older sources of authority? What is the difference between empiricism and rationalism?
- **Key concepts**: Louis XIV and Versailles court culture; Peter the Great and Catherine the Great; the English Civil War, Commonwealth, Restoration, and the Glorious Revolution (1688–89); Copernicus, Kepler, Galileo, Newton; Vesalius and Harvey; Bacon's induction and Descartes's deduction.
- **Applied skills**: a comparative essay outline on absolutist versus constitutional state power, or on the methods of the Scientific Revolution.
- **Common traps**: reading Versailles as merely aesthetic rather than political; assuming westernising tsars abolished serfdom (it expanded); reversing the Copernicus-to-Newton chronology; collapsing empiricism and rationalism into one method.

## Chapter 4: Enlightenment, Revolution, and Napoleon
- **Core questions**: How did Enlightenment ideas become political action? Why did the French Revolution break out in 1789? Did Napoleon preserve or betray revolutionary ideals?
- **Key concepts**: Locke, Voltaire, Montesquieu, Rousseau, Wollstonecraft, Adam Smith; natural rights, separation of powers, the general will, freedom of expression; enlightened absolutism; the phases of the French Revolution (Assembly, Convention/Terror, Directory, Napoleon); the Napoleonic Code; the Congress of Vienna and Metternich.
- **Applied skills**: an LEQ on causation, continuity, or change in revolutionary Europe; reading the Napoleonic Code as an Enlightenment-meets-Bonapartism complexity case.
- **Common traps**: reducing 1789 to one cause (royal personalities, or proletarian revolution); scrambling the Terror–Directory–Napoleon sequence; treating the Congress of Vienna as a liberal-nationalist settlement rather than a conservative restoration.

## Chapter 5: Industrialization, Ideologies, and Social Change
- **Core questions**: Why did industrialisation begin in Britain first? How did liberalism, conservatism, nationalism, and socialism respond to social change? Why did 1848 matter even though it largely "failed"?
- **Key concepts**: the First and Second Industrial Revolutions; urbanisation and the factory system; liberalism, conservatism, nationalism, socialism, and Marxism; the 1848 revolutions; the Crimean War and the cracking of the Concert of Europe.
- **Applied skills**: a thematic timeline linking economic change to political ideology; stacking multi-causal explanations for British industrial primacy (coal, capital, institutions, agriculture).
- **Common traps**: explaining "why Britain first" with a single inventor or resource; calling 1848 a success in establishing stable liberal democracies; mistaking the Crimean War's combatants or outcome.

## Chapter 6: Nationalism, Imperialism, and Modern Culture
- **Core questions**: How did nationalism both unify and divide Europe? What drove the late-19th-century scramble for empire? What made culture distinctively "modern"?
- **Key concepts**: Italian unification (Mazzini, Garibaldi, Cavour) and German unification (Bismarck's three wars, Realpolitik); new imperialism and the scramble for Africa; Social Darwinism and the "civilising mission"; romanticism, realism, and modernism; Freud and Einstein.
- **Applied skills**: a comparison of unification movements, of imperial motives, or of cultural responses to modernity; defining Realpolitik as a method rather than a goal.
- **Common traps**: treating the scramble for Africa as purely religious or purely economic; muddling the order or combatants of Bismarck's three wars; assuming nationalism was always unifying.

## Chapter 7: Global Conflict, Cold War, and Contemporary Europe
- **Core questions**: What made twentieth-century war "total"? Why did democracy collapse in some interwar states? How did Europe rebuild, divide, and then reintegrate after 1945?
- **Key concepts**: causes of World War I and the Schlieffen Plan; the Russian Revolution (February and October 1917); the Treaty of Versailles; fascism, Nazism, and Stalinism; World War II and the Holocaust; the Cold War (iron curtain, Marshall Plan, NATO and the Warsaw Pact); decolonisation; European integration (ECSC, EEC, Maastricht, the euro); 1989 and the collapse of communism; the Yugoslav wars; EU enlargement, Brexit, and migration.
- **Applied skills**: an SAQ and thesis-practice set on twentieth-century conflict and contemporary Europe; tracing one theme (state power, identity, technology) across the period.
- **Common traps**: treating Hitler's 1933 rise as an electoral majority or a foreign imposition; confusing the Marshall Plan's aims with later military alliances; describing the Yugoslav breakup as a peaceful, Czechoslovak-style "velvet divorce."

## Chapter 8: AP European History Exam Studio
- **Core questions**: Which piece of a source's context actually matters for the argument? What outside evidence is strongest and most specific? How can an argument demonstrate genuine complexity rather than asserting it?
- **Key concepts**: stimulus multiple-choice strategy; SAQ precision (answer every part, cite specifics); DBQ sourcing (HIPP — historical situation, intended audience, point of view, purpose) and document grouping; LEQ planning, thesis construction, and the complexity point; the seven AP themes.
- **Applied skills**: one timed DBQ, one timed LEQ, and a skill-by-skill correction log; turning a vague claim into a thesis with a defensible line of reasoning.
- **Common traps**: offering a trend ("trade increased") where the rubric wants one specific named fact as outside evidence; summarising documents instead of using them as evidence; asserting complexity without actually qualifying or connecting the argument.

## Capstone
Assemble an AP European History portfolio: a set of unit maps spanning 1450 to the present, at least four annotated source analyses, two complete SAQ sets, one timed DBQ, and one timed LEQ. Conclude with a reflection that traces a single AP theme — such as state power, national and European identity, or technology and innovation — across at least four distinct periods, supported by specific evidence and demonstrating change and continuity over time.
