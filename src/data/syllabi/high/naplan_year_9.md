# NAPLAN Prep (Year 9)
Mature literacy and advanced middle-years numeracy for Australia's final NAPLAN, taught as transferable reading, writing, and reasoning skills rather than last-minute drilling.

**ID**: `naplanYear9` · **Discipline**: Exam Prep

## Course Aim
This course prepares Australian Year 9 students for their final NAPLAN assessment by strengthening mature reading, controlled writing, accurate language conventions, advanced middle-years numeracy, and strategic online-test behaviour. NAPLAN tests four domains — Reading, Writing, Conventions of Language, and Numeracy — and the Year 9 papers reach the upper edge of the F–10 curriculum. Students learn to work from evidence, select efficient methods, write with purpose, and use diagnostic feedback to build confidence rather than panic.

NAPLAN is now fully online and uses tailored (adaptive) testing, so the difficulty of items shifts in response to a student's answers. That makes calm pacing, careful reading, and steady accuracy more valuable than racing. The Writing test runs 42 minutes from a single stimulus; the Numeracy test runs 65 minutes and spans Number and Algebra, Measurement and Geometry, and Statistics and Probability, with a non-calculator section and a calculator section plus online tools such as an on-screen ruler and protractor.

The deeper aim is durable skill, not score-chasing. Because the literacy and numeracy demands of Year 9 NAPLAN are essentially the demands of early senior secondary, every chapter teaches a method students will reuse in their own subjects: how to defend an inference with evidence, how to plan a persuasive line of reasoning, how to edit for clarity, and how to choose an efficient mathematical strategy and check it.

## Course Design Notes
This course targets Australian lower-secondary students approaching upper-secondary expectations. Practice should be rigorous but steady: use NAPLAN-style tasks to diagnose patterns, then reteach the underlying reading, writing, conventions, and numeracy skills. Literacy work aligns with the Australian Curriculum (v9.0) English Year 9 emphasis on interpretation, analysis, evaluation, and crafted texts; numeracy aligns with the Mathematics Year 9 strands — Number, Algebra, Measurement, Space, Statistics, and Probability. Include timed online rehearsals, modelled thinking, error analysis, and student-owned target setting. The questions in this track are multiple-choice for clean self-marking, but they deliberately mirror the reasoning that the open online formats (text entry, drag-and-drop, interactive tools) demand.

## Chapter 1: Final NAPLAN Readiness and Test Intelligence
- **Core questions**: What skill is hidden inside this item? Which questions should be answered first, checked, or flagged? How should a student use an error log to choose the next practice set?
- **Key concepts**: the four test domains, online navigation, tailored (adaptive) testing, command words, time allocation, item triage, estimation, flagging and review, stamina, and diagnostic error categories.
- **Applied skills**: build a personal time routine for the 42-minute Writing and 65-minute Numeracy tests; triage items into answer-now, flag, and check; classify errors by cause rather than by topic.
- **Common traps**: assuming adaptive tests "lock in" answers; spending equal time on every item; leaving no time to review flagged questions; reading scores instead of error patterns.

## Chapter 2: Reading Inference, Tone, and Authorial Purpose
- **Core questions**: Which evidence best supports a subtle inference? How does tone shift across a passage? Which answer is attractive but not fully supported by the text?
- **Key concepts**: explicit evidence, inference, implication, tone shift, point of view, audience, purpose, bias, reliability, context clues, and distractor analysis.
- **Applied skills**: locate the exact sentence that justifies an answer; distinguish what the text states from what a reader assumes; explain why a tempting wrong option fails.
- **Common traps**: choosing the answer that "sounds deep" rather than the supported one; treating a single emotive word as the whole tone; importing outside knowledge the passage never gives.

## Chapter 3: Reading Complex and Multimodal Texts
- **Core questions**: How does structure shape meaning? What does a graph or image add to an argument? How do two sources agree, qualify, or contradict each other?
- **Key concepts**: satire, irony, extended metaphor, structural choices, visual features, data within texts, source comparison, synthesis, conflicting perspectives, and evaluation.
- **Applied skills**: read across text and image together; trace how paragraph order builds an argument; compare two sources by claim, evidence, tone, and reliability.
- **Common traps**: reading the picture or graph as decoration; missing irony and taking a satirical claim literally; assuming the longer source is the more reliable one.

## Chapter 4: Writing Arguments With Control
- **Core questions**: Which contention gives the clearest direction? Where does the reasoning need stronger evidence? How can a counterargument make a response more convincing?
- **Key concepts**: prompt analysis, contention, thesis, line of reasoning, paragraph sequence, evidence, elaboration, counterargument, rebuttal, modality, cohesion, and conclusion.
- **Applied skills**: plan a persuasive response under time pressure; write a body paragraph with claim, evidence, elaboration, and link; use modality and connectives to control emphasis.
- **Common traps**: restating the prompt instead of taking a position; listing reasons without elaborating; an overlong introduction that leaves no time for the body; a conclusion that adds a brand-new idea.

## Chapter 5: Narrative Craft and Writing Precision
- **Core questions**: Which opening establishes conflict most efficiently? How does sentence length affect pace? Which revision shows rather than explains a character's emotion?
- **Key concepts**: narrative perspective, character motivation, setting, tension, pacing, flashback, dialogue control, imagery, precise verbs, sentence rhythm, paragraphing, and revision.
- **Applied skills**: open a scene with conflict already in motion; vary sentence length to control pace; revise "telling" lines into "showing" through action and detail.
- **Common traps**: spending the time budget on backstory before any conflict; relying on adverbs instead of precise verbs; punctuating dialogue incorrectly; one undivided block of text.

## Chapter 6: Language Conventions for Mature Editing
- **Core questions**: Which spelling fits both the meaning and the word family? Which sentence has the clearest grammar? Where does punctuation clarify the relationship between ideas?
- **Key concepts**: complex spelling, etymology, affixes, homophones, subject–verb agreement in complex sentences, tense control, pronoun clarity, active and passive voice, commas, apostrophes, colons, semicolons, and quotation punctuation.
- **Applied skills**: use word families and affixes to choose correct spellings; fix agreement and tense across long sentences; apply apostrophes, colons, and semicolons to clarify meaning.
- **Common traps**: confusing homophones (their/there/they're, affect/effect); apostrophes in plurals; comma splices joining two sentences; vague pronouns with no clear antecedent.

## Chapter 7: Numeracy — Number, Algebra, Measurement, and Geometry
- **Core questions**: How do you solve a pair of simultaneous equations? What length does Pythagoras or trigonometry give here? Which formula and units fit this measurement problem?
- **Key concepts**: rational numbers, percentages, rates, scientific notation, index laws, expansion and factorisation, linear and simultaneous equations, linear graphs, Pythagoras' theorem, similarity, surface area, volume, right-angled trigonometry, angles, and units.
- **Applied skills**: choose between non-calculator estimation and exact methods; apply index laws and Pythagoras correctly; convert and check units; read gradient and intercept from a line.
- **Common traps**: dropping a negative sign when solving equations; mixing up area and perimeter formulas; forgetting to square-root after Pythagoras; ignoring unit conversion (cm vs m).

## Chapter 8: Numeracy — Statistics, Probability, and Mixed Reasoning
- **Core questions**: What does this scatter plot show about association? How do you compute a compound probability? Is this sample or conclusion reliable?
- **Key concepts**: data displays, scatter plots, association, line of best fit, mean, median, range, spread, outliers, skew, sample bias, independent and dependent events, two-way tables, tree diagrams, and multistep modelling.
- **Applied skills**: describe association and trend from a scatter plot; calculate mean/median and judge which summary fits; combine probabilities with tables and tree diagrams; spot a biased sample.
- **Common traps**: confusing correlation with causation; treating dependent events as independent; using the mean when an outlier makes the median fairer; misreading a two-way table's totals.

## Capstone
Students complete a final NAPLAN readiness portfolio. It includes: a complex reading-and-synthesis set with evidence labels; one timed persuasive or narrative writing task with two drafts and revision notes; a conventions editing log of corrected NAPLAN-style items with rule explanations; a mixed numeracy practice set with worked methods, diagrams, and unit checks; and a short reflection explaining how the student will approach the online test calmly and strategically. The portfolio is graded on independence, accuracy, revision quality, diagnostic insight, and readiness for senior secondary learning.
