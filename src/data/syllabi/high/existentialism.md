# Existentialism
Freedom, anxiety, and the search for meaning — reading the great existentialists with honesty and care.

**ID**: `existentialism` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
Existentialism is a serious, readable way to think about the questions that do not go away: Am I free? What am I responsible for? Why does so much of life feel anxious, absurd, or strange? How do I live honestly when no one can hand me a ready-made meaning? This course treats existentialism as rigorous philosophy rather than mood or slogan, giving high-school students the vocabulary and habits of mind to interpret difficult thinkers without either worshipping or dismissing them.

Students read closely from the tradition's central figures — Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Sartre, Camus, and Beauvoir — and trace existential themes through literature and film, from Dostoevsky and Kafka to contemporary stories about conformity, surveillance, and identity. Along the way they learn to tell the difference between a genuine constraint and a self-protective excuse, between creating values and merely rebelling, between despair as a feeling and despair as a philosophical condition.

The tone is intellectually honest and emotionally careful. The course can discuss anxiety, absurdity, and meaninglessness, but always through thoughtful interpretation and argument, never through shock or vague self-expression. The aim is precision: students should be able to use terms like bad faith, the absurd, facticity, and ressentiment accurately, support readings with textual evidence, and connect rigorous philosophy to their own real choices.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve existentialism, absurdism, authenticity, bad faith, freedom and responsibility, anxiety and despair, alienation, meaning-making, or the major existential thinkers — Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Sartre, Camus, Beauvoir — and existential themes in literature and film by writers such as Kafka, Dostoevsky, and Frankl. Questions test interpretation, accurate use of terminology, and the ability to apply ideas to concrete cases, not memorized biography. Distractors should target the common confusions: collapsing bad faith into ordinary lying, mistaking the absurd for mere pessimism, reading "God is dead" as a triumphant boast, or treating radical freedom as the claim that circumstances do not matter. Keep everything age-appropriate for the high-school tier: challenging ideas, careful framing.

## Chapter 1: What Is an Existential Question?
**Core questions**: What makes a question existential rather than merely practical? Can a person be both free and trapped? Is meaning discovered, invented, inherited, or negotiated?
**Key concepts**: Existence vs. essence, meaning, freedom, responsibility, anxiety, alienation, authenticity, absurdity, subjectivity, the examined life.
**Applied skills**: Define core terms through concrete examples; distinguish existential questions from technical or factual ones; keep a precise concept journal.
**Common traps**: Treating "existential" as a synonym for "deep" or "sad"; assuming meaning must be either purely objective or purely made up; confusing emotional venting with philosophical analysis.

## Chapter 2: Kierkegaard, Anxiety, and the Leap
**Core questions**: Why might too much possibility create anxiety? What is the difference between knowing a truth and living it? Can commitment be rational without certainty?
**Key concepts**: Subjective truth, despair, anxiety as the "dizziness of freedom," the three stages (aesthetic, ethical, religious), the leap of faith, inwardness, the single individual.
**Applied skills**: Explain one Kierkegaardian concept through a real decision or literary scene; track how a character moves between life-stages.
**Common traps**: Treating the leap of faith as mere wishful thinking; confusing anxiety with ordinary fear of a specific object; assuming the ethical stage is the highest.

## Chapter 3: Nietzsche, Value, and Self-Overcoming
**Core questions**: What happens when inherited values lose authority? Is creating values liberation, danger, or both? How can critique of conformity avoid becoming cruelty?
**Key concepts**: "God is dead," nihilism, value creation, master and slave morality, ressentiment, will to power, self-overcoming, the Ubermensch, eternal recurrence.
**Applied skills**: Map the argument of a Nietzsche passage; critique it without hero worship or dismissal; apply master/slave morality to a real conflict.
**Common traps**: Reading "God is dead" as celebration rather than diagnosis; treating the Ubermensch as a brute or tyrant; equating will to power with simple domination.

## Chapter 4: Sartre, Freedom, and Bad Faith
**Core questions**: Are people responsible for who they become? What limits freedom — facts, other people, fear, or self-deception? What does bad faith look like in ordinary life?
**Key concepts**: Existence precedes essence, radical freedom, responsibility, facticity, transcendence, bad faith, "the look," objectification, anguish, authenticity.
**Applied skills**: Build a bad-faith case study distinguishing genuine constraint from excuse-making; analyze how "the look" turns a person into an object.
**Common traps**: Reducing bad faith to ordinary lying to others; reading radical freedom as the claim that circumstances do not matter; ignoring facticity entirely.

## Chapter 5: Camus and the Absurd
**Core questions**: What is absurd about wanting meaning in a silent universe? Why does Camus reject easy consolation? Can rebellion be joyful rather than bitter?
**Key concepts**: The absurd as a confrontation, lucidity, philosophical suicide, hope as evasion, revolt, freedom, passion, Sisyphus, living without appeal.
**Applied skills**: Close-read a Camus passage for how tone, image, and argument work together; explain why Camus says we must imagine Sisyphus happy.
**Common traps**: Equating the absurd with pessimism or despair; thinking Camus recommends suicide; mistaking "philosophical suicide" for actual suicide rather than evading the absurd through false hope.

## Chapter 6: Beauvoir, Ambiguity, and the Other
**Core questions**: How can freedom depend on social conditions? What does it mean to be treated as the Other? Why might an ethics of freedom require concern for others' freedom?
**Key concepts**: Ambiguity, situated freedom, oppression, the Other, reciprocity, "one is not born, but rather becomes, a woman," responsibility for others' freedom, ethical relation.
**Applied skills**: Compare Beauvoir and Sartre on a social role or relationship; distinguish a chosen situation from imposed oppression.
**Common traps**: Reading Beauvoir as merely applying Sartre; ignoring how oppression shapes the freedom available to people; collapsing "the Other" into "anyone different."

## Chapter 7: Existential Literature and Film
**Core questions**: What makes a story existential rather than just bleak? How do Kafka, Dostoevsky, and modern films show people becoming strange to themselves? Can popular media handle existential ideas without flattening them?
**Key concepts**: Alienation, absurd bureaucracy, underground consciousness, metamorphosis, the trial without a charge, conformity, surveillance, the antihero, authenticity.
**Applied skills**: Analyze a scene for its existential conflict; explain how the medium (image, structure, point of view) makes an idea felt, not just stated.
**Common traps**: Reducing a work to a single "message"; mistaking grimness for existential depth; ignoring how form, not just plot, carries meaning.

## Chapter 8: Meaning, Work, Technology, and Modern Life
**Core questions**: Does modern life make authenticity harder? Can constrained work still be meaningful? How do technology and performance change the self, and what responsibilities come with freedom?
**Key concepts**: Meaning-making, vocation, attention, consumer culture, social media and the performed self, automation, responsibility gaps, solidarity, practical wisdom, Frankl's logotherapy.
**Applied skills**: Apply existential ideas to a contemporary dilemma in a public-philosophy piece; distinguish meaning found in work from meaning imposed by performance.
**Common traps**: Treating authenticity as "just be yourself"; assuming meaning must come from large achievements; blaming technology while ignoring the freedom and responsibility that remain.

## Capstone
Students build an existential inquiry portfolio with three linked parts: a close reading of one primary text or scene, a comparative theory section relating at least two thinkers, and a modern application to a contemporary dilemma. Possible focuses include authenticity online, the pressure to choose a future, school and conformity, grief and meaning, AI and identity, meaningful work, joyful rebellion, or the ethics of hope. The portfolio should show precision with terms, textual evidence, emotional maturity, and a clear argument that connects rigorous philosophy to a real human choice.

## Assessment Ideas
Use concept journals, passage annotations, seminar questions, argument maps, close-reading essays, film and literature analyses, comparative thinker charts, and the final inquiry portfolio. Assess philosophical accuracy, precise use of terminology, quality of textual evidence, emotional maturity, and the ability to connect personal reflection to disciplined argument.
