# Existentialism
**ID**: `existentialism` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: 11 (ages 15-16)

## Course Aim
Introduce existentialism as a serious, readable way to think about freedom, responsibility, anxiety, meaning, authenticity, alienation, and life under uncertainty. Students learn to connect difficult philosophical texts to literature, film, public life, and their own choices without turning philosophy into vague self-expression.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve existentialism, absurdism, authenticity, bad faith, freedom, responsibility, alienation, meaning, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Sartre, Camus, Beauvoir, Kafka, Dostoevsky, Frankl, or existential themes in literature and film. Tone should be intellectually honest and emotionally careful: the course can discuss despair and absurdity, but always through thoughtful interpretation, not shock.

## Chapter 1: What Is an Existential Question?
**Key concepts**: Existence, essence, meaning, freedom, responsibility, anxiety, alienation, authenticity, absurdity, subjectivity, and the examined life.
**Practice questions**: What makes a question existential rather than merely practical? Can a person be free and trapped at the same time? Is meaning discovered, invented, inherited, or negotiated?
**Student output**: A concept journal that defines core terms through examples from school, family, work, media, and public life.

## Chapter 2: Kierkegaard, Anxiety, and the Leap
**Key concepts**: Subjective truth, despair, anxiety, dread, choice, faith, leap, aesthetic life, ethical life, religious life, and inwardness.
**Practice questions**: Why might too much possibility create anxiety? What is the difference between knowing a truth and living it? Is commitment rational only when certainty is available?
**Student output**: A short interpretive essay explaining one Kierkegaardian concept through a concrete decision or literary scene.

## Chapter 3: Nietzsche, Value, and Self-Overcoming
**Key concepts**: "God is dead," value creation, nihilism, herd morality, master morality, slave morality, resentment, will to power, self-overcoming, and Ubermensch.
**Practice questions**: What happens when inherited values lose authority? Is creating values liberation, danger, or both? How can critique of conformity avoid becoming cruelty or arrogance?
**Student output**: An argument map of a Nietzsche passage, followed by a careful critique that avoids both hero worship and dismissal.

## Chapter 4: Sartre, Freedom, and Bad Faith
**Key concepts**: Existence precedes essence, radical freedom, responsibility, facticity, transcendence, bad faith, the look, objectification, and authenticity.
**Practice questions**: Are people responsible for who they become? What limits freedom: facts, other people, social roles, fear, or self-deception? What does bad faith look like in ordinary life?
**Student output**: A bad-faith case study that distinguishes genuine constraint from self-protective excuse-making.

## Chapter 5: Camus and the Absurd
**Key concepts**: Absurdity, rebellion, lucidity, philosophical suicide, hope, defiance, routine, Sisyphus, indifference, and revolt.
**Practice questions**: What is absurd about wanting meaning in a silent universe? Why does Camus reject easy consolation? Can rebellion be joyful rather than bitter?
**Student output**: A close reading of a Camus passage or scene that explains how tone, image, and argument work together.

## Chapter 6: Beauvoir, Ambiguity, and the Other
**Key concepts**: Ambiguity, freedom, oppression, the Other, situation, reciprocity, gender, responsibility for others' freedom, and ethical relation.
**Practice questions**: How can freedom depend on social conditions? What does it mean to be treated as the Other? Why might an ethics of freedom require concern for other people's freedom too?
**Student output**: A comparative analysis applying Beauvoir and Sartre to a social role, institution, or fictional relationship.

## Chapter 7: Existential Literature and Film
**Key concepts**: Alienation, absurd bureaucracy, underground consciousness, authenticity, conformity, choice, surveillance, metamorphosis, trial, and antihero.
**Practice questions**: What makes a story existential? How do Kafka, Dostoevsky, or modern films show people becoming strange to themselves? Can popular media handle existential ideas without simplifying them?
**Student output**: A scene analysis of a novel, short story, play, or film that identifies an existential conflict and explains how the medium makes it felt.

## Chapter 8: Meaning, Work, Technology, and Modern Life
**Key concepts**: Meaning-making, vocation, attention, consumer culture, social media, automation, authenticity online, responsibility gaps, solidarity, and practical wisdom.
**Practice questions**: Does modern life make authenticity harder? Can work be meaningful even when it is constrained? How do technology and performance change the self? What responsibilities come with freedom?
**Student output**: A public philosophy piece, such as an essay, podcast script, dialogue, or video plan, that applies existentialism to a contemporary dilemma.

## Capstone
Students create an existential inquiry portfolio combining a close reading, a comparative theory section, and a modern application. Possible topics include authenticity online, pressure to choose a future, school and conformity, grief and meaning, AI and identity, work, rebellion, or the ethics of hope.

## Assessment Ideas
Use concept journals, passage annotations, seminar questions, argument maps, close-reading essays, film/literature analyses, comparative thinker charts, and a final inquiry portfolio. Assess precision, textual evidence, emotional maturity, philosophical accuracy, and the ability to connect personal reflection to rigorous argument.
