# Existentialism
**ID**: `existentialism` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 11 (ages 15–16)

## Course Philosophy
A deep dive into the philosophical movement that emphasizes individual freedom, responsibility, and the search for meaning in an inherently meaningless (or absurd) universe. This course challenges students to grapple with the "big questions" of identity, choice, and authenticity.

## Module 1: The Precursors: Kierkegaard and Nietzsche (20%)
- Søren Kierkegaard: The "Leap of Faith," subjective truth, and the anxiety/dread of absolute freedom
- Friedrich Nietzsche: "God is dead" (the collapse of objective moral frameworks), the Will to Power, and the Übermensch (Overman)
- Master vs Slave Morality
- **Questions**: Analyzing the meaning of "God is dead" in its historical context, explaining Kierkegaard's concept of anxiety

## Module 2: The Core: Jean-Paul Sartre (25%)
- "Existence precedes Essence": Humans are not born with a pre-defined purpose; we exist first, then define ourselves through action
- Radical Freedom and absolute responsibility
- "Condemned to be free": The burden of choice
- "Bad Faith" (Mauvaise Foi): Lying to oneself to avoid the anxiety of freedom (e.g., Sartre's example of the waiter)
- "Hell is other people" (*No Exit*): The Look (The Gaze) and how others objectify us
- **Questions**: Explaining "existence precedes essence," identifying examples of Bad Faith in modern life

## Module 3: The Absurd: Albert Camus (20%)
- The Absurd: The conflict between the human desire for meaning and the cold, silent universe
- *The Myth of Sisyphus*: Embracing the struggle. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
- Philosophical suicide vs Physical suicide vs Rebellion (living defiantly despite the absurdity)
- *The Stranger* (Meursault) and the rejection of societal norms
- **Questions**: Defining the Absurd, explaining how Sisyphus represents the human condition

## Module 4: Feminist Existentialism: Simone de Beauvoir (15%)
- *The Second Sex*: "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman."
- Woman as "The Other": How patriarchal society defines women relative to men (the Subject)
- The ethics of ambiguity: Balancing personal freedom with the freedom of others
- **Questions**: Analyzing the concept of "The Other" in societal structures, applying existential ethics to feminism

## Module 5: Existentialism in Literature and Film (20%)
- Franz Kafka: *The Metamorphosis* and *The Trial* (bureaucratic absurdity and alienation)
- Fyodor Dostoevsky: *Notes from Underground* and the rejection of utopian rationalism
- Existential themes in modern cinema (e.g., *The Matrix*, *Blade Runner*, *Everything Everywhere All at Once*)
- **Questions**: Analyzing Kafkaesque elements in literature, identifying existential themes (authenticity vs conformity) in film

## Stretch Zone
- Phenomenology: Edmund Husserl and Martin Heidegger (Dasein, "Being-in-the-world")
- Victor Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning* (Logotherapy and finding meaning through suffering)
