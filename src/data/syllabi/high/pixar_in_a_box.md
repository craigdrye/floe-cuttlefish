# Pixar in a Box
**ID**: `pixarInABox` · **Discipline**: Arts/Maths · **Year**: 11 (ages 15–16)

## Course Philosophy
Based on the renowned Khan Academy curriculum, this course explores how Pixar Animation Studios uses mathematics and computer science to create their films. It bridges the gap between creative storytelling and rigorous technical application.

## Module 1: The Art of Storytelling (20%)
- Character development: Needs vs Wants, the "spine" of a character
- Story structure: The pitch, storyboarding, and the story reel
- Visual language: Composition, color scripts, and lighting to convey emotion
- **Questions**: Analyzing a scene's color script, identifying the internal want vs external need of a protagonist

## Module 2: Modeling and Curves (20%)
- Environmental modeling: Creating virtual sets using polygons
- Character modeling: Using subdivision surfaces to create smooth shapes from coarse meshes
- Mathematics of curves: Using parabolas, cubic polynomials, and Bezier curves to define shapes and motion paths
- The concept of weighted averages (interpolation) in creating smooth curves
- **Questions**: Explaining how subdivision works, identifying the mathematical function behind a motion path

## Module 3: Animation and Rigging (20%)
- The animator's toolkit: Splines and keyframes
- Rigging: Building a digital skeleton and defining how joints move (forward and inverse kinematics)
- The Principles of Animation: Squash and stretch, anticipation, staging, follow-through, and overlapping action
- Mathematics of motion: Translation, rotation, and scaling (using matrices conceptually)
- **Questions**: Applying the squash and stretch principle to a bouncing ball animation, explaining inverse kinematics

## Module 4: Simulation and Effects (20%)
- Particle systems: Simulating fire, smoke, and explosions using basic physics engines
- Hair simulation: Using mass-spring systems to model millions of individual hairs
- Cloth simulation: Modeling friction, gravity, and collision detection to make fabric fold realistically
- Crowds: Using basic AI and state machines to simulate herd behavior
- **Questions**: Identifying the variables needed for a mass-spring system, explaining collision detection

## Module 5: Rendering and Lighting (20%)
- Virtual cameras: Focal length, depth of field, and perspective
- Ray tracing: The physics of light bouncing off surfaces
- Shading: BRDFs (Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Functions) to determine how materials (plastic, metal, skin) react to light
- Rendering equations and the computational cost of a final frame
- **Questions**: Comparing ray tracing to rasterization, predicting how a specific material will scatter light

## Stretch Zone
- Procedural generation (using noise functions like Perlin noise to create natural textures)
- Advanced matrix transformations for 3D graphics
