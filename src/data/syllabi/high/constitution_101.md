# Constitution 101
**ID**: `constitution101` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Year**: 10 (ages 14-15)

## Course Aim
Constitution 101 introduces students to the U.S. Constitution as a historical document, a working plan of government, and a practical tool for civic judgment. Students learn what the Constitution says, why it was written, how it has changed, and how citizens, courts, Congress, presidents, states, and social movements continue to argue about its meaning.

The course is designed for high-school students who are still building civic confidence. It is nonpartisan, concrete, and source-based. Students practice reading constitutional text, applying rules to real scenarios, checking official sources, discussing disagreements with care, and explaining how constitutional government affects news, elections, rights, and public responsibilities.

## Course Design Notes
- Teach constitutional study as a civic habit: read the text, identify the government actor, name the principle, consider both sides, and explain the evidence.
- Use accessible excerpts from founding documents, Reconstruction sources, landmark cases, official House and Senate resources, and National Constitution Center materials.
- Keep current events practical and balanced. Students should learn how to ask constitutional questions before taking policy positions.
- Repeat civil dialogue routines: listen accurately, restate fairly, challenge with evidence, revise when needed, and distinguish facts from opinions.
- Build toward civic readiness rather than legal specialization. Students should leave able to follow public issues, recognize basic rights and powers, and later vote with more understanding.
- Use simulations, briefs, maps, timelines, hearings, and short arguments so students practice doing constitutional reasoning, not only memorizing it.

## Chapter 1: Constitutional Conversation and Civic Reasoning
Students begin by learning how to ask constitutional questions. They explore the Constitution's structure, the difference between political and constitutional disagreement, and the habits needed for civil discussion.

**Key concepts**
- Constitution, constitutional question, political question, rule of law, limited government, popular sovereignty, civic responsibility, civil dialogue, and evidence.
- Structure of the Constitution: Preamble, Articles I-VII, amendments, clauses, and the relationship between text and practice.
- Interpretive tools: text, history, structure, precedent, purpose, consequences, and democratic accountability.
- Civic reasoning routine: actor, power, right, limit, evidence, counterargument, and conclusion.
- Reliable civic sources: National Constitution Center, iCivics, official court opinions, House.gov, Senate.gov, Congress.gov, and state or local government sites.

**Practice questions**
- What makes a disagreement constitutional rather than only political?
- Why does constitutional argument require both text and judgment?
- How can two people disagree strongly while still using evidence fairly?
- What source would you check first for a claim about Congress, the courts, or voting rules?

**Student output**
- A constitutional reasoning notebook setup with terms, clauses, cases, and reflection prompts.
- A short scenario analysis identifying the actor, constitutional issue, strongest arguments, and likely source to consult.
- A civil dialogue reflection after a structured discussion.

## Chapter 2: Revolution, Confederation, and Founding Principles
Students trace the path from colonial grievance to independence, state constitutions, the Articles of Confederation, and the call for a stronger national government. They study ideals and contradictions together.

**Key concepts**
- Natural rights, consent of the governed, social contract, republican government, civic virtue, liberty, equality, representation, and common good.
- Declaration of Independence as a statement of principles and grievances.
- Articles of Confederation: state sovereignty, weak national taxation, commerce problems, defense problems, amendment difficulty, and Shays' Rebellion.
- Founding tensions involving slavery, property, representation, Native nations, women, and who counted as part of "the people."
- Federalist No. 10 and Anti-Federalist concerns about factions, size, representation, and concentrated power.

**Practice questions**
- What problems did the Declaration of Independence say justified separation from Britain?
- Why did the Articles of Confederation create a weak national government?
- Which founding fears still appear in modern political debate?
- How should students study founding ideals while also recognizing founding exclusions?

**Student output**
- A one-page diagnosis of the Articles of Confederation with evidence from primary sources.
- A founding principles chart connecting ideals to specific phrases in the Declaration or Constitution.
- A short discussion preparation note comparing Federalist and Anti-Federalist concerns.

## Chapter 3: Convention, Compromise, and Ratification
Students study the Constitutional Convention as a set of choices and tradeoffs. They examine representation, federal power, executive power, slavery, ratification, and the promise of a Bill of Rights.

**Key concepts**
- Constitutional Convention, Virginia Plan, New Jersey Plan, Great Compromise, Three-Fifths Compromise, slave trade compromise, Electoral College, ratification, Federalists, Anti-Federalists, and amendment.
- Separation of powers, checks and balances, federalism, republicanism, and limited government.
- Article V and the amendment process as a way to change the Constitution without abandoning it.
- Bill of Rights as a ratification settlement and protection against abuse of power.
- Federalist No. 51 and the logic of ambition checking ambition.

**Practice questions**
- Why did large and small states disagree about representation?
- How did compromise make ratification possible while leaving serious injustices unresolved?
- Why did Anti-Federalists demand a Bill of Rights?
- How does Article V balance stability and change?

**Student output**
- A convention simulation record showing a proposal, tradeoffs, objections, and final vote.
- A ratification speech using one Federalist or Anti-Federalist argument.
- A diagram of checks and balances using examples from Articles I, II, and III.

## Chapter 4: Congress, Lawmaking, and Representation
Students learn how Article I creates a national legislature with democratic energy and built-in friction. They connect congressional structure to lawmaking, oversight, impeachment, budgeting, and representation.

**Key concepts**
- Article I, bicameralism, House, Senate, enumerated powers, necessary and proper clause, commerce power, taxation, spending, oversight, impeachment, and veto.
- Representation models: delegate, trustee, and politico.
- Committees, leadership, party caucuses, hearings, amendments, floor debate, conference committees, and roll-call votes.
- Filibuster, cloture, majority rule, minority rights, compromise, and gridlock.
- Official resources: House.gov, Senate.gov, Congress.gov, committee pages, and Congressional Budget Office summaries.
- Cases for federal power context: McCulloch v. Maryland, Gibbons v. Ogden, and United States v. Lopez.

**Practice questions**
- Why does the Constitution make lawmaking difficult?
- How do House and Senate rules shape different kinds of representation?
- When is compromise a civic strength, and when can it block needed action?
- How can a citizen track what Congress is doing without relying only on social media?

**Student output**
- A bill-tracking worksheet using Congress.gov and an official committee or chamber source.
- A lawmaking flowchart from idea to public law or failure.
- A short constituent letter that states a position respectfully and cites evidence.

## Chapter 5: The Presidency, Administration, and Emergency Power
Students examine Article II and the growth of executive power. They compare formal powers with informal powers shaped by parties, media, crises, bureaucracy, war, and public expectations.

**Key concepts**
- Article II, commander in chief, chief executive, take care clause, appointments, treaties, veto, pardons, impeachment, and Electoral College.
- Federalist No. 70 and the argument for energy, unity, secrecy, and dispatch in the executive.
- Executive orders, executive agreements, signing statements, agenda setting, public persuasion, and emergency action.
- Bureaucracy, cabinet departments, independent agencies, rulemaking, implementation, discretion, and accountability.
- Checks from Congress, courts, elections, public opinion, inspectors general, and federalism.
- Cases and examples: Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, United States v. Nixon, and the 12th, 22nd, and 25th Amendments.

**Practice questions**
- Why might the country need an energetic executive, and why might that be risky?
- How can Congress and courts check a president?
- What is the difference between making a law and carrying out a law?
- In an emergency, how should leaders balance speed, rights, and accountability?

**Student output**
- A crisis advisory memo identifying presidential authority, legal limits, and available checks.
- A chart distinguishing formal powers, informal powers, and political constraints.
- A short explanation of one executive branch agency and how citizens can find its rules or services.

## Chapter 6: Courts, Judicial Review, and Constitutional Argument
Students study Article III, judicial independence, judicial review, and the role of precedent. They learn how cases move through courts and how judges explain constitutional decisions.

**Key concepts**
- Article III, Supreme Court, lower federal courts, jurisdiction, standing, appeals, judicial independence, life tenure, and appointments.
- Judicial review, Marbury v. Madison, precedent, stare decisis, majority opinion, concurrence, dissent, remedy, and implementation.
- Federalist No. 78 and the argument for an independent judiciary.
- Constitutional interpretation through text, history, structure, precedent, and consequences.
- Cases for practice: Brown v. Board of Education, Cooper v. Aaron, Tinker v. Des Moines, and selected current docket summaries from official court sources.

**Practice questions**
- Why is judicial review powerful even though courts do not control the budget or military?
- What makes precedent useful, and when might judges disagree about it?
- Why do dissents matter if they do not control the outcome?
- How can students read a case without getting lost in legal language?

**Student output**
- A case brief with facts, issue, holding, reasoning, dissent or counterargument, and civic significance.
- A moot court argument applying precedent to a school speech, search, or equal protection scenario.
- A comparison paragraph explaining how two cases treat a similar constitutional principle.

## Chapter 7: Civil Liberties and the Bill of Rights
Students study protections for speech, religion, press, privacy, property, and fair process. The emphasis is practical: rights are powerful, but most require interpretation and balancing in real situations.

**Key concepts**
- Bill of Rights, incorporation, due process, free speech, symbolic speech, student speech, prior restraint, establishment clause, free exercise clause, time-place-manner rules, and public safety.
- Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Eighth Amendment protections: searches, warrants, self-incrimination, counsel, trial rights, and punishment.
- Second Amendment and incorporation through McDonald v. Chicago.
- Landmark cases: Tinker v. Des Moines, New York Times Co. v. United States, Engel v. Vitale, Wisconsin v. Yoder, Mapp v. Ohio, Miranda v. Arizona, Gideon v. Wainwright, and McDonald v. Chicago.
- Technology and rights: phones, school accounts, location data, online speech, privacy, and digital evidence.

**Practice questions**
- Why are speech and religion cases often difficult even when the First Amendment text is short?
- What should students know about rights at school?
- How do rights of the accused protect both individuals and the legitimacy of the justice system?
- How should courts balance public safety and individual liberty?

**Student output**
- A rights-in-real-life portfolio analyzing three practical scenarios.
- A student-friendly rights guide that explains one amendment, one case, and one limit.
- A short oral argument using constitutional text and precedent.

## Chapter 8: Reconstruction, Equal Protection, Voting, and Civic Participation
Students end by studying constitutional transformation after the Civil War, the long struggle for civil rights, and the role of voting and participation in constitutional democracy.

**Key concepts**
- Slavery and constitutional compromise, Civil War, Reconstruction, Second Founding, 13th Amendment, 14th Amendment, 15th Amendment, birthright citizenship, due process, equal protection, and state action.
- Segregation, civil rights statutes, social movement litigation, Brown v. Board of Education, Loving v. Virginia, and later equal protection disputes.
- Voting rights: 15th, 17th, 19th, 23rd, 24th, and 26th Amendments; Voting Rights Act of 1965; Baker v. Carr; Reynolds v. Sims; Shelby County v. Holder.
- Participation: voting, contacting officials, public comment, jury service, local meetings, petitions, peaceful protest, parties, interest groups, media literacy, and community problem-solving.
- Article V reform proposals and the difficulty of constitutional amendment.

**Practice questions**
- How did the Reconstruction Amendments change the Constitution's relationship to liberty and equality?
- Why did constitutional text alone not immediately produce equal citizenship?
- How do voting rules shape political power?
- What responsibilities come with constitutional rights?

**Student output**
- A constitutional change timeline showing text, courts, Congress, presidents, states, and civic movements.
- A nonpartisan voter readiness guide explaining registration, offices, ballot research, and reliable election sources.
- An amendment or voting reform proposal with benefits, objections, and the constitutional path for adoption.

## Capstone
Students complete a constitutional issue brief on a current or local civic question. The brief asks students to separate policy preferences from constitutional claims and to explain how a responsible citizen could learn more or participate.

The capstone must include:
- A focused constitutional or civic question.
- Relevant constitutional text, amendment, structure, or principle.
- At least one primary source, founding document, landmark case excerpt, or official government source.
- A fair explanation of the strongest argument on more than one side.
- A short section on which institution has authority: Congress, president, courts, states, local government, or citizens.
- A practical civic action step, such as contacting an official, attending a meeting, reading a ballot guide, commenting on a rule, or helping others find reliable information.

## Assessment Ideas
- Constitutional reasoning notebook entries for scenarios, clauses, cases, and reflections.
- Short primary-source annotations from the Declaration, Constitution, Federalist Papers, Anti-Federalist writings, Reconstruction Amendments, and landmark opinions.
- Civil dialogue checks that assess listening, fair restatement, evidence, and respectful challenge.
- Case briefs and comparison paragraphs.
- Simulated convention, congressional hearing, executive crisis room, moot court, or public meeting.
- Document-based essay on liberty, equality, power, or constitutional change.
- Final portfolio defense explaining how the student's constitutional reasoning improved.
