# Constitution 101
**ID**: `constitution101` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 10 (ages 14–15)

## Course Philosophy
A focused study of the United States Constitution, its historical context, its structure, and its interpretation over time. Students will learn the mechanics of the federal government and their rights as citizens.

## Module 1: The Founding Principles (20%)
- The philosophical roots: John Locke (natural rights), Montesquieu (separation of powers)
- The failures of the Articles of Confederation (Shays' Rebellion)
- The Constitutional Convention of 1787: The Great Compromise, the 3/5ths Compromise, the Electoral College
- The Federalist Papers (specifically No. 10 on factions and No. 51 on checks and balances)
- **Questions**: Weaknesses of the Articles, identifying compromises, arguments of the Federalists/Anti-Federalists

## Module 2: The Legislative Branch (Article I) (20%)
- The bicameral legislature: House of Representatives (population) vs Senate (equal representation)
- Enumerated powers (e.g., declare war, coin money, regulate interstate commerce) vs Implied powers (the Necessary and Proper Clause / Elastic Clause)
- How a bill becomes a law (the committee system, filibusters, presidential veto)
- **Questions**: House vs Senate differences, identifying enumerated powers, tracing the path of a bill

## Module 3: The Executive & Judicial Branches (Articles II & III) (20%)
- Article II: Roles of the President (Commander in Chief, Chief Diplomat), the Cabinet, the impeachment process
- The Electoral College mechanics
- Article III: The Supreme Court and the federal court system
- *Marbury v. Madison* and the establishment of Judicial Review
- **Questions**: Presidential powers, how the Electoral College works, defining Judicial Review

## Module 4: The Bill of Rights (20%)
- The First Amendment: Freedom of Religion (Establishment Clause vs Free Exercise Clause), Speech, Press, Assembly, Petition
- The Second Amendment (bear arms), Third (quartering), Fourth (search and seizure, warrants)
- Rights of the Accused (5th, 6th, 8th Amendments): Miranda rights, double jeopardy, cruel and unusual punishment
- **Questions**: Applying the First Amendment to scenarios, understanding 4th Amendment protections (probable cause)

## Module 5: Federalism and Later Amendments (20%)
- Federalism: The division of power between the national government and the states (10th Amendment, Supremacy Clause)
- The Reconstruction Amendments (13th, 14th, 15th): Abolishing slavery, Equal Protection Clause, voting rights
- Expanding the franchise: 19th Amendment (women's suffrage), 24th (poll taxes), 26th (18-year-old voting age)
- **Questions**: Defining Federalism, the impact of the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause

## Stretch Zone
- *McCulloch v. Maryland* and the expansion of federal power
- Understanding Incorporation (how the 14th Amendment applied the Bill of Rights to the states)
