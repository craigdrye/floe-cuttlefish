# Introduction to Ethics
**ID**: `introToEthics` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 7 (ages 11–12)

## Course Philosophy
This course introduces formal moral philosophy to middle school students. It moves beyond "what is fair" to the structural frameworks philosophers use to determine right and wrong. The focus is on critical reasoning, identifying ethical frameworks, and applying them to modern dilemmas.

## Module 1: Foundations of Moral Philosophy (20%)
- What is Ethics? The difference between legality, etiquette, and morality
- Objective vs. Subjective morality (Moral relativism vs. Moral absolutism)
- The role of empathy and reasoning in moral decision-making
- The "Is-Ought" problem (David Hume): Just because something *is* a certain way, doesn't mean it *ought* to be
- **Questions**: Distinguishing law vs morality, evaluating relativist arguments

## Module 2: Utilitarianism (Consequentialism) (20%)
- Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill
- The core principle: The greatest good for the greatest number
- Hedonic calculus: Measuring pleasure vs pain
- The flaws of Utilitarianism: The tyranny of the majority, the problem of predicting consequences
- Thought experiments: The Trolley Problem variants (The Fat Man, The Doctor)
- **Questions**: Applying utilitarian logic to dilemmas, identifying flaws in utilitarian outcomes

## Module 3: Deontology (Rule-Based Ethics) (20%)
- Immanuel Kant and Duty
- The Categorical Imperative: Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law
- People as ends, not means: You cannot use a person merely as a tool to get what you want
- The strictness of Kant: The inquiring murderer (Is it ever okay to lie?)
- **Questions**: Applying the categorical imperative, contrasting Kant with Utilitarianism

## Module 4: Virtue Ethics (20%)
- Aristotle and the ancient Greek approach
- Focusing on character rather than individual actions: "What kind of person should I be?"
- The Golden Mean: Virtue as the midpoint between two vices (e.g., Courage is between cowardice and recklessness)
- Eudaimonia (Human flourishing)
- **Questions**: Identifying the "golden mean" of various traits, evaluating character-based ethics

## Module 5: Applied Ethics (Modern Dilemmas) (20%)
- Bioethics: Medical testing, cloning, resource allocation in hospitals
- Environmental Ethics: Do trees or animals have moral standing? Anthropocentrism vs biocentrism
- Technology Ethics: Social media algorithms, AI decision-making (e.g., self-driving cars programming)
- **Questions**: Applying the three major frameworks (Utilitarian, Kantian, Virtue) to modern tech/environmental dilemmas

## Stretch Zone
- Social Contract Theory (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau)
- Care Ethics (Carol Gilligan): Focusing on relationships and responsibilities rather than universal rules
- The concept of Moral Luck (Thomas Nagel)
