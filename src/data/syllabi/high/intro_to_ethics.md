# Introduction to Ethics
*A first course in moral philosophy: learning to ask "What should we do, and why?" and to defend an answer with reasons.*

**ID**: `introToEthics` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This course introduces students to ethics as a disciplined way of thinking, not a list of rules to memorize. Students learn to recognize when a question is genuinely moral, to separate morality from law, etiquette, and mere preference, and to build and evaluate moral arguments made of claims, reasons, and evidence. The aim is reasoning that is fair, careful, and honest about uncertainty.

Students meet the major frameworks of normative ethics: consequentialism and utilitarianism, deontology and duty-based reasoning, virtue ethics, and the ethics of care. Each framework is taught as a lens that highlights a different feature of a problem, such as outcomes, duties, character, or relationships. Students practice applying several frameworks to the same dilemma and reaching an all-things-considered judgment they can explain and defend.

The course is concrete and discussion-rich, beginning with familiar cases from school, friendship, family, online life, and the community before naming the larger philosophical ideas. It is age-appropriate for early high school: rigorous in method but accessible in language, and built around charitable disagreement rather than winning debates. Students leave able to name frameworks, spot common fallacies in moral reasoning, and write a structured ethical case analysis.

## Course Design Notes
The course moves from method to frameworks to application. Chapters 1 and 2 build the toolkit: classifying questions and constructing fair arguments. Chapters 3 through 6 introduce the four major frameworks one at a time, each anchored to a concrete case and a signature concept (the greatest happiness, the categorical imperative, the golden mean, attentiveness to dependence). Chapters 7 and 8 compare frameworks and extend them to metaethics and applied issues in technology, animals, and the environment. The Capstone integrates everything into a single structured analysis.

Frameworks are presented as complementary tools, not rival teams. Students are graded on the quality of their reasoning, the fairness of their treatment of opposing views, and their willingness to revise a position when given a good objection, never on reaching a particular "approved" conclusion. Historical figures (Bentham, Mill, Kant, Aristotle, Gilligan, Noddings) are introduced briefly to anchor ideas, but the emphasis is on the concepts, not biography.

## Chapter 1: What Makes a Question Ethical?
- **Core questions**: Is this a moral question, a legal question, a question of manners, or a matter of taste? Who are the stakeholders affected by the choice? What kinds of reasons count as moral reasons?
- **Key concepts**: ethics, morality, law, custom, preference, harm, fairness, stakeholder, the fact-value distinction, descriptive vs. normative claims.
- **Applied skills**: Sort dilemmas into categories (moral, legal, etiquette, preference); identify everyone affected by a decision; distinguish a claim about what *is* the case from a claim about what *ought* to be the case.
- **Common traps**: Treating "it's against the law" as identical to "it's wrong"; assuming a strong feeling of disgust settles a moral question; confusing a personal preference with a moral obligation.

## Chapter 2: Reasons, Arguments, and Fair Disagreement
- **Core questions**: What is the speaker's main claim? Which reason supports it, and is that reason true and relevant? What is the strongest objection, and how would a fair person reply?
- **Key concepts**: claim, premise, conclusion, evidence, assumption, validity, soundness, charitable interpretation (the principle of charity), the appeal-to-popularity and ad hominem fallacies.
- **Applied skills**: Map a short argument into premises and a conclusion; build a "claim, reason, objection, reply" paragraph; restate an opposing view fairly before criticizing it.
- **Common traps**: Attacking the person instead of the argument; assuming "most people do it" makes it right (the bandwagon fallacy); confusing a valid argument with a true one.

## Chapter 3: Consequences and the Greatest Good (Utilitarianism)
- **Core questions**: What outcomes could follow from each option? Who is helped and who is harmed, and by how much? Which action produces the greatest overall good?
- **Key concepts**: consequentialism, utilitarianism, utility, the greatest happiness principle, Jeremy Bentham, John Stuart Mill, act vs. rule utilitarianism, the tyranny-of-the-majority objection.
- **Applied skills**: Build a consequence map weighing benefits and harms across stakeholders; distinguish judging a single act from judging a general rule; identify whose interests a calculation might overlook.
- **Common traps**: Counting only short-term or visible consequences; ignoring a small minority that bears a large harm; assuming "the most popular choice" is the same as "the greatest good."

## Chapter 4: Duties, Rights, and Respect for Persons (Deontology)
- **Core questions**: What duty is being honored or broken? Could the rule behind this action be willed as a universal law? Does the choice treat each person as an end in themselves, not merely as a means?
- **Key concepts**: deontology, duty, the categorical imperative, universalizability, human dignity, rights, treating people as ends vs. means, Immanuel Kant.
- **Applied skills**: Apply the universalizability test to a proposed action; distinguish using a person merely as a tool from treating them with respect; compare a consequence-based verdict with a duty-based verdict on the same case.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the categorical imperative with the Golden Rule; thinking deontology forbids ever considering outcomes; treating a rule's exceptions as proof the rule is worthless.

## Chapter 5: Character, Virtue, and Flourishing (Virtue Ethics)
- **Core questions**: What kind of person does this action make me? Where does the right amount of a trait lie between too little and too much? What would a person of good character do here?
- **Key concepts**: virtue ethics, character, habit, the golden mean, excess and deficiency, courage between cowardice and recklessness, eudaimonia (flourishing), practical wisdom, Aristotle.
- **Applied skills**: Place a virtue on a "deficiency / mean / excess" line; reason from character rather than from a single rule or outcome; explain how habits shape who a person becomes.
- **Common traps**: Treating every virtue as "more is always better"; confusing acting virtuously with merely following feelings; assuming virtue ethics gives no guidance in hard cases.

## Chapter 6: Care, Relationships, and Responsibility (Care Ethics)
- **Core questions**: Who depends on whom in this situation? What would caring well actually require? When does loyalty to one person become unfair to others?
- **Key concepts**: care ethics, relationships, dependence and vulnerability, responsibility, attentiveness, partiality vs. impartiality, Carol Gilligan, Nel Noddings.
- **Applied skills**: Map who depends on whom in a case; explain how focusing on relationships changes the moral question; weigh special responsibilities to particular people against fairness to everyone.
- **Common traps**: Assuming care ethics is "just being nice"; letting loyalty justify clear unfairness; ignoring that impartial frameworks and care ethics can reach different verdicts.

## Chapter 7: Comparing Frameworks and Metaethical Questions
- **Core questions**: How does each framework view the same dilemma? When frameworks conflict, how do you reach an all-things-considered judgment? Are moral claims objective, or just opinions and culture?
- **Key concepts**: framework comparison, moral conflict, all-things-considered judgment, the trolley problem, moral relativism vs. objectivism, cultural relativism, moral uncertainty.
- **Applied skills**: Fill a four-column table (consequences / duties / virtues / care) for one shared dilemma; evaluate cultural relativism with its strengths and problems; defend a judgment while acknowledging what is uncertain.
- **Common traps**: Concluding "it's all just opinion" because people disagree; assuming one framework must always win; treating tolerance as proof that no moral claim can be mistaken.

## Chapter 8: Applied Ethics — Technology, Animals, and the Environment
- **Core questions**: What makes online harm real even through a screen? Do animals or ecosystems have moral standing? How should scarce resources be shared fairly?
- **Key concepts**: privacy, consent, digital footprint, moral standing, animal welfare and sentience, environmental ethics, the common good, intergenerational responsibility, scarce resources.
- **Applied skills**: Write a short case brief on a modern issue (data tracking, AI homework help, factory farming, food waste); argue for or against extending moral standing to animals or nature; apply a framework to a resource-sharing problem.
- **Common traps**: Assuming online actions have no real victims; treating "natural" as automatically "right"; confusing what is legal to do online with what is ethical to do.

## Capstone
Students produce a structured "Ethics Case Analysis" of a realistic dilemma drawn from school, online life, home, or the community. The analysis must state the situation and key facts, identify all stakeholders, list at least three genuine options, and evaluate the dilemma through all four frameworks (consequences, duties, virtues, care). Students then defend an all-things-considered recommendation, state the strongest objection to it, and reply. They present in a small group and revise their recommendation in light of questions, demonstrating that changing a view in response to a good reason is a strength, not a weakness.

## Assessment Ideas
- Vocabulary-in-use checks: students must use terms such as stakeholder, consequentialism, categorical imperative, golden mean, and moral standing correctly in original examples.
- Argument-mapping tasks: break a moral argument into premises and conclusion, then name any fallacy present.
- Single-case, multi-framework analyses applying two or more frameworks to one dilemma.
- Dilemma journals where students name the framework used and state one strong objection to their own view.
- Capstone grading for realistic scenario design, accurate framework comparison, quality of the objection-and-reply, respectful presentation, and thoughtful revision.
