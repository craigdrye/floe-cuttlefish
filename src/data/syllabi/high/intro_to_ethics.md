# Introduction to Ethics
**ID**: `introToEthics` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: 7 (ages 11-12)

## Course Aim
Introduce younger secondary students to ethical thinking as a careful way of asking, "What should we do, and why?" Students learn to tell the difference between law, manners, preference, and morality; use basic ethical vocabulary; compare major moral frameworks; and give reasons for decisions in everyday dilemmas.

## Course Design Notes
This course is concrete, discussion-rich, and age-appropriate. It begins with familiar school, family, friendship, online, and community cases before naming larger philosophical ideas. Students are not asked to memorize difficult texts; instead, they practice careful comparison, fair listening, simple argument maps, and respectful disagreement. The main frameworks are taught as tools that help students see different parts of a problem, not as teams they must join.

## Chapter 1: What Makes a Question Ethical?
- **Key concepts**: ethics, morality, law, rule, custom, preference, harm, fairness, reason, stakeholder.
- **Practice questions**: Is this a moral question, a legal question, a manners question, or a taste question? Who is affected by the choice? What reasons would count as fair reasons?
- **Student output**: a dilemma sorting chart that explains why examples such as queue-jumping, lying to avoid trouble, returning lost money, and breaking a school rule belong in different categories.

## Chapter 2: Reasons, Feelings, and Fair Discussion
- **Key concepts**: claim, reason, evidence, example, assumption, objection, empathy, bias, charitable listening, disagreement.
- **Practice questions**: What is the speaker's main claim? Which reason supports it? What feeling is important here, and what reason still needs to be given?
- **Student output**: a short "claim, reason, objection, reply" paragraph about a classroom or friendship dilemma.

## Chapter 3: Consequences and the Greatest Good
- **Key concepts**: consequence, benefit, harm, happiness, suffering, majority, minority, prediction, utilitarianism, tradeoff.
- **Practice questions**: What good outcomes could happen? What bad outcomes could happen? Who might be helped, and who might be overlooked?
- **Student output**: a consequence map for a concrete case, such as whether a school should cancel a fun event to save money for a community need.

## Chapter 4: Rules, Duties, and Respect for People
- **Key concepts**: duty, promise, honesty, respect, rights, universal rule, using someone, dignity, exception, deontology.
- **Practice questions**: What rule is being followed or broken? Could everyone fairly use that rule? Does the choice treat a person as important in themselves?
- **Student output**: a rule test comparing two cases of lying, one to avoid embarrassment and one to protect someone from unfair harm.

## Chapter 5: Character, Habits, and Flourishing
- **Key concepts**: virtue, character, habit, courage, honesty, kindness, patience, excess, deficiency, flourishing, role model.
- **Practice questions**: What kind of person would this action help someone become? Is there too little, too much, or the right amount of a trait? What would a wise friend do?
- **Student output**: a virtue triangle that places courage between cowardice and recklessness, then applies the pattern to honesty, generosity, or loyalty.

## Chapter 6: Care, Relationships, and Responsibilities
- **Key concepts**: care ethics, relationship, responsibility, dependence, trust, loyalty, need, attention, partiality, fairness.
- **Practice questions**: Who depends on whom in this situation? What would caring well require? When does loyalty become unfair to others?
- **Student output**: a relationship web for a family, team, or friendship case, with a short explanation of how care changes the moral focus.

## Chapter 7: Comparing Frameworks in Real Dilemmas
- **Key concepts**: framework, comparison, conflict, compromise, all-things-considered judgment, trolley problem, moral luck, uncertainty.
- **Practice questions**: How would a consequence-focused thinker view this case? How would a rule-focused thinker view it? How would a virtue or care thinker view it?
- **Student output**: a four-column comparison table applying consequences, duties, virtues, and care to one shared dilemma, such as whether to tell an adult about a friend's risky secret.

## Chapter 8: Ethics in School, Technology, Animals, and the Environment
- **Key concepts**: privacy, consent, digital footprint, animal welfare, environment, responsibility, common good, scarce resource, moral standing.
- **Practice questions**: What makes online harm real even when it happens through a screen? Do animals or nature count morally, and in what way? How should people share limited resources?
- **Student output**: a mini case brief on a modern issue, such as phone tracking, AI homework help, food waste, classroom fairness, pet care, or playground space.

## Capstone
Students create an "Ethics Case Card" for a realistic dilemma that could happen at school, online, at home, or in the community. The card must include the situation, the people affected, key facts, at least three possible choices, and a comparison of consequences, duties, virtues, and care. Students present the case in a small-group discussion and revise their recommendation after hearing questions from classmates.

## Assessment Ideas
- Vocabulary checks focused on using terms such as ethics, stakeholder, consequence, duty, virtue, care, and fairness in complete examples.
- Dilemma journals where students explain a choice, name the framework they used, and identify one possible objection.
- Discussion assessment based on listening, giving reasons, asking fair questions, and changing or sharpening a view when appropriate.
- Short comparison tasks that ask students to apply two or more frameworks to the same concrete case.
- Capstone grading for realistic scenario design, accurate framework comparison, respectful presentation, and thoughtful revision.

## Research Notes
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "The Definition of Morality": useful teacher background for distinguishing morality from law, etiquette, and personal preference. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/morality-definition/
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Consequentialism": teacher background for the idea that outcomes matter morally and for the strengths and limits of consequence-based reasoning. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consequentialism/
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Deontological Ethics": teacher background for duty, rights, and respect for persons. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ethics-deontological/
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Virtue Ethics": supports the course's focus on character, habits, and flourishing. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ethics-virtue/
- Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Ethics": accessible overview of normative ethics, metaethics, and applied ethics for teacher planning. https://iep.utm.edu/ethics/
- PLATO, "Pre-College Philosophy": supports age-appropriate philosophy through questions, discussion, reasoning, and classroom inquiry. https://plato-apa.org/
- PLATO, "Philosophy Learning and Teaching Organization Resources": useful source for lessons and discussion practices for younger philosophy students. https://plato-apa.org/resources/
- National High School Ethics Bowl, "How Ethics Bowl Works": useful model for collaborative ethical discussion, adapted here for younger students without competition pressure. https://nhseb.org/how
