# 8th Grade Maths Collections
Turn middle-grade arithmetic into the connected algebraic tools that open the door to Algebra 1.

**ID**: `col-8th-grade-math` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
Eighth grade is the bridge year: the place where number sense, proportional reasoning, and informal geometry harden into the algebra and function thinking that high-school mathematics depends on. This course prepares students for Algebra 1 by treating linear relationships as the central idea and showing how slope, equations, tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions are four views of one thing. Students learn to work fluently with linear equations and functions, compare representations, solve introductory systems, reason with integer exponents and scientific notation, distinguish rational from irrational numbers, analyze transformations and similarity, apply the Pythagorean theorem, model the volume of round solids, and interpret bivariate data.

The course follows the Common Core Grade 8 strands and the New York Next Generation Learning Standards, with coverage that also fits the "Get Ready for 8th Grade" review track. The throughline is structure: students are asked again and again to name the rule before computing, to translate a situation among graph, table, equation, and words, and to check whether an answer is reasonable. Geometry is anchored to the coordinate plane so that distance, transformation, and the Pythagorean theorem reinforce the same algebraic habits rather than living in a separate world.

By the end, a student should be able to read a real or realistic data set or comparison situation, build a linear model or a system from it, interpret slope and intercept (or an intersection point) in plain language, attach a geometry or measurement connection where it fits, and say honestly where the model breaks down. That package is exactly the readiness Algebra 1 assumes.

## Course Design Notes
Keep the course explicit, visual, and practice-rich, moving steadily from worked examples to general rules. Route questions here when they involve linear equations, slope, functions, systems of equations, integer exponent rules, scientific notation, rational and irrational numbers, transformations, congruence and similarity, the Pythagorean theorem and distance, the volume of cylinders, cones, and spheres, scatterplots and association, two-way tables, or general algebra-readiness review. Encourage students to connect graphs, tables, equations, and verbal descriptions on nearly every topic, and to support error analysis (spotting a broken exponent rule or a miscomputed slope) as a first-class skill. Questions should stay at grade level: numbers small enough to reason about, one core idea per item, and distractors that mirror the specific mistake an eighth grader actually makes.

## Chapter 1: Number Systems, Exponents, and Scientific Notation
- **Core questions**: Classify a number as rational or irrational; estimate an irrational square root between consecutive integers; apply integer exponent rules; convert between standard and scientific notation; compute with quantities in scientific notation.
- **Key concepts**: Rational vs. irrational numbers, terminating and repeating decimal expansions, square and cube roots, the laws of integer exponents (product, quotient, power, zero, and negative exponents), powers of 10, and scientific notation for very large and very small quantities.
- **Applied skills**: Comparing the size of numbers written in scientific notation, estimating where an irrational root sits on a number line, and choosing the right exponent rule for a given expression.
- **Common traps**: Treating a non-terminating but repeating decimal as irrational; adding exponents when bases differ or when multiplying unlike terms; forgetting that a negative exponent means a reciprocal, not a negative number; mishandling the sign of the power of 10 for small quantities.

## Chapter 2: Linear Equations in One Variable
- **Core questions**: Solve multi-step equations with parentheses and fractions; collect variables on both sides; decide whether an equation has one solution, no solution, or infinitely many; write an equation from a word situation and check the solution.
- **Key concepts**: Inverse operations, the distributive property, combining like terms, variables on both sides, the three solution cases, and verifying a solution by substitution.
- **Applied skills**: Translating a real situation into an equation, clearing fractions cleanly, and reading the structure of an equation to predict its number of solutions before solving.
- **Common traps**: Distributing to only the first term inside parentheses; sign errors when moving a term across the equals sign; concluding "no solution" when the result is actually a true statement (infinitely many) or vice versa.

## Chapter 3: Proportional Relationships, Slope, and Lines
- **Core questions**: Find slope from a graph, a table, two points, or a story; identify the y-intercept and its meaning; write a line in y = mx + b; compare two linear relationships given in different forms.
- **Key concepts**: Unit rate as slope, proportional graphs through the origin (y = mx), rate of change, initial value, y = mx + b, and equivalence of representations.
- **Applied skills**: Computing slope as change in y over change in x, interpreting slope and intercept in context, and judging which of two relationships is steeper or cheaper directly from its form.
- **Common traps**: Inverting the slope formula (change in x over change in y); reading the y-intercept off the wrong axis; assuming every line is proportional when it does not pass through the origin.

## Chapter 4: Functions and Algebra Readiness
- **Core questions**: Decide whether a relation is a function; evaluate a simple function rule; interpret a graph qualitatively; build a function from a context; compare a linear function with a nonlinear one.
- **Key concepts**: Functions as input-output rules, one output per input, introductory function notation, linear vs. nonlinear behavior, rate of change, and initial value.
- **Applied skills**: Using the vertical-line test, matching a story to a graph (increasing, decreasing, constant), and identifying a function as linear from equal differences in a table.
- **Common traps**: Calling a relation "not a function" because two inputs share an output (which is allowed); assuming any curve is "not a function"; treating a table with unequal x-steps as automatically nonlinear without checking the rate.

## Chapter 5: Systems of Linear Equations
- **Core questions**: Solve a simple system by graphing; check whether an ordered pair satisfies both equations; use substitution in accessible cases; interpret an intersection as a break-even or comparison point; identify systems with no solution or infinitely many.
- **Key concepts**: A system as two conditions at once, the intersection point as the simultaneous solution, substitution, and the graphical meaning of parallel (no solution) and identical (infinitely many) lines.
- **Applied skills**: Modeling two competing plans or prices, explaining what the intersection means in everyday terms, and relating slope and intercept to whether lines cross.
- **Common traps**: Reading the intersection coordinates in the wrong order; assuming a solution exists when the lines are parallel; checking a candidate point in only one of the two equations.

## Chapter 6: Transformations, Congruence, and Similarity
- **Core questions**: Describe translations, reflections, and rotations on the coordinate plane; apply a dilation and find the scale factor; identify congruent vs. similar figures; solve angle problems with triangles, parallel lines, and transversals.
- **Key concepts**: Rigid motions and congruence, dilations and similarity, scale factor, coordinate rules for transformations, the triangle angle-sum, exterior angles, and angle relationships formed by a transversal.
- **Applied skills**: Predicting image coordinates under a transformation, using similarity to find a missing side, and combining angle facts to find an unknown angle.
- **Common traps**: Confusing congruence (same size) with similarity (same shape, scaled); adding instead of multiplying by the scale factor; misnaming corresponding angles when a transversal crosses parallel lines.

## Chapter 7: Pythagorean Theorem, Distance, and Volume
- **Core questions**: Find a missing side of a right triangle; use the converse to test whether a triangle is right; compute the distance between two points; find the volume of a cylinder, cone, or sphere.
- **Key concepts**: The Pythagorean theorem a² + b² = c², its converse, distance on the coordinate plane as a hypotenuse, and the volume formulas V = πr²h (cylinder), V = (1/3)πr²h (cone), and V = (4/3)πr³ (sphere).
- **Applied skills**: Identifying the hypotenuse before applying the theorem, setting up a distance calculation from coordinates, and choosing and substituting into the correct volume formula with units.
- **Common traps**: Treating the longest given side as a leg, or solving for the hypotenuse when a leg is unknown; forgetting the 1/3 factor for a cone; using diameter in place of radius.

## Chapter 8: Scatterplots, Association, and Modeling Studio
- **Core questions**: Interpret a scatterplot's pattern; sketch an informal line of fit; use a linear model to make a prediction; read a two-way frequency or relative-frequency table.
- **Key concepts**: Bivariate data, clusters and outliers, positive/negative and linear/nonlinear association, an informal line of best fit, slope and intercept in a data context, and two-way tables.
- **Applied skills**: Describing association in words, estimating a prediction from a fitted line, and computing a relative frequency from a two-way table.
- **Common traps**: Confusing association with causation; reading a strong association as "proof" of a cause; mixing up row vs. column relative frequencies; forcing a straight line onto clearly curved data.

## Capstone
Students complete an algebra-readiness modeling project using a real or realistic data set or comparison situation. The final product should include a linear equation or a system, a graph, a table, a written interpretation of slope and intercept (or the meaning of an intersection), one geometry or measurement connection when appropriate, and a reflection on the limits of the model — for example, where extrapolation stops being reasonable, or where association should not be read as causation. The project is assessed for algebraic accuracy, quality and consistency of representations, clarity of interpretation, sound data reasoning, and overall readiness for Algebra 1.
