# US Government and Civics
How power actually works in the United States — who can do what, under which rule, and how citizens respond with evidence instead of panic.

**ID**: `usGovAndCivics` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This course helps students understand how the U.S. constitutional system works: how political power is granted and limited, how the three branches and the levels of federalism interact, how rights are protected and contested, and how ordinary citizens can participate effectively. It keeps the constitutional spine of a strong AP-style government course while staying anchored to one practical question that recurs in every chapter: "Who can do what, under which rule, and how can people respond?"

Students read founding documents and landmark Supreme Court cases as evidence rather than slogans, and they practice tracing real public problems to the correct venue — Congress, the president, an agency, a court, a state legislature, a city council, or a school board. The goal is useful precision: being able to explain what the Constitution says, what each actor can and cannot do, and why a popular idea can still fail to become policy.

The course also builds the civic skills students need outside the classroom: reading a bill summary or roll-call vote, evaluating a poll, spotting a misleading headline, following campaign money without assuming every influence is bribery, and writing a clear, evidence-based message to a public official. Throughout, the aim is participation that is lawful, specific, and timed to the actual decision — strategy over outrage.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus covered the classic AP-style modules but underweighted civic skills. This version keeps the constitutional foundations and the branches of government while strengthening document analysis, data and polling interpretation, media literacy, the federal/state/local authority map, policy tracing, and concrete citizen action. It is grounded in the College Board AP U.S. Government and Politics framework and the NCSS C3 inquiry/civic-action framework, but it is written to be age-appropriate and usable for any motivated high-school student, not only exam-takers.

Each chapter combines a conceptual core with a real-world application and a set of common traps — predictable errors such as confusing civil liberties with civil rights, assuming the president can act alone, or treating a poll's headline number as the whole story. Assessments emphasize evidence use, institutional reasoning, and nonpartisan clarity over memorized vocabulary, and the course culminates in a civic action portfolio on a genuine public issue.

## Chapter 1: Constitutional Foundations and Democratic Ideas
**Core questions**: What problem was each founding document trying to solve? How does the Constitution both authorize and limit government power? Why did Federalists and Anti-Federalists disagree, and what tradeoffs did their compromise lock in?
**Key concepts**: natural rights, popular sovereignty, social contract, republicanism, limited government, rule of law, separation of powers, checks and balances, factions, the Declaration of Independence, the Articles of Confederation weaknesses, Federalist No. 10, Federalist No. 51, Brutus No. 1.
**Applied skills**: Use specific constitutional text as evidence in a claim; compare Federalist and Anti-Federalist arguments fairly; explain how a principle (e.g., checks and balances) constrains a real action.
**Common traps**: Treating the Declaration as legally binding law; assuming the Articles of Confederation had a strong central government; reducing the Anti-Federalists to people who simply "opposed the Constitution" rather than people worried about concentrated power and missing rights protections.

## Chapter 2: Federalism and "Who Can Fix This?"
**Core questions**: Which level of government has authority over a given problem? When does national uniformity matter and when does local experimentation help? How do funding and the supremacy clause shape what states actually do?
**Key concepts**: enumerated powers, reserved powers (Tenth Amendment), concurrent powers, supremacy clause, commerce clause, federal grants and mandates, preemption, state policy variation, the role of local government.
**Applied skills**: Assign a real public problem to the correct federal, state, local, agency, or court venue; trace how conditional funding shapes state and local choices.
**Common traps**: Assuming "the government" is one thing; believing the federal government controls schools, policing, or zoning directly; ignoring that states can resist, implement, or exceed federal policy.

## Chapter 3: Congress and Lawmaking
**Core questions**: Why can a popular bill still fail? How do committees, party leadership, district incentives, and Senate rules shape outcomes? What is the difference between authorization, appropriation, and oversight?
**Key concepts**: bicameralism, House vs. Senate, representation, committees, party leadership, filibuster and cloture (60-vote threshold), reconciliation, impeachment, oversight, the budget process, gerrymandering and redistricting.
**Applied skills**: Trace a bill from introduction through committee, floor action, conference/amendment, presidential action, and implementation; read a roll-call vote or bill summary accurately.
**Common traps**: Thinking a majority alone passes everything in the Senate; confusing passing a bill with funding a program; assuming oversight always means meaningful accountability rather than political theater.

## Chapter 4: Presidency, Bureaucracy, and Executive Power
**Core questions**: What can a president do alone, and what requires Congress, courts, agencies, states, or funding? Why do executive orders matter yet remain vulnerable? How does the bureaucracy turn promises into rules, permits, benefits, and enforcement?
**Key concepts**: commander in chief, veto and override, executive orders, appointments and confirmation, the cabinet, independent agencies, administrative discretion, rulemaking, enforcement priorities, emergency powers.
**Applied skills**: Distinguish a campaign promise, an executive action, an agency rule, and a statute; read a Federal Register notice or agency explainer as a civic document.
**Common traps**: Believing executive orders are permanent or equal to laws; assuming the president directly runs every agency; ignoring that funding and the courts can blunt executive action.

## Chapter 5: Courts, Civil Liberties, and Civil Rights
**Core questions**: How did courts gain the power of judicial review? How are liberties and rights extended to the states? What does a court actually decide, and what is left to other actors?
**Key concepts**: judicial review (Marbury v. Madison), precedent and stare decisis, the Bill of Rights, selective incorporation via the Fourteenth Amendment, due process, equal protection, free speech, religion clauses, rights of the accused, civil rights and voting rights; cases such as Brown v. Board, Gideon v. Wainwright, Tinker v. Des Moines, Engel v. Vitale.
**Applied skills**: Separate civil liberties from civil rights; apply a constitutional test or precedent to a new fact pattern; explain what a ruling did and did not do, and what political actors may do next.
**Common traps**: Confusing civil liberties (protections from government) with civil rights (protection from discrimination); thinking the Bill of Rights always applied to states; assuming a court "settles" an issue permanently.

## Chapter 6: Political Ideologies, Public Opinion, and Media
**Core questions**: How do people form political beliefs? How should a citizen read a poll, a chart, or a campaign ad? How do you tell a value apart from an empirical claim or a policy tool?
**Key concepts**: liberalism, conservatism, libertarianism, populism, political socialization, party identification, polling, random sampling, margin of error, question wording, media framing, misinformation, the public agenda.
**Applied skills**: Evaluate a poll using sample, date, wording, margin of error, and trend; rewrite a misleading headline to be accurate; identify evidence, emotion, omission, and persuasion in messaging.
**Common traps**: Treating a poll's headline number as exact truth; ignoring margin of error and sampling; mistaking a value disagreement for a factual one.

## Chapter 7: Elections, Parties, Interest Groups, and Money
**Core questions**: Why do parties matter even when voters dislike them? How does money shape access and attention? Why do different elections reward different strategies?
**Key concepts**: voter registration and turnout, primaries and caucuses, the Electoral College (538 electors, 270 to win), congressional elections, major and third parties, interest groups, lobbying, PACs and Super PACs, campaign finance (Citizens United v. FEC), mobilization.
**Applied skills**: Distinguish vote share, seat share, turnout, and governing power; follow lobbying or campaign finance without assuming every influence is bribery; build a voter guide from nonpartisan sources.
**Common traps**: Assuming the popular-vote winner always wins the presidency; treating all money in politics as identical; equating high turnout in one group with overall electoral dominance.

## Chapter 8: Public Policy and Civic Action Lab
**Core questions**: How does a concern become a policy proposal? Which actor can act now, which must wait, and which is being blamed unfairly? What civic action is lawful, specific, evidence-based, and timed to the decision?
**Key concepts**: agenda setting, policy formulation, adoption, implementation, evaluation, fiscal vs. monetary policy, social and foreign policy, public comment, petitions, representative contact, deliberation.
**Applied skills**: Write a concise message to a representative, agency, school board, or city council; build a public-comment or testimony outline with a clear ask and supporting evidence; reflect on accuracy, privacy, and respectful disagreement.
**Common traps**: Aiming civic action at the wrong actor or after the decision is made; confusing outrage with strategy; assuming one email or petition automatically changes policy.

## Capstone
Students complete a civic action portfolio on a real public issue. The portfolio includes a constitutional or institutional authority map ("who can fix this?"), an evidence packet drawing on primary sources and reliable data, a stakeholder analysis, a media check that compares coverage across sources, a concrete and lawful action step aimed at the correct decision-maker, and a written reflection on responsible participation. The portfolio is assessed for civic accuracy, evidence use, institutional reasoning, and nonpartisan clarity.
