# US Government and Civics
**ID**: `usGovAndCivics` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 11 (ages 15–16)

## Course Philosophy
This course provides a comprehensive understanding of the structure, function, and processes of the United States government. It emphasizes the practical application of civic knowledge, focusing on elections, political parties, and how citizens can influence public policy.

## Module 1: Foundations of American Democracy (20%)
- The philosophical underpinnings of the US government (Locke, Hobbes, Montesquieu)
- The Declaration of Independence and the Articles of Confederation
- The Constitutional Convention: compromises, federalism, and the separation of powers
- The Federalist vs Anti-Federalist debates (Federalist No. 10 and No. 51)
- **Questions**: Explaining the concept of checks and balances, analyzing the arguments for and against ratification

## Module 2: The Three Branches of Government (25%)
- The Legislative Branch (Congress): bicameralism, committee system, the legislative process, gerrymandering, and the role of leadership (Speaker, Majority Leader)
- The Executive Branch (Presidency): formal and informal powers, the Electoral College, the bureaucracy (cabinet departments, independent agencies)
- The Judicial Branch (Federal Courts): structure of the court system, judicial review (Marbury v. Madison), judicial activism vs restraint
- **Questions**: Explaining how a bill becomes a law, analyzing the expansion of presidential power, understanding the Supreme Court confirmation process

## Module 3: Civil Liberties and Civil Rights (20%)
- Civil Liberties (protection *from* the government): The Bill of Rights, selective incorporation (14th Amendment Due Process Clause)
- First Amendment rights: speech, press, religion (Establishment and Free Exercise clauses), assembly
- Rights of the accused: 4th, 5th, 6th, and 8th Amendments (search and seizure, Miranda rights)
- Civil Rights (protection *by* the government against discrimination): The 14th Amendment Equal Protection Clause
- Key Supreme Court cases: *Brown v. Board of Education*, *Roe v. Wade*, *Gideon v. Wainwright*
- **Questions**: Differentiating between civil liberties and civil rights, applying the 1st Amendment to modern scenarios (e.g., student speech)

## Module 4: Political Participation and Elections (20%)
- Political socialization: how individuals form their political beliefs (family, media, education)
- Political parties: their role, structure, and the two-party system
- Interest groups and lobbying: how they influence policy, Political Action Committees (PACs) and Super PACs
- The electoral process: primaries, caucuses, the general election, and the role of the Electoral College
- Voter turnout and factors influencing voting behavior
- **Questions**: Analyzing the impact of money in politics, explaining the function of the Electoral College, evaluating strategies used by interest groups

## Module 5: Public Policy (15%)
- The policy-making process: agenda setting, formulation, adoption, implementation, and evaluation
- Economic policy: fiscal policy (taxing and spending by Congress/President) vs monetary policy (the Federal Reserve)
- Social policy: healthcare, education, welfare programs (Social Security, Medicare)
- Foreign policy: the President's role as Commander in Chief vs Congress's power to declare war
- **Questions**: Differentiating between fiscal and monetary policy, analyzing the trade-offs in major social policy decisions

## Stretch Zone
- Analyze the impact of *Citizens United v. FEC* on campaign finance
- Evaluate the arguments for and against abolishing the Electoral College
