# US Government and Civics
**ID**: `usGovAndCivics` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: High School

## Course Aim
Help students understand how the U.S. constitutional system works, how political power is exercised, how rights are protected and contested, and how citizens can participate with evidence rather than panic. This course is grounded in U.S. Government content but keeps asking the practical civic question: "Who can do what, under which rule, and how can people respond?"

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus covered the classic AP-style modules, but it needed stronger civic skills: document analysis, data interpretation, media literacy, local/state/federal authority, policy tracing, and citizen action. This rewrite keeps the constitutional spine while making the course more usable for voting, news-reading, and civic participation.

## Chapter 1: Constitutional Foundations and Democratic Ideas
**Key concepts**: natural rights, popular sovereignty, republicanism, social contract, limited government, rule of law, federalism, separation of powers, checks and balances, factions, constitutional compromise.

**Core documents and debates**
- Declaration of Independence
- Articles of Confederation
- U.S. Constitution
- Federalist No. 10 and Federalist No. 51
- Brutus-style Anti-Federalist concerns

**Civic skills**
- Explain how a constitutional principle limits or authorizes government action.
- Compare Federalist and Anti-Federalist arguments without treating either side as a cartoon.
- Use constitutional text as evidence in a claim.

**Assessment idea**
- Founding debate brief: identify a constitutional problem, two competing solutions, and one lasting tradeoff.

## Chapter 2: Federalism and "Who Can Fix This?"
**Key concepts**: enumerated powers, reserved powers, concurrent powers, supremacy clause, commerce clause, grants, mandates, preemption, state policy variation, local government.

**Practical questions**
- Which level of government has authority over elections, schools, policing, health, environment, immigration, taxes, and infrastructure?
- When does national uniformity matter, and when does local experimentation help?
- How do states resist, implement, or expand federal policy?

**Civic skills**
- Assign a real public problem to the correct federal, state, local, court, agency, or private venue.
- Trace how funding can shape state and local choices.

**Assessment idea**
- "Who can fix this?" memo for a local or national issue, naming authority, process, and likely bottlenecks.

## Chapter 3: Congress and Lawmaking
**Key concepts**: bicameralism, House, Senate, representation, committees, leadership, rules, filibuster, cloture, impeachment, oversight, budget, authorization, appropriation, gerrymandering.

**Practical questions**
- Why can a popular bill fail?
- How do committees, party leadership, district incentives, and Senate rules shape outcomes?
- What is oversight, and when does it become theater?

**Civic skills**
- Track a bill through introduction, committee, floor action, conference or amendment, presidential action, and implementation.
- Read a roll-call vote, committee page, or bill summary without losing the plot.

**Assessment idea**
- Committee hearing simulation with testimony, amendment strategy, and media summary.

## Chapter 4: Presidency, Bureaucracy, and Executive Power
**Key concepts**: commander in chief, veto, executive order, appointments, cabinet, independent agencies, administrative discretion, rulemaking, enforcement priorities, emergency power, bureaucracy.

**Practical questions**
- What can a president do alone, and what requires Congress, courts, agencies, states, or funding?
- Why do executive orders matter but remain vulnerable?
- How does bureaucracy turn political promises into permits, forms, rules, benefits, and enforcement?

**Civic skills**
- Distinguish a campaign promise, executive action, agency rule, and statute.
- Read a Federal Register or agency explainer as a civic document.

**Assessment idea**
- Executive action analysis: authority claimed, likely supporters/opponents, implementation obstacles, and court risk.

## Chapter 5: Courts, Civil Liberties, and Civil Rights
**Key concepts**: judicial review, precedent, constitutional interpretation, Bill of Rights, selective incorporation, due process, equal protection, free speech, religion clauses, privacy, rights of the accused, civil rights, voting rights.

**Core cases and case types**
- Marbury v. Madison
- Brown v. Board of Education
- Gideon v. Wainwright
- Tinker v. Des Moines
- Engel v. Vitale
- Citizens United v. FEC
- voting rights and equal protection disputes

**Civic skills**
- Separate civil liberties from civil rights.
- Apply a constitutional test or precedent to a new fact pattern.
- Explain what a court did, what it did not do, and what political actors may do next.

**Assessment idea**
- Supreme Court case brief with facts, question, holding, reasoning, dissent or counterargument, and civic consequence.

## Chapter 6: Political Ideologies, Public Opinion, and Media
**Key concepts**: liberalism, conservatism, libertarianism, populism, political socialization, party identification, polling, margin of error, sample, question wording, media framing, misinformation, public agenda.

**Practical questions**
- How do people form political beliefs?
- How should citizens read polls, charts, campaign ads, and political headlines?
- What is the difference between a value, an empirical claim, and a policy tool?

**Civic skills**
- Evaluate a poll using sample, wording, date, margin of error, and trend.
- Rewrite a misleading political headline into a more accurate one.
- Identify evidence, emotion, omission, and persuasion in campaign messaging.

**Assessment idea**
- Political media log comparing one issue across three sources and one primary document.

## Chapter 7: Elections, Parties, Interest Groups, and Money
**Key concepts**: voter registration, turnout, primaries, caucuses, Electoral College, congressional elections, political parties, third parties, interest groups, lobbying, PACs, Super PACs, campaign finance, mobilization.

**Practical questions**
- Why do parties matter even when voters dislike parties?
- How does money shape campaigns, access, and attention?
- Why do different elections reward different strategies?

**Civic skills**
- Explain the difference between vote share, seat share, turnout, and governing power.
- Follow campaign finance or lobbying influence without assuming every influence is identical to bribery.
- Build a voter guide for a real ballot using nonpartisan sources.

**Assessment idea**
- Election strategy memo: district/state context, electorate, institutions, media, money, and likely turnout problems.

## Chapter 8: Public Policy and Civic Action Lab
**Key concepts**: agenda setting, policy formulation, adoption, implementation, evaluation, fiscal policy, monetary policy, social policy, foreign policy, public comment, petitions, representative contact, deliberation.

**Practical questions**
- How does a concern become a policy proposal?
- Which actor can act now, which actor must wait, and which actor is being blamed unfairly?
- What civic action is lawful, specific, evidence-based, and timed to the decision?

**Civic skills**
- Write a concise message to a representative, agency, school board, city council, or public body.
- Make a public comment or testimony outline with a clear ask and supporting evidence.
- Reflect on civic responsibility, accuracy, privacy, and disagreement.

**Assessment idea**
- Civic action portfolio: issue brief, authority map, evidence packet, public-meeting observation or stakeholder interview, action plan, and reflection.

## Course-Wide Assessments
- Constitutional principle checks
- Primary-source document analysis
- Supreme Court case briefs
- Data and polling interpretation
- Bill or policy tracing project
- Election and media literacy tasks
- Civic action portfolio

## Capstone
Students complete a civic action portfolio on a real public issue, including a constitutional or institutional authority map, evidence packet, stakeholder analysis, media check, practical action step, and reflection on responsible participation.

## Assessment Ideas
Use constitutional principle checks, primary-source analysis, Supreme Court briefs, polling and data interpretation, bill tracing, media literacy tasks, simulations, and the civic action portfolio. Assess civic accuracy, evidence use, institutional reasoning, and nonpartisan clarity.

## Final Outcomes
Students should leave able to explain the U.S. system with useful precision: what the Constitution says, what each branch can do, how federalism complicates responsibility, how rights claims are argued, how elections and interest groups shape incentives, how to read political news, and how to participate without confusing outrage for strategy.
