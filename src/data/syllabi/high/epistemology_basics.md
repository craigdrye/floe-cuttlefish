# Epistemology Basics
**ID**: `epistemologyBasics` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 10 (ages 14–15)

## Course Philosophy
This course introduces the philosophical study of knowledge. It asks the fundamental question: "How do we know what we know?" Students will explore the limits of human perception, the definition of truth, and the problem of skepticism.

## Module 1: The Definition of Knowledge (20%)
- JTB Theory: Knowledge as Justified True Belief (Plato)
- The Gettier Problem: When JTB isn't enough (having the right answer for the wrong reason)
- Types of knowledge: Propositional (knowing *that*), Procedural (knowing *how*), Acquaintance (knowing *who/what*)
- **Questions**: Applying JTB theory to scenarios, identifying Gettier problem situations

## Module 2: Rationalism vs Empiricism (20%)
- Rationalism (Descartes, Spinoza): Knowledge comes primarily from reason and innate ideas
- Empiricism (Locke, Hume): Knowledge comes primarily from sensory experience (Tabula Rasa / Blank Slate)
- A priori knowledge (independent of experience) vs A posteriori knowledge (dependent on experience)
- **Questions**: Distinguishing rationalist vs empiricist arguments, identifying a priori vs a posteriori statements

## Module 3: Radical Skepticism (20%)
- Descartes' Method of Doubt and the "Evil Demon" thought experiment
- "Cogito, ergo sum" (I think, therefore I am)
- The Brain in a Vat / The Matrix simulation hypothesis
- Solipsism: The idea that only one's own mind is sure to exist
- **Questions**: Analyzing the Cartesian method of doubt, evaluating the Brain in a Vat hypothesis

## Module 4: The Problem of Induction (20%)
- David Hume and the problem of induction: Just because the sun rose yesterday, how do we *know* it will rise tomorrow?
- The Uniformity of Nature assumption
- Karl Popper and Falsification: Science doesn't "prove" things true; it proves things false
- **Questions**: Explaining the problem of induction, applying Popper's concept of falsifiability to scientific claims

## Module 5: Truth and Relativism (20%)
- Correspondence Theory of Truth: Truth corresponds to reality
- Coherence Theory of Truth: Truth is what fits consistently within a web of beliefs
- Pragmatic Theory of Truth: Truth is what "works" or has practical utility
- Postmodernism and the rejection of objective/absolute truth
- **Questions**: Comparing theories of truth, evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of relativism

## Stretch Zone
- Epistemic injustice (Miranda Fricker): When someone's capacity as a knower is unfairly diminished (testimonial vs hermeneutical injustice)
- Standpoint epistemology: How one's social position affects what they can know
