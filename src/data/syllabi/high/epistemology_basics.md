# Epistemology Basics
**ID**: `epistemologyBasics` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: High School

## Course Aim
Introduce students to the philosophy of knowledge: what it means to know, how belief can be justified, why skepticism is tempting, how evidence works, and how responsible thinkers handle uncertainty. The course connects classic epistemology to science, media, expertise, misinformation, disagreement, and everyday judgment.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus covered the essential landmarks: justified true belief, Gettier, rationalism, empiricism, skepticism, induction, and truth theories. This rewrite keeps that spine while adding testimony, expertise, evidence standards, epistemic injustice, intellectual virtues, and practical "how should I believe?" skills.

## Chapter 1: What Is Knowledge?
**Key concepts**: belief, truth, justification, knowledge-that, knowledge-how, acquaintance knowledge, cognitive success, epistemic value, luck.

**Essential questions**
- Is knowledge more than true belief?
- Why do we value knowing rather than merely guessing correctly?
- What kinds of things can people know?

**Skills**
- Distinguish belief, truth, justification, understanding, and certainty.
- Apply knowledge categories to ordinary and academic examples.
- Explain why luck creates problems for knowledge claims.

**Assessment idea**
- Knowledge sorting lab: classify examples as belief, true belief, justified true belief, knowledge-how, acquaintance knowledge, or not knowledge.

## Chapter 2: Justified True Belief and the Gettier Shock
**Key concepts**: justified true belief, necessary condition, sufficient condition, Gettier case, epistemic luck, defeater, reliability, safety, sensitivity.

**Essential questions**
- What does the traditional JTB account get right?
- Why did Gettier-style cases change the conversation?
- Can knowledge survive luck, or must justification connect to truth in the right way?

**Skills**
- Build and analyze simple Gettier cases.
- Identify the difference between good evidence and accidentally true belief.
- Compare anti-luck responses such as defeasibility, reliabilism, safety, and sensitivity at an introductory level.

**Assessment idea**
- Gettier workshop: students write their own lucky-true-belief case and propose a repair to the definition of knowledge.

## Chapter 3: Sources of Knowledge
**Key concepts**: perception, memory, introspection, reason, testimony, expertise, inference, a priori, a posteriori, rationalism, empiricism.

**Essential questions**
- What can perception tell us, and when does it mislead?
- How much of what we know depends on trusting other people?
- Are math and logic known differently from history or science?

**Skills**
- Distinguish a priori from a posteriori claims.
- Evaluate testimony by expertise, track record, incentives, independence, and domain fit.
- Compare rationalist and empiricist explanations of knowledge.

**Assessment idea**
- Source reliability brief: evaluate whether a claim should be accepted, doubted, investigated, or rejected based on source and evidence type.

## Chapter 4: Skepticism, Doubt, and Certainty
**Key concepts**: Cartesian skepticism, method of doubt, dream argument, evil demon, brain in a vat, solipsism, external world, fallibilism, contextualism.

**Essential questions**
- What can survive radical doubt?
- Does the possibility of error destroy knowledge?
- Should knowledge require certainty?

**Skills**
- Reconstruct a skeptical argument in standard form.
- Distinguish practical doubt from philosophical doubt.
- Explain fallibilism: the idea that knowledge may be possible without absolute certainty.

**Assessment idea**
- Skepticism dialogue: one student argues the skeptical challenge, another gives the best reply, and both identify what remains unresolved.

## Chapter 5: Induction, Science, and Evidence
**Key concepts**: induction, uniformity of nature, prediction, confirmation, falsification, hypothesis, explanation, model, correlation, causation, underdetermination.

**Essential questions**
- Why does past evidence support future expectations, if it does?
- Can science prove theories true?
- What makes evidence strong enough for action?

**Skills**
- Explain Hume's problem of induction in plain language.
- Compare confirmation and falsification.
- Evaluate scientific and statistical claims without demanding impossible certainty.

**Assessment idea**
- Evidence threshold memo: decide whether a school, government, doctor, or scientist has enough evidence to act under uncertainty.

## Chapter 6: Truth, Relativism, and Disagreement
**Key concepts**: correspondence theory, coherence theory, pragmatic theory, relativism, objectivity, subjectivity, peer disagreement, polarization, intellectual humility.

**Essential questions**
- Is truth what corresponds to reality, what coheres with a system, or what works?
- When does disagreement show that someone is wrong, and when does it show that the issue is hard?
- What is wrong with "that's just my truth" when the claim affects other people?

**Skills**
- Separate taste, perspective, interpretation, and factual claim.
- Respond to disagreement with clarification, evidence, revision, or suspension of judgment.
- Identify when relativism is being used carefully versus lazily.

**Assessment idea**
- Disagreement map: students analyze a contested claim by separating facts, values, definitions, evidence, and uncertainty.

## Chapter 7: Social Epistemology and Epistemic Justice
**Key concepts**: testimony, trust, expert disagreement, echo chamber, filter bubble, misinformation, disinformation, testimonial injustice, hermeneutical injustice, standpoint epistemology.

**Essential questions**
- How do communities help or harm knowledge?
- Who gets treated as credible, and who gets ignored?
- How should citizens navigate experts, algorithms, social media, and institutional trust?

**Skills**
- Evaluate expert disagreement without pretending all opinions are equal.
- Recognize credibility deficits and interpretive gaps.
- Build healthier information habits.

**Assessment idea**
- Information ecosystem audit: trace one claim through social media, news, expert sources, official data, and personal assumptions.

## Chapter 8: Capstone: Responsible Belief Portfolio
Students choose one claim they care about and produce:
- a precise knowledge question
- source and evidence map
- skeptical challenge
- strongest support and strongest objection
- uncertainty statement
- revised conclusion with confidence level
- reflection on intellectual virtues used or needed

## Capstone
Students complete a Responsible Belief Portfolio on one claim they care about, including source mapping, skeptical challenge, strongest support, strongest objection, uncertainty statement, revised confidence level, and reflection on intellectual virtues.

## Course-Wide Assessments
- Knowledge concept checks
- Gettier case workshop
- Skepticism dialogue
- Source reliability brief
- Evidence threshold memo
- Disagreement map
- Responsible Belief Portfolio

## Assessment Ideas
Use knowledge concept checks, Gettier workshops, skepticism dialogues, source reliability briefs, evidence threshold memos, disagreement maps, information ecosystem audits, and the Responsible Belief Portfolio. Assess careful uncertainty, source evaluation, and fair treatment of disagreement.

## Final Outcomes
Students should leave able to ask what would make a belief knowledge, evaluate evidence and testimony, explain skepticism without getting trapped in it, reason about science and induction, handle disagreement carefully, and take responsibility for what they believe and share.
