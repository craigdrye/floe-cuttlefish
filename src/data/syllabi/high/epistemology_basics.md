# Epistemology Basics
*Truth, belief, skepticism, evidence, and the question of how anyone actually knows anything.*

**ID**: `epistemologyBasics` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
Epistemology is the branch of philosophy that asks what knowledge is, where it comes from, and how a belief earns the right to be called knowledge rather than a lucky guess. This course introduces high-school students to the central problems of the theory of knowledge: the difference between belief, truth, and justification; the famous Gettier challenge to the "justified true belief" definition; the rival pictures of where knowledge comes from (reason versus experience); and the skeptical arguments that ask whether we can be certain of anything at all.

The course deliberately connects classic epistemology to the information world students actually live in. The same tools that let you analyze Descartes' evil-demon argument also let you reason about scientific evidence, statistical claims, expert disagreement, testimony, misinformation, and the trustworthiness of sources online. Students learn to treat "How do I know?" not as a trick question but as a practical, repeatable habit of careful thinking.

By the end, students should be able to ask what would turn one of their beliefs into knowledge, evaluate evidence and testimony fairly, reconstruct a skeptical argument without getting trapped by it, reason about science and induction under uncertainty, handle disagreement with intellectual humility, and take responsibility for what they believe and share.

## Course Design Notes
The spine of the course follows the standard landmarks of an introductory theory-of-knowledge unit: justified true belief, Gettier, sources of knowledge, skepticism, induction and evidence, and theories of truth. Around that spine the course adds the contemporary material that makes epistemology feel urgent for teenagers: testimony and expertise, social epistemology, epistemic injustice, intellectual virtues, and the practical skill of deciding "how confident should I be, and what should I do about it?"

Each chapter is built around concrete cases rather than abstract definitions alone. Gettier cases, the dream argument, the brain-in-a-vat scenario, Hume's "will the sun rise tomorrow?", and real-world misinformation examples give students something to argue about. Assessment favors short structured tasks (sort these examples, repair this definition, reconstruct this argument, separate fact from value) over memorization. The capstone asks students to apply the whole toolkit to one claim they personally care about.

## Chapter 1: What Is Knowledge?
**Core questions**
- Is knowledge anything more than a true belief?
- Why do we value knowing something over merely guessing it correctly?
- What different kinds of knowing are there?

**Key concepts**: belief, truth, justification, propositional knowledge (knowledge-that), procedural knowledge (knowledge-how), acquaintance knowledge, epistemic value, the role of luck.

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish belief, truth, justification, understanding, and certainty.
- Classify ordinary and academic examples by the kind of knowing involved.
- Explain why a correct guess does not normally count as knowledge.

**Common traps**
- Treating "I'm sure" as the same thing as "I know."
- Assuming any true belief automatically counts as knowledge.
- Confusing knowing-how (riding a bike) with knowing-that (a fact).

## Chapter 2: Justified True Belief and the Gettier Shock
**Core questions**
- What does the traditional "justified true belief" (JTB) account get right?
- Why did Gettier-style cases change the whole conversation in 1963?
- Can a belief be justified and true yet still fail to be knowledge?

**Key concepts**: justified true belief, necessary versus sufficient conditions, Gettier case, epistemic luck, the search for a fourth condition, no-false-lemmas, defeasibility, reliabilism, safety, sensitivity.

**Applied skills**
- Build and analyze a simple Gettier case.
- Tell the difference between good evidence and an accidentally true belief.
- Compare introductory anti-luck repairs (no false lemmas, defeasibility, reliabilism, safety).

**Common traps**
- Thinking Gettier shows that justification is pointless (it shows JTB is not *enough*).
- Reading Gettier cases as denying that truth is objective.
- Believing one tidy fourth condition has settled the problem for good.

## Chapter 3: Sources of Knowledge
**Core questions**
- What can perception tell us, and when does it mislead?
- How much of what we "know" actually rests on trusting other people?
- Are mathematics and logic known in a different way from history or science?

**Key concepts**: perception, memory, introspection, reason, testimony, expertise, inference, a priori, a posteriori, rationalism, empiricism, the Kantian synthesis.

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish a priori claims (knowable independent of experience) from a posteriori claims (knowable through experience).
- Evaluate testimony using expertise, track record, incentives, independence, and domain fit.
- Compare how rationalists and empiricists explain where knowledge comes from.

**Common traps**
- Assuming perception is a neutral, error-free recording of reality.
- Treating "a priori" as simply meaning "obvious" or "before you learn it."
- Forgetting how much everyday knowledge depends entirely on testimony.

## Chapter 4: Skepticism, Doubt, and Certainty
**Core questions**
- What, if anything, can survive radical doubt?
- Does the mere possibility of error destroy knowledge?
- Should knowledge require absolute certainty at all?

**Key concepts**: Cartesian skepticism, method of doubt, the dream argument, the evil-demon argument, "cogito ergo sum," brain-in-a-vat, the external-world problem, fallibilism, contextualism.

**Applied skills**
- Reconstruct a skeptical argument in clear premise-and-conclusion form.
- Distinguish ordinary practical doubt from philosophical skeptical doubt.
- Explain fallibilism: that knowledge can be real without being absolutely certain.

**Common traps**
- Concluding "we can never know anything" from the bare possibility of error.
- Treating Descartes' doubt as genuine disbelief rather than a deliberate method.
- Demanding 100% certainty before allowing the word "know."

## Chapter 5: Induction, Science, and Evidence
**Core questions**
- Why should past evidence support expectations about the future, if it does at all?
- Can science ever prove a theory true?
- How much evidence is enough to justify acting?

**Key concepts**: induction, the uniformity of nature, the problem of induction (Hume), prediction, confirmation, falsification (Popper), hypothesis, correlation versus causation, underdetermination.

**Applied skills**
- State Hume's problem of induction in plain language.
- Contrast confirmation with Popper's falsification.
- Judge scientific and statistical claims without demanding impossible certainty.

**Common traps**
- Saying a theory is "proven" when it has only been strongly supported.
- Reading correlation as automatic causation.
- Treating any single counterexample as instantly fatal, or none as ever relevant.

## Chapter 6: Truth, Relativism, and Disagreement
**Core questions**
- Is truth what matches reality, what fits a system, or what works in practice?
- When does disagreement show that someone is wrong, and when does it show the question is genuinely hard?
- What is wrong with "that's just my truth" when the claim affects other people?

**Key concepts**: correspondence theory, coherence theory, pragmatic theory, relativism, objectivity, subjectivity, peer disagreement, polarization, intellectual humility.

**Applied skills**
- Separate matters of taste, perspective, interpretation, and factual claim.
- Respond to disagreement by clarifying, weighing evidence, revising, or suspending judgment.
- Spot the difference between careful and lazy uses of "it's all relative."

**Common traps**
- Sliding from "people disagree" to "there is no fact of the matter."
- Confusing a subjective preference with a factual claim.
- Using relativism selectively to dodge inconvenient evidence.

## Chapter 7: Social Epistemology and Epistemic Justice
**Core questions**
- How do communities and institutions help or harm knowledge?
- Who gets treated as a credible knower, and who gets ignored?
- How should citizens navigate experts, algorithms, and social media?

**Key concepts**: testimony, trust, expert disagreement, echo chamber, filter bubble, misinformation, disinformation, testimonial injustice (Fricker), hermeneutical injustice, standpoint epistemology.

**Applied skills**
- Weigh expert disagreement without pretending every opinion is equal.
- Recognize credibility deficits and gaps in shared interpretive resources.
- Build healthier, more deliberate information habits.

**Common traps**
- Treating "do your own research" as a substitute for relevant expertise.
- Assuming a confident, popular source is automatically a reliable one.
- Dismissing a speaker because of identity prejudice rather than evidence.

## Chapter 8: Intellectual Virtues and Responsible Belief
**Core questions**
- What habits of mind make someone a good thinker rather than just a clever one?
- How should I decide how confident to be, and what to do about that confidence?
- When is it responsible to suspend judgment rather than pick a side?

**Key concepts**: intellectual virtues (open-mindedness, intellectual humility, curiosity, intellectual courage), intellectual vices (dogmatism, gullibility, closed-mindedness), calibration, proportioning belief to evidence, suspension of judgment.

**Applied skills**
- Match a belief's confidence level to the strength of its evidence.
- Identify which intellectual virtue a situation calls for.
- Decide responsibly when to believe, doubt, investigate, or suspend judgment.

**Common traps**
- Mistaking stubbornness for principled conviction.
- Treating open-mindedness as believing everything equally.
- Acting on a claim with far more confidence than the evidence supports.

## Capstone: Responsible Belief Portfolio
Students choose one claim they genuinely care about and build a portfolio that applies the full course toolkit:
- a precise knowledge question ("What would it take to *know* that ___?")
- a source-and-evidence map distinguishing testimony, observation, and inference
- a reconstructed skeptical challenge to the claim
- the strongest support and the strongest objection, fairly stated
- an explicit uncertainty statement
- a revised conclusion with a stated confidence level
- a reflection on which intellectual virtues the inquiry used or still needs

The portfolio is assessed on careful uncertainty, fair source evaluation, honest treatment of disagreement, and clear reasoning rather than on reaching a particular conclusion.
