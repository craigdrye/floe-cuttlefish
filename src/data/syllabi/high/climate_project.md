# Climate Project
Read the climate news, weigh the tradeoffs, and design a grounded local action project.

**ID**: `climateProject` · **Discipline**: Science/Humanities

## Course Aim
This course gives students enough climate literacy to read the news critically, evaluate competing claims, understand the tradeoffs behind every climate decision, and design a grounded local action project. It is deliberately interdisciplinary: students move between Earth science, data literacy, economics, policy, ethics, and communication, because real climate decisions never stay inside one subject.

The spine of the course is reasoning, not memorisation. Students learn to separate weather anecdotes from climate evidence, forcing from feedback, mitigation from adaptation, and stocks from flows. They practise reading graphs and risk maps the way scientists and policymakers do: asking what changed, over what timescale, compared with what counterfactual, and with how much confidence.

The course is built around a capstone project. Every chapter feeds a piece of the final climate action plan, so students leave able to name a real local problem, marshal evidence, compare options honestly, and propose an action with measurable goals and stated limits.

## Course Design Notes
This is a project-centred course, not a miniature climate science degree. Route questions here when they involve climate evidence, greenhouse gases, energy systems, impacts, mitigation, adaptation, policy tools, climate justice, or the communication of risk and uncertainty. Keep the level appropriate to ages 14-15 (roughly Year 10 / NGSS high-school Earth and space science): concrete mechanisms, real data sources, and clean reasoning rather than university-level modelling. Where a fact has a number (a carbon budget, a global warming potential), prefer the current IPCC AR6 figures and frame them as estimates with uncertainty.

## Chapter 1: Climate System and Evidence
- **Core questions**: How do we distinguish a weather claim from a climate claim? What makes multiple independent lines of evidence stronger than any single record?
- **Key concepts**: Weather versus climate, the greenhouse effect, Earth's energy balance, paleoclimate proxies (ice cores, tree rings), the instrumental record, satellite observations, and how scientists express confidence.
- **Applied skills**: Interpret a temperature or sea-level graph, identify a trend versus year-to-year variability, and build an evidence explainer that combines at least three kinds of data.
- **Common traps**: Treating a single cold winter as evidence against warming; confusing the natural greenhouse effect with the enhanced (human) one; reading short noisy stretches of a graph as if they overturn a long trend.

## Chapter 2: Carbon Cycle and Human Drivers
- **Core questions**: How does carbon move between reservoirs, and why do cumulative emissions matter more than any single year's emissions?
- **Key concepts**: Carbon reservoirs (atmosphere, ocean, land, fossil deposits), fossil fuel combustion, land-use change, sinks, radiative forcing, feedbacks, and the difference between a stock and a flow.
- **Applied skills**: Trace carbon through a system, compare emission sources, and draw a carbon-flow diagram for a household, school, city, or sector.
- **Common traps**: Confusing emissions (a flow) with atmospheric concentration (a stock); assuming cutting emissions to a lower level cools the planet, when it only slows the rate of accumulation; ignoring feedbacks that amplify an initial forcing.

## Chapter 3: Impacts, Risk, and Vulnerability
- **Core questions**: Why does the same climate event harm two communities very differently? How do hazard, exposure, and vulnerability combine into risk?
- **Key concepts**: Heat, flooding, drought, sea-level rise, food and water systems, health, infrastructure, and the risk triad of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability (plus adaptive capacity).
- **Applied skills**: Read a risk map, separate hazard from exposure and vulnerability, and build a local or regional risk profile with evidence, uncertainties, and affected stakeholders.
- **Common traps**: Treating a hazard as identical to a risk; assuming impacts are shared equally; ignoring adaptive capacity and the role of wealth, infrastructure, and prior planning.

## Chapter 4: Energy Systems and Mitigation
- **Core questions**: Where do emissions actually come from, and which mitigation options fit a given constraint of cost, speed, or feasibility?
- **Key concepts**: Electricity generation, transport, buildings, industry, efficiency, electrification, renewables, energy storage, demand reduction, and lifecycle tradeoffs.
- **Applied skills**: Read an energy-mix chart, compare mitigation options, and assemble a mitigation portfolio balancing impact, feasibility, cost, and equity.
- **Common traps**: Assuming one silver-bullet technology solves everything; ignoring intermittency and storage for renewables; comparing only operating emissions while ignoring lifecycle and manufacturing emissions.

## Chapter 5: Policy, Economics, and Public Decisions
- **Core questions**: Why is climate change called a market failure, and how do policy tools change the incentives that drive emissions?
- **Key concepts**: Externalities, the social cost of carbon, carbon pricing (carbon tax and cap-and-trade), regulation, subsidies, public goods, collective action, and international agreements.
- **Applied skills**: Match policy tools to problems, identify winners and losers, and write a policy memo recommending one intervention with tradeoffs stated explicitly.
- **Common traps**: Confusing a carbon tax (sets a price, lets quantity float) with cap-and-trade (sets a quantity, lets price float); ignoring who bears the cost of a policy; assuming a voluntary appeal can fix a collective-action problem.

## Chapter 6: Adaptation and Resilience
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between cutting emissions and preparing for impacts, and where are the limits to adaptation?
- **Key concepts**: Adaptation versus mitigation, resilience, managed retreat, heat-action planning, flood defence, emergency preparedness, green infrastructure, and hard limits to adaptation.
- **Applied skills**: Sort mitigation from adaptation, compare short-term and long-term resilience, and critique a resilience plan for missing stakeholders or false confidence.
- **Common traps**: Labelling every climate response "mitigation"; assuming adaptation removes the need to cut emissions; treating a sea wall or AC as a permanent fix rather than a buffer with limits.

## Chapter 7: Climate Communication and Misinformation
- **Core questions**: How do you communicate genuine uncertainty without weakening the evidence, and how do you spot a misleading climate claim?
- **Key concepts**: Scientific consensus, uncertainty, risk communication, misleading graphs (truncated axes, cherry-picked start years), false balance, values, identity, and audience-aware messaging.
- **Applied skills**: Spot a misleading chart or headline, revise a message for a different audience, and explain consensus and uncertainty accurately in plain language.
- **Common traps**: Treating uncertainty as ignorance ("scientists don't know, so we can ignore it"); giving fringe and mainstream views equal airtime (false balance); cherry-picking a start year to hide a trend.

## Chapter 8: Climate Action Studio
- **Core questions**: How do you turn a vague concern into a scoped, measurable project that survives contact with real stakeholders?
- **Key concepts**: Project scoping, stakeholder mapping, measurable goals (SMART-style), feasibility, implementation plans, feedback loops, and impact evaluation.
- **Applied skills**: Narrow an overlarge project, choose metrics, and revise an action plan after stakeholder feedback.
- **Common traps**: Generic "awareness" goals with no measurable behaviour, policy, or systems change; metrics that count activity (posters made) instead of impact; skipping the stakeholders who would actually have to act.

## Capstone
Students produce a local climate project that names a specific problem, uses evidence, compares options honestly, and proposes an action with measurable goals and stated limits. Strong capstones avoid generic awareness campaigns unless they include a measurable behaviour, policy, or systems goal, and they are defensible to an authentic audience: a council, a school board, a community group, or a panel of peers.

## Assessment Ideas
- Evidence checks using NASA, NOAA, IPCC, or local data sources.
- Policy and tradeoff memos with explicit stakeholder analysis.
- Communication critiques of headlines, charts, and public claims.
- A final climate action defence with impact metrics and stated limitations.
