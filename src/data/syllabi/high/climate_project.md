# Climate Project
**ID**: `climateProject` · **Discipline**: Science/Humanities · **Year**: 10 (ages 14-15)

## Course Aim
Give students enough climate literacy to read the news, evaluate claims, understand tradeoffs, and design a grounded local action project. The course blends Earth science, data literacy, policy, economics, communication, and practical decision-making.

## Course Design Notes
This is a project-centered course, not a miniature climate science degree. Route questions here when they involve climate evidence, greenhouse gases, energy systems, impacts, mitigation, adaptation, policy tools, climate justice, or communication of risk and uncertainty.

## Chapter 1: Climate System and Evidence
- **Key concepts**: Weather versus climate, greenhouse effect, energy balance, paleoclimate evidence, instrumental records, satellite observations, and scientific confidence.
- **Practice questions**: Distinguish weather claims from climate claims; interpret temperature or sea-level graphs; explain what makes multiple evidence lines stronger than one.
- **Student output**: A climate evidence explainer using at least three kinds of data.

## Chapter 2: Carbon Cycle and Human Drivers
- **Key concepts**: Carbon reservoirs, fossil fuels, land use, feedbacks, radiative forcing, emissions, sinks, and the difference between flow and stock.
- **Practice questions**: Trace carbon through a system; compare emissions sources; explain why cumulative emissions matter.
- **Student output**: A carbon-flow diagram for a school, household, city, or sector.

## Chapter 3: Impacts, Risk, and Vulnerability
- **Key concepts**: Heat, flooding, drought, sea-level rise, food and water systems, health, infrastructure, vulnerability, exposure, adaptive capacity, and unequal impacts.
- **Practice questions**: Read a risk map; compare hazard, exposure, and vulnerability; explain why the same climate event affects communities differently.
- **Student output**: A local or regional risk profile with evidence, uncertainties, and affected stakeholders.

## Chapter 4: Energy Systems and Mitigation
- **Key concepts**: Electricity generation, transport, buildings, industry, efficiency, electrification, renewables, storage, demand reduction, and lifecycle tradeoffs.
- **Practice questions**: Compare mitigation options; read an energy mix chart; evaluate which intervention fits a constraint.
- **Student output**: A mitigation portfolio that balances impact, feasibility, cost, and equity.

## Chapter 5: Policy, Economics, and Public Decisions
- **Key concepts**: Externalities, carbon pricing, regulation, subsidies, public goods, collective action, lobbying, international agreements, and policy evaluation.
- **Practice questions**: Match policy tools to problems; analyze winners and losers; identify incentives in a climate decision.
- **Student output**: A policy memo recommending one climate intervention with tradeoffs clearly stated.

## Chapter 6: Adaptation and Resilience
- **Key concepts**: Adaptation, resilience, managed retreat, heat planning, flood defense, emergency preparedness, green infrastructure, and limits to adaptation.
- **Practice questions**: Sort mitigation from adaptation; compare short-term and long-term resilience; critique a resilience plan for missing stakeholders.
- **Student output**: A school or community resilience checklist tied to a specific climate risk.

## Chapter 7: Climate Communication and Misinformation
- **Key concepts**: Uncertainty, scientific consensus, risk communication, misleading graphs, cherry-picking, false balance, values, identity, and audience-aware messaging.
- **Practice questions**: Spot misleading climate claims; revise a message for a different audience; explain uncertainty without weakening the evidence.
- **Student output**: A public-facing climate communication piece with annotations explaining the choices.

## Chapter 8: Climate Action Studio
- **Key concepts**: Project scoping, stakeholder interviews, measurable goals, feasibility, implementation plans, feedback loops, and impact evaluation.
- **Practice questions**: Narrow an overlarge project; choose metrics; revise an action plan after stakeholder feedback.
- **Student output**: A complete climate action plan, prototype, campaign, or briefing ready for an authentic audience.

## Capstone
Students produce a local climate project that names a problem, uses evidence, compares options, and proposes an action. Good capstones avoid generic awareness campaigns unless they include a measurable behavior, policy, or systems goal.

## Assessment Ideas
- Evidence checks using NASA, NOAA, IPCC, or local data sources.
- Policy and tradeoff memos with explicit stakeholder analysis.
- Communication critiques of headlines, charts, and public claims.
- A final climate action defense with impact metrics and limitations.
