# AP Biology
**Covers**: `col-ap-biology` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: 12 (ages 16-18)

## Course Aim
Prepare students for AP Biology by connecting evolution, cellular processes, energy, communication, genetic information, ecology, inquiry labs, statistics, and evidence-based scientific argument.

## Course Design Notes
The current AP Biology framework emphasizes inquiry-based investigations and science practices: concept explanation, visual representations, questions and methods, data representation, statistical tests, and argumentation. This syllabus keeps the content units visible while making data and experimental reasoning constant.

## Chapter 1: Biology Practices and Chemistry of Life
- **Key concepts**: experimental design, claims and evidence, water, pH, macromolecules, structure-function relationships.
- **Practice questions**: What question is testable? What molecular structure explains the function? What evidence supports the claim?
- **Lab output**: a short investigation plan with variables, controls, predicted data, and limitations.

## Chapter 2: Cell Structure, Membranes, and Transport
- **Key concepts**: organelles, surface area-to-volume ratio, membrane structure, diffusion, osmosis, active transport, water potential.
- **Practice questions**: How does structure support function? Which way will water move? What limits cell size?
- **Lab output**: a transport model or osmosis analysis with graph and explanation.

## Chapter 3: Cellular Energetics
- **Key concepts**: enzymes, activation energy, photosynthesis, cellular respiration, chemiosmosis, ATP, feedback regulation.
- **Practice questions**: How does an enzyme change reaction rate? What evidence shows energy transfer? What disrupts ATP production?
- **Lab output**: an enzyme or photosynthesis data analysis with rate calculation.

## Chapter 4: Cell Communication and Cell Cycle
- **Key concepts**: signal transduction, receptors, second messengers, feedback, mitosis, checkpoints, cancer, apoptosis.
- **Practice questions**: Where can a pathway be interrupted? What response follows the signal? What happens if a checkpoint fails?
- **Lab output**: a pathway diagram explaining reception, transduction, response, and regulation.

## Chapter 5: Heredity, Gene Expression, and Biotechnology
- **Key concepts**: meiosis, inheritance, recombination, DNA replication, transcription, translation, gene regulation, PCR, gel electrophoresis, CRISPR.
- **Practice questions**: What inheritance pattern fits the data? How does a mutation change expression? What does the gel show?
- **Lab output**: a genetics problem set with chi-square or recombination reasoning.

## Chapter 6: Evolution and Phylogeny
- **Key concepts**: natural selection, Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, genetic drift, gene flow, speciation, evidence, cladograms.
- **Practice questions**: Which force changes allele frequencies? What evidence supports relatedness? What condition violates Hardy-Weinberg?
- **Lab output**: an evolution data task with allele-frequency calculations and argument.

## Chapter 7: Ecology and Systems Interactions
- **Key concepts**: population growth, carrying capacity, energy flow, nutrient cycles, community interactions, biodiversity, disruptions, climate effects.
- **Practice questions**: What limits population growth? How does energy move through trophic levels? What makes an ecosystem resilient?
- **Lab output**: an ecology model with graph, prediction, and evidence.

## Chapter 8: AP Biology Exam Studio
- **Key concepts**: visual analysis, experimental design, statistics, graphing, short answers, long free response, claim-evidence-reasoning.
- **Practice questions**: What does the figure show? Which statistical test fits? What evidence justifies the biological claim?
- **Lab output**: a timed FRQ set with scoring reflection and targeted revision.

## Capstone
Create an AP Biology investigation portfolio with one cell process analysis, one genetics/statistics problem, one evolution model, one ecology argument, and one timed FRQ correction log.

## Assessment Ideas
- Lab tasks graded on design, data handling, and biological explanation.
- FRQs graded on AP-style precision and evidence use.
- Capstone graded on cross-unit connections and statistical reasoning.
