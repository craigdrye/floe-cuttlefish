# AP Biology
A college-level introductory biology course built around the four Big Ideas, evolution, energetics, information storage and transmission, and systems interactions, with inquiry labs, data analysis, and evidence-based argument woven through every unit.

**ID**: `apBiology` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
This course prepares students for the AP Biology exam and for first-year college biology by connecting molecular and cellular detail to the large explanatory ideas that organize the discipline. Students learn to treat biology not as a list of facts but as a set of interacting systems, where structure predicts function, energy and matter are conserved as they flow and transform, information is stored and expressed through nucleic acids and signals, and populations and ecosystems change over time. The redesigned College Board framework (effective Fall 2025) anchors the year in four Big Ideas, Evolution (EVO), Energetics (ENE), Information Storage and Transmission (IST), and Systems Interactions (SYI), and this syllabus keeps those ideas visible across all eight content units.

Just as important as content is the science-practice strand the exam now emphasizes. Throughout the year students design controlled investigations, build and interpret visual models, choose and run statistical tests such as chi-square and the standard error of the mean, read graphs and data tables critically, and construct claim-evidence-reasoning arguments. Quantitative skills, including reading a chi-square or t-distribution table, applying the Hardy-Weinberg equations, and calculating reaction rates from data, are practiced as routine tools rather than isolated topics.

By the end of the course students should explain biological phenomena across scales, from a single enzyme to a whole ecosystem, predict how a system responds when a variable changes, and defend conclusions with appropriate evidence and statistics. They should be ready for the multiple-choice and free-response sections of the AP exam and confident moving among molecules, cells, organisms, populations, and the data that describe them.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve the chemistry of life, cell structure and transport, cellular energetics, cell communication and the cell cycle, heredity and gene expression and biotechnology, evolution and phylogeny, ecology and systems interactions, or AP-style experimental design and data analysis. Keep the work cumulative: structure-function reasoning introduced with water and macromolecules reappears in membranes, enzymes, and proteins; the conservation of energy and matter threads from photosynthesis and respiration through trophic energy flow; and the central dogma connects replication, mutation, gene regulation, and the molecular tools of biotechnology.

Keep practice forward and error-aware. Every wrong answer should reflect a genuine student misconception, confusing osmosis with active transport, predicting water moves toward lower water potential's opposite, reading a cladogram as a ladder of progress, or treating Hardy-Weinberg p and q as the genotype frequencies, with a specific reason it fails and a hint toward the right move. Favor data-grounded prompts that ask students to interpret a graph, predict an experimental result, or justify a claim, since that is how the exam assesses understanding. Mind the age-appropriate boundary: keep biochemical detail at the AP level (name the stages and net products of respiration without demanding every intermediate), use real statistical tests at the level the CED expects, and emphasize reasoning and modeling over rote memorization.

## Chapter 1: Chemistry of Life and Biology Practices
**Core questions:** How do the properties of water and the structure of macromolecules explain biological function, and what makes a biological question testable with controlled evidence?
**Key concepts:** polarity, hydrogen bonding, cohesion, adhesion, and the emergent properties of water; pH and buffers; carbon skeletons and functional groups; the four macromolecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids) and their monomers; dehydration synthesis and hydrolysis; structure-function relationships; testable questions, hypotheses, independent and dependent variables, controls.
**Applied skills:** relating water's polarity to its biological roles; identifying macromolecules and their monomers; predicting how a structural change alters function; designing an investigation with a clear control and identified variables; stating claims supported by evidence.
**Common traps:** treating all "organic" molecules as identical rather than distinguishing functional groups; confusing monomers with polymers; assuming a hypothesis is just a guess rather than a testable prediction; omitting a control or confounding more than one variable.

## Chapter 2: Cell Structure, Membranes, and Transport
**Core questions:** How does cellular structure support function, and how do membranes regulate what moves into and out of a cell?
**Key concepts:** prokaryotic versus eukaryotic cells; organelles and the endomembrane system; surface-area-to-volume ratio and limits on cell size; the fluid mosaic membrane model; passive transport, diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and osmosis; active transport and the role of ATP; water potential and tonicity (hypertonic, hypotonic, isotonic).
**Applied skills:** matching organelles to functions; explaining why cell size is limited by surface-area-to-volume ratio; predicting the direction of water movement from water potential or solute concentration; distinguishing passive from active transport; interpreting an osmosis or diffusion data set.
**Common traps:** confusing osmosis (water) with diffusion of solutes or with active transport; thinking water moves "to dilute the higher concentration" without using water potential correctly; assuming larger cells are always more efficient; forgetting that active transport requires energy.

## Chapter 3: Cellular Energetics
**Core questions:** How do enzymes control reaction rates, and how do photosynthesis and cellular respiration capture, transform, and release energy while conserving matter?
**Key concepts:** enzymes, active sites, substrate specificity, activation energy, and factors affecting enzyme activity (temperature, pH, inhibitors); ATP as the energy currency; photosynthesis (light reactions and the Calvin cycle); cellular respiration (glycolysis, Krebs cycle, oxidative phosphorylation); chemiosmosis and the proton gradient; fermentation; feedback regulation.
**Applied skills:** explaining how enzymes lower activation energy; interpreting enzyme-rate data and identifying the effect of an inhibitor; tracing energy and carbon through photosynthesis and respiration; calculating a reaction or oxygen-consumption rate from a graph; predicting how disrupting a step (for example, an uncoupler) affects ATP yield.
**Common traps:** thinking enzymes are consumed in reactions or change the reaction's equilibrium; confusing the reactants and products of photosynthesis versus respiration; assuming ATP is "stored energy" like a battery for the long term; ignoring that chemiosmosis depends on an intact membrane gradient.

## Chapter 4: Cell Communication and the Cell Cycle
**Core questions:** How do cells receive, transmit, and respond to signals, and how is the cell cycle regulated so that division is controlled?
**Key concepts:** signal reception, transduction, and response; ligands, receptors, and second messengers; signal amplification and feedback; the cell cycle phases (G1, S, G2, M) and interphase; checkpoints and regulatory proteins; mitosis and cytokinesis; loss of control, cancer, and apoptosis.
**Applied skills:** diagramming a signaling pathway from reception to response; identifying where a pathway could be interrupted by a drug or mutation; predicting the outcome of a failed checkpoint; relating uncontrolled division to cancer; interpreting cell-cycle or flow-cytometry data.
**Common traps:** assuming a signal travels into the cell unchanged rather than being transduced and amplified; confusing the order of cell-cycle phases; thinking checkpoints only occur once; treating apoptosis as accidental cell damage rather than regulated programmed death.

## Chapter 5: Heredity, Gene Expression, and Biotechnology
**Core questions:** What inheritance patterns explain genetic data, how does the central dogma turn DNA into proteins, and how do biotechnology tools manipulate genetic information?
**Key concepts:** meiosis, crossing over, and independent assortment as sources of variation; Mendelian inheritance, dominance, and non-Mendelian patterns (incomplete dominance, codominance, sex linkage); DNA replication; transcription and translation; mutations and their effects; gene regulation (operons, epigenetics); chi-square analysis; PCR, gel electrophoresis, and CRISPR.
**Applied skills:** predicting offspring ratios and testing them with chi-square; tracing information flow from gene to protein; classifying mutation effects (silent, missense, nonsense, frameshift); reading a gel or pedigree; explaining how a biotechnology tool achieves its result.
**Common traps:** confusing mitosis with meiosis or miscounting chromosome number after each; assuming every trait follows simple dominant-recessive inheritance; treating a chi-square value as a probability rather than comparing it to a critical value; reading a gel by assuming larger fragments travel farther.

## Chapter 6: Evolution and Phylogeny
**Core questions:** What forces change allele frequencies in a population, and how do we use evidence to reconstruct evolutionary relationships?
**Key concepts:** natural selection, fitness, and adaptation; the Hardy-Weinberg principle and its five conditions; genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, and nonrandom mating; speciation and reproductive isolation; evidence for evolution (fossil, molecular, anatomical, biogeographical); phylogenetic trees and cladograms; common ancestry.
**Applied skills:** using p + q = 1 and p² + 2pq + q² = 1 to calculate allele and genotype frequencies; identifying which condition is violated when a population evolves; reading a cladogram to infer shared derived traits and relatedness; constructing an evidence-based argument for common ancestry.
**Common traps:** stating that organisms evolve traits "in order to" survive (teleology) rather than through differential reproduction; treating Hardy-Weinberg p and q as genotype rather than allele frequencies; reading a cladogram as a ladder where later means "more advanced"; confusing genetic drift with natural selection.

## Chapter 7: Ecology and Systems Interactions
**Core questions:** What limits population growth, how do energy and matter move through ecosystems, and what makes communities and ecosystems stable or vulnerable to disruption?
**Key concepts:** exponential and logistic growth, carrying capacity, and limiting factors; trophic levels, food webs, and the roughly 10% energy transfer between levels; biogeochemical cycles (carbon, nitrogen); community interactions (competition, predation, symbiosis); biodiversity and ecosystem resilience; responses to disturbance, including invasive species and climate change.
**Applied skills:** modeling population growth from data and identifying carrying capacity; constructing an energy pyramid and explaining energy loss between trophic levels; tracing matter through a nutrient cycle; predicting how a disturbance propagates through a community; using evidence to argue about ecosystem resilience.
**Common traps:** assuming energy is recycled like matter rather than flowing and dissipating as heat; expecting populations to grow exponentially indefinitely; thinking higher trophic levels hold more total energy; treating biodiversity as merely the number of species without considering its role in stability.

## Chapter 8: AP Biology Exam Studio
**Core questions:** How do I read a figure, choose the right statistical test, and build a claim-evidence-reasoning argument under AP exam conditions?
**Key concepts:** experimental design (variables, controls, replication, sample size); visual representations and model construction; graphing conventions and data interpretation; statistical tools (mean, standard error, chi-square, comparing to critical values); the structure of multiple-choice, short-answer, and long free-response questions; claim-evidence-reasoning.
**Applied skills:** interpreting an unfamiliar figure or data table; selecting and applying the appropriate statistical test; designing or critiquing an experiment; writing a justified biological claim with quantitative evidence; managing time across exam question types.
**Common traps:** describing a graph without analyzing the trend or comparing groups; choosing a statistical test that does not match the data type; making a claim without tying it to specific evidence; restating the prompt instead of answering the question asked.

## Capstone: AP Biology Investigation Portfolio
Build and defend an AP Biology portfolio that connects content units to the four Big Ideas and the science practices. The finished work should pull the whole course together:
- One cellular-process analysis (an enzyme, photosynthesis, respiration, or transport investigation) with a rate calculation and an explanation linked to energetics.
- One genetics and statistics problem that uses a chi-square test or recombination reasoning to evaluate a prediction against data.
- One evolution task that applies the Hardy-Weinberg equations to calculate allele frequencies and argues from evidence about a population or phylogeny.
- One ecology argument built from a model or data set, predicting how a system responds to a disturbance.
- One timed free-response correction log that scores an FRQ against the AP rubric and records targeted revisions and reflection.
