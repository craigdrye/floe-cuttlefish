# Middle School Chemistry
From "stuff I can see" to "particles I can reason about" — the foundation course that turns everyday materials into atoms, bonds, and conserved mass.

**ID**: `middleSchoolChemistry` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
Middle School Chemistry moves students from describing materials to explaining them with particle models. The big idea is that a small set of ideas — atoms, bonding, mixtures, reactions, energy, and conservation of mass — can account for an enormous range of what students see in the lab and in everyday life. Rather than memorizing facts in isolation, students build a model of matter that they can use to predict and explain new situations.

The course is deliberately positioned as a bridge. It takes the qualitative, hands-on science of the elementary years and adds the quantitative reasoning, symbolic representation, and structure-property thinking that high-school chemistry will demand. Students learn to read a periodic table as a map of patterns, to track atoms through a reaction so that mass balances, and to connect what particles are doing to what they can measure on a balance, a thermometer, or a pH strip.

Throughout, the course keeps two habits front and center: explaining observations with particles, and respecting evidence. Every claim a student makes about a material should be defensible at the particle level and supported by data they could, in principle, collect safely in a lab. By the end, students should be able to look at an unfamiliar substance, mixture, or chemical change and ask the right questions about it.

## Course Design Notes
Keep the course hands-on and model-rich; particle diagrams and physical demonstrations should anchor abstract ideas. Route questions here when they involve states of matter, density, atoms and subatomic particles, periodic patterns, elements/compounds/mixtures, separation techniques, simple bonding, chemical reactions and word/balanced equations, conservation of mass, solutions, or acids, bases, and pH. The tier is high-school-entry: arithmetic and ratios are fair game, but stoichiometry, moles, and equilibrium are out of scope and belong to later chemistry courses. Frameworks of reference: US NGSS Middle School Physical Science (MS-PS1) and UK KS3 Chemistry.

## Chapter 1: Matter and Particle Models
- **Core questions**: What is matter, and how do particle arrangements explain solids, liquids, and gases? How does heating or cooling change state, and how do we calculate density?
- **Key concepts**: Mass and volume; the particle model; solids, liquids, gases; melting, freezing, boiling, condensation; diffusion; density as mass/volume; the limits of simple models.
- **Applied skills**: Drawing and comparing particle diagrams; calculating density and predicting whether something floats; explaining a state change in terms of particle motion and spacing.
- **Common traps**: Thinking particles themselves expand or melt; confusing mass with weight; assuming a substance with more particles is automatically denser without considering volume.

## Chapter 2: Atoms, Elements, and Isotopes
- **Core questions**: What is an atom made of, and how do we count its parts? How do atomic number and mass number identify an element, and what makes two atoms isotopes?
- **Key concepts**: Protons, neutrons, electrons; nucleus and electron arrangement; atomic number vs. mass number; elements and chemical symbols; isotopes; an introductory look at ions; the development of atomic models.
- **Applied skills**: Reading subatomic counts from atomic number and mass number; identifying isotopes; sketching a simplified atomic model and stating its limitations.
- **Common traps**: Confusing atomic number with mass number; thinking electrons live in the nucleus; assuming isotopes are different elements.

## Chapter 3: Periodic Table Patterns
- **Core questions**: How is the periodic table organized, and why do elements in the same group behave alike? What can position tell us about an element's properties?
- **Key concepts**: Groups and periods; metals, nonmetals, and metalloids; valence (outer) electrons; reactivity trends; alkali metals, halogens, and noble gases; the table as evidence-based ordering by proton number.
- **Applied skills**: Locating elements by group and period; predicting broad properties from position; explaining shared behavior in a group using outer electrons.
- **Common traps**: Reading the table left-to-right as a single number line; assuming all metals are equally reactive; thinking noble gases are reactive because they are "noble."

## Chapter 4: Elements, Compounds, Mixtures, and Separation
- **Core questions**: How do we classify a sample as element, compound, or mixture? Which separation method matches which kind of mixture, and why?
- **Key concepts**: Pure substances vs. mixtures; elements and compounds; homogeneous vs. heterogeneous mixtures; solutions; filtration, evaporation, distillation, and chromatography as physical separations.
- **Applied skills**: Classifying substances from descriptions or diagrams; choosing and justifying a separation method; interpreting filtration, evaporation, or chromatography results.
- **Common traps**: Calling a compound a mixture because it has more than one element; thinking separation changes the substances chemically; confusing "clear" with "pure."

## Chapter 5: Bonding and Structure Basics
- **Core questions**: Why do atoms bond, and how do ionic and covalent bonding differ? How does the way particles are held together explain a substance's properties?
- **Key concepts**: Electron transfer (ionic) vs. electron sharing (covalent); ions and simple formulas; properties of ionic compounds (high melting points, conduct when molten/dissolved) vs. simple covalent substances (low melting points, poor conductors); the structure-property link.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguishing ionic from covalent examples; matching bonding type to observed properties; interpreting simple chemical formulas.
- **Common traps**: Thinking all compounds conduct electricity; assuming covalent always means liquid or gas; treating a formula's subscripts as if they could be changed at will.

## Chapter 6: Chemical Reactions and Conservation of Mass
- **Core questions**: How do we know a chemical reaction happened, and what does a word or balanced equation tell us? Why is mass conserved, and when does it only appear to change?
- **Key concepts**: Reactants and products; evidence of reaction (gas, precipitate, color change, energy change); word equations and simple balanced equations; conservation of mass; open vs. closed systems; endothermic and exothermic changes.
- **Applied skills**: Identifying reaction evidence; balancing simple equations by counting atoms; explaining apparent mass loss or gain in open systems (gas escaping or being taken up).
- **Common traps**: Believing mass is "used up" in a reaction; balancing by changing subscripts instead of coefficients; assuming a closed-jar reaction that bubbles must lose mass.

## Chapter 7: Solutions, Acids, Bases, and pH
- **Core questions**: What happens to particles when something dissolves, and how do concentration and solubility differ? How does the pH scale rank acids and bases, and what occurs during neutralization?
- **Key concepts**: Solute, solvent, solution; concentration vs. amount; solubility and saturation; the 0-14 pH scale (acidic < 7, neutral = 7, basic > 7); indicators; neutralization producing a salt and water; safe handling of household chemicals.
- **Applied skills**: Interpreting pH data and indicator color changes; distinguishing concentration from total amount; predicting in words the products of a simple acid-base reaction.
- **Common traps**: Thinking dissolved solute disappears; confusing a strong acid with a concentrated one; assuming a higher pH number means more acidic.

## Chapter 8: Chemistry Investigation Studio
- **Core questions**: How do we design a fair, safe chemistry investigation? When does evidence actually support a chemical claim, and how should we revise an explanation that does not fit the data?
- **Key concepts**: Lab safety; fair tests and controlled variables; measurement and units; repeated trials; data tables and graphs; sources of error; evidence-based revision of explanations.
- **Applied skills**: Spotting and fixing an unsafe or unfair procedure; choosing independent, dependent, and controlled variables; judging whether data support a particle-level claim.
- **Common traps**: Changing more than one variable at a time; trusting a single trial; concluding causation from a single coincidental observation.

## Capstone
Students choose a material, mixture, household process, or local environmental-chemistry issue and explain it from the particle level all the way to the visible outcome. A strong capstone classifies the substances involved, identifies any chemical changes and the evidence for them, accounts for mass and energy, and (where relevant) includes a small fair-test investigation with data, a graph, a safety note, and a conclusion that could be revised if new evidence appeared.
