# AP US History
**Covers**: `col-ap-u-s-history`, `col-ap-us-history` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 12 (ages 16-18)

## Course Aim
Prepare students for AP U.S. History by building content knowledge from c. 1491 to the present and the historical thinking skills needed for source analysis, causation, comparison, continuity and change, DBQs, LEQs, and SAQs.

## Course Design Notes
The College Board frames AP U.S. History as an introductory college-level course organized around historical periods, themes, source analysis, and argument writing. This syllabus keeps the nine-period spine while making writing practice a recurring habit rather than a last-minute exam unit.

## Chapter 1: Historical Thinking and AP Writing
- **Key concepts**: contextualization, sourcing, thesis, evidence, complexity, causation, comparison, continuity and change.
- **Practice questions**: What is the historical context? What is the author's point of view? What evidence would support a defensible thesis?
- **Student output**: a skill notebook with DBQ, LEQ, and SAQ sentence frames and sample revisions.

## Chapter 2: Period 1, 1491-1607
- **Key concepts**: Indigenous societies, regional environments, European contact, Columbian Exchange, Spanish colonization.
- **Practice questions**: How did environment shape Indigenous societies? What changed after contact? How did exchange alter populations and economies?
- **Student output**: a comparison chart of pre-contact societies and early European imperial goals.

## Chapter 3: Period 2, 1607-1754
- **Key concepts**: British colonies, regional economies, slavery, religion, imperial competition, transatlantic exchange.
- **Practice questions**: Why did colonial regions develop differently? How did labor systems change? What tensions grew within empire?
- **Student output**: a short essay comparing Chesapeake, New England, middle colony, and southern colonial development.

## Chapter 4: Period 3, 1754-1800
- **Key concepts**: French and Indian War, Revolution, Articles of Confederation, Constitution, early republic, political parties.
- **Practice questions**: What caused the Revolution? How revolutionary was the Revolution? Why did the Constitution replace the Articles?
- **Student output**: a DBQ-style document set analysis on revolutionary ideology or constitutional compromise.

## Chapter 5: Periods 4-5, 1800-1877
- **Key concepts**: democracy, market revolution, reform, westward expansion, sectionalism, Civil War, Reconstruction.
- **Practice questions**: How did democracy expand and remain limited? Why did compromise fail? What did Reconstruction achieve and fail to secure?
- **Student output**: an LEQ on causation or continuity from the market revolution through Reconstruction.

## Chapter 6: Periods 6-7, 1865-1945
- **Key concepts**: industrialization, labor, immigration, urbanization, Populism, Progressivism, imperialism, World War I, New Deal, World War II.
- **Practice questions**: How did industrial capitalism reshape society? How did reformers respond? When and why did the United States expand global power?
- **Student output**: a thematic timeline linking work, exchange, technology, politics, and migration.

## Chapter 7: Periods 8-9, 1945-Present
- **Key concepts**: Cold War, civil rights, suburbanization, Vietnam, Great Society, conservatism, globalization, terrorism, demographic change.
- **Practice questions**: How did Cold War priorities shape domestic and foreign policy? How did rights movements change politics? What explains the rise of modern conservatism?
- **Student output**: an SAQ and thesis practice set using Cold War, civil rights, and post-1980 prompts.

## Chapter 8: Exam Studio and Historical Argument
- **Key concepts**: timed writing, stimulus interpretation, document grouping, outside evidence, complexity, review cycles.
- **Practice questions**: What is the fastest defensible thesis? Which documents can be grouped? Where can outside evidence strengthen the argument?
- **Student output**: one full timed DBQ, one LEQ, and a correction log by skill category.

## Capstone
Complete a mini APUSH portfolio: period concept maps, two SAQ sets, one LEQ, one DBQ, and a reflective error log identifying the strongest and weakest historical reasoning skills.

## Assessment Ideas
- Source analysis graded on sourcing, context, and evidence use.
- Writing tasks graded with AP-style thesis, evidence, reasoning, and complexity criteria.
- Capstone graded on content coverage, historical argument, and revision quality.
