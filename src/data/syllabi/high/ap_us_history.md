# AP US History
**Covers**: `col-ap-u-s-history`, `col-ap-us-history`
**Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 12 (ages 16–18)
**Frameworks**: US College Board AP U.S. History (APUSH)

## Course Philosophy
APUSH is a rigorous, college-level survey of US History from 1491 to the present. The focus is on Historical Thinking Skills: sourcing primary documents, understanding cause and effect, recognizing patterns of continuity and change, and constructing historical arguments (DBQs and LEQs).

## Module 1: Pre-Columbian to the Early Republic (Periods 1-3: 1491-1800) (20%)
- Period 1 (1491-1607): Native American societies before contact, the Columbian Exchange, and Spanish exploration (Encomienda system)
- Period 2 (1607-1754): British colonization (Jamestown, Plymouth), regional differences (New England vs Chesapeake), the transatlantic slave trade, and the First Great Awakening
- Period 3 (1754-1800): The French and Indian War, causes of the American Revolution, the Declaration of Independence, the Articles of Confederation, the Constitution, and Washington/Adams presidencies
- **Questions**: Analyzing the demographic impact of the Columbian Exchange, comparing colonial regions, analyzing the compromises of the Constitution

## Module 2: Expansion, Division, and the Civil War (Periods 4-5: 1800-1877) (20%)
- Period 4 (1800-1848): Jeffersonian democracy, the Market Revolution, the Second Great Awakening, social reform movements (Abolition, Women's Rights/Seneca Falls), and Jacksonian Democracy
- Period 5 (1844-1877): Manifest Destiny, the Mexican-American War, the sectional crisis over slavery (Compromise of 1850, Kansas-Nebraska Act, Dred Scott), the Civil War (Emancipation Proclamation), and Reconstruction (13th, 14th, 15th Amendments and their eventual failure)
- **Questions**: Evaluating the impact of the Market Revolution, tracing the breakdown of political compromise leading to the Civil War, analyzing the successes and failures of Reconstruction

## Module 3: Industrialization and Global Emergence (Periods 6-7: 1865-1945) (25%)
- Period 6 (1865-1898): The Gilded Age, the rise of big business (trusts/monopolies), labor unions, urbanization, immigration, and the Populist movement in the West/South
- Period 7 (1890-1945): American Imperialism (Spanish-American War), the Progressive Era (Roosevelt, Wilson), World War I and the domestic impact, the Roaring Twenties, the Great Depression, the New Deal, and World War II
- **Questions**: Comparing the goals of Populists and Progressives, analyzing the shift from isolationism to interventionism in WWI/WWII, evaluating the effectiveness of the New Deal

## Module 4: The Cold War and Modern America (Periods 8-9: 1945-Present) (20%)
- Period 8 (1945-1980): The Cold War (containment, Korea, Vietnam, Cuban Missile Crisis), the 1950s (suburbanization, conformity), the Civil Rights Movement (MLK, Malcolm X, Civil Rights Act of 1964), the Great Society, and the cultural upheaval of the 1960s/70s (Watergate, stagflation)
- Period 9 (1980-Present): The rise of the conservative movement (Reagan Revolution), the end of the Cold War, the digital revolution/globalization, the War on Terror (9/11), and modern demographic shifts
- **Questions**: Analyzing Cold War foreign policy, comparing the Civil Rights strategies of the 1950s vs the late 1960s, evaluating the impact of the Reagan presidency

## Module 5: AP Historical Reasoning and Writing (15%)
- Analyzing Primary and Secondary Sources (HIPP: Historical context, Intended audience, Purpose, Point of view)
- Writing the Document-Based Question (DBQ): thesis, contextualization, using 6+ documents, sourcing 3+ documents, and outside evidence
- Writing the Long Essay Question (LEQ) and Short Answer Questions (SAQ)
- Historical thinking skills: Causation, Continuity and Change over Time (CCOT), Comparison
- **Questions**: Sourcing a political cartoon using HIPP, writing a DBQ thesis statement under time constraints

## Stretch Zone
- Historiography: analyzing conflicting historical interpretations (e.g., Consensus vs Conflict historians on the American Revolution)
- In-depth study of Supreme Court jurisprudence (e.g., the Marshall Court vs the Warren Court)
