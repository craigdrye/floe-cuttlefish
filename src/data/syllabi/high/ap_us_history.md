# AP U.S. History
A college-level survey of the American past from c. 1491 to the present, paired with the source analysis and argument writing the AP exam rewards.

**ID**: `col-ap-u-s-history` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This course prepares students for the AP U.S. History (APUSH) exam by building durable content knowledge across the College Board's nine chronological periods, from pre-contact Indigenous societies through the twenty-first century. Students learn not just what happened but how historians know it — reading primary and secondary sources for point of view, purpose, audience, and context, and weighing competing interpretations of contested events.

Equally important are the historical thinking skills the exam measures: developments and processes, sourcing and situation, claims and evidence in sources, contextualization, making connections (causation, comparison, continuity and change over time), and argumentation. These skills are practiced continuously rather than crammed, so that by exam day a student can read a stimulus, group documents, marshal outside evidence, and defend a thesis under time pressure.

The course is designed for the high-school tier and assumes no prior college coursework. It moves quickly but scaffolds writing from sentence frames toward full essays, treating the DBQ, LEQ, and SAQ as habits to rehearse all year rather than a final unit to survive.

## Course Design Notes
The College Board organizes APUSH around nine periods, seven themes (American and National Identity; Work, Exchange, and Technology; Geography and the Environment; Migration and Settlement; Politics and Power; America in the World; American and Regional Culture and Society), and six reasoning skills. This syllabus keeps the nine-period chronological spine because students remember a story better than a list, but each chapter explicitly tags the themes and skills in play so review can be done either by chronology or by theme. Writing practice is embedded in every chapter — a thesis here, a document-grouping there — so the Exam Studio chapter consolidates rather than introduces. Question banks emphasize stimulus-based reasoning over rote recall, mirroring the redesigned multiple-choice format.

## Chapter 1: Historical Thinking and AP Writing
- **Core questions**: What is historical context, and how does it differ from background detail? How do sourcing elements (point of view, purpose, audience, situation) change a document's meaning? What makes a thesis defensible rather than merely true?
- **Key concepts**: contextualization, sourcing, corroboration, the six AP reasoning skills, thesis construction, line of reasoning, complexity, the DBQ/LEQ/SAQ rubrics.
- **Applied skills**: write a defensible thesis from a prompt; annotate a document for HIPP (historical context, intended audience, author's purpose, point of view); convert a list of facts into a line of reasoning.
- **Common traps**: confusing summary with analysis; restating the prompt as a "thesis"; treating sourcing as a formula instead of a claim about reliability and meaning.

## Chapter 2: Period 1, 1491-1607
- **Core questions**: How did distinct environments shape the political organization, economy, and culture of Indigenous societies? What changed, demographically and ecologically, after sustained European contact? How did Spanish colonization differ from later English models?
- **Key concepts**: Indigenous diversity (Pueblo, Mississippian, Iroquoian, Plains, Great Basin), the Columbian Exchange, demographic collapse, the encomienda system, the Spanish caste system, the Pueblo Revolt's roots.
- **Applied skills**: compare pre-contact societies by region; explain bidirectional ecological exchange; analyze a contact-era source for European framing of Indigenous peoples.
- **Common traps**: assuming continental uniformity among Indigenous peoples; reversing the direction of crop and disease flows; back-dating the large-scale transatlantic slave trade into the 1500s.

## Chapter 3: Period 2, 1607-1754
- **Core questions**: Why did the Chesapeake, New England, middle, and southern colonies develop differently? How and why did labor systems shift from indentured servitude toward racialized chattel slavery? What tensions grew within the British Atlantic empire?
- **Key concepts**: Jamestown and the headright system, Puritan New England, the Chesapeake tobacco economy, Bacon's Rebellion, the slave codes, the Middle Passage, salutary neglect, the First Great Awakening, mercantilism and the Navigation Acts.
- **Applied skills**: compare regional colonial economies and social structures; trace causation from Bacon's Rebellion to expanded slavery; read an Awakening-era sermon for purpose and audience.
- **Common traps**: treating "the colonies" as a single unit; assuming slavery was racialized from day one; mistaking salutary neglect for an explicit British policy.

## Chapter 4: Period 3, 1754-1800
- **Core questions**: What caused the imperial crisis after 1763, and how revolutionary was the Revolution? Why did the Constitution replace the Articles of Confederation? How did early political parties emerge despite the founders' distrust of them?
- **Key concepts**: the Seven Years' War and its debt, taxation without representation, the Coercive Acts, Common Sense, the Declaration, Articles of Confederation, Shays' Rebellion, the Constitutional Convention's compromises, the Bill of Rights, the Federalist–Democratic-Republican split, Washington's precedents.
- **Applied skills**: build a DBQ document set on revolutionary ideology or constitutional compromise; explain continuity and change in ideas about representation; analyze a Federalist Paper for argument and audience.
- **Common traps**: portraying the Revolution as universally liberating (women, enslaved people, and Loyalists complicate it); confusing the Articles with the Constitution; assuming parties were planned rather than emergent.

## Chapter 5: Periods 4-5, 1800-1877
- **Core questions**: How did American democracy expand while remaining sharply limited? Why did sectional compromise repeatedly succeed and then ultimately fail? What did Reconstruction achieve, and what did it fail to secure?
- **Key concepts**: the market revolution, the Second Great Awakening and reform, Jacksonian democracy and Indian Removal, Manifest Destiny, the Missouri and 1850 Compromises, Bleeding Kansas, secession, the Emancipation Proclamation and 13th–15th Amendments, Radical Reconstruction, the Compromise of 1877.
- **Applied skills**: write an LEQ on causation or continuity from the market revolution through Reconstruction; weigh the relative weight of slavery versus states' rights as a cause of the war; assess Reconstruction's successes and limits.
- **Common traps**: crediting Jacksonian "democracy" without naming its exclusions; reducing the Civil War's cause to a single factor; overstating or dismissing Reconstruction's gains.

## Chapter 6: Periods 6-7, 1865-1945
- **Core questions**: How did industrial capitalism reshape work, cities, and migration? How did Populists, Progressives, and the New Deal respond to inequality and instability? When and why did the United States project power abroad?
- **Key concepts**: the Gilded Age and trusts, labor organizing, the New Immigration and nativism, Populism, Progressive reform and muckraking, the Spanish-American War and imperialism, World War I and the Red Scare, the 1920s, the Great Depression, the New Deal, World War II and the home front.
- **Applied skills**: build a thematic timeline linking work, technology, migration, and politics; compare Populist and Progressive responses; analyze a wartime propaganda source.
- **Common traps**: conflating Populism with Progressivism; treating the New Deal as a single coherent program; assuming U.S. expansion abroad began only after 1945.

## Chapter 7: Periods 8-9, 1945-Present
- **Core questions**: How did Cold War priorities shape both foreign and domestic policy? How did the civil rights and other rights movements change American politics? What explains the rise of modern conservatism after 1968?
- **Key concepts**: containment and the early Cold War, McCarthyism, suburbanization and the affluent society, the civil rights movement, the Great Society, Vietnam, the New Right and Reagan, the end of the Cold War, globalization, 9/11 and the War on Terror, demographic change.
- **Applied skills**: write SAQs and theses on Cold War, civil rights, and post-1980 prompts; trace continuity and change in the size and role of the federal government; analyze a movement-era speech or document.
- **Common traps**: flattening the civil rights movement into a single leader or year; treating modern conservatism as inevitable rather than built; assuming the Cold War only affected foreign policy.

## Chapter 8: Exam Studio and Historical Argument
- **Core questions**: What is the fastest defensible thesis under timed conditions? Which documents can be grouped, and on what basis? Where can outside evidence strengthen an argument the documents leave incomplete?
- **Key concepts**: timed writing strategy, stimulus interpretation, document grouping by claim, outside (beyond-the-document) evidence, the complexity point, targeted review cycles, error logging by skill.
- **Applied skills**: complete one full timed DBQ and one LEQ; group a document set three different ways; build a correction log organized by reasoning skill rather than by period.
- **Common traps**: writing a document-by-document summary instead of an argument; forgetting outside evidence; chasing the complexity point before the basic thesis and evidence points are locked.

## Capstone
Assemble a mini APUSH portfolio that demonstrates both content coverage and historical reasoning: a set of period concept maps spanning all nine periods, two SAQ sets, one full LEQ, and one full timed DBQ, plus a reflective error log that names the student's strongest and weakest reasoning skills and a concrete revision plan for each weakness. The portfolio should show at least one essay revised after feedback, with the before-and-after rubric scores noted.

## Assessment Ideas
- Source analysis tasks graded on sourcing (HIPP), contextualization, and accurate use of evidence.
- Writing tasks graded with AP-style rubrics for thesis, evidence, reasoning, and complexity.
- Capstone graded on breadth of content coverage, quality of historical argument, and demonstrated revision.
