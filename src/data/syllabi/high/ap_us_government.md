# AP US Government & Politics
**Covers**: `col-ap-us-government-politics`
**Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: 12 (ages 16–18)
**Frameworks**: US College Board AP US Government & Politics

## Course Philosophy
This college-level course analyzes the structure and processes of the US political system. It requires in-depth analysis of Foundational Documents (e.g., Federalist Papers) and landmark Supreme Court cases, moving beyond basic mechanics to understand political behavior and policy outcomes.

## Module 1: Foundations of American Democracy (15%)
- Democratic ideals: Natural rights, social contract, popular sovereignty, republicanism
- The Articles of Confederation vs The Constitution
- Federalists vs Anti-Federalists (Brutus No. 1 and Federalist No. 10)
- The compromises of the Convention: Great Compromise, Electoral College, 3/5 Compromise
- Separation of Powers and Checks and Balances (Federalist No. 51)
- Federalism: Dual vs Cooperative Federalism, categorical vs block grants, mandates, the Commerce Clause, the Necessary and Proper Clause, and the 10th Amendment (*McCulloch v. Maryland*, *US v. Lopez*)
- **Questions**: Analyzing how Federalist 10 addresses the danger of factions, comparing the Commerce Clause interpretations in McCulloch and Lopez

## Module 2: Interactions Among Branches of Government (25%)
- Congress (Article I): Bicameralism, enumerating/implied powers, leadership, the committee system, the filibuster and cloture, gerrymandering/redistricting (*Baker v. Carr*, *Shaw v. Reno*), pork barrel legislation
- The Presidency (Article II): Formal/informal powers, vetoes, executive orders, executive agreements, signing statements, the bully pulpit, Federalist No. 70 (energetic executive)
- The Judiciary (Article III): Judicial review (*Marbury v. Madison*), judicial activism vs restraint, stare decisis, Federalist No. 78
- The Bureaucracy: Cabinet departments, independent agencies, the merit system, the iron triangle/issue networks, congressional oversight
- **Questions**: Analyzing the tension between the President and Congress over war powers, explaining how the iron triangle influences policy

## Module 3: Civil Liberties and Civil Rights (20%)
- Civil Liberties (The Bill of Rights): Selective incorporation via the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause
- First Amendment: Religion (Establishment Clause - *Engel v. Vitale*, *Wisconsin v. Yoder*), Speech (*Schenck v. US*, *Tinker v. Des Moines*, *Citizens United v. FEC*), Press (*NY Times v. US*)
- Second Amendment: *McDonald v. Chicago*
- Rights of the Accused: 4th Amendment (exclusionary rule), 5th/6th Amendments (*Gideon v. Wainwright*), 8th Amendment
- Right to Privacy: *Roe v. Wade*
- Civil Rights: 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause, African American civil rights (MLK's "Letter from Birmingham Jail", *Brown v. Board of Education*, Civil Rights Act of 1964, Voting Rights Act of 1965), Women's rights, Title IX, Affirmative Action
- **Questions**: Applying the concept of selective incorporation, analyzing the constitutional basis for the right to privacy

## Module 4: American Political Ideologies and Beliefs (15%)
- Political socialization: The process by which individuals acquire political beliefs (family, peers, media)
- Core values: Individualism, equality of opportunity, free enterprise, rule of law, limited government
- Ideology and Public Policy: Liberal vs Conservative vs Libertarian views on the economy (Keynesian vs Supply-side economics), social issues, and government intervention
- Measuring public opinion: Polling methodology, margin of error, sampling error, and how polls influence elections
- **Questions**: Categorizing policy proposals as liberal/conservative/libertarian, identifying flaws in polling methodology

## Module 5: Political Participation (25%)
- Voting behavior: Rational choice, retrospective, prospective, and party-line voting; factors affecting voter turnout; structural barriers to voting
- Political Parties: Linkage institutions, candidate recruitment, campaign management, party realignment/dealignment, barriers to third parties
- Interest Groups: Lobbying, amicus curiae briefs, iron triangles, the free-rider problem
- Elections and Campaigns: Incumbency advantage, primary/caucus system, the Electoral College, campaign finance (PACs, Super PACs, *Citizens United*)
- The Media: Gatekeeper, scorekeeper (horse-race journalism), watchdog roles, media bias
- **Questions**: Analyzing the impact of *Citizens United* on campaign finance, explaining why third parties struggle in the US system

## Stretch Zone
- Writing the Argument Essay: Developing a thesis supported by required Foundational Documents
- In-depth analysis of quantitative political data (graphs/charts)
