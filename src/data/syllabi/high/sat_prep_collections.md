# SAT Prep Collections
Master the digital SAT through content fluency, adaptive-test strategy, smart pacing, and disciplined error review.

**ID**: `col-sat-math` · **Discipline**: Exam Prep

## Course Aim
The digital SAT is a two-section, adaptive college-admissions test scored on a 400-1600 scale, delivered in two stages per section through College Board's Bluebook app. This course prepares students for both halves of the test: a Reading and Writing section (two modules of 27 questions, 32 minutes each) and a Math section (two modules of 22 questions, 35 minutes each). Because the second module's difficulty adapts to first-module performance, score growth depends not only on content knowledge but on consistent early accuracy, pacing, and composure.

The course pairs targeted content review with the test-taking habits that distinguish high scorers from strong students who underperform on test day. Students learn the question types College Board actually writes, the trap structures that recur across forms, and the verification routines that catch careless errors. Math instruction integrates the built-in Desmos graphing calculator and answer-checking strategies; Reading and Writing instruction emphasizes short-passage discipline, command-of-evidence logic, and precise grammar rules.

The aim is not to "teach to a trick" but to build durable skills: reading carefully for what a passage supports, modeling situations with algebra, reasoning about data, and reviewing mistakes by type so each practice test produces a concrete next action. Students leave with a personalized study plan, a maintained error log, and a calm, repeatable test-day routine.

## Course Design Notes
Designed for the US high-school tier (Year 12, ages 16-18). The course follows official College Board specifications for the digital SAT and uses Bluebook full-length practice tests as its anchor assessments. Chapters alternate between Reading and Writing and Math so students never neglect one half, and every chapter closes with a section-specific drill plus an entry in a running error log. Strategy content (adaptivity, pacing, Desmos, error taxonomy) is woven through rather than isolated, so students apply it on real questions immediately. Calculators are permitted on all Math questions, but problems are designed so the built-in Desmos tool is a verifier and shortcut, not a crutch.

## Chapter 1: Digital SAT Format and Study Plan
- **Core questions**: How is the digital SAT structured into sections, modules, and stages? What does a diagnostic reveal about where points are being lost? What is the highest-return next drill?
- **Key concepts**: two-section structure, multistage adaptive testing, Bluebook app, section scores (200-800 each), 400-1600 composite, diagnostic test, target score, study calendar.
- **Applied skills**: interpreting a diagnostic by question type, setting a realistic target score, building a weekly plan with practice sets and review blocks, scheduling official full-length checkpoints.
- **Common traps**: studying topics you already know, ignoring pacing in early practice, treating practice tests as scores rather than as diagnostic data.

## Chapter 2: Reading and Writing: Information and Ideas
- **Core questions**: What central idea or detail does the passage support? Which choice is best backed by textual or quantitative evidence? What does an accompanying graph or table actually add?
- **Key concepts**: central ideas and details, command of evidence (textual and quantitative), logical inference, data-to-text synthesis, single short passages.
- **Applied skills**: locating the specific lines that justify an answer, reading graphs and tables for the one value a question needs, completing an inference that the text logically implies, rejecting choices that overstate or go beyond the passage.
- **Common traps**: choosing a true statement that the passage does not actually support, picking the "extreme" inference, using outside knowledge instead of the text, misreading a data label or axis.

## Chapter 3: Reading and Writing: Craft and Structure
- **Core questions**: What does a word or phrase mean in this specific context? Why is a sentence or detail included? How do two paired texts relate?
- **Key concepts**: words in context, text structure and purpose, function of a sentence, tone and rhetorical purpose, cross-text connections.
- **Applied skills**: substituting a precise meaning that fits surrounding context, naming the rhetorical job a sentence performs, comparing two authors' claims or attitudes, distinguishing structure questions from main-idea questions.
- **Common traps**: defaulting to a word's most common meaning, confusing what a sentence says with what it does, assuming two paired authors disagree when they only differ in emphasis.

## Chapter 4: Reading and Writing: Expression and Conventions
- **Core questions**: Which transition matches the logical relationship? Is this a complete, correctly punctuated sentence? Which choice meets the rhetorical goal most effectively?
- **Key concepts**: transitions (contrast, cause, addition, example), rhetorical synthesis from bulleted notes, sentence boundaries, punctuation (commas, semicolons, colons, dashes), subject-verb agreement, pronoun-antecedent agreement, verb tense, modifiers, parallelism.
- **Applied skills**: selecting a transition from the logic rather than the "sound," joining or splitting clauses with correct punctuation, fixing agreement and modifier errors, picking the synthesis choice that satisfies the stated goal.
- **Common traps**: choosing a transition by feel instead of logic, comma splices and run-ons, matching a verb to the nearest noun rather than the true subject, ignoring the specific goal in rhetorical-synthesis prompts.

## Chapter 5: Math: Algebra and Linear Relationships
- **Core questions**: What does a slope or intercept represent in context? Which equation models the situation? Does the answer make physical sense?
- **Key concepts**: linear equations and inequalities, systems of equations, slope and intercepts, linear functions, units and rates, translating word problems into algebra.
- **Applied skills**: writing an equation from a verbal description, solving systems by substitution, elimination, or Desmos graphing, interpreting slope/intercept in real terms, checking answers by back-substitution.
- **Common traps**: mixing up x- and y-intercepts, dropping or flipping units, reversing an inequality sign when multiplying by a negative, answering for the wrong variable.

## Chapter 6: Math: Advanced Math, Geometry, and Trigonometry
- **Core questions**: Which form of a quadratic exposes the feature you need? What theorem or relationship applies to this figure? Can Desmos reveal the structure faster than algebra?
- **Key concepts**: quadratics and the structure of standard/vertex/factored forms, polynomials and zeros, rational and radical expressions, exponent rules, function transformations, circles and their equations, triangle and angle relationships, area and volume, right-triangle trigonometry (sine, cosine, tangent), the Pythagorean theorem.
- **Applied skills**: choosing the useful form of a quadratic for a given question, finding zeros and vertices, applying similarity and the Pythagorean theorem, setting up SOH-CAH-TOA, using Desmos to graph and confirm solutions.
- **Common traps**: mishandling negative signs in exponent and factoring work, forgetting both roots of a square root, confusing radius and diameter, mixing up which side is opposite vs. adjacent in trig.

## Chapter 7: Math: Problem Solving, Data, and Desmos Strategy
- **Core questions**: What two quantities are being compared? Which statistic actually answers the question? How can the calculator verify a result quickly?
- **Key concepts**: ratios, rates, proportions, percentages and percent change, mean/median/range, standard deviation intuition, scatterplots and lines of best fit, two-way tables, probability, sample-based inference, unit conversion.
- **Applied skills**: setting up and solving proportions, computing percent change correctly, reading scatterplots and two-way tables for the requested quantity, estimating probabilities, using Desmos for regression and quick checks.
- **Common traps**: computing percent change from the wrong base, confusing mean with median in skewed data, misreading a two-way table margin, treating correlation as causation.

## Chapter 8: Full Practice, Review, and Test-Day Execution
- **Core questions**: Was a missed question a content gap, a misread, a timing slip, or a careless error? Which question types deserve one more focused pass? What is the retake plan?
- **Key concepts**: pacing per module, digital navigation and flagging in Bluebook, the "always guess" rule (no penalty for wrong answers), stamina, official full-length practice tests, error taxonomy, score goals, retake strategy.
- **Applied skills**: completing a full Bluebook practice test under timing, classifying every miss by cause, building a targeted next-drill list from the error log, refining a test-day routine for breaks, snacks, and resets.
- **Common traps**: leaving questions blank, spending too long on the first module's hard items, not flagging and returning, reviewing scores without analyzing miss types.

## Capstone
Complete one full official-style practice cycle: take a timed Bluebook diagnostic, run targeted drills against the weakest chapters, sit a full-length practice test, build a complete error log classified by miss type, and produce a revised study plan plus a written test-day checklist. The capstone is assessed on the quality of the error analysis and the specificity of the resulting improvement plan, not on the practice score alone.

## Assessment Ideas
- Reading and Writing drills graded on correctly identifying the supporting evidence and the governing grammar rule, not just the right letter.
- Math drills graded on setup, solution efficiency, and a verification step (back-substitution or Desmos check).
- Capstone graded on error-taxonomy accuracy and the actionability of the next-steps plan.
