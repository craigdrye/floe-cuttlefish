# Earth Science
**ID**: `earthScience` · **Discipline**: Science · **Stage**: High School

## Course Aim
Help students understand Earth as a connected system of rock, water, air, life, energy, time, and human activity. Students learn to interpret maps, data, models, and evidence from Earth history while connecting local hazards and climate patterns to larger Earth-system processes.

## Course Design Notes
The course aligns with high-school Earth and Space Science ideas in NGSS-style instruction and uses NASA Earth-system framing. It keeps geology, weather, climate, resources, and data literacy together rather than treating them as disconnected units.

## Chapter 1: Earth Systems and Scientific Evidence
- **Key concepts**: systems, reservoirs, feedbacks, matter and energy flow, models, uncertainty, scale.
- **Practice questions**: What system components interact? What evidence supports the model? What scale is being studied?
- **Lab output**: a system diagram for a local Earth-science phenomenon.

## Chapter 2: Minerals, Rocks, and the Rock Cycle
- **Key concepts**: minerals, igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic rocks, weathering, erosion, deposition, heat, pressure.
- **Practice questions**: What process formed this rock? How does matter move through the rock cycle? What evidence is visible?
- **Lab output**: a rock-cycle transformation chart with sample observations.

## Chapter 3: Plate Tectonics and Earth Structure
- **Key concepts**: mantle convection, crust, lithosphere, plate boundaries, earthquakes, volcanoes, mountain building, seafloor spreading.
- **Practice questions**: Which boundary type fits the evidence? Why do hazards cluster? How does interior energy shape the surface?
- **Lab output**: a plate-boundary hazard map with explanation.

## Chapter 4: Earth History and Deep Time
- **Key concepts**: relative dating, absolute dating, fossils, stratigraphy, unconformities, geologic time, mass extinctions.
- **Practice questions**: Which layer is older? What event interrupted deposition? What does fossil evidence show about change over time?
- **Lab output**: a geologic timeline or stratigraphic column interpretation.

## Chapter 5: Atmosphere, Weather, and Forecasting
- **Key concepts**: air pressure, humidity, fronts, clouds, wind, storms, convection, Coriolis effect, weather maps.
- **Practice questions**: What weather is likely after this front passes? Why does air rise or sink? What data would improve the forecast?
- **Lab output**: a three-day weather analysis using maps or local observations.

## Chapter 6: Oceans, Water, and Climate
- **Key concepts**: water cycle, watersheds, groundwater, ocean circulation, heat capacity, albedo, greenhouse effect, carbon cycle.
- **Practice questions**: How does water move through the system? What feedback changes temperature? How do oceans store and move heat?
- **Lab output**: a water or climate feedback model with cause-and-effect arrows.

## Chapter 7: Natural Resources, Hazards, and Human Impacts
- **Key concepts**: energy resources, minerals, land use, flooding, drought, landslides, mitigation, risk, resilience.
- **Practice questions**: What hazard risk is present? Which mitigation reduces harm? What tradeoff comes with this resource use?
- **Lab output**: a local hazard or resource-risk briefing.

## Chapter 8: Earth Data and Capstone Investigation
- **Key concepts**: satellite data, remote sensing, graphs, maps, paleoclimate proxies, uncertainty, communicating evidence.
- **Practice questions**: What trend appears in the data? What alternative explanation must be checked? What claim can the evidence support?
- **Lab output**: a data-based Earth-system claim with graph, map, or model.

## Capstone
Investigate a local or global Earth-system question: flooding risk, heat islands, drought, coastline change, volcanic hazard, air quality, or climate trends. Submit a claim, evidence, model, uncertainty statement, and mitigation or adaptation recommendation.

## Assessment Ideas
- Map and data tasks graded on evidence interpretation.
- Lab writeups graded on systems reasoning and uncertainty.
- Capstone graded on scientific argument, visuals, and local/global connection.
