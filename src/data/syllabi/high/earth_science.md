# Earth Science
Earth as one connected system of rock, water, air, life, energy, and deep time, read through maps, models, and data.

**ID**: `earthScience` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
Earth Science treats the planet not as a list of separate topics but as a single system in which rock, water, air, living things, and energy continually exchange matter and feed back on one another. The aim is for students to explain familiar phenomena, an earthquake, a thunderstorm, a flooding river, a warming decade, in terms of the underlying flows of energy and matter that drive them, and to reason about the timescales over which those processes act, from a passing cold front to the billion-year history recorded in rock.

A second aim is data literacy. Earth scientists rarely observe a process directly; they read it from evidence, a stratigraphic column, a seismic record, a weather map, an ice-core proxy, or a satellite image. Students learn to interpret these representations, distinguish a trend from noise, weigh alternative explanations, and state the uncertainty that comes with every measurement. Claims are tied to evidence, and evidence is tied to a mechanism.

The course keeps geology, weather, climate, water, resources, and data reasoning together rather than as disconnected units, so that the same crosscutting ideas, systems and feedbacks, energy and matter conservation, scale, stability and change, recur across the year. It is written for the high-school tier and aligns with high-school Earth and Space Science (HS-ESS) disciplinary core ideas in NGSS-style instruction, supported by NASA and NOAA Earth-system framing.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve Earth as a system: the rock cycle and minerals; plate tectonics and Earth's interior; geologic time and the rock record; the atmosphere and weather; the water cycle, oceans, and climate; natural resources, hazards, and human impacts; or the interpretation of Earth-science data and models. The course deliberately threads a few ideas through every chapter. Conservation of matter and energy connects the rock cycle, the water cycle, weather, and the carbon and climate systems. Feedbacks (reinforcing and stabilizing) reappear from tectonics to albedo to ocean circulation. Scale, of distance, of energy, and especially of time, is revisited until students can place a thunderstorm, a glacial cycle, and the age of Earth on the same conceptual ruler.

Questions favor reasoning over recall: "what process formed this," "which boundary fits the evidence," "what does the data trend support," "what alternative must be ruled out." Where a number matters (a half-life, a percentage of parent isotope, a pressure-gradient direction) it is used to support an explanation, not as an end in itself. The final chapter is investigation-focused, drilling the move from observation to evidence-based claim with an honest statement of uncertainty.

## Chapter 1: Earth Systems and Scientific Evidence
- **Core questions**: Which spheres (geosphere, hydrosphere, atmosphere, biosphere) interact in this phenomenon, and how do matter and energy move between them? What evidence supports the model, and at what scale does the process act?
- **Key concepts**: systems and subsystems; reservoirs and fluxes; reinforcing and stabilizing (positive and negative) feedbacks; conservation of matter and energy; models and their limits; uncertainty; spatial and temporal scale.
- **Applied skills**: drawing a system diagram with reservoirs and arrows for matter/energy flow; identifying inputs, outputs, and feedbacks; matching a question to the right scale; distinguishing an observation from an inference.
- **Common traps**: treating Earth's spheres as isolated rather than coupled; confusing a reinforcing feedback (amplifies change) with a stabilizing one (resists change); assuming a model is reality rather than a useful simplification; ignoring scale when comparing processes.

## Chapter 2: Minerals, Rocks, and the Rock Cycle
- **Core questions**: What process formed this rock, and what observable evidence shows it? How does matter cycle among igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rock, and what energy drives each transformation?
- **Key concepts**: minerals and their identifying properties (hardness on the Mohs scale, cleavage, luster, streak); the three rock families; weathering, erosion, deposition; compaction and cementation; heat and pressure in metamorphism; cooling and crystallization in igneous rock; the rock cycle as multiple pathways, not a single loop.
- **Applied skills**: classifying a rock from texture and composition; ordering steps from sediment to sedimentary rock; using relative hardness to identify a mineral; tracing one atom through several rock-cycle pathways.
- **Common traps**: assuming the rock cycle runs in one fixed sequence; confusing weathering (breakdown in place) with erosion (transport); thinking metamorphism melts rock (it does not, melting makes magma); equating crystal size only with mineral type rather than with cooling rate.

## Chapter 3: Plate Tectonics and Earth's Interior
- **Core questions**: Which plate-boundary type fits the evidence (earthquakes, volcanoes, mountains, trenches, ridges)? How does energy from Earth's interior shape the surface, and why do hazards cluster where they do?
- **Key concepts**: Earth's layered interior (crust, mantle, outer and inner core); lithosphere and asthenosphere; mantle convection and ridge-push/slab-pull; divergent, convergent, and transform boundaries; subduction, seafloor spreading, mountain building; P-waves and S-waves and what the S-wave shadow zone reveals about a liquid outer core.
- **Applied skills**: classifying a boundary from its landforms and seismic pattern; using seismic-wave behavior to infer interior structure; explaining the global distribution of earthquakes and volcanoes; reading a plate-boundary map.
- **Common traps**: thinking continents plow through oceanic crust rather than plates moving together; assuming all volcanoes sit at boundaries (hotspots do not); confusing P-waves (compressional, travel through liquids) with S-waves (shear, stopped by liquids); treating the crust and lithosphere as the same layer.

## Chapter 4: Earth History and Deep Time
- **Core questions**: Which rock layer is older, and what events (deposition, erosion, intrusion) does the sequence record? How do relative and absolute dating combine to build the geologic time scale?
- **Key concepts**: original horizontality, superposition, and cross-cutting relationships; unconformities; index fossils and faunal succession; radiometric (absolute) dating, parent and daughter isotopes, half-life (carbon-14 ~5,730 years; potassium-40 ~1.26 billion years); the geologic time scale; mass extinctions.
- **Applied skills**: ordering events in a stratigraphic column; identifying an unconformity and the gap it represents; computing age from the number of half-lives or the parent fraction remaining; choosing an appropriate isotope for a given age range.
- **Common traps**: using carbon-14 to date rocks millions of years old (its range is roughly 100 to 50,000 years); thinking each half-life removes a fixed amount rather than half of what remains; reading a tilted or folded sequence by present orientation instead of by depositional order; confusing relative age (older/younger) with absolute age (years).

## Chapter 5: Atmosphere, Weather, and Forecasting
- **Core questions**: What weather is likely as this front passes, and why? Why does air rise or sink, and how does that produce clouds, storms, and wind? What data would improve the forecast?
- **Key concepts**: air pressure and the pressure-gradient force; the pressure-gradient direction (wind moves from high toward low pressure); humidity, dew point, and condensation; cold, warm, stationary, and occluded fronts; cloud types (cirrus, stratus, cumulus, cumulonimbus); convection; the Coriolis effect; high- and low-pressure systems; reading weather maps.
- **Applied skills**: predicting weather changes as a cold or warm front passes; relating cloud type to the air motion that formed it; explaining wind direction from a pressure map; using the Coriolis effect to explain rotation of pressure systems.
- **Common traps**: thinking warm air sinks and cold air rises (it is the reverse for rising motion that forms clouds); assuming a cold front and warm front bring the same weather; treating humidity and temperature as the same thing; forgetting that wind is set by the pressure gradient first, then deflected by rotation.

## Chapter 6: The Water Cycle, Oceans, and Climate
- **Core questions**: How does water move and store energy through the Earth system? Which feedbacks raise or lower temperature, and how do the oceans store and redistribute heat?
- **Key concepts**: the water cycle (evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, infiltration), driven mainly by solar energy; watersheds and groundwater; water's high heat capacity; surface and deep ocean circulation and the role of density (temperature and salinity); albedo; the greenhouse effect (greenhouse gases absorb and re-emit outgoing infrared radiation); the carbon cycle; ice-albedo and other climate feedbacks.
- **Applied skills**: tracing water and energy through the cycle; explaining why coastal climates are milder than inland ones; predicting how a feedback (for example, melting ice lowering albedo) changes temperature; distinguishing weather from climate; reading the difference between the greenhouse effect and the ozone layer.
- **Common traps**: thinking greenhouse gases block incoming sunlight rather than trapping outgoing infrared; confusing the greenhouse effect with the ozone hole; assuming oceans only respond to climate rather than driving it; treating a single hot year as proof of (or against) a climate trend.

## Chapter 7: Natural Resources, Hazards, and Human Impacts
- **Core questions**: What hazard risk is present, and which mitigation actually reduces harm? What tradeoffs come with using a given resource, and which resources are renewable versus finite?
- **Key concepts**: renewable versus nonrenewable energy and mineral resources; fossil fuels and the carbon released by burning them; land and water use; hazards (flooding, drought, landslides, earthquakes, volcanic and coastal hazards); risk as a function of hazard and exposure/vulnerability; mitigation, adaptation, and resilience.
- **Applied skills**: assessing the hazard risk of a site from maps and history; selecting mitigation matched to a specific hazard; identifying the tradeoff in a resource-use decision; classifying resources as renewable or nonrenewable.
- **Common traps**: calling every natural resource "renewable" or assuming groundwater refills as fast as it is pumped; treating a hazard (the event) and a risk (likely harm to people) as identical; assuming mitigation is free of tradeoffs; ignoring that human activity can increase a natural hazard's impact.

## Chapter 8: Earth Data and the Capstone Investigation
- **Core questions**: What trend does this data set actually support, and over what timescale? What alternative explanation must be ruled out, and how large is the uncertainty?
- **Key concepts**: satellite remote sensing and Earth observation; graphs, maps, and the choice of axes and scale; paleoclimate proxies (ice cores, tree rings, sediment and coral records); signal versus noise; correlation versus causation; uncertainty and error; communicating an evidence-based claim.
- **Applied skills**: reading a trend from a graph and stating its timescale; choosing the data needed to test a claim; identifying and addressing an alternative explanation; writing a claim-evidence-reasoning statement with an honest uncertainty note.
- **Common traps**: reading a short record as if it were a long-term trend; treating correlation as proof of cause; cherry-picking a start year to exaggerate or hide a trend; reporting a result without any statement of uncertainty.

## Capstone
Investigate a local or global Earth-system question of the student's choosing, for example flooding risk in a watershed, urban heat islands, drought, coastline or glacier change, volcanic or earthquake hazard, air quality, or a regional climate trend. The deliverable is a short investigation that states a claim, supports it with evidence (a graph, map, model, or proxy record), explains the mechanism, gives an honest statement of uncertainty and at least one alternative explanation considered, and closes with a mitigation or adaptation recommendation appropriate to the scale of the problem.
