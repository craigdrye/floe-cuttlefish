# Logic & Argumentation
*Make your reasons visible, test them honestly, and argue like someone who still has to live with the other side afterward.*

**ID**: `logicAndArgumentation` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This course teaches students to recognize, build, test, and repair arguments wherever they appear: schoolwork, public debate, advertising, science reporting, online disagreement, and their own private thinking. It treats logic as a practical craft rather than a debate-club weapon. The goal is not to win every argument but to make reasons visible enough that anyone can evaluate them fairly.

Students move from the anatomy of a single argument (claim, premise, conclusion, hidden assumption) to argument mapping, charitable interpretation, deductive validity, inductive and causal reasoning, evidence standards, informal fallacies, rhetoric, and the cognitive biases that derail even smart people. Each unit pairs a vocabulary of precise terms with repeated, hands-on practice so students learn the moves, not just the labels.

By the end, students should be able to slow down a claim, reconstruct its strongest fair version, test its structure, weigh its evidence, name what actually went wrong when reasoning fails, and produce better arguments of their own. The course is calibrated for the high-school tier: rigorous about structure and evidence, but anchored in everyday examples rather than symbolic-logic formalism.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus had the right backbone: premises, conclusions, validity, fallacies, and rhetoric. This rewrite keeps that spine and adds argument mapping, the principle of charity and steelmanning, explicit evidence and causal-reasoning standards, a fallacy unit that explains the defect rather than playing "fallacy bingo," a cognitive-bias unit, and a capstone argument portfolio. Throughout, chapters list common traps so students can pre-empt the predictable mistakes (confusing validity with truth, naming a fallacy instead of refuting it, mistaking correlation for cause). Sourcing draws on open logic textbooks and high-school and college critical-thinking comparators listed in Research Notes.

## Chapter 1: What Counts as an Argument?
**Core questions**
- How is an argument different from a fight, an explanation, a story, or a bare opinion?
- What has to be true for this conclusion to actually follow?
- Which assumptions are doing quiet work in the background?

**Key concepts**: claim, premise, conclusion, inference, reason, evidence, explanation vs. argument, disagreement, assumption, hidden (unstated) premise, standard form, conclusion and premise indicators.

**Applied skills**
- Identify conclusion indicators ("therefore," "so") and premise indicators ("because," "since").
- Put short arguments into standard form with numbered premises and a conclusion.
- Separate an explanation of *why* something happened from an argument that something *is true*.

**Common traps**
- Treating any strong opinion or emotional outburst as an argument.
- Confusing an explanation ("the bridge fell because of corrosion") with an argument for a contested claim.
- Missing the unstated premise that the whole inference secretly depends on.

## Chapter 2: Argument Mapping and Fair Interpretation
**Core questions**
- What is the strongest fair version of this argument?
- Which reasons work together, and which stand on their own?
- What objection would actually matter, rather than just sounding clever?

**Key concepts**: argument map, linked premises, independent (convergent) premises, objection, rebuttal, principle of charity, steelmanning, burden of proof, relevance.

**Applied skills**
- Build simple argument maps with support arrows and objection arrows.
- Improve weak wording without changing the argument unfairly.
- State an opposing view accurately enough that its own defender would recognize it.

**Common traps**
- Steelmanning's opposite, the straw man: attacking a distorted, weaker version.
- Treating linked premises (which fail if one is removed) as independent, or vice versa.
- Shifting the burden of proof onto whoever happens to be skeptical.

## Chapter 3: Deductive Logic
**Core questions**
- Can an argument be valid but have a false conclusion?
- What makes a counterexample decisive?
- Why do "if," "only if," "unless," and "or" cause so many small disasters?

**Key concepts**: deductive argument, validity, soundness, conditional, antecedent, consequent, necessary condition, sufficient condition, modus ponens, modus tollens, affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, disjunctive syllogism, counterexample.

**Applied skills**
- Test validity by structure rather than by gut feeling.
- Distinguish validity (form) from soundness (form plus true premises).
- Spot affirming the consequent and denying the antecedent.
- Translate everyday conditionals into clean logical form.

**Common traps**
- Calling an argument "true" or "false" when the right words are valid/invalid and sound/unsound.
- Reading "if P then Q" as if it also meant "if Q then P."
- Treating a necessary condition as if it were sufficient.

## Chapter 4: Inductive, Causal, and Statistical Reasoning
**Core questions**
- When is a sample good enough to support a general claim?
- What would distinguish a real cause from a coincidence?
- How can numbers persuade honestly, and how can they mislead?

**Key concepts**: inductive strength, cogency, generalization, sample size, representativeness, bias in sampling, analogy, correlation, causation, confounder, base rate, probability, causal mechanism.

**Applied skills**
- Evaluate polls, anecdotes, trend claims, analogies, and causal claims.
- Identify missing comparison groups and plausible alternative explanations.
- Rewrite overconfident conclusions into appropriately cautious ones.

**Common traps**
- Generalizing from a tiny or biased sample.
- Sliding from correlation to causation without ruling out confounders or reverse causation.
- Ignoring the base rate when a percentage sounds dramatic.

## Chapter 5: Informal Fallacies Without Fallacy Bingo
**Core questions**
- Why is naming a fallacy not the same as refuting an argument?
- When can an appeal to authority, emotion, or a slippery-slope worry actually be legitimate?
- What exactly went wrong in the reasoning here?

**Key concepts**: ad hominem, straw man, red herring, false dilemma, slippery slope, hasty generalization, circular reasoning / begging the question, appeal to authority, appeal to popularity (bandwagon), equivocation, loaded question.

**Applied skills**
- Explain the reasoning defect, not just slap on a label.
- Distinguish similar fallacies by their underlying error.
- Repair a fallacious argument into a stronger, fairer version.

**Common traps**
- Crying "fallacy" as a conversation-ending move instead of an analysis.
- Mistaking every appeal to authority or emotion for a fallacy, even legitimate ones.
- Confusing a relevant tu-quoque observation with a genuine rebuttal.

## Chapter 6: Rhetoric, Persuasion, and Responsible Debate
**Core questions**
- How can emotion support reasoning without replacing it?
- What makes a source credible *for this particular question*?
- How should people argue when they still have to live together afterward?

**Key concepts**: ethos, pathos, logos, audience, framing, tone, concession, counterargument, evidence quality, source credibility, deliberation, persuasion ethics.

**Applied skills**
- Analyze how speeches, ads, essays, and campaign messages mix credibility, emotion, and logic.
- Build a persuasive argument that includes evidence, a counterargument, and stated limits.
- Practice discussion moves: clarify, challenge, concede, revise, and summarize.

**Common traps**
- Mistaking confidence or polish for evidence.
- Treating any appeal to emotion as illegitimate manipulation.
- Citing a credible source on the wrong topic (an expert outside their field).

## Chapter 7: Cognitive Biases and Intellectual Habits
**Core questions**
- Why do smart, informed people still reason badly?
- What habits make disagreement more truth-seeking rather than just louder?
- Do we have responsibilities for what we believe and what we share?

**Key concepts**: confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, anchoring, availability heuristic, Dunning-Kruger effect, intellectual humility, open-mindedness, epistemic responsibility.

**Applied skills**
- Recognize common bias patterns in oneself, not just in opponents.
- Design a "slow thinking" checklist for high-stakes claims.
- Decide when the honest move is to suspend judgment.

**Common traps**
- Spotting bias only in people you disagree with.
- Mistaking a vivid, memorable anecdote (availability) for a representative statistic.
- Treating high confidence as evidence of competence.

## Chapter 8: Capstone Argument Studio
**Core questions**
- What real, contestable question is worth a full argument portfolio?
- Where is my own argument weakest, and how honestly have I addressed it?
- What changed in my view as I built and defended the case?

**Key concepts**: research question, evidence packet, argument portfolio, objection and reply, revision, oral defense, reflective metacognition.

**Applied skills**
- Frame a clear, answerable research question.
- Assemble an evidence packet and an argument map for a real position.
- Anticipate the strongest objection and answer it on the record.

**Common traps**
- Choosing a question so broad or one-sided that no real argument is possible.
- Padding the evidence packet with sources that restate the claim rather than support it.
- Treating the objection-and-reply as a formality instead of a real test.

## Capstone
Students choose a real, contestable question from school life, science, culture, politics, technology, sports, or ethics and produce a complete argument portfolio: a clear research question, an argument map, an evidence packet, one strong objection with a reply, a revised written argument, and a short oral defense. Assessment weighs precision, fairness to opponents, evidence quality, and the honesty of the revision.
