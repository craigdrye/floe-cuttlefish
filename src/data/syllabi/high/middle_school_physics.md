# Middle School Physics
A first physics toolkit for explaining motion, forces, energy, waves, electricity, and magnetism using careful observation, simple measurement, and everyday examples.

**ID**: `middleSchoolPhysics` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
This course gives students their first organized way to explain how the physical world behaves. Learners build the vocabulary and reasoning to describe motion, predict the effect of forces, trace energy as it moves and changes form, and account for waves, heat, electricity, and magnetism. The emphasis is on noticing patterns, measuring carefully, and explaining causes rather than memorizing isolated facts.

Mathematics is kept light and purposeful. Students calculate average speed, read distance-time and velocity-time graphs, find net force, and apply the wave-speed relationship, but every formula is tied to a concrete situation so the number has a meaning. Diagrams, graphs, and clear sentences carry as much weight as arithmetic, preparing students for the heavier algebra of high-school physics.

The course is aligned to the US NGSS Middle School Physical Science performance expectations (MS-PS2, MS-PS3, MS-PS4) and the UK Key Stage 3 physics programme of study. It is designed for students roughly ages 13-14 who are ready to reason about cause and effect but still benefit from grounding every idea in something they can see, build, or test.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve speed and motion graphs, balanced and unbalanced forces, Newton's laws, mass versus weight, friction, energy stores and transfers, conservation of energy, heat transfer, wave properties, sound, light, simple circuits, magnets, electromagnets, or low-stakes engineering design and fair testing. Keep prompts qualitative or single-step quantitative; multi-step algebra and trigonometry belong to the high-school physics track.

## Chapter 1: Motion, Measurement, and Graphs
- **Core questions**: How do we describe where something is and how fast it moves? What is the difference between speed and velocity? How do distance-time and velocity-time graphs reveal motion?
- **Key concepts**: Position, distance, displacement, speed (distance ÷ time), velocity as speed with direction, acceleration, SI units, proportional reasoning, distance-time graphs, and velocity-time graphs.
- **Applied skills**: Calculate average speed, read and sketch motion graphs, convert simple units, and translate a graph into a plain-language description of motion.
- **Common traps**: Confusing speed with velocity, reading a flat distance-time line as motion, mixing up steepness (speed) with height (position), and forgetting units.

## Chapter 2: Forces and Newton's Laws
- **Core questions**: What does a force do? Why does an object speed up, slow down, or turn? How are the three laws of motion related?
- **Key concepts**: Contact and non-contact forces, balanced and unbalanced forces, net force, inertia, Newton's first law (no net force means constant motion), second law (acceleration depends on net force and mass), third law (equal and opposite pairs), and free-body diagrams.
- **Applied skills**: Draw free-body diagrams, add forces to find a net force, and use Newton's laws to predict everyday motion.
- **Common traps**: Thinking a moving object always has a force pushing it forward, treating action-reaction pairs as acting on the same object, and assuming heavier always means faster.

## Chapter 3: Gravity, Friction, and Fields
- **Core questions**: Why is weight not the same as mass? How do friction and air resistance change motion? How can forces act without touching?
- **Key concepts**: Gravity, mass (amount of matter) versus weight (gravitational force), friction, air resistance, normal force, tension, magnetic and electrostatic forces, and fields as invisible regions of interaction.
- **Applied skills**: Compare mass and weight in different gravity, identify where friction helps or hinders, and explain attraction and repulsion using fields.
- **Common traps**: Saying mass changes on the Moon, believing heavier objects always fall faster in air, and confusing magnetic with electrostatic effects.

## Chapter 4: Energy Stores and Transfers
- **Core questions**: What are the main ways energy is stored? How does energy move and change form? What does conservation of energy really mean?
- **Key concepts**: Kinetic, gravitational potential, elastic, chemical, thermal, light, sound, and electrical energy; energy transfer pathways; conservation of energy; efficiency; and energy-transfer (Sankey-style) diagrams.
- **Applied skills**: Trace energy transformations through a device, identify wasted (usually thermal) energy, and reason qualitatively about kinetic and potential energy during motion.
- **Common traps**: Saying energy is "used up" or destroyed, calling energy types "forces," and ignoring the energy wasted as heat or sound.

## Chapter 5: Heat Transfer and Materials
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between temperature and thermal energy? How does heat move? Why do some materials warm up faster than others?
- **Key concepts**: Temperature versus thermal energy, particle motion, conduction, convection, radiation, insulation, conductors versus insulators, and specific heat treated qualitatively.
- **Applied skills**: Identify the heat-transfer mechanism in a scenario, compare insulators and conductors, and interpret temperature-versus-time data.
- **Common traps**: Treating temperature and heat as the same thing, thinking cold "flows" into objects, and forgetting that radiation needs no medium while conduction and convection do.

## Chapter 6: Waves, Sound, and Light
- **Core questions**: What features describe any wave? How are sound and light alike and different? What happens when a wave meets a boundary?
- **Key concepts**: Wavelength, frequency, amplitude, wave speed (speed = frequency × wavelength), transverse and longitudinal waves, reflection, refraction, absorption, transmission, sound as a wave needing a medium, light as part of the electromagnetic spectrum, and the visible spectrum.
- **Applied skills**: Label wave diagrams, use the wave-speed relationship, and predict reflection, refraction, absorption, or transmission at a boundary.
- **Common traps**: Confusing amplitude with wavelength, assuming sound travels through a vacuum, and thinking louder means higher pitch.

## Chapter 7: Electricity and Magnetism
- **Core questions**: What flows in a circuit and what pushes it? How do series and parallel circuits differ? What links electricity and magnetism?
- **Key concepts**: Static charge, electric current, voltage (potential difference), resistance, conductors and insulators, series versus parallel circuits, magnetic poles, magnetic fields, electromagnets, and safe circuit building.
- **Applied skills**: Read and diagnose simple circuit diagrams, compare brightness or current in series versus parallel, and predict how to strengthen an electromagnet.
- **Common traps**: Thinking current is "used up" by a bulb, swapping the roles of voltage and current, and believing all metals are magnetic.

## Chapter 8: Physics Design Studio
- **Core questions**: How do we test a physics idea fairly? Which measurement best answers the question? How does evidence drive a redesign?
- **Key concepts**: Design constraints, independent, dependent, and controlled variables, fair tests, repeat trials, measurement quality, tradeoffs, and evidence-based iteration.
- **Applied skills**: Improve a flawed test by controlling variables, choose an appropriate metric, and justify a redesign with data.
- **Common traps**: Changing more than one variable at once, relying on a single trial, and judging a design by appearance rather than measured performance.

## Capstone
Students choose an everyday phenomenon or simple device and explain it using at least three physics ideas from across the course. The explanation must include a labeled diagram, at least one calculation or piece of measured data, and clear cause-and-effect reasoning, then defend the explanation against an alternative everyday misconception.

## Assessment Ideas
- Diagram-and-explain checks for forces, energy, waves, and circuits.
- Short graph interpretation and single-step calculation prompts.
- Lab notebooks that reward measurement quality and revision over neatness.
- A final design defense focused on evidence, not decoration.
