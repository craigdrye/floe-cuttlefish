# US History
A chronological and source-driven survey of the American past from Indigenous North America to the present, built around the historical thinking skills students need for college and civic life.

**ID**: `usHistory` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
This course builds a connected, chronological understanding of United States history from Indigenous North America through the twenty-first century. Students learn not only the sequence of major events but the forces that drove them: geography and environment, migration and settlement, the growth and limits of democracy, the long struggle over slavery and race, the rise of an industrial and then global economy, and the changing relationship between citizens and their government. The aim is a coherent narrative students can reason with, not a list of names and dates to memorize.

Alongside content, the course develops the historical thinking skills that define the discipline: sourcing (asking who made a source, when, and why), contextualization, corroboration across multiple sources, and the reasoning moves of causation, comparison, and continuity and change over time. Students practice turning evidence into an argument, distinguishing a defensible claim from a personal opinion, and weighing competing interpretations of contested events.

The course is designed for the high-school tier and assumes no prior college coursework. It uses the AP U.S. History periodization and skill language as a reference point for structure and rigor without becoming an exam-prep survey, and it keeps political topics balanced by separating evidence, claim, perspective, and judgment so students learn to argue with sources rather than slogans.

## Course Design Notes
The eight chapters follow a standard high-school chronological spine so the past reads as a story with cause and consequence rather than disconnected units. Each chapter pairs narrative history with primary and secondary sources drawn from institutions such as the Library of Congress and the National Archives, and each asks students to compare at least two perspectives on a contested question. Recurring themes — democracy and its limits, slavery and freedom, economic transformation, federal power, and America's role in the world — are tagged across chapters so the course can be reviewed either by period or by theme. Writing is scaffolded throughout, moving from one-sentence thesis statements toward document-based paragraphs and full arguments, so the capstone consolidates skills rather than introducing them. Question banks emphasize reasoning from a stimulus over rote recall, mirroring how historians actually work.

## Chapter 1: Indigenous America, Encounter, and Colonization, to 1754
- **Core questions**: How did distinct environments shape Indigenous societies before sustained European contact? What changed, demographically and ecologically, after 1492, and what continued? Why did the British, Spanish, French, and Dutch develop such different colonial systems?
- **Key concepts**: Indigenous diversity and sovereignty, regional environments, the Columbian Exchange, demographic collapse from disease, the encomienda system, the Pueblo Revolt of 1680, the Atlantic slave trade and the Middle Passage, colonial regions (New England, middle, Chesapeake, southern), mercantilism and salutary neglect.
- **Applied skills**: compare pre-contact societies by region; explain the bidirectional flow of crops, animals, and disease; read a contact-era European source for the framing it imposes on Indigenous peoples.
- **Common traps**: treating all Indigenous peoples as a single uniform group; reversing the direction of the Columbian Exchange (e.g., placing maize in Europe before contact); assuming slavery was racialized from the first arrivals rather than hardening into a legal caste over decades.

## Chapter 2: Revolution, Founding, and the Early Republic, 1754-1824
- **Core questions**: What turned colonial protest into a movement for independence? How revolutionary was the Revolution for women, enslaved people, and Loyalists? Why did the Constitution replace the Articles of Confederation, and which founding debates still shape politics?
- **Key concepts**: the French and Indian War and imperial debt, taxation without representation, Enlightenment and republican ideas, the Declaration of Independence, the Articles of Confederation, the Constitutional Convention's compromises (Great Compromise, Three-Fifths Compromise), the Bill of Rights, Federalists versus Democratic-Republicans, Washington's precedents.
- **Applied skills**: trace causation from war debt to colonial resistance; explain the problem each constitutional compromise solved and the cost it carried; analyze a founding-era source for purpose and audience.
- **Common traps**: confusing the Articles of Confederation with the Constitution; portraying the Revolution as universally liberating; assuming political parties were planned by the founders rather than emerging despite their distrust of "factions."

## Chapter 3: Expansion, Democracy, Reform, and Sectionalism, 1820-1860
- **Core questions**: How did political democracy expand for white men while many groups remained excluded? How did westward expansion intensify conflict over slavery? How did reformers combine moral, religious, and political arguments?
- **Key concepts**: the market revolution, Jacksonian democracy, Indian Removal and the Trail of Tears, Manifest Destiny, the Missouri Compromise (1820), the cotton kingdom, the Second Great Awakening and reform, abolition, the Seneca Falls Convention and women's rights, the Mexican-American War, the Compromise of 1850 and Kansas-Nebraska.
- **Applied skills**: build a cause-and-effect chain linking economic change, territorial expansion, and sectional crisis; compare reform movements by their goals and methods; evaluate how a compromise either delayed or accelerated conflict.
- **Common traps**: treating "Jacksonian democracy" as inclusive when it excluded women, enslaved people, and Native nations; assuming Manifest Destiny was uncontested; confusing the Missouri Compromise line with later compromises.

## Chapter 4: Civil War and Reconstruction, 1860-1877
- **Core questions**: Why did decades of compromise over slavery finally fail? How did enslaved and free Black Americans shape the meaning of the war? What did Reconstruction achieve, and why were so many of its gains reversed?
- **Key concepts**: secession and the Confederacy, the Emancipation Proclamation (1863) and its limits, total war, the Gettysburg Address, the Reconstruction (13th, 14th, 15th) amendments, Black political participation, Radical Reconstruction, the Compromise of 1877, sharecropping, the foundations of Jim Crow.
- **Applied skills**: evaluate Reconstruction with constitutional, political, economic, and social evidence; distinguish what the Emancipation Proclamation did and did not do; explain why a constitutional right can fail without enforcement.
- **Common traps**: believing the Emancipation Proclamation freed all enslaved people immediately; reversing the order or content of the Reconstruction amendments; treating Reconstruction's end as inevitable rather than a political bargain.

## Chapter 5: Industrial America, Immigration, and Reform, 1877-1920
- **Core questions**: Were industrial leaders best understood as innovators, exploiters, or both? How did workers, farmers, immigrants, women, and reformers respond to rapid change? Why did the United States expand its role overseas?
- **Key concepts**: industrial capitalism and railroads, the Gilded Age and "robber barons," labor unions and collective bargaining, new immigration and urbanization, Populism, Progressivism and muckrakers, antitrust reform, women's suffrage and the 19th Amendment, the Spanish-American War and imperialism, World War I.
- **Applied skills**: write a claim-and-counterclaim argument on a Gilded Age or Progressive debate; analyze a muckraking source for purpose and effect; compare the strategies of workers, farmers, and reformers.
- **Common traps**: assuming monopolies always lowered prices; equating labor unions with monopolies; treating Progressivism as a single unified program rather than a coalition with competing aims.

## Chapter 6: Modernity, Crisis, and Global War, 1920-1945
- **Core questions**: What tensions defined the 1920s? How did the New Deal change the relationship between citizens and the federal government? How should historians weigh military, diplomatic, ethical, and civilian perspectives on World War II decisions?
- **Key concepts**: consumer culture and the Harlem Renaissance, nativism and immigration restriction, the Great Depression, the New Deal and the welfare state, isolationism versus interventionism, Pearl Harbor and U.S. entry into WWII, the home front, Japanese American incarceration, the Holocaust, the atomic bomb.
- **Applied skills**: connect a New Deal program to the federal role it expanded; compare stated policy goals with lived experience during the Depression or the war; evaluate a wartime decision from multiple perspectives.
- **Common traps**: assuming the New Deal ended the Depression; describing the New Deal as a return to limited government; treating WWII home-front policies as uniformly just (e.g., overlooking incarceration of Japanese Americans).

## Chapter 7: Cold War America, Rights Movements, and Political Realignment, 1945-1980
- **Core questions**: How did Cold War priorities shape both foreign and domestic policy? Why did rights movements use both legal and grassroots strategies? What explains the political realignments of the late twentieth century?
- **Key concepts**: containment and the Cold War, the Korean and Vietnam Wars, suburbanization, the civil rights movement, Brown v. Board of Education (1954), the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Voting Rights Act of 1965, the Great Society, second-wave feminism, the Chicano and American Indian movements, Watergate, the rise of the conservative movement.
- **Applied skills**: link a Cold War policy abroad to its domestic consequences; compare legal and direct-action strategies within a single movement; use a source to trace shifting public trust in government.
- **Common traps**: treating the civil rights movement as the work of a few leaders rather than mass organizing; assuming Brown immediately desegregated schools; confusing containment with isolationism.

## Chapter 8: Contemporary United States and Historical Argument, 1980-Present
- **Core questions**: What changed after 1980 in the economy, politics, and global role of the United States? Which contemporary debates have deep historical roots? How can historians evaluate very recent events with incomplete evidence?
- **Key concepts**: globalization and deindustrialization, the conservative ascendancy, the end of the Cold War, the digital revolution, 9/11 and the security state, immigration and demographic change, economic inequality, the culture wars, climate and energy debates, digital citizenship and media literacy.
- **Applied skills**: trace a current issue back to at least two earlier periods; evaluate the reliability of recent and online sources (lateral reading); construct a historically grounded argument under uncertainty.
- **Common traps**: assuming recent history is too "current" to analyze historically; mistaking opinion for evidence-based argument; treating present-day partisan labels as if they have always meant the same thing.

## Capstone
Build a U.S. history inquiry portfolio organized around a research question that crosses at least three periods (for example, "How has the meaning of citizenship changed from Reconstruction to the present?"). The portfolio includes the research question, a set of annotated primary sources, a contextual timeline, a short historiographical note that fairly presents at least two interpretations, a thesis-driven essay or presentation that uses the sources as evidence, and a reflection on how the student's interpretation changed after corroborating evidence. It is assessed on question quality, range and use of sources, chronological reasoning, the strength of the argument, and the honesty of the reflection.
