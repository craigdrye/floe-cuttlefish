# US History
**ID**: `usHistory` ; **Discipline**: Humanities ; **Year**: 10 (ages 14-15)
**Frameworks**: Standard US High School History

## Course Aim
Build a chronological and source-driven understanding of United States history from Indigenous North America to the present while practicing the historical thinking skills students need for civic life: sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, causation, comparison, continuity and change, and evidence-based argument.

## Course Design Notes
This course follows a broad high-school U.S. history sequence, with AP U.S. History used as a reference for periodization, themes, and skill language without turning the class into an AP survey. Each chapter pairs narrative history with primary and secondary sources from institutions such as the Library of Congress and National Archives, asks students to weigh competing interpretations, and keeps political discussion balanced by separating evidence, claim, perspective, and judgment.

## Chapter 1: Indigenous America, Encounter, and Colonization, to 1754
- **Key concepts**: Indigenous societies, regional environments, sovereignty, Columbian Exchange, Spanish, French, Dutch, and British colonization, Atlantic trade, slavery, religious diversity, colonial regions.
- **Practice questions**: How did geography shape Indigenous societies before European contact? What changed and what continued after contact? Why did colonial regions develop different labor systems, economies, and cultures?
- **Student output**: an annotated source comparison using one Indigenous account, one European account, and one map or material culture source.

## Chapter 2: Revolution, Founding, and the Early Republic, 1754-1824
- **Key concepts**: imperial crisis, Enlightenment ideas, republicanism, Declaration of Independence, Revolutionary War, Articles of Confederation, Constitution, Bill of Rights, Federalists and Democratic-Republicans, early foreign policy.
- **Practice questions**: What caused colonists to move from protest to independence? How revolutionary was the Revolution for different groups? Which founding debates still shape American politics?
- **Student output**: a short constitutional argument explaining one founding compromise, the problem it addressed, and the tradeoffs it created.

## Chapter 3: Expansion, Democracy, Reform, and Sectionalism, 1820-1860
- **Key concepts**: market revolution, Jacksonian democracy, Indian Removal, Manifest Destiny, reform movements, abolition, women's rights, immigration, cotton kingdom, sectional conflict, Mexican-American War.
- **Practice questions**: How did democracy expand while many people remained excluded? How did westward expansion intensify conflict over slavery and sovereignty? How did reformers use moral, religious, and political arguments?
- **Student output**: a cause-and-effect timeline connecting economic change, territorial expansion, reform, and sectional crisis.

## Chapter 4: Civil War and Reconstruction, 1860-1877
- **Key concepts**: secession, Union and Confederacy, emancipation, total war, military strategy, wartime politics, Reconstruction amendments, Black political participation, federal authority, resistance, sharecropping, Jim Crow foundations.
- **Practice questions**: Why did compromise over slavery fail? How did enslaved and free Black Americans shape the meaning of the war? What did Reconstruction achieve, and why were many of its gains limited or reversed?
- **Student output**: a document-based paragraph evaluating Reconstruction through constitutional, political, economic, and social evidence.

## Chapter 5: Industrial America, Immigration, and Reform, 1877-1920
- **Key concepts**: industrial capitalism, railroads, corporations, labor unions, immigration, urbanization, Populism, Progressivism, segregation, women's suffrage, conservation, imperialism, Spanish-American War, World War I.
- **Practice questions**: Were industrial leaders best understood as innovators, exploiters, or both? How did workers, farmers, immigrants, women, and reformers respond to rapid change? Why did the United States expand its role overseas?
- **Student output**: a balanced claim-and-counterclaim essay on one Gilded Age, Progressive Era, or imperialism debate.

## Chapter 6: Modernity, Crisis, and Global War, 1920-1945
- **Key concepts**: consumer culture, Harlem Renaissance, nativism, Great Depression, New Deal, labor and welfare state, isolationism and interventionism, World War II, home front, Japanese American incarceration, Holocaust, atomic bomb.
- **Practice questions**: What tensions defined the 1920s? How did the New Deal change the relationship between citizens and the federal government? How should historians weigh military, diplomatic, ethical, and civilian perspectives on World War II decisions?
- **Student output**: a source packet analysis that compares public policy goals with lived experiences during the Depression or World War II.

## Chapter 7: Cold War America, Rights Movements, and Political Realignment, 1945-1980
- **Key concepts**: containment, Cold War institutions, suburbanization, civil rights movement, Great Society, Vietnam War, feminism, Chicano movement, American Indian Movement, environmentalism, Watergate, conservative movement.
- **Practice questions**: How did Cold War priorities shape domestic and foreign policy? Why did rights movements use both legal and grassroots strategies? What explains the political realignments of the late twentieth century?
- **Student output**: an oral-history or archive-based mini-investigation linking a local or family story to a national postwar theme.

## Chapter 8: Contemporary United States and Historical Argument, 1980-Present
- **Key concepts**: globalization, deindustrialization, technology, immigration, conservatism and liberalism, end of the Cold War, terrorism and security, culture wars, economic inequality, demographic change, climate and energy debates, digital citizenship.
- **Practice questions**: What changed after 1980 in the economy, politics, and global role of the United States? Which contemporary debates have deep historical roots? How can historians evaluate very recent history with incomplete evidence?
- **Student output**: a historically grounded policy brief that traces one current issue to at least two earlier periods.

## Capstone
Create a U.S. history inquiry portfolio on a question that crosses at least three periods. The portfolio includes a research question, annotated primary sources, a contextual timeline, a balanced historiographical note, a thesis-driven essay or presentation, and a reflection on how the student's interpretation changed after corroborating evidence.

## Assessment Ideas
- Source analysis checks using sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, and close reading.
- Chapter writing tasks graded for thesis, evidence, reasoning, historical accuracy, and fair treatment of competing viewpoints.
- Seminar discussions assessed on preparation, citation of evidence, listening, and revision of claims.
- Capstone graded on question quality, source range, chronological understanding, argument, and reflection.
