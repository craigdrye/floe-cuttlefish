# A-Level Sciences (Biology, Chemistry, Physics)
A serious but navigable map of the three UK A-Level sciences: deep subject knowledge, practical endorsement skills, mathematical fluency, and exam-style reasoning.

**ID**: `col-a-level-biology` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
This course gives sixth-form students a coherent route through A-Level Biology, Chemistry, and Physics as taught on the major UK specifications (AQA, Edexcel, OCR). It treats the three sciences as related but distinct disciplines: each has its own core models, its own required calculations, and its own examiner expectations. The aim is not to compress three full A-Levels into one shallow survey, but to build the shared reasoning habits — define the rule, match it to the problem, carry units, sense-check the result — that strong candidates use across all three.

Every chapter pairs conceptual understanding with the quantitative work that A-Level examiners reward. Students practise mole and titration calculations, mechanics and field problems, enzyme and inheritance reasoning, and the interpretation of graphs, spectra, and experimental data. Throughout, the course foregrounds the practical endorsement: planning, uncertainty, calibration, and the difference between a measurement and a conclusion.

By the end, a student should be able to triage an unfamiliar exam question, identify which model or equation applies, write a structured extended response, and defend at least one substantial synoptic explanation in each science.

## Course Design Notes
Use this syllabus as a routing hub for advanced UK science questions. Route biology questions here when they require A-Level detail (biochemistry, cell biology, genetics, physiology, ecology); chemistry questions when they involve physical, inorganic, organic, or analytical chemistry; and physics questions when they involve A-Level mechanics, electricity, waves, particles, fields, thermal physics, or nuclear physics. Questions are tagged to the chapter whose model they test, so a single track can serve all three subjects without blurring the boundaries between them. Mathematical content stays at the level the specifications require — logs and exponentials for decay and pH, basic trigonometry for vectors, and rate equations — and answers are expected with correct units and sensible significant figures.

## Chapter 1: Science Skills and the Practical Endorsement
- **Core questions**: How do you choose apparatus for a required practical? How is absolute and percentage uncertainty calculated and combined? When is a result limited by systematic versus random error?
- **Key concepts**: Practical planning, risk assessment, resolution and uncertainty, calibration, anomalies, line of best fit and gradients, significant figures, and examiner command words.
- **Applied skills**: Calculate percentage uncertainty in a derived quantity; read a gradient and intercept from a linearised graph; distinguish precision from accuracy.
- **Common traps**: Confusing precision with accuracy; quoting more significant figures than the data justify; treating a systematic error as something repeats can remove.

## Chapter 2: Biology — Biological Molecules, Cells, and Exchange
- **Core questions**: How does enzyme structure explain specificity and the effect of temperature, pH, and inhibitors? Why are exchange surfaces large, thin, and well-supplied? How do diffusion, osmosis, facilitated diffusion, and active transport differ?
- **Key concepts**: Carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids, ATP, enzyme action and inhibition, cell ultrastructure, the cell-surface membrane, transport mechanisms, gas exchange, and mass transport.
- **Applied skills**: Interpret enzyme rate data and inhibitor graphs; explain a transport mechanism in terms of energy and gradients; relate exchange-surface features to Fick's law.
- **Common traps**: Saying enzymes are "used up" in reactions; confusing competitive and non-competitive inhibition; describing active transport as moving down a gradient.

## Chapter 3: Biology — Genetics, Energy, and Coordination
- **Core questions**: How does the sequence of DNA determine protein structure? How do meiosis and independent assortment generate variation? How is the Hardy-Weinberg principle used to estimate allele frequencies? What happens in the light-dependent and light-independent stages of photosynthesis?
- **Key concepts**: DNA, transcription and translation, mutation, meiosis, inheritance, the Hardy-Weinberg equation, photosynthesis, respiration, nervous and synaptic transmission, homeostasis, and gene technologies.
- **Applied skills**: Solve monohybrid and Hardy-Weinberg problems; trace energy and electron flow in photosynthesis and respiration; explain synaptic transmission step by step.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting Hardy-Weinberg uses p + q = 1 and p² + 2pq + q² = 1; placing the Calvin cycle in the thylakoid; saying impulses "jump" across the synapse rather than using neurotransmitters.

## Chapter 4: Chemistry — Physical Chemistry
- **Core questions**: How are amounts of substance and titration results calculated from concentrations and volumes? How does Hess's law let you find an enthalpy change you cannot measure directly? How does Le Chatelier's principle predict the shift of an equilibrium?
- **Key concepts**: Atomic structure, the mole, bonding and shapes, energetics and Hess's law, kinetics and the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, equilibria and Kc, acids and bases, and pH.
- **Applied skills**: Complete mole and titration calculations; build a Hess cycle from enthalpies of formation or combustion; predict equilibrium shifts; calculate the pH of a strong acid.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting to balance equations before mole ratios; reversing the direction of an arrow in a Hess cycle; assuming a catalyst shifts the position of equilibrium.

## Chapter 5: Chemistry — Inorganic and Organic Chemistry
- **Core questions**: How do periodic trends in Group 2 and Group 7 arise? Why do transition metals form coloured ions and complexes? How do you deduce an organic structure from its reactions and spectra, and plan a multi-step synthesis?
- **Key concepts**: Periodicity, Group 2 and Group 7 trends, Period 3 oxides, transition-metal chemistry, organic nomenclature, isomerism, reaction mechanisms, synthesis routes, and analytical techniques (mass spectrometry, infrared, NMR).
- **Applied skills**: Predict trends in reactivity and solubility; draw nucleophilic substitution and electrophilic addition mechanisms; deduce a structure from a molecular formula and spectra.
- **Common traps**: Confusing electrophilic addition with nucleophilic substitution; mixing up the trends in Group 2 (reactivity increases down) and Group 7 (reactivity decreases down); misreading an IR fingerprint region as diagnostic.

## Chapter 6: Physics — Mechanics, Materials, Waves, and Electricity
- **Core questions**: When can the SUVAT equations be used and when can they not? How are stress, strain, and the Young modulus read from a graph? What conditions produce a stationary wave, and how does internal resistance affect terminal pd?
- **Key concepts**: Vectors, kinematics and SUVAT, Newton's laws, momentum, work-energy and power, materials and the Young modulus, progressive and stationary waves, superposition, and circuits with internal resistance.
- **Applied skills**: Solve multi-step constant-acceleration problems; calculate the Young modulus from a stress-strain gradient; apply terminal pd = emf − Ir.
- **Common traps**: Using SUVAT when acceleration is not constant; confusing stress with strain or force; forgetting that emf is shared between the load and internal resistance.

## Chapter 7: Physics — Particles, Quantum, Fields, and Nuclear Physics
- **Core questions**: How does the photoelectric effect support the photon model, and what is the threshold frequency? How are gravitational and electric fields described by inverse-square laws? How do capacitor discharge and radioactive decay both follow exponential laws?
- **Key concepts**: Particles and radiation, the photoelectric equation hf = Φ + E_k(max), simple harmonic motion (a = −ω²x), gravitational and electric fields, capacitors and the time constant RC, magnetic fields and induction, and radioactive decay.
- **Applied skills**: Apply the photoelectric equation and find a work function; use λ = ln 2 / t½ and N = N₀e^(−λt); calculate the time constant and discharge of a capacitor.
- **Common traps**: Thinking brighter light raises the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons; confusing half-life with the time constant; dropping the minus sign in a = −ω²x.

## Chapter 8: Synoptic Revision and Exam Strategy
- **Core questions**: How do you decode a command word and structure a six-mark answer? How do you tell apart methods that look similar across the three sciences? How do you build an efficient revision cycle from past-paper error patterns?
- **Key concepts**: Specification mapping, command words, practical-linked questions, extended responses, multi-topic integration, common traps, spaced retrieval, and exam triage.
- **Applied skills**: Plan a six-mark extended response; map a question back to its specification point; compare similar-looking methods (e.g. Hess cycles versus Born-Haber, SUVAT versus energy methods).
- **Common traps**: Writing everything you know instead of answering the command word; assuming a familiar-looking question uses the familiar method; revising by re-reading rather than retrieving.

## Capstone
Students complete a cross-science investigation, literature review, or exam portfolio that demonstrates practical reasoning, mathematical fluency with correct units, and at least one substantial synoptic explanation drawn from each of biology, chemistry, and physics — for example, linking diffusion gradients (biology), reaction rates (chemistry), and exponential decay (physics) as different expressions of the same mathematical behaviour.

## Assessment Ideas
- Topic tests that require shown working, correct units, and written interpretation.
- Practical notebooks checked against endorsement-style criteria (planning, uncertainty, analysis).
- Synoptic question sets that force links across modules and across the three sciences.
- Oral defences of one biology, one chemistry, and one physics explanation.
