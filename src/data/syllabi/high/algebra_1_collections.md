# Algebra 1 Collections
A first full algebra course: seeing structure in expressions, solving equations and inequalities with reasons, and modeling real situations with functions, systems, and quadratics.

**ID**: `col-algebra-1` · **Discipline**: Maths

## Course Aim
This course builds a steady, explanation-rich Algebra 1 in which students learn to see structure, solve with reasons, model real situations, and move confidently among expressions, equations, tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions. It uses the Common Core high school Algebra and Functions standards as its spine, with deliberate review time for students arriving from different middle-school pathways. The aim is to balance procedural fluency with explanation, estimation, and assessment-ready problem solving.

Algebra 1 is where arithmetic generalizes into reasoning about quantities that vary. Students move from "find the number" to "describe the relationship," learning to treat a variable as a quantity that can change, an expression as an object with structure, and an equation as a statement that can be true, false, or always true. The course stays concrete before it goes abstract: a context, a table, or a graph comes first, then a rule students can defend.

By the end, students should simplify and rewrite expressions to reveal useful quantities, solve and justify linear equations, inequalities, and systems, work fluently with function notation and graph features, distinguish linear from exponential growth, and analyze quadratic functions through factoring, zeros, and vertex form. Just as important, they should be able to interpret a solution in context, judge whether it is reasonable, and explain the limits of a model.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve expressions and equivalence, one-variable linear and literal equations, inequalities and constraints, functions and graphs, linear functions and data models, systems of equations and inequalities, integer exponents and polynomials, linear-versus-exponential growth, and introductory quadratics. Keep the work cumulative: linear equations reappear inside systems and quadratics, function notation threads through every later chapter, and signed-number and distributive-property fluency underlie all of it.

Keep practice forward and error-aware. Every wrong answer should reflect a genuine student misconception, distributing to only the first term, forgetting to flip an inequality after dividing by a negative, reading slope as the y-intercept, treating any U-shaped data as quadratic, with a specific reason it fails and a forward hint toward the right move. Favor relatable contexts (pricing, motion, savings, design limits, school data) so the algebra stays grounded, and ask students throughout to say what a solution means and whether it is reasonable. Mind the age-appropriate boundary: completing the square and the quadratic formula appear as enrichment, while heavier topics such as rational functions, logarithms, and conic sections belong to Algebra 2 and beyond.

## Chapter 1: Algebraic Habits and Expressions
**Core questions:** What are the parts of an expression, and how do I rewrite an expression so it reveals a useful quantity without changing its value?
**Key concepts:** variables, terms, factors, coefficients; order of operations; properties of operations (commutative, associative, distributive); equivalent expressions; substitution and evaluation; units and the meaning of expression structure.
**Applied skills:** identifying parts of an expression; evaluating formulas for given inputs; combining like terms and applying the distributive property; checking equivalence by substitution; rewriting an expression to expose a rate, total, or factor.
**Common traps:** distributing to only the first term inside parentheses; combining unlike terms (3x + 2 becoming 5x); ignoring order of operations when evaluating; assuming two expressions are equal because one test value matches.

## Chapter 2: Linear Equations and Literal Equations
**Core questions:** How do I solve a one-variable linear equation while justifying each move, and how do I rearrange a formula to solve for a chosen variable?
**Key concepts:** inverse operations; the distributive property in solving; variables on both sides; identities and no-solution cases; equivalent equations; literal equations and solving for a variable.
**Applied skills:** solving multi-step equations and checking the solution; justifying each algebraic step; recognizing infinitely-many-solution and no-solution outcomes; rearranging formulas such as distance, temperature, simple interest, or geometry relationships.
**Common traps:** changing only one side of the equation when applying an operation; sign errors when moving terms across the equals sign; concluding "no solution" when the variable simply cancels to a true statement (an identity); dividing before distributing.

## Chapter 3: Inequalities and Constraint Reasoning
**Core questions:** How do I solve and graph a linear inequality, when must I reverse the inequality sign, and how do I turn a real-world limit into a constraint?
**Key concepts:** inequality symbols and solution sets; number-line and interval representations; the rule for reversing the sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative; compound inequalities (and / or); absolute value as distance; constraints from real limits.
**Applied skills:** solving and graphing one-variable inequalities; deciding when to flip the inequality; writing and interpreting compound inequalities; translating budget, time, or capacity limits into inequalities and reading their boundaries.
**Common traps:** forgetting to reverse the inequality after multiplying or dividing by a negative; graphing with the wrong open/closed endpoint; reading "at most" or "at least" as the wrong symbol; treating an inequality solution as a single number instead of a set.

## Chapter 4: Functions, Tables, and Graphs
**Core questions:** What makes a relation a function, and how do I read and interpret f(x), domain, range, and key graph features?
**Key concepts:** function definition and the vertical-line idea; domain and range; function notation f(x); independent and dependent variables; intercepts; increasing and decreasing intervals; average rate of change over an interval.
**Applied skills:** deciding whether a relation is a function from a table, graph, or mapping; evaluating and interpreting f(x); matching contexts to tables and graphs; describing intercepts, intervals, and average rate of change; stating a reasonable domain from context.
**Common traps:** reading f(x) as multiplication of f and x; swapping domain and range; calling a graph a function when one input maps to two outputs; reporting an unrealistic domain (negative or fractional counts) for a real context.

## Chapter 5: Linear Functions and Data Models
**Core questions:** How do I write a linear equation from a graph, points, table, or context, and how do I interpret and critique a linear model of data?
**Key concepts:** slope as rate of change; x- and y-intercepts; slope-intercept, point-slope, and standard forms; direct variation; scatterplots, trend (line-of-best-fit) reasoning, residuals; the difference between correlation and causation.
**Applied skills:** computing slope from two points; writing equations from graphs, points, tables, or stories; interpreting slope and intercept in context; fitting and judging a linear trend line for a small dataset.
**Common traps:** confusing slope with the y-intercept (or inverting rise/run); misreading the sign of slope; assuming correlation proves causation; extrapolating a linear model far beyond the data and overclaiming.

## Chapter 6: Systems of Equations and Inequalities
**Core questions:** How do I solve a system of two linear equations exactly and approximately, and how do I represent and read a feasible region for a system of inequalities?
**Key concepts:** systems as simultaneous constraints; solving by graphing, substitution, and elimination; equivalent systems; one, none, or infinitely many solutions; linear inequalities in two variables and their feasible regions.
**Applied skills:** solving systems by each method and choosing an efficient one; identifying the number of solutions from slopes/intercepts; graphing a system of inequalities and shading the feasible region; modeling break-even and mixture situations.
**Common traps:** stopping after finding one variable and not back-substituting; adding equations to eliminate when subtraction is needed (sign error); concluding "no solution" for an identity or vice versa; shading the wrong side of a boundary line.

## Chapter 7: Exponents, Polynomials, and Exponential Patterns
**Core questions:** How do I apply the exponent rules and operate on polynomials, and how do I tell linear growth from exponential growth?
**Key concepts:** integer (including zero and negative) exponents; product, quotient, and power rules; scientific notation; adding, subtracting, and multiplying polynomials; linear (constant difference) versus exponential (constant ratio) growth; geometric patterns and simple exponential models y = a·b^x.
**Applied skills:** simplifying expressions using exponent rules; converting to and from scientific notation; multiplying binomials and combining polynomial terms; classifying growth from a table; writing a simple exponential function from a context.
**Common traps:** adding exponents when multiplying bases vs. multiplying them (mixing the product and power rules); treating x^0 as 0; thinking a negative exponent makes a value negative; calling a pattern linear when each step multiplies rather than adds.

## Chapter 8: Quadratics and Algebra 1 Modeling Studio
**Core questions:** How do I find the zeros and key features of a quadratic, and how do I connect a table, graph, equation, and context for a quadratic model?
**Key concepts:** quadratic functions and parabolas; vertex, axis of symmetry, and intercepts; standard and factored forms; factoring (GCF, trinomials, difference of squares); completing the square and the quadratic formula as enrichment; data-linked modeling.
**Applied skills:** factoring quadratics and finding zeros from factored form; identifying the vertex, axis, and intercepts; choosing a solution method; solving projectile, area, and revenue-style problems and interpreting the result.
**Common traps:** taking the zeros of (x − 3)(x + 5) as 3 and 5 instead of 3 and −5; forgetting to factor out a GCF first; assuming any U-shaped data is exactly quadratic; reporting a negative time or length without checking the context.

## Capstone: Algebra 1 Modeling Portfolio
Build and defend an Algebra 1 modeling portfolio around one practical question, such as pricing, motion, population change, design constraints, or school data. The finished work should pull the whole course together:
- At least one linear model written from data or a context, with slope and intercept interpreted in real terms.
- One system or two-variable inequality constraint, solved or graphed, with its feasible region or solution explained.
- One nonlinear pattern, either an exponential model or a quadratic, supported by a table, a graph, and an equation.
- A clear written explanation that states what each solution means, checks it for reasonableness, and reflects honestly on the limits of the model.
