# High School Biology
A living-systems course where students explain how cells, genes, organisms, and ecosystems behave by reasoning from evidence, models, and data rather than memorizing vocabulary.

**ID**: `col-high-school-biology-ngss` · **Discipline**: Science

## Course Aim
High School Biology gives students a working toolkit for the science of life: how cells transform matter and energy, how genetic information is copied and expressed, how traits move through generations, how evolution explains the diversity and unity of life, and how ecosystems respond to change. The course sits below AP Biology but stays rigorous, treating biology as a quantitative, evidence-driven discipline rather than a list of terms.

Across every chapter, students practice an investigation habit alongside the content: building and revising models, interpreting graphs and data tables, constructing arguments from evidence, and designing or critiquing experiments. The aim is that a student can look at an unfamiliar biological diagram, dataset, or scenario and explain what is happening using molecular, cellular, organismal, and ecological reasoning.

The course is mobile-first and aligned to NGSS high school life-science performance expectations (HS-LS1 through HS-LS4). It draws on OpenStax Biology, HHMI BioInteractive, and OER assessment banks so that explanations connect molecular details to whole-organism and ecosystem outcomes.

## Course Design Notes
- Each chapter pairs core biology with one investigation skill: modeling, data interpretation, argument from evidence, or experimental design.
- Questions from the biology and NGSS banks (`col-high-school-biology` and `col-high-school-biology-ngss`) route here. The two collection IDs share this syllabus; questions are wired under the NGSS track id.
- Distractors target documented misconceptions (for example, "evolution acts on individuals," "energy is recycled," "mitosis halves chromosome number") so wrong answers teach.
- Calculations stay accessible: Punnett-square probabilities, the 10% energy rule, surface-area-to-volume reasoning, and reading codon tables, rather than AP-level quantitative modeling.
- Scope deliberately excludes AP-only depth (detailed metabolic intermediates, full signal-transduction cascades, advanced population-genetics math); those belong to the AP Biology track.

## Chapter 1: Biology Practices and the Chemistry of Life
- **Core questions**: What makes a claim scientific? How do water's properties and the four macromolecule classes support life? How do enzymes change reaction rates?
- **Key concepts**: Claim-evidence-reasoning, independent/dependent/controlled variables, polarity and hydrogen bonding, pH, carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids, enzymes and activation energy.
- **Applied skills**: Identify controls and variables in an experiment; interpret an enzyme-rate graph; explain how molecular structure determines biological function.
- **Common traps**: Confusing a control group with a constant; assuming enzymes are consumed as products; thinking denaturation breaks covalent bonds in the backbone rather than changing shape.

## Chapter 2: Cells, Membranes, and Transport
- **Core questions**: How are prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells organized? How does the membrane control what enters and leaves? Why does cell size matter?
- **Key concepts**: Organelle structure and function, the fluid-mosaic membrane, simple and facilitated diffusion, osmosis, active transport, surface-area-to-volume ratio, homeostasis.
- **Applied skills**: Predict water movement in hypertonic, hypotonic, and isotonic solutions; match organelles to functions; reason from diagrams of transport proteins and gradients.
- **Common traps**: Believing facilitated diffusion requires ATP; thinking water moves toward lower solute concentration; assuming larger cells are always more efficient.

## Chapter 3: Energy in Living Systems
- **Core questions**: How do photosynthesis and cellular respiration move matter and energy? How are the two processes linked? What limits each one?
- **Key concepts**: ATP as energy currency, photosynthesis (light reactions and Calvin cycle, in outline), aerobic respiration, fermentation, energy transfer and matter cycling, limits of simplified equations.
- **Applied skills**: Trace carbon and energy through photosynthesis and respiration; read graphs from light, temperature, and CO2 experiments; compare aerobic and anaerobic pathways.
- **Common traps**: Saying plants only photosynthesize and never respire; thinking energy is recycled like matter; confusing reactants and products of the two processes.

## Chapter 4: DNA, Cell Division, and Gene Expression
- **Core questions**: How is genetic information stored, copied, and used to build proteins? How do mitosis and meiosis differ in purpose and outcome?
- **Key concepts**: DNA structure and base pairing, semiconservative replication, mitosis, meiosis, the cell cycle, transcription, translation, the genetic code, mutations.
- **Applied skills**: Model meiosis outcomes; transcribe and translate a DNA or mRNA sequence using a codon table; explain how a mutation can change, preserve, or disrupt protein function.
- **Common traps**: Thinking mitosis halves chromosome number; treating every mutation as harmful; confusing transcription with translation or DNA with RNA.

## Chapter 5: Inheritance and Biotechnology
- **Core questions**: How do alleles produce predictable trait patterns? What can a genetic test or DNA technology actually prove?
- **Key concepts**: Mendelian inheritance, Punnett squares, genotype vs phenotype, pedigrees, incomplete dominance, codominance, sex-linkage, polygenic traits, gel electrophoresis, PCR.
- **Applied skills**: Solve monohybrid and simple dihybrid crosses; interpret a pedigree; evaluate the limits of a biotechnology result.
- **Common traps**: Equating dominant with "most common"; confusing genotype ratios with phenotype ratios; assuming a DNA match alone proves a conclusion.

## Chapter 6: Evolution and Biological Diversity
- **Core questions**: How does evolution explain both diversity and shared features? What mechanisms change allele frequencies over generations?
- **Key concepts**: Variation, natural selection, fitness, genetic drift, gene flow, mutation, common ancestry, phylogenetic trees, adaptation, speciation, evidence from fossils, anatomy, and DNA.
- **Applied skills**: Read a phylogenetic tree; distinguish individual change from population change; build a selection argument from variation, inheritance, and differential survival.
- **Common traps**: Saying organisms evolve to "try" to adapt; thinking individuals evolve; confusing homologous with analogous structures; treating "fittest" as strongest.

## Chapter 7: Ecology and Human Impacts
- **Core questions**: How does matter cycle and energy flow through ecosystems? How do populations grow and interact, and how do human actions ripple through a system?
- **Key concepts**: Populations, communities, food webs, trophic levels, the 10% energy rule, carbon and nitrogen cycles, carrying capacity, exponential and logistic growth, biodiversity, succession, human impacts.
- **Applied skills**: Interpret population and energy-pyramid graphs; predict how a change travels through a food web; analyze ecosystem services and tradeoffs.
- **Common traps**: Thinking energy is recycled; assuming energy increases up a food web; ignoring decomposers; confusing carrying capacity with current population size.

## Chapter 8: Biology Investigation Studio
- **Core questions**: What makes biological evidence trustworthy? How do scientists communicate and revise claims?
- **Key concepts**: Experimental design, sample size and replication, data quality, graph selection, uncertainty, peer critique, scientific communication, responsible use of biological data.
- **Applied skills**: Debug a flawed experiment; choose the best graph for a dataset; revise a claim when new evidence appears.
- **Common traps**: Confusing correlation with causation; over-generalizing from a tiny sample; cherry-picking data that fits a prior belief.

## Capstone
Students produce a "living systems explainer" on a biological question they care about. Strong projects connect at least two scales (molecules and cells, cells and organisms, or populations and ecosystems), use original or provided data, and defend a claim with evidence and clearly stated uncertainty. Assessment emphasizes the quality of reasoning and evidence over presentation polish, mirroring NGSS expectations to construct explanations and argue from evidence.
