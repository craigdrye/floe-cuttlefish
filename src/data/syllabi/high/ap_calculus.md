# AP Calculus (AB & BC)
The mathematics of continuous change - limits, derivatives, integrals, and series - built for confident AP exam performance and clear mathematical communication.

**ID**: `col-ap-calculus` · **Discipline**: Mathematics

## Course Aim
AP Calculus is the College Board's flagship secondary-school calculus sequence, taken at ages 16-18 in two flavours. AP Calculus AB is organised into eight units and is roughly a first-semester college calculus course, covering limits and continuity, differentiation and its applications, integration and accumulation of change, and an introduction to differential equations. AP Calculus BC adds two further units to that same foundation - advanced integration techniques together with parametric, polar, and vector-valued functions, and a full treatment of infinite sequences and series. This course prepares students to sit either exam with confidence.

The emphasis throughout is on connecting procedure to meaning. A derivative is not just a rule to apply but an instantaneous rate of change; a definite integral is an accumulation, not merely an antiderivative evaluated at two endpoints. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is the hinge that ties differentiation and integration together, and students should be able to read it in both directions. Because the AP exam awards most of its free-response credit for correct setup, notation, and justification rather than a final number, written communication is treated as part of the mathematics rather than an afterthought.

By the end of the course a student can move fluidly between graphs, tables, symbolic expressions, and worded contexts; evaluate and interpret limits, derivatives, and integrals; set up and solve a separable differential equation; and - for BC - test a series for convergence and build a Taylor or Maclaurin polynomial approximation. The exam is half multiple choice and half free response, with explicit calculator and non-calculator sections, so calculator fluency and disciplined hand computation are both required.

## Course Design Notes
Use this syllabus for AP Calculus AB and BC support. Chapters marked "(BC)" are BC-only extensions; AB students can treat them as enrichment or skip them. Keep the AB core - limits, derivatives, integrals, and accumulation - tightly connected through the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus rather than teaching the differential and integral calculus as separate subjects. Require precise notation (correct use of dy/dx, the definite-integral symbol with limits, the constant of integration, and limit notation), explicit justifications keyed to the relevant theorem, and a clear distinction between what the calculator produces and what must be shown by hand. Worked examples should foreground AP command verbs ("justify", "explain", "estimate", "interpret the meaning of ... in context") and the difference between a numerical answer and a complete argument.

## Chapter 1: Limits and Continuity
- **Core questions**: What value does a function approach near a point, and is that the same as the function's value there? When does a limit fail to exist? What does the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantee?
- **Key concepts**: limits from graphs, tables, and algebra; one-sided limits; limits at infinity and infinite limits; horizontal and vertical asymptotes; the squeeze theorem; continuity at a point and on an interval; removable, jump, and infinite discontinuities; the Intermediate Value Theorem.
- **Applied skills**: estimate a limit numerically and confirm it algebraically; classify a discontinuity from a graph; resolve a 0/0 form by factoring or rationalising; apply the IVT to argue a root exists on an interval.
- **Common traps**: assuming a limit equals the function value when there is a hole or jump; concluding a limit exists when the one-sided limits disagree; mishandling limits at infinity by ignoring the dominant term; using the IVT without first confirming continuity.

## Chapter 2: Derivatives Foundation
- **Core questions**: What does the derivative measure, and how does the limit definition produce it? Which differentiation rule fits a given function? How can a derivative be estimated from a table of values?
- **Key concepts**: average versus instantaneous rate of change; the derivative as the limit of a difference quotient; the tangent line; differentiability versus continuity; power, product, quotient, and chain rules; derivatives of trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic functions; implicit differentiation; higher-order derivatives.
- **Applied skills**: differentiate from first principles for a simple function; choose and apply the product, quotient, or chain rule correctly; differentiate implicitly to find dy/dx; estimate f'(a) from tabulated data using a symmetric difference quotient.
- **Common traps**: treating the derivative of a product as the product of derivatives; forgetting the inner derivative in the chain rule; assuming continuity implies differentiability (corners and cusps); sign slips in the quotient rule.

## Chapter 3: Derivatives Applications
- **Core questions**: What rate is changing in a related-rates situation? How is a 0/0 or infinity/infinity limit resolved? What does the Mean Value Theorem assert?
- **Key concepts**: related rates; linear approximation and differentials; L'Hopital's Rule; the Mean Value Theorem and Rolle's Theorem; motion along a line with position, velocity, and acceleration; interpreting the derivative in applied contexts with correct units.
- **Applied skills**: set up a related-rates problem by differentiating a relationship with respect to time; apply L'Hopital's Rule after confirming an indeterminate form; use velocity and acceleration to describe a particle's motion (speeding up versus slowing down).
- **Common traps**: differentiating before relating the quantities in related rates; applying L'Hopital's Rule to a form that is not indeterminate; confusing speed with velocity; ignoring units when interpreting a rate in context.

## Chapter 4: Curve Sketching
- **Core questions**: Where is a function increasing or decreasing, and where are its extrema? How does the second derivative reveal concavity and inflection points? How is a candidate extremum justified?
- **Key concepts**: critical points; the first derivative test; absolute (global) versus local extrema on closed intervals; the candidates test; concavity; inflection points; the second derivative test; optimisation modelling; relating the graphs of f, f', and f''.
- **Applied skills**: build a sign chart for f' and f'' to describe a graph's shape; justify a maximum or minimum using the first or second derivative test; solve an optimisation problem from a worded constraint; read information about f from the graph of f'.
- **Common traps**: reporting a critical point as an extremum without a justification; forgetting to check endpoints for an absolute extremum; confusing a point where f''=0 with a guaranteed inflection point; mixing up the graphs of f and f'.

## Chapter 5: Integration Foundation
- **Core questions**: What quantity does a definite integral accumulate? How do Riemann sums and the trapezoidal rule approximate it? What do the two parts of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus say?
- **Key concepts**: antiderivatives and the constant of integration; the definite integral as a limit of Riemann sums; left, right, and midpoint sums and the trapezoidal rule; properties of definite integrals; the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Parts 1 and 2; u-substitution; net change.
- **Applied skills**: approximate a definite integral from a table using a Riemann sum or trapezoidal rule and state whether it over- or under-estimates; evaluate an integral by u-substitution; use the FTC to differentiate an accumulation function; interpret a definite integral as net change in context.
- **Common traps**: omitting +C on an indefinite integral; failing to change the limits (or back-substitute) after u-substitution; reversing the order of subtraction in the FTC; confusing total distance with net displacement.

## Chapter 6: Integration Applications
- **Core questions**: Which setup matches the region or solid described? What does an area or volume integral represent? How is the average value of a function found?
- **Key concepts**: area between curves; volumes of solids of revolution by disks and washers; volumes by known cross sections; the average value of a function; accumulation in motion problems; BC additions - integration by parts, partial fractions, and improper integrals.
- **Applied skills**: set up an area integral with the correct top-minus-bottom (or right-minus-left) order; choose disks versus washers and identify the radius functions; compute an average value; (BC) integrate by parts and evaluate a convergent improper integral.
- **Common traps**: subtracting the curves in the wrong order; forgetting to square the radius in a volume integral; omitting the inner radius in the washer method; (BC) assuming an improper integral converges without taking the limit.

## Chapter 7: Differential Equations
- **Core questions**: What behaviour does a slope field reveal about solutions? How is a separable equation solved with an initial condition? What model fits exponential or logistic growth?
- **Key concepts**: differential equations and their solutions; slope fields; separation of variables; particular solutions from initial conditions; exponential growth and decay; the logistic model and its carrying capacity; BC addition - Euler's method for numerical approximation.
- **Applied skills**: sketch or interpret a slope field and match it to an equation; solve a separable differential equation and apply an initial condition; identify the carrying capacity and the value where a logistic population grows fastest; (BC) approximate a solution value using Euler's method.
- **Common traps**: forgetting +C and the initial condition when separating variables; algebra errors when solving for y; confusing the exponential and logistic models; (BC) using the wrong step size or slope in Euler's method.

## Chapter 8: Series (BC)
- **Core questions**: Does a given infinite series converge, and how do we know? What function does a power series represent, and where? How well does a Taylor polynomial approximate a function?
- **Key concepts**: sequences and series; geometric, p-, and harmonic series; the nth-term, integral, comparison, limit comparison, ratio, and alternating series tests; absolute versus conditional convergence; power series, radius and interval of convergence; Taylor and Maclaurin series; the Lagrange error bound.
- **Applied skills**: select an appropriate convergence test and apply it correctly; find the radius and interval of convergence of a power series; construct a Maclaurin polynomial for a standard function; bound the error of a Taylor approximation.
- **Common traps**: concluding convergence from the nth-term test (it can only show divergence); forgetting to check endpoints of the interval of convergence; confusing the ratio-test limit conditions; misapplying the alternating series remainder.

## Chapter 9: Parametric, Polar, Vector (BC)
- **Core questions**: How is a derivative or arc length found for a parametric curve? How is area computed inside a polar curve? How does a vector-valued function describe motion in the plane?
- **Key concepts**: parametric equations and dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt); second derivatives of parametric curves; arc length for parametric curves; polar coordinates and curves; area enclosed by polar curves; vector-valued functions, velocity, acceleration, and speed.
- **Applied skills**: find the slope of a parametric curve at a point; compute the area of a region bounded by a polar curve over an interval; differentiate a vector-valued position function to find velocity and speed; set up an arc-length integral.
- **Common traps**: dividing dy/dt by dx/dt incorrectly or forgetting the chain for the second derivative; using the wrong angular limits in a polar-area integral; confusing speed (the magnitude of velocity) with the velocity vector; dropping the 1/2 factor in the polar-area formula.

## Chapter 10: Synthesis and AP Exam Strategy
- **Core questions**: What must be justified to earn full credit on a free-response question? Which steps belong in the calculator section versus the non-calculator section? How should a conclusion be stated in context with units?
- **Key concepts**: the multiple-choice and free-response structure and timing; allowed calculator operations (graph, root, numerical derivative, numerical integral); justification language tied to theorems; notation conventions; reading the FRQ scoring guidelines; error analysis and targeted review.
- **Applied skills**: decide which work the calculator may do and which must be shown by hand; write a justification that names the theorem and the condition it relies on; interpret the meaning of a derivative or integral value with correct units; review a mark scheme to diagnose lost points.
- **Common traps**: showing only a calculator answer where setup is required; stating "increasing" without citing f' > 0; omitting units or context in an interpretation; mismanaging time across the four free-response parts.

## Capstone
Build an AP Calculus readiness portfolio containing: a limits-and-continuity analysis presenting one function through graph, table, and algebra with a discontinuity classified and the IVT applied; a differentiation application (an optimisation or related-rates problem) with full setup, a justified conclusion, and correct units; an accumulation task that approximates a definite integral from data, evaluates it exactly via the FTC, and interprets the result as net change; a differential-equation model with a slope field, a separable solution from an initial condition, and a contextual prediction; a BC extension (a series convergence argument with a Taylor approximation, or a parametric/polar problem) or, for AB students, an enrichment preview; and a timed free-response correction log that diagnoses each lost point against the AP scoring guidelines and sets a retrieval-practice plan.
