# AP Human Geography
Spatial thinking for a crowded planet: read the maps, name the patterns, and explain the human processes behind them.

**ID**: `apHumanGeography` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
AP Human Geography is often a student's first college-level course, and it rewards a single durable habit: looking at the world spatially. Where are things, why are they there, and how does location shape the rest? Across the year students learn to describe patterns, explain the processes that produce them, and reason about how those patterns change when you zoom from a city block to the whole globe. The course covers the seven College Board units in order: thinking geographically, population and migration, culture, political organization, agriculture and rural land use, cities and urban land use, and industrialization and economic development.

The course is built around evidence, not memorized definitions. Students work constantly with maps, population pyramids, models such as von Thunen and the demographic transition, satellite imagery, and real demographic and economic data. The goal is to make claims that a stimulus can actually support, then explain the chain of reasoning from observed pattern to underlying process. Vocabulary matters, but only because precise terms let students explain spatial relationships clearly.

By the end of the year a student should be able to identify a pattern on an unfamiliar map, propose the process that created it, apply the right model, choose an example that proves the point, and write it all up in the tight, command-term-driven prose the AP free-response section demands. Those skills transfer well beyond the exam: reading the news about migration, elections, supply chains, or urban change becomes a spatial-reasoning exercise rather than a stream of disconnected facts.

## Course Design Notes
- Follow the College Board's seven-unit sequence, but spiral five core skills every week: concept application, spatial-relationship analysis, data-source and visual analysis, scale analysis, and argumentation.
- Treat scale as a recurring lens, not a single lesson. Re-ask "does this pattern hold at the local, regional, national, and global scale?" in every unit.
- Anchor each unit to the models students must apply: demographic and epidemiologic transition, Ravenstein, gravity model, von Thunen, central place theory, the urban models (concentric zone, sector, multiple nuclei, galactic, Latin American), Weber, Rostow, and Wallerstein.
- Practice the AP free-response command terms explicitly (identify, define, describe, explain, compare). The most common point loss is answering a lower-order verb when the prompt asked for explanation.
- Use a running map-and-model notebook so students accumulate worked examples, annotated stimuli, and self-corrected FRQ responses across the year.
- Reserve the final unit as an exam studio: timed stimulus sets and the three FRQ types (no-stimulus, one-stimulus, two-stimulus) with command-term scoring.

## Chapter 1: Thinking Geographically
Students build the spatial vocabulary and reasoning that the rest of the course depends on. They learn to read different map types, distinguish absolute from relative location, and think about how the meaning of a pattern shifts with scale.

**Core questions**
- What pattern appears on this map, and what data would let me describe it precisely?
- How does the interpretation change if I zoom from local to global scale?
- Which map type or geographic technology best fits this question?

**Key concepts**: absolute vs. relative location, place, sense of place, region (formal, functional, perceptual), scale of analysis, spatial patterns (clustered, dispersed, linear), distance decay, friction of distance, time-space compression, map projections and their distortions, choropleth, dot-density, graduated symbol, cartogram and isoline maps, GIS, GPS, remote sensing, and census data.

**Applied skills**: read and classify an unfamiliar map; describe a spatial pattern with directional and distributional language; explain how scale changes the conclusion; match a geographic technology (GIS, GPS, remote sensing) to a real task.

**Common traps**: confusing absolute and relative location; assuming a pattern visible at one scale holds at every scale; describing what a map shows without explaining the process behind it; forgetting that every projection distorts something (area, shape, distance, or direction).

## Chapter 2: Population and Migration
Students study where people live, how populations grow and age, and why people move. They work heavily with population pyramids and the demographic and epidemiologic transition models.

**Core questions**
- What does this population pyramid predict about future needs and dependency?
- Which stage of the demographic transition fits this country, and what follows next?
- Which push and pull factors dominate this migration stream, and at what scale?

**Key concepts**: arithmetic, physiological, and agricultural density; population distribution; crude birth and death rates, total fertility rate, rate of natural increase, doubling time; population pyramids and dependency ratio; demographic transition model; epidemiologic transition; Malthusian theory and neo-Malthusian/critiques; push and pull factors; Ravenstein's laws; gravity model; forced vs. voluntary migration; refugees, asylum seekers, and internally displaced persons; intervening obstacles and opportunities; remittances.

**Applied skills**: interpret a population pyramid for growth, aging, and policy implications; place a country in the demographic transition and justify it; classify a migration stream by type and dominant factor; calculate and compare density measures.

**Common traps**: confusing arithmetic, physiological, and agricultural density; reading a pyramid's shape backwards (wide base means growth, not decline); treating the demographic transition as a guaranteed timeline; mixing up push factors (origin) with pull factors (destination).

## Chapter 3: Cultural Patterns and Processes
Students examine how culture is expressed in landscapes and how cultural traits spread. Diffusion types and the cultural landscape are the central tools.

**Core questions**
- How did this cultural trait diffuse, and which type of diffusion explains the pattern?
- How does belief or language shape the visible landscape?
- What happens to cultural identity when the scale of analysis changes?

**Key concepts**: cultural landscape, sequent occupance, placemaking, cultural relativism and ethnocentrism; folk vs. popular culture; centripetal and centrifugal cultural forces; language families, dialects, lingua franca, pidgin, creole, isogloss, toponyms; universalizing vs. ethnic religions and their distribution; relocation diffusion; expansion diffusion (contagious, hierarchical, stimulus); acculturation, assimilation, and syncretism.

**Applied skills**: classify an example of diffusion by type; read a cultural landscape for evidence of religion, language, or ethnicity; distinguish folk from popular culture and explain the spatial consequences of each; explain how toponyms reveal settlement history.

**Common traps**: confusing the four types of expansion diffusion (especially contagious vs. hierarchical); mislabeling relocation diffusion as expansion; treating "culture" as fixed rather than changing through acculturation and assimilation; conflating ethnicity, race, and nationality.

## Chapter 4: Political Patterns and Processes
Students study how political power is organized over space: states, nations, boundaries, and the forces that pull countries together or apart.

**Core questions**
- What boundary type is shown, and what conflicts does it invite?
- Is this force centripetal or centrifugal, and how does it affect state stability?
- How does redistricting change representation without changing votes?

**Key concepts**: state, nation, nation-state, stateless nation, multinational and multistate states; sovereignty, self-determination, autonomous and semi-autonomous regions; boundary types (antecedent, subsequent, superimposed, relict, geometric, consequent/ethnographic); definitional, locational, operational, and allocational boundary disputes; centripetal and centrifugal forces; devolution; supranationalism (EU, UN); unitary vs. federal systems; gerrymandering, reapportionment, and redistricting; territoriality, neocolonialism, and shatterbelts.

**Applied skills**: classify a boundary by origin and by dispute type; evaluate whether a force unifies or fragments a state; explain how a redistricting map dilutes or concentrates a group's voting power; distinguish a nation from a state with a concrete example.

**Common traps**: using "nation" and "state" interchangeably; confusing antecedent and subsequent boundaries; assuming devolution and supranationalism are opposites rather than simultaneous trends; treating gerrymandering as illegal-by-definition rather than as a districting pattern.

## Chapter 5: Agriculture and Rural Land Use
Students connect farming systems to land-use patterns, using von Thunen's model and the agricultural revolutions to explain why crops and livestock locate where they do.

**Core questions**
- What land-use pattern does von Thunen predict, and why does distance to market drive it?
- Which agricultural revolution produced this change, and what tradeoff came with it?
- Is this system subsistence or commercial, intensive or extensive?

**Key concepts**: First (Neolithic), Second, and Green Revolutions; subsistence vs. commercial agriculture; intensive vs. extensive farming; von Thunen model and the bid-rent/distance-to-market logic; agribusiness, monoculture, and commodity chains; the agricultural hearths; clustered, dispersed, and linear rural settlement; metes and bounds, township and range, and long-lot survey systems; food security, food deserts, and sustainability; GMOs, organic farming, and the consequences of the Green Revolution.

**Applied skills**: apply von Thunen's rings to a hypothetical or real region and justify the ordering; classify a farm system on the subsistence/commercial and intensive/extensive axes; identify a survey/settlement pattern from a landscape; weigh the costs and benefits of an agricultural change.

**Common traps**: getting von Thunen's rings out of order or ignoring its perishability/transport-cost assumptions; confusing intensive (high inputs per area) with extensive (low inputs over large area); assuming the Green Revolution had only benefits; mixing up the three survey systems.

## Chapter 6: Cities and Urban Land Use
Students analyze why cities grow where they do and how land use is organized within them, applying central place theory and the major urban models.

**Core questions**
- Why did this city grow at this site and situation?
- Which urban model best fits the internal pattern, and what does it miss?
- Who gains and who loses when a neighborhood is redeveloped?

**Key concepts**: site vs. situation; urbanization, suburbanization, counter-urbanization, and reurbanization; rank-size rule, primate city, and the urban hierarchy; central place theory (threshold, range, market area); concentric zone, sector, multiple nuclei, galactic/peripheral, and Latin American (Griffin-Ford) models; megacities and metacities; world/global cities; the bid-rent curve; gentrification, redlining, blockbusting, urban sprawl, edge cities, and the urban-growth boundary; gravity model applied to cities.

**Applied skills**: distinguish site from situation in an explanation of a city's growth; match an internal land-use pattern to the correct urban model; apply central place theory to predict how many of a service a region supports; analyze the winners and losers of gentrification or sprawl.

**Common traps**: confusing site and situation; forcing a North American urban model onto a city it was not built for; mixing up the rank-size rule with the primate-city pattern; treating gentrification as purely positive or purely negative rather than a tradeoff.

## Chapter 7: Industrialization and Economic Development
Students study how economies industrialize, where production locates, and how development is measured and theorized, including its environmental consequences.

**Core questions**
- Where should this firm locate, and which Weberian cost (transport, labor, agglomeration) decides it?
- Which development indicator best captures what this question is really asking?
- How does this economic activity alter the environment, and at what scale?

**Key concepts**: Industrial Revolution and its diffusion; primary, secondary, tertiary, quaternary, and quinary sectors; Weber's least-cost theory (transport, labor, agglomeration; bulk-reducing vs. bulk-gaining); break-of-bulk points; Fordism vs. post-Fordism and just-in-time; development indicators (GDP/GNI per capita, GII, HDI); Rostow's stages of growth; Wallerstein's world-systems theory (core, periphery, semi-periphery); dependency theory; comparative advantage, outsourcing, offshoring, and the new international division of labor; special economic zones, export-processing zones, and maquiladoras; sustainable development and ecotourism.

**Applied skills**: apply Weber's model to a bulk-reducing vs. bulk-gaining firm; choose and defend the most appropriate development indicator for a claim; compare Rostow's modernization view with Wallerstein's structural view; trace the environmental consequences of an economic activity across scales.

**Common traps**: confusing bulk-reducing (locate near raw materials) with bulk-gaining (locate near market) logic; using GDP alone to claim "development" while ignoring distribution and well-being; treating Rostow and Wallerstein as compatible rather than competing theories; mislabeling economic sectors.

## Chapter 8: AP Exam Studio
A skills-and-strategy capstone unit. Students consolidate the seven content units while drilling the specific moves the AP exam rewards: precise stimulus reading, model application under time pressure, and command-term-accurate writing.

**Core questions**
- What pattern must I identify in this stimulus before I can explain it?
- Which command term is the prompt actually using, and what does a full-credit response to it look like?
- Which specific real-world example proves my point?

**Key concepts**: stimulus interpretation (maps, charts, graphs, infographics, images, landscapes); the three FRQ types (no stimulus, one stimulus, two stimuli); command terms (identify, define, describe, explain, compare); explanation chains from pattern to process; using scale and specific examples as evidence; pacing the 60-question / 60-minute MCQ section and the 75-minute three-FRQ section.

**Applied skills**: extract a defensible claim from an unfamiliar stimulus; write a full-credit FRQ point for each command term; self-score responses against a rubric and revise; manage exam time across both sections.

**Common traps**: answering a higher-order verb with a lower-order response (describing when asked to explain, or vice versa); restating the prompt instead of advancing an explanation; giving a vague example ("a country in Africa") instead of a specific one; spending FRQ words on definitions the prompt did not request.

## Capstone
Produce a geographic case study of a single place or process: a city, a country, a region, or a migration corridor. The study must combine maps with demographic, cultural or political, and economic-development evidence, and it must culminate in a written explanation of the spatial patterns at more than one scale.

The capstone must include:
- A clear, spatially framed question that real evidence can answer.
- At least two map types or geographic visualizations (for example a choropleth plus a population pyramid).
- Demographic evidence interpreted with course models (density, transition stage, or migration type).
- Cultural, political, or both contexts that explain centripetal or centrifugal forces at work.
- An economic-development analysis using at least two indicators and one relevant model (Weber, Rostow, or Wallerstein).
- A written explanation that names the pattern, explains the process, and shows how the conclusion shifts with scale.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly retrieval quizzes on vocabulary, models, and command terms.
- AP-style multiple-choice sets, roughly a third built on stimulus material (maps, charts, images, landscapes).
- Timed FRQs in all three formats, scored on command-term accuracy and specific-example evidence.
- Map-and-model labs graded on pattern-to-process explanation rather than recall.
- The capstone, graded on spatial reasoning, scale analysis, model application, and data use.
- A cumulative mock AP exam with reflection and a targeted review plan.
