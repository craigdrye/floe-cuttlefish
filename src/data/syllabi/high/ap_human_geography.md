# AP Human Geography
**ID**: `apHumanGeography` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: High School (Years 9–10)

## Course Philosophy
AP Human Geography introduces students to the systematic study of patterns and processes that have shaped human understanding, use, and alteration of Earth's surface. It blends sociology, economics, and geography to understand spatial relationships.

## Module 1: Thinking Geographically and Population (25%)
- Spatial concepts: Scale, space, place, pattern, and regionalization
- Maps and Data: Projections, GIS (Geographic Information Systems), GPS, and remote sensing
- Population Geography: Population density, physiological/agricultural density, and population pyramids
- The Demographic Transition Model (DTM) and Epidemiologic Transition Model
- Malthusian theory and Neo-Malthusians
- Migration: Push/pull factors, forced vs voluntary migration, refugees, and internally displaced persons
- **Questions**: Analyzing a population pyramid to predict future economic challenges (e.g., an aging population in Japan), identifying push factors in a historical migration wave

## Module 2: Cultural Patterns and Processes (20%)
- The concept of culture: Material vs non-material culture, cultural traits, and cultural relativism
- Cultural Diffusion: Relocation, expansion, hierarchical, contagious, and stimulus diffusion
- Language: Language families (e.g., Indo-European), dialects, lingua francas, and language extinction
- Religion: Universalizing religions (Christianity, Islam, Buddhism) vs Ethnic religions (Hinduism, Judaism)
- The cultural landscape: How human belief systems shape the physical environment
- **Questions**: Tracing the hierarchical diffusion of a cultural phenomenon, analyzing the impact of a lingua franca on global business

## Module 3: Political Organization of Space (15%)
- The State: Nations, states, nation-states, stateless nations, and multinational states
- Boundaries: Superimposed, antecedent, subsequent, and relic boundaries; border disputes
- Geopolitics: Shatterbelts, chokepoints, and the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS)
- Internal political boundaries: Gerrymandering and redistricting
- Supranationalism (UN, EU, NATO) vs Devolution (forces that divide states, such as ethnic separatism)
- **Questions**: Evaluating the impact of superimposed boundaries in post-colonial Africa, explaining how gerrymandering affects political representation

## Module 4: Agriculture and Rural Land Use (20%)
- The Origins of Agriculture: The First (Neolithic), Second (Industrial), and Third (Green) Agricultural Revolutions
- Agricultural practices: Subsistence vs commercial, intensive vs extensive, shifting cultivation, pastoral nomadism, plantation farming
- Von Thünen's Model of agricultural land use (spatial arrangement based on transportation costs)
- Modern agricultural challenges: Agribusiness, GMOs, desertification, and sustainability
- **Questions**: Applying von Thünen's model to a modern regional economy, evaluating the environmental tradeoffs of the Green Revolution

## Module 5: Urbanization and Industrialization (20%)
- Urban Development: Site and situation, megacities, edge cities, and gentrification
- Urban Models: Burgess Concentric Zone, Hoyt Sector, and Multiple Nuclei models
- Central Place Theory (Christaller): Threshold and range
- Industrialization: The Industrial Revolution and Weber's Least Cost Theory (bulk-reducing vs bulk-gaining industries)
- Economic Development: Rostow's Stages of Economic Growth, Wallerstein's World Systems Theory (Core, Periphery, Semi-Periphery)
- Deindustrialization, the rise of the service sector, and outsourcing
- **Questions**: Applying Weber's Least Cost Theory to locate a new manufacturing plant, analyzing the socioeconomic effects of gentrification in a major city

## Stretch Zone
- Advanced spatial analysis using QGIS software
- Deep dives into specific geopolitical shatterbelts (e.g., the Caucasus, the Balkans)
