# AP Human Geography
**ID**: `apHumanGeography` · **Discipline**: Humanities · **Year**: High School (Years 9-10)

## Course Aim
Prepare students for AP Human Geography by teaching spatial thinking, map and data analysis, population, culture, politics, agriculture, cities, development, industry, and environmental change.

## Course Design Notes
AP Human Geography is often a student's first AP course, so this syllabus pairs college-level spatial reasoning with explicit study and writing routines. Students learn to explain patterns, processes, scale, place, and human-environment interaction using maps, models, and evidence.

## Chapter 1: Thinking Geographically
- **Key concepts**: location, place, scale, region, spatial pattern, diffusion, GIS, GPS, remote sensing, map projection.
- **Practice questions**: What pattern appears on the map? What scale changes the interpretation? What data would improve the claim?
- **Student output**: a map-analysis notebook with scale, pattern, and process notes.

## Chapter 2: Population and Migration
- **Key concepts**: density, distribution, population pyramids, demographic transition, epidemiologic transition, Malthus, push-pull migration, refugees.
- **Practice questions**: What does the pyramid predict? Which migration factor dominates? How does population structure shape policy?
- **Student output**: a population and migration case study using graphs and maps.

## Chapter 3: Cultural Patterns and Processes
- **Key concepts**: culture, language, religion, diffusion, cultural landscape, identity, placemaking, cultural ecology.
- **Practice questions**: How did this trait diffuse? How does belief shape landscape? What happens when scale changes cultural identity?
- **Student output**: a cultural landscape field observation or image analysis.

## Chapter 4: Political Geography
- **Key concepts**: state, nation, nation-state, stateless nation, boundaries, geopolitics, gerrymandering, devolution, supranationalism.
- **Practice questions**: What boundary type is present? What force promotes unity or division? How does representation change with redistricting?
- **Student output**: a political map analysis with boundary, scale, and power claims.

## Chapter 5: Agriculture and Rural Land Use
- **Key concepts**: agricultural revolutions, subsistence and commercial farming, von Thunen, agribusiness, food security, sustainability.
- **Practice questions**: What land-use pattern appears? How does distance affect production? What tradeoff comes with agricultural change?
- **Student output**: an agricultural land-use model applied to a real or fictional region.

## Chapter 6: Cities and Urban Land Use
- **Key concepts**: site, situation, urbanization, megacities, suburbanization, gentrification, urban models, central place theory.
- **Practice questions**: Why did the city grow here? Which model fits the pattern? Who benefits or loses from redevelopment?
- **Student output**: an urban model comparison using maps or local examples.

## Chapter 7: Industry, Development, and Human-Environment Interaction
- **Key concepts**: industrialization, Weber, economic sectors, development indicators, world-systems theory, outsourcing, sustainability, climate vulnerability.
- **Practice questions**: Where should production locate? What development indicator is most useful? How does economic activity alter environments?
- **Student output**: a development and industry briefing with at least two indicators and one map.

## Chapter 8: AP Human Geography Exam Studio
- **Key concepts**: stimulus interpretation, map evidence, model application, FRQ command terms, scale, explanation chains.
- **Practice questions**: What pattern must be identified? What process explains it? Which example proves the point?
- **Student output**: a timed FRQ set with command-term corrections.

## Capstone
Create a geographic case study of a city, country, region, or migration corridor. Include maps, demographic evidence, cultural or political context, economic development analysis, and a written explanation of spatial patterns.

## Assessment Ideas
- Map tasks graded on pattern-process explanation.
- FRQs graded on command-term accuracy and evidence.
- Capstone graded on spatial thinking, scale, and data use.
