# 10th Grade Reading and Vocab
**ID**: `col-10th-grade-reading-and-vocab` · **Discipline**: English · **Year**: 11 (ages 15–16)
**Frameworks**: US Common Core ELA Grade 9-10 (Advanced)

## Course Philosophy
In 10th grade, students refine the analytical skills introduced in 9th grade. The focus sharpens on analyzing the author's rhetorical choices, evaluating the validity of complex arguments, and synthesizing information from diverse historical and literary texts. Vocabulary study is geared towards advanced academic and literary terminology.

## Module 1: Advanced Literary Analysis (25%)
- Analyze how an author draws on and transforms source material in a specific work (e.g., how Shakespeare treats a theme or topic from Ovid or the Bible or how a later author draws on a play by Shakespeare)
- Determine the meaning of words and phrases as they are used in the text, including figurative and connotative meanings; analyze the cumulative impact of specific word choices on meaning and tone
- Analyze how an author's choices concerning how to structure a text, order events within it, and manipulate time create such effects as mystery, tension, or surprise
- Analyze how complex characters (e.g., those with multiple or conflicting motivations) develop over the course of a text, interact with other characters, and advance the plot or develop the theme
- **Questions**: Comparing source material to modern adaptations, deep structural analysis of nonlinear narratives, tracking conflicting character motivations

## Module 2: Rhetoric and Argumentation in Informational Text (25%)
- Determine an author's point of view or purpose in a text and analyze how an author uses rhetoric to advance that point of view or purpose (analyzing ethos, pathos, logos, and rhetorical devices like anaphora, chiasmus, polysyndeton)
- Delineate and evaluate the argument and specific claims in a text, assessing whether the reasoning is valid and the evidence is relevant and sufficient; identify false statements and fallacious reasoning (e.g., straw man, ad hominem, slippery slope)
- Analyze seminal U.S. documents of historical and literary significance, including how they address related themes and concepts
- Analyze in detail how an author's ideas or claims are developed and refined by particular sentences, paragraphs, or larger portions of a text
- **Questions**: Identifying specific rhetorical devices and explaining their effect, dissecting logical fallacies in political speeches, comparing rhetorical strategies across historical eras

## Module 3: Vocabulary and Nuance (25%)
- Use context as a clue to the meaning of a word or phrase, including nuanced academic and domain-specific words
- Identify and correctly use patterns of word changes that indicate different meanings or parts of speech (morphology and advanced etymology)
- Interpret figures of speech (e.g., euphemism, oxymoron, paradox) in context and analyze their role in the text
- Analyze nuances in the meaning of words with similar denotations (e.g., pedantic vs meticulous)
- **Questions**: Analyzing paradoxical statements, distinguishing highly nuanced synonyms, advanced Greek/Latin root application

## Module 4: Synthesis and Language Conventions (25%)
- Integrate and evaluate multiple sources of information presented in different media or formats (e.g., visually, quantitatively) as well as in words in order to address a question or solve a problem
- Use parallel structure effectively for rhetorical impact
- Use various types of phrases and clauses to convey specific meanings and add variety and interest to writing
- Use a semicolon (and perhaps a conjunctive adverb) to link two or more closely related independent clauses
- Use a colon to introduce a list or quotation
- **Questions**: Synthesizing data from a graph and a text to support a claim, editing for advanced parallel structure, precise semicolon/colon placement

## Stretch Zone
- Analyze the impact of unreliable narrators in modernist literature
- Deconstruct the rhetoric of supreme court dissenting opinions
