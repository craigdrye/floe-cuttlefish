# 10th Grade Reading and Vocab
Read closely, argue from evidence, and build the vocabulary muscle that carries you into AP English, the SAT, and college reading.

**ID**: `col-10th-grade-reading-and-vocab` · **Discipline**: English

## Course Aim
This course strengthens the analytical reading, vocabulary, rhetoric, and evidence skills expected of a strong tenth grader. Building on the foundations of ninth-grade English, it moves students from understanding what a text says to analyzing how and why it works: how a writer develops a theme across a whole text, how a complex character's conflicting motivations drive a plot, how word choice shapes tone, and how an argument either earns or fails to earn the reader's assent. The aim is independent, evidence-anchored reading of literature, poetry, argument, and informational sources.

A second, equal aim is vocabulary that scales. Tenth graders meet a steep rise in academic and technical vocabulary, and roughly three-quarters of those words are built from a relatively small set of Greek and Latin roots and affixes. By teaching morphology, context clues, and the difference between denotation and connotation, the course turns vocabulary from rote memorization into a tool students can use to decode unfamiliar words on sight and choose precise words in their own writing.

Throughout, the course is deliberately a bridge. It aligns with Common Core grades 9-10 reading, language, and writing expectations and explicitly prepares students for AP English Language and Literature, the SAT Reading and Writing section, and the kind of source synthesis and citation discipline that college work demands. Every chapter pairs reading analysis with concrete vocabulary and evidence practice so the two strands reinforce each other.

## Course Design Notes
The course is organized into eight chapters that progress from close-reading habits through literary, poetic, rhetorical, and informational analysis, into vocabulary and morphology, multi-source synthesis, and finally a timed exam-readiness studio. Each chapter is skills-first: students are expected to produce evidence, not just recognize correct answers. Questions are written to reward the student who can name the device or standard at work, locate the supporting line, and explain the inference, rather than the student who simply picks the most familiar-sounding option.

Distractors are built around the genuine errors tenth graders make: confusing plot summary with theme, mistaking tone for mood, treating connotation as denotation, calling pathos a fallacy, or accepting an argument because it agrees with them. Where exam alignment matters (SAT-style evidence questions, AP-style rhetorical analysis), prompts mirror the question stems students will see. The capstone consolidates the year into a portfolio that demonstrates analysis, synthesis, vocabulary precision, and revision.

## Chapter 1: Close Reading Habits
- **Core questions**: What does the text state explicitly versus what must I infer? Which specific words or details support a given inference? How do I cite strong and thorough textual evidence?
- **Key concepts**: annotation, explicit statement vs. inference, textual evidence, theme vs. topic vs. summary, tone, connotation, rereading, text-dependent questions.
- **Applied skills**: annotate a passage, distinguish a defensible inference from a guess, quote and frame evidence to support a claim, write an objective summary that omits opinion.
- **Common traps**: treating a personal reaction as textual evidence; confusing the topic of a passage with its theme; summarizing plot instead of stating a central idea; over-reading details the text does not support.

## Chapter 2: Literary Analysis and Character
- **Core questions**: How does a complex character with conflicting motivations change over a text? How do conflict, setting, and plot structure develop the theme? How is a theme shaped and refined by specific details?
- **Key concepts**: complex vs. flat character, motivation, internal and external conflict, plot structure (exposition, rising action, climax, resolution), setting, symbolism, theme development, source/adaptation.
- **Applied skills**: trace a character's change with cited evidence, explain how a structural choice (flashback, parallel plot) builds tension, write an analytical paragraph with embedded quotation.
- **Common traps**: calling a static character "dynamic"; confusing the protagonist's goal with the work's theme; mistaking what happens (plot) for what it means (theme); using a quotation without explaining its function.

## Chapter 3: Poetry, Figurative Language, and Sound
- **Core questions**: Which image or figure carries the poem's meaning? How does a line break, stanza, or sound device shape emphasis and tone? Who is the speaker, and how do they differ from the poet?
- **Key concepts**: metaphor, simile, personification, paradox, oxymoron, imagery, symbol, rhythm/meter, line break and enjambment, stanza, speaker, tone.
- **Applied skills**: identify and interpret a controlling image, explain the effect of an enjambed line, distinguish speaker from poet, write a short explication tying device to meaning.
- **Common traps**: labeling any comparison a metaphor when it is a simile; treating figurative language as decoration rather than meaning; equating the speaker with the author; describing rhyme without explaining its effect.

## Chapter 4: Rhetoric and Argument
- **Core questions**: Is the reasoning valid and the evidence sufficient? How do ethos, pathos, and logos advance the writer's purpose for a specific audience? Where does the argument fall into a fallacy, and does it answer its counterclaim?
- **Key concepts**: claim, evidence, reasoning/warrant, ethos, pathos, logos, audience, purpose, counterclaim and rebuttal, common fallacies (ad hominem, straw man, hasty generalization, slippery slope, false dilemma, circular reasoning).
- **Applied skills**: map an argument into claim/evidence/reasoning, label rhetorical appeals with justification, name a fallacy and explain why it is one, evaluate whether evidence is relevant and sufficient.
- **Common traps**: calling an emotional appeal (pathos) a fallacy by default; confusing ethos (credibility) with logos (logic); accepting an argument because you agree with its conclusion; mistaking a rebuttal of a weak version of the opposing view (straw man) for a real refutation.

## Chapter 5: Informational Text and Historical Documents
- **Core questions**: How does the author build and develop a central idea across paragraphs? What context and purpose shape a seminal U.S. document or informational source? Which sentence refines or qualifies the central claim?
- **Key concepts**: central idea, text structure (cause/effect, problem/solution, compare/contrast, chronological), point of view, author's purpose, seminal documents and historical context, claim development.
- **Applied skills**: track how an idea is introduced and developed, identify the function of a paragraph or transition, situate a document in its historical context, distinguish fact from interpretation.
- **Common traps**: reading the first sentence as automatically the main idea; ignoring how purpose colors point of view; treating a supporting detail as the central claim; missing qualifying or hedging language that limits a claim.

## Chapter 6: Vocabulary, Roots, and Nuance
- **Core questions**: What context clue defines an unknown word? How does a Greek or Latin root, prefix, or suffix shift meaning? Which near-synonym is most precise for this sentence, given connotation?
- **Key concepts**: context clues (definition, contrast, example, inference), Greek and Latin roots, prefixes and suffixes, morphology, denotation vs. connotation, word families, academic and domain-specific vocabulary, register.
- **Applied skills**: infer meaning from context, decompose a word into morphemes to predict meaning, choose the most precise synonym, distinguish positive from negative connotation.
- **Common traps**: choosing a synonym that matches denotation but clashes in connotation; misreading a contrast clue as a definition clue; assuming a familiar root always means the same thing; treating all synonyms as interchangeable.

## Chapter 7: Synthesis Across Sources and Media
- **Core questions**: What does a chart, graph, or image add that the prose does not? Which of two sources is more credible, and why? How do two texts answer the same question differently?
- **Key concepts**: source reliability and bias, quantitative and visual evidence, multimedia analysis, comparison and synthesis, attribution and citation, patchwriting and plagiarism.
- **Applied skills**: read and integrate a data display into an argument, evaluate source credibility, synthesize two or more sources into one claim, cite and paraphrase without patchwriting.
- **Common traps**: treating a vivid chart as proof regardless of what it measures; judging credibility by tone rather than evidence and authorship; blending sources without attribution; paraphrasing so closely that it becomes patchwriting.

## Chapter 8: Reading Studio and Exam Readiness
- **Core questions**: Which question type causes the most misses, and why? What evidence did I overlook? Which reading habit should change to raise accuracy under time pressure?
- **Key concepts**: reading stamina, timed reading strategy, SAT/AP question stems (main idea, inference, evidence, vocabulary-in-context, function), evidence-pairing questions, error pattern analysis.
- **Applied skills**: work a timed passage set, match a "best evidence" answer to its claim, decode vocabulary-in-context items, log and categorize errors to target review.
- **Common traps**: choosing an answer that is true but does not answer the question asked; picking the most extreme option; ignoring line references in evidence questions; running out of time by over-annotating early passages.

## Capstone
Build a tenth-grade reading portfolio that demonstrates the full arc of the course: (1) one literary analysis paragraph tracing a complex character's development with embedded, cited evidence; (2) one rhetorical analysis identifying claim, appeals, and at least one fallacy or counterclaim in a real argument; (3) one poem explication connecting a figurative or sound device to meaning; (4) a vocabulary notebook sample showing roots, connotation notes, and original sentences; (5) one synthesis paragraph drawing on at least two sources including one visual or quantitative source, properly attributed; and (6) a timed-reading error log that names recurring mistakes and the habit change to fix them. The portfolio is assessed on accuracy of analysis, strength and integration of evidence, precision of vocabulary, and quality of revision.
