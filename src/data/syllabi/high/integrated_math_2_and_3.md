# Integrated Math 2 & 3
**Covers**: `col-integrated-math-2`, `col-integrated-math-3`
**Discipline**: Maths | **Year**: 11 (ages 15-16)

## Course Aim
Build a connected Integrated Math 2 and 3 pathway where students extend algebraic fluency, deepen function analysis, reason geometrically, and use probability and statistics to make evidence-based claims. The course prepares students for advanced mathematics by asking them to choose representations, justify methods, and explain how models behave beyond a single answer.

## Course Design Notes
Use Common Core high school mathematics standards and public integrated pathway guides as the organizing frame. Route questions here when they involve quadratics, polynomials, rational or radical expressions, advanced functions, coordinate geometry, similarity, right-triangle trigonometry, circles, conics, probability, statistics, or exponential and logarithmic models. Maintain assessment clarity: students should know whether a task is asking them to solve, prove, model, compare representations, or interpret a result.

## Chapter 1: Quadratic Functions and Complex Solutions
- **Key concepts**: Quadratic functions, parabolas, vertex, axis of symmetry, intercepts, standard form, factored form, vertex form, factoring, completing the square, quadratic formula, discriminant, and complex numbers as solutions.
- **Practice questions**: Graph quadratics; convert among forms; solve by factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula; interpret zeros, vertex, and discriminant in context.
- **Student output**: A quadratic model brief that connects equation form, graph features, solution method, and a contextual interpretation such as area, motion, revenue, or design.

## Chapter 2: Polynomials, Rational Expressions, and Algebraic Structure
- **Key concepts**: Polynomial operations, factoring patterns, zeros, multiplicity, end behavior, polynomial division, Remainder Theorem, rational expressions, rational equations, radical expressions, rational exponents, and extraneous solutions.
- **Practice questions**: Add, multiply, factor, and divide polynomials; use factors and zeros to sketch behavior; simplify and solve rational or radical equations while checking restrictions.
- **Student output**: An algebraic-structure portfolio showing how rewriting an expression reveals zeros, restrictions, end behavior, or solution pathways.

## Chapter 3: Function Analysis, Composition, and Inverses
- **Key concepts**: Function families, transformations, domain and range, composition, inverse functions, piecewise functions, absolute value functions, rational functions, asymptotes, and rate of change.
- **Practice questions**: Analyze transformed functions; find and verify inverses; compose functions in symbolic and contextual settings; identify domain restrictions and asymptotes.
- **Student output**: A function comparison sheet that analyzes two function families across equations, graphs, tables, domains, and contextual uses.

## Chapter 4: Coordinate Geometry, Proof, and Conics
- **Key concepts**: Distance, midpoint, slope, parallel and perpendicular lines, coordinate proofs, equations of circles, completing the square, parabolas as focus-directrix sets, and selected conics where appropriate.
- **Practice questions**: Prove geometric claims with coordinates; classify figures from coordinate data; find circle centers and radii; connect algebraic equations to geometric features.
- **Student output**: A coordinate proof or analytic-geometry report that uses algebra to verify a geometric claim about a figure, circle, or conic.

## Chapter 5: Similarity, Right Triangles, and Trigonometry
- **Key concepts**: Dilations, scale factor, similarity transformations, proportional reasoning, triangle similarity criteria, Pythagorean Theorem, special right triangles, sine, cosine, tangent, angle of elevation, angle of depression, and radian measure as appropriate.
- **Practice questions**: Prove triangles similar; solve right triangles; choose trigonometric ratios; model height, distance, slope, navigation, or measurement error.
- **Student output**: A similarity and trigonometry field task with labeled diagram, calculations, assumptions, and interpretation of precision.

## Chapter 6: Circles, Measurement, and Geometric Modeling
- **Key concepts**: Radius, diameter, chords, tangents, secants, arcs, central angles, inscribed angles, cyclic relationships, arc length, sector area, three-dimensional measurement, surface area, volume, and model limitations.
- **Practice questions**: Use circle angle relationships; solve tangent, secant, and chord problems; calculate arc length and sector area; model composite area, volume, or capacity.
- **Student output**: A circle or measurement explainer that proves or demonstrates one relationship and applies it to a real or designed object.

## Chapter 7: Probability, Statistics, and Inference
- **Key concepts**: Sample surveys, experiments, observational studies, randomization, bias, conditional probability, independence, two-way tables, expected value, normal distributions, z-scores, sampling variability, and margin of error as appropriate.
- **Practice questions**: Compute and interpret conditional probabilities; evaluate study design; use normal models; estimate population quantities from samples; distinguish association from causation.
- **Student output**: A statistical investigation memo with data source, display, probability or inference calculation, limitations, and a clear claim supported by evidence.

## Chapter 8: Exponential, Logarithmic, and Integrated Modeling Studio
- **Key concepts**: Exponential growth and decay, geometric sequences and series, logarithms as inverses of exponentials, logarithm properties, continuous compounding, model fitting, residuals, transformations, and comparison of linear, quadratic, polynomial, and exponential models.
- **Practice questions**: Solve exponential and logarithmic equations; interpret parameters; compare growth models; use residuals or graph behavior to choose an appropriate model.
- **Student output**: An integrated modeling studio report that compares at least two function models for one dataset or scenario and explains the selected model's strengths and limits.

## Capstone
Students complete an Integrated Math 2 and 3 portfolio around a substantial modeling or design question such as motion, finance, population change, measurement, signal behavior, sports analytics, architecture, or public data. The final product must include at least one advanced function model, one geometric or coordinate argument, one probability or statistics component, technology-supported representation where appropriate, and a reflection on assumptions, uncertainty, and transfer to future mathematics.

## Assessment Ideas
- Retrieval checks that keep linear, systems, graphing, and equation-solving skills active inside advanced tasks.
- Function-analysis quizzes requiring students to state domains, restrictions, transformations, key features, and contextual meaning.
- Proof and modeling prompts scored on claim, evidence, representation choice, method accuracy, and interpretation.
- Statistics and probability tasks that require students to identify assumptions, randomization, bias, and limits of inference.
- Capstone graded on mathematical accuracy, integrated concept use, representation fluency, communication, and assessment-ready reflection on model limits.
