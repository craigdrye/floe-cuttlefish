# Integrated Math 2 & 3
A connected pathway through quadratics, advanced functions, geometry, trigonometry, probability, and exponential modeling for the integrated high-school sequence.

**ID**: `col-integrated-math-2` · **Discipline**: Mathematics

## Course Aim
Integrated Math 2 and 3 form the middle and upper sections of the integrated high-school pathway, where algebra, functions, geometry, statistics, and modeling are braided together rather than taught as separate year-long subjects. The goal is for students to extend their algebraic fluency into quadratics, polynomials, rational and radical expressions, and exponential and logarithmic relationships, while simultaneously deepening geometric reasoning through similarity, right-triangle trigonometry, circles, and coordinate proof. Students should leave able to move comfortably between equations, graphs, tables, and contextual descriptions of the same relationship.

A second aim is reasoning maturity. By this stage students should not only get answers but justify methods, choose efficient representations, attend to domain and validity, and interpret what a result means in context. The course repeatedly asks students to decide whether a task wants them to solve, prove, model, compare, or interpret, and to recognize when a computed answer is extraneous, out of range, or otherwise meaningless for the situation.

A third aim is statistical and modeling literacy. Students learn to compute and interpret conditional probabilities, evaluate study design, use normal models and z-scores, and fit and compare linear, quadratic, polynomial, and exponential models against real data. The course prepares students for precalculus and beyond by building habits of careful modeling: stating assumptions, quantifying uncertainty, and explaining the limits of any model they build.

## Course Design Notes
The course is organized around Common Core High School Mathematics standards and public integrated-pathway guides (Illustrative Mathematics, California Mathematics Framework). Route questions here when they involve quadratics and complex solutions, polynomial and rational/radical algebra, function analysis and inverses, coordinate geometry and conics, similarity and trigonometry, circle relationships and measurement, probability and inference, or exponential and logarithmic modeling. Questions should be unambiguous about whether the task is to solve, prove, model, compare representations, or interpret a result.

Assessment should keep prerequisite skills (linear equations, systems, graphing, basic factoring) active inside richer tasks rather than testing them in isolation. Each chapter pairs procedural fluency with conceptual interpretation, and traps are written around the specific, recurring errors students make at this level (sign errors when completing the square, dropping the minus on the discriminant, forgetting radian mode for arc length, confusing P(A|B) with P(B|A), or treating dependent draws as independent).

## Chapter 1: Quadratic Functions and Complex Solutions
- **Core questions**: How do standard, factored, and vertex forms each reveal different features of a parabola? When does a quadratic have real versus complex roots, and how do you find them?
- **Key concepts**: Parabolas, vertex, axis of symmetry, intercepts; standard, factored, and vertex form; factoring, completing the square, the quadratic formula; the discriminant; complex numbers as solutions.
- **Applied skills**: Convert among forms, solve by the most efficient method, use the discriminant to predict root types, and interpret vertex and zeros in area, motion, revenue, or design contexts.
- **Common traps**: Reading the vertex sign backward from vertex form, dropping the negative inside the discriminant, and assuming "no real roots" means "no solutions" rather than complex solutions.

## Chapter 2: Polynomials, Rational Expressions, and Algebraic Structure
- **Core questions**: How do factors, zeros, and multiplicity control a polynomial's graph and end behavior? Why must rational and radical equations be checked for extraneous solutions?
- **Key concepts**: Polynomial operations and factoring patterns, zeros and multiplicity, end behavior, polynomial division and the Remainder Theorem, rational expressions and equations, radical expressions, rational exponents, extraneous solutions.
- **Applied skills**: Add, multiply, factor, and divide polynomials; sketch behavior from factored form; simplify and solve rational/radical equations while tracking domain restrictions.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting that even multiplicity touches and odd multiplicity crosses, canceling factors that change the domain, and accepting a solution that makes a denominator zero or a radicand negative.

## Chapter 3: Function Analysis, Composition, and Inverses
- **Core questions**: How do transformations move and reshape a parent function? How do you find and verify an inverse, and when does one exist as a function?
- **Key concepts**: Function families and transformations, domain and range, composition, inverse functions, piecewise and absolute-value functions, rational functions and asymptotes, average rate of change.
- **Applied skills**: Analyze transformed graphs, compose functions symbolically and in context, find and verify inverses, and identify domain restrictions and asymptotes.
- **Common traps**: Confusing horizontal and vertical shifts, swapping composition order, assuming every function has an inverse function, and forgetting that inverses swap domain and range.

## Chapter 4: Coordinate Geometry, Proof, and Conics
- **Core questions**: How can algebra on coordinates prove a geometric claim? How does completing the square turn a general circle equation into center-radius form?
- **Key concepts**: Distance, midpoint, slope; parallel and perpendicular lines; coordinate proofs; equations of circles; completing the square for conics; parabolas as focus-directrix sets.
- **Applied skills**: Prove claims with coordinates, classify figures from coordinate data, find circle centers and radii, and connect algebraic equations to geometric features.
- **Common traps**: Using the slope test for perpendicular lines as equal rather than negative reciprocal, sign errors completing the square for the center, and mis-reading r^2 as r in a circle equation.

## Chapter 5: Similarity, Right Triangles, and Trigonometry
- **Core questions**: When are two triangles similar, and what does that let you conclude? Which trigonometric ratio or law solves a given triangle?
- **Key concepts**: Dilations and scale factor, similarity transformations, similarity criteria (AA, SAS, SSS), the Pythagorean Theorem, special right triangles, sine/cosine/tangent, angles of elevation and depression, Law of Sines and Law of Cosines, radian measure.
- **Applied skills**: Prove triangles similar, solve right and oblique triangles, choose the correct ratio or law, and model height, distance, slope, and navigation problems.
- **Common traps**: Choosing SOH-CAH-TOA when the triangle is not right (needing Law of Sines/Cosines), mixing up elevation and depression, and using degree mode where radians are required.

## Chapter 6: Circles, Measurement, and Geometric Modeling
- **Core questions**: How do central, inscribed, tangent, and secant relationships constrain angles and segments in a circle? How do arc length and sector area depend on the central angle?
- **Key concepts**: Radius, diameter, chords, tangents, secants, arcs, central and inscribed angles, cyclic relationships, arc length (s = rθ), sector area (A = ½r²θ), surface area and volume, model limitations.
- **Applied skills**: Apply circle angle and segment relationships, compute arc length and sector area in radians, and model composite area, volume, and capacity for real objects.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting an inscribed angle is half its central angle, using degrees in s = rθ, and confusing arc length with sector area or chord length.

## Chapter 7: Probability, Statistics, and Inference
- **Core questions**: How do you compute and interpret a conditional probability? How does study design determine whether you can claim causation?
- **Key concepts**: Sample surveys, experiments, observational studies, randomization and bias, conditional probability and independence, two-way tables, expected value, normal distributions, z-scores, sampling variability, margin of error.
- **Applied skills**: Compute conditional probabilities from two-way tables, test for independence, evaluate study design, use normal models and z-scores, and distinguish association from causation.
- **Common traps**: Swapping P(A|B) with P(B|A), treating dependent draws as independent, assuming correlation implies causation, and reading the z-score as a raw value rather than standardized.

## Chapter 8: Exponential, Logarithmic, and Integrated Modeling Studio
- **Core questions**: How are exponential and logarithmic functions inverses, and how do you solve equations using each? How do you decide which model best fits a dataset?
- **Key concepts**: Exponential growth and decay, geometric sequences and series, logarithms as inverses, logarithm properties, continuous compounding (A = Pe^{rt}), model fitting, residuals, comparison of linear/quadratic/polynomial/exponential models.
- **Applied skills**: Solve exponential and logarithmic equations, interpret growth parameters, compare models, and use residuals or graph behavior to justify a model choice.
- **Common traps**: Misapplying log properties (log of a sum is not the sum of logs), confusing growth rate with growth factor, and choosing a model by single-point fit instead of residual pattern.

## Capstone
Students complete an Integrated Math 2 and 3 portfolio built around a substantial modeling or design question such as projectile motion, compound-interest finance, population change, measurement of an inaccessible structure, sports analytics, or public data. The final product must include at least one advanced function model (quadratic, polynomial, rational, or exponential/logarithmic), one geometric or coordinate argument, one probability or statistics component, technology-supported representation where appropriate, and a reflection on assumptions, uncertainty, and transfer to future mathematics.

## Assessment Ideas
- Retrieval checks that keep linear, systems, graphing, and factoring skills active inside advanced tasks.
- Function-analysis quizzes requiring students to state domains, restrictions, transformations, key features, and contextual meaning.
- Proof and modeling prompts scored on claim, evidence, representation choice, method accuracy, and interpretation.
- Statistics and probability tasks that require identifying assumptions, randomization, bias, and limits of inference.
- Capstone graded on mathematical accuracy, integrated concept use, representation fluency, communication, and reflection on model limits.
