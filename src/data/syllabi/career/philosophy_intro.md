# Philosophy: The Big Questions
A friendly tour of the questions humans have argued about for 2,500 years — and the thinkers, branches, and thought experiments that frame them.

**ID**: `philosophyIntro` · **Discipline**: Humanities

## Course Aim
Philosophy can feel like a locked club with a Greek password. This course unlocks it. We treat philosophy as something a curious adult actually does at the kitchen table: noticing a slippery assumption, asking "wait, how do I really know that?", and reasoning it through with a little structure. No prior reading required, no dumbing-down either.

You will meet the six main branches (metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, logic, aesthetics, political philosophy) and the heavyweight thinkers — Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Mill, Nietzsche, Wittgenstein, Rawls — not as names to memorize but as people wrestling with real puzzles. Along the way you will play with the famous thought experiments: the trolley problem, the brain in a vat, the ship of Theseus, the veil of ignorance, and Plato's cave.

The goal is not to make you agree with any one philosopher. It is to give you the map, the vocabulary, and the habits of careful thinking so the big questions — about knowledge, reality, morality, free will, and meaning — feel less intimidating and more like yours to explore.

## Course Design Notes
This is a multiple-choice quiz bank a learner returns to, organized so that each chapter both teaches a cluster of ideas and rehearses the vocabulary needed for the next. Distractors are built from the most common real-world confusions (mixing up validity with truth, "natural" with "good," begging the question with raising a question), so wrong answers teach as much as right ones. Every question carries a plain-language lesson that names the idea, the thinker, and why it matters. The arc runs roughly historical-and-thematic: from "what is philosophy?" through the ancients and moderns, into the perennial questions (knowledge, ethics, reality, justice), and out to the 20th century and the tools of clear reasoning.

## Chapter 1: What Is Philosophy? The Branches
**Core questions**
- Which branch handles "what exists" versus "how do I know it"?
- What is the difference between a valid argument and a true one?
- Where do beauty, justice, and right conduct each belong?

**Key concepts**: metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, logic, aesthetics, political philosophy, validity vs soundness, a priori vs a posteriori.

**Common traps**: confusing metaphysics (reality) with epistemology (knowledge); thinking a valid argument must have a true conclusion; assuming "good" always means morally good.

## Chapter 2: The Ancients
**Core questions**
- What was the Socratic method, and why did Socrates write nothing?
- Why did Plato think abstract Forms are more real than physical things?
- How does Aristotle locate virtue between extremes?

**Key concepts**: Socratic questioning, theory of Forms, the Allegory of the Cave, the golden mean, eudaimonia, practical wisdom.

**Common traps**: picturing Socrates as a lecturer; reading the Forms as mere mental concepts or labels; mistaking eudaimonia for a passing good mood.

## Chapter 3: How Do We Know? Epistemology
**Core questions**
- Is knowledge built from experience or from reason?
- What does it take to truly *know* something rather than luckily guess it?
- Can we justify expecting the future to resemble the past?

**Key concepts**: rationalism vs empiricism, blank slate vs innate ideas, justified true belief, the problem of induction, skepticism, Gettier cases.

**Common traps**: thinking a true belief alone is knowledge; treating skepticism as denial rather than doubt; assuming knowledge requires mathematical certainty.

## Chapter 4: Modern Minds
**Core questions**
- What survives total doubt, and why does "I think" prove "I am"?
- How can an immaterial mind interact with a physical brain?
- How did Kant claim the mind shapes the world we experience?

**Key concepts**: Cartesian doubt, cogito, mind-body dualism, Hume's fork, the is-ought gap, Kant's Copernican turn, the categorical imperative.

**Common traps**: reading the cogito as a proof of God; confusing dualism with materialism or idealism; thinking Kant believed we know reality "in itself."

## Chapter 5: How Should We Live? Ethics
**Core questions**
- Do we judge actions by outcomes, rules, or character?
- What is utilitarianism's "greatest happiness," and what does it miss?
- Why do our intuitions flip between trolley-problem variations?

**Key concepts**: consequentialism, utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, moral relativism, the trolley problem, the naturalistic fallacy.

**Common traps**: collapsing the three ethical theories into one; thinking utilitarianism counts only the agent's happiness; sliding from "is natural" to "is good."

## Chapter 6: Reality, Identity, and Free Will
**Core questions**
- If every part is replaced, is it still the same thing — or the same you?
- Can free will survive in a universe where everything is caused?
- Could you ever prove you are not a brain in a vat?

**Key concepts**: Ship of Theseus, personal identity and memory, hard determinism, libertarian free will, compatibilism, brain-in-a-vat, the hard problem of consciousness.

**Common traps**: treating identity puzzles as questions about materials or law; confusing compatibilism with hard determinism; conflating the "easy" and "hard" problems of consciousness.

## Chapter 7: Justice and the State
**Core questions**
- What makes a government legitimate rather than merely powerful?
- When may society limit an individual's freedom?
- How would you design fair rules if you didn't know your place in society?

**Key concepts**: social contract, state of nature, Hobbes vs Locke, Mill's harm principle, Rawls's veil of ignorance, the difference principle, negative vs positive liberty.

**Common traps**: grounding authority in divine right or raw force; thinking the harm principle permits paternalism; assuming the veil of ignorance erases all knowledge, not just personal position.

## Chapter 8: Meaning, Doubt, and the 20th Century
**Core questions**
- What did Nietzsche mean by "God is dead," and what crisis followed?
- If life has no built-in purpose, where does meaning come from?
- What separates a scientific claim from an unfalsifiable one?

**Key concepts**: nihilism, "God is dead," existentialism (existence precedes essence), the absurd, Wittgenstein on language-as-use, falsifiability, Occam's razor, begging the question, necessary vs sufficient conditions.

**Common traps**: reading "God is dead" literally or as celebration; thinking existentialism denies the self; misusing "begs the question" to mean "raises the question."

## Capstone
Bring it all together: take a real-world claim you care about — a moral stance, a political argument, a confident "everybody knows" belief — and run it through the philosopher's toolkit. Identify which branch the question lives in, name your hidden assumptions, check the argument for validity and common fallacies (is-ought, begging the question, necessary-vs-sufficient), and locate where a Hume, a Kant, a Mill, or a Rawls would push back. The capstone is not about reaching the "correct" answer; it is about demonstrating that you can think the question through clearly, charitably, and on your own.
