# Credit and LevFin Roadmap
**ID**: `creditLevFinRoadmap` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Credit analysis answers one stubborn question: will I get my money back, and what happens if I don't? Equity investors dream about upside; credit investors live in the basement of the capital structure, where the only good outcome is being repaid in full and on time, and every other outcome is a gradient of loss. This course trains the judgment a leveraged-finance analyst actually uses — at a bank arranging the debt, at a direct lender or CLO buying it, at a rating agency scoring it, or at a fund deciding whether a stressed name is a bargain or a falling knife. The throughline is cash, not earnings: a borrower defaults when it runs out of cash to pay a maturity or a coupon, regardless of what the income statement says.

Leveraged finance is where this judgment is tested hardest, because the borrowers are deliberately loaded with debt — private-equity buyouts, dividend recaps, debt-funded acquisitions — and the margin for error is thin. You will learn to read a capital structure from the top of the waterfall to the bottom, build a credit model that survives a downside case, price and structure a new financing the market will actually buy, and read credit agreements the way a litigator reads them: looking for the basket, the add-back, and the escape hatch that lets a sponsor move your collateral out of reach. The post-2020 wave of liability-management exercises — uptier exchanges, drop-down financings, the "J.Crew" and "Serta" and "Chewy" maneuvers — means a modern analyst cannot stop at leverage ratios; covenant and document risk now decides recoveries as much as enterprise value does.

By the end, a learner should be able to take a fictional or real leveraged borrower and produce a desk-grade credit view: a structure map with priority and security, a base-and-downside cash-flow model, a financing recommendation with leverage, pricing, and structure, a covenant read that flags the dangerous flexibility, and a recovery estimate that says what lenders get if the deal goes wrong. The goal is not vocabulary — it is the ability to say "lend," "pass," or "lend, but only on these terms," and defend it.

## Course Design Notes
The course follows the order an analyst actually works a credit: understand the business and the structure, prove out the cash flow, size and price the debt, read the documents, then stress for distress. Each chapter assumes the prior ones. Route questions here when they test credit fundamentals, leveraged-finance structuring, debt capacity and sizing, rating logic, covenants and credit-agreement mechanics, syndication and primary markets, or distress, restructuring, and recovery. Keep numerical questions on round figures so learners reason about leverage and coverage mechanics rather than arithmetic, and keep the perspective explicitly that of a *lender* — the question is always downside protection and recoverability, never upside. Common traps are flagged in every chapter, because in credit the expensive mistakes are subtle: the friendly-looking add-back, the leverage ratio that ignores the maturity wall, the "covenant" that turns out to be incurrence-only.

## Desk Rhythm and How to Use This Course
A credit view is built in layers, and skipping a layer is how analysts get surprised. The working rhythm: read the business and the existing stack first, then the cash flow, then the proposed or outstanding debt against that cash flow, then the documents that govern it, then the downside. Treat every chapter's "Applied skills" as a deliverable you could hand a credit committee, not a reading. A realistic cadence is one borrower per chapter for practice and one full borrower for the capstone. Keep a running "red flag log" across the whole course — aggressive add-backs, thin covenants, looming maturities, customer concentration — because in real diligence the kill reasons accumulate quietly across documents rather than appearing in one headline number.

| Layer | What you produce | Decision it supports |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Capital-structure map with priority, security, maturity | Where do I sit, and who is ahead of me? |
| Cash flow | Base and downside model, leverage and coverage | Can it service and repay the debt? |
| Sizing and pricing | Debt quantum, spread, structure recommendation | How much debt, on what terms, will clear? |
| Documents | Covenant and flexibility read | What can the borrower do *to* me later? |
| Distress | Triggers, recovery estimate, recommended action | If it breaks, what do I recover, and what do I do now? |

## Chapter 1: The Borrower, the Business, and the Capital Structure
**Core questions**
- What does this business actually do, and how stable and predictable is its cash generation through a cycle?
- Reading top to bottom, who gets paid first, what secures each layer, and where would a new piece of debt sit?
- Is this a "good business with too much debt" or a "bad business at any leverage"?

**Key concepts**: business and industry risk (cyclicality, customer/supplier concentration, competitive moat, capex intensity), the capital-structure waterfall, senior secured vs senior unsecured vs subordinated, first-lien vs second-lien, revolver vs term loan A vs institutional term loan B vs high-yield bond, secured vs unsecured, structural vs contractual subordination, guarantees and the difference between an operating company and a holding company, pari passu ranking, enterprise value as the recovery pool.

**Applied skills**
- Build a capital-structure snapshot: each tranche with amount, maturity, priority, security, pricing, and lender type.
- Distinguish structural subordination (debt at an opco with the assets) from contractual subordination (a junior agreement at the same entity) and explain why holdco debt is usually worse off.

**Common traps**
- Assuming "secured" means "safe" without checking what the collateral is actually worth in a downside, not at book.
- Missing structural subordination — bond at the holdco looks senior on paper but sits behind every opco creditor where the cash and assets live.
- Treating leverage as a property of the company rather than of the *tranche*: 3x through the first lien and 6x through the whole stack are different bets.

## Chapter 2: Credit Metrics and the Cash-Flow Engine
**Core questions**
- Can this borrower service its debt from operating cash flow, and repay or refinance it at maturity?
- What is real, sustainable EBITDA — and how far has it been inflated by adjustments and add-backs?
- What drains cash before it ever reaches lenders: working capital, capex, taxes, cash interest?

**Key concepts**: EBITDA and its limits, total and net leverage (debt/EBITDA), senior vs total leverage, interest coverage (EBITDA/interest), fixed-charge coverage, free cash flow and cash conversion, maintenance vs growth capex, working-capital swing, the "EBITDA to free cash flow" walk, liquidity (cash plus undrawn revolver), the difference between a liquidity problem and a solvency problem, pro forma and run-rate adjustments, the danger of management add-backs ("synergies," "one-time" items that recur).

**Applied skills**
- Build a one-page credit-metrics view with a base case and a coherent downside (revenue decline + margin compression + working-capital drag), and read where coverage breaks.
- Walk reported EBITDA down to actual free cash flow available for debt service, and flag each adjustment as defensible or aggressive.

**Common traps**
- Trusting "adjusted EBITDA" with large run-rate or synergy add-backs that haven't been realized — leverage looks fine on a number that doesn't exist yet.
- Confusing EBITDA with cash flow and forgetting that capex, cash taxes, and working capital all sit between them.
- Calling a borrower healthy on coverage while ignoring that the debt all matures in eighteen months (a refinancing/maturity-wall problem, not a coverage one).

## Chapter 3: Sizing the Debt and Leveraged-Finance Structuring
**Core questions**
- How much debt can this cash flow safely carry, and how much will the market actually fund?
- For a buyout or acquisition, what mix of revolver, term loan B, and bonds, at what leverage, makes the sources tie to the uses?
- Where is the line between an aggressive-but-financeable structure and one that won't clear?

**Key concepts**: sponsor LBO mechanics, sources and uses, equity contribution and equity cushion, debt capacity, the term-loan-B vs high-yield-bond choice (floating vs fixed, secured vs unsecured, prepayability, investor base), first-lien/second-lien structures, unitranche and direct lending, leverage and coverage as sizing constraints, purchase-price multiple vs leverage multiple, the role of the equity check as loss-absorbing first.

**Applied skills**
- Build a sources-and-uses for a leveraged acquisition and propose a debt structure (tranches, leverage, security) that the cash flow in Chapter 2 can support.
- Argue the TLB-vs-bond and first-lien-vs-second-lien choices for a specific borrower based on cash-flow stability, asset coverage, and prepayment needs.

**Common traps**
- Sizing debt to what the model's *base* case can service rather than what survives the downside — the equity cushion is the lender's margin of safety.
- Ignoring that floating-rate TLB debt re-prices the interest burden when base rates rise, so coverage in a high-rate environment can be far worse than at signing.
- Treating the sponsor's equity as committed protection without checking how much is real cash versus rollover, prefs, or shareholder loans that behave like debt.

## Chapter 4: Markets, Ratings, and Syndication
**Core questions**
- At what spread and on what terms will the loan or bond market actually buy this debt today?
- What rating will the agencies assign, and how does that drive pricing and the eligible investor base?
- Where could a syndication fail, and what levers (flex, structure, sweeteners) fix it?

**Key concepts**: corporate vs issue (instrument) ratings, investment grade vs high yield / "leveraged," the major rating scales and notching for priority and security, recovery ratings, credit spread, coupon vs yield, original issue discount (OID), call protection and non-call periods, the primary syndication process (commitment, bookbuilding, allocation), market flex (price and structure flex), the CLO bid and why it shapes term-loan demand, ratings triggers and the cost of a downgrade, secondary trading levels as a real-time credit verdict.

**Applied skills**
- Write a financing recommendation: tranche sizes, indicative ratings, spread/OID/yield, call protection, and the two or three market risks most likely to force a flex.
- Map how a one-notch downgrade or a wider market would change pricing, demand, and whether the deal still clears.

**Common traps**
- Confusing the *issuer* rating with the *instrument* rating — a second-lien loan is notched below the senior secured even at the same company.
- Quoting a spread as if it were the all-in cost and ignoring OID, fees, and the floating base rate that together set true yield.
- Assuming appetite is constant: a structure that cleared easily six months ago can be untouchable after a market sells off, and the analyst who misses that misprices the deal.

## Chapter 5: Covenants, Credit Agreements, and Document Risk
**Core questions**
- What does the credit agreement actually let the borrower do — and what does it let them do *to* lenders?
- Maintenance or incurrence covenants, and how much real protection does that give?
- Where are the baskets, add-backs, and definitions that a sponsor can use to move value away from existing lenders?

**Key concepts**: maintenance covenants (tested every period) vs incurrence covenants (tested only on an action), "cov-lite" loans and why they dominate the institutional market, the EBITDA definition as the master variable, restricted-payments and investment baskets, debt and lien incurrence capacity, "free and clear"/ratio debt baskets, restricted vs unrestricted subsidiaries, asset-sale and collateral-leakage provisions, the mechanics behind liability-management exercises — drop-down financings (J.Crew/Chewy-style) that move collateral to an unrestricted sub, and uptier exchanges (Serta/Boardriders-style) that prime existing lenders with the consent of a majority.

**Applied skills**
- Produce a covenant summary: protections lenders have, flexibility the borrower has, and the two or three terms that most weaken recovery.
- Trace a drop-down or uptier exercise through a sample agreement and explain which basket or amendment mechanic enabled it and how lenders could have blocked it.

**Common traps**
- Reading "there are covenants" as protection without checking whether they are maintenance (real) or incurrence-only (mostly cosmetic in a slow decline).
- Underweighting the EBITDA definition — generous add-backs in the *definition* inflate every ratio-based basket and covenant simultaneously.
- Assuming your collateral and your priority are safe; modern liability-management means a cooperative majority can leave a minority lender subordinated and stripped, all within the four corners of the document.

## Chapter 6: Distress, Restructuring, and Recovery
**Core questions**
- What is the earliest credible sign this credit is deteriorating, before a missed payment?
- If it defaults, what do lenders at each level of the stack actually recover?
- In a workout, what is the realistic menu — amend-and-extend, exchange, out-of-court deal, or Chapter 11 — and which leaves my tranche best off?

**Key concepts**: early-warning signals (covenant headroom shrinking, liquidity drawdown, revolver fully drawn, downgrades, secondary price collapse, auditor going-concern flag), the maturity wall and refinancing risk, amend-and-extend, distressed exchange, payment-in-kind (PIK) toggles as a stress symptom, recovery analysis (enterprise value vs total claims, the recovery waterfall by priority), recovery rates by seniority and security, the difference between a liquidity event and balance-sheet insolvency, out-of-court restructuring vs Chapter 11, the absolute-priority rule and where it bends, debtor-in-possession financing, the "fulcrum" security that converts to the new equity.

**Applied skills**
- Write a distress watch note: the trigger events you are monitoring, the liquidity runway, and a recommended action (hold, sell, hedge, engage).
- Build a simple recovery waterfall — distressed enterprise value flowing down the priority stack — and identify the fulcrum security and which tranches are impaired.

**Common traps**
- Waiting for a covenant breach or missed payment when secondary prices and revolver draws have already told the story.
- Confusing illiquidity with insolvency: a solvent business can default on a maturity it can't refinance, and an over-levered one can limp along on cash for years.
- Assuming senior secured always recovers par — recovery depends on enterprise value in distress and on whether liability management quietly moved the collateral or the priority before you got there.

## Capstone: Full Credit and LevFin Packet
Take one leveraged borrower — a fictional sponsor-owned company or a real high-yield issuer — and produce a complete, committee-ready credit packet:
- a business and industry risk summary with the two or three factors that most drive credit quality,
- a capital-structure map (priority, security, maturity, pricing) and a read on subordination,
- a credit model with base and downside cases: leverage, coverage, liquidity, and the EBITDA-to-free-cash-flow walk,
- a financing recommendation (or refinancing view): tranche mix, leverage, indicative ratings, spread/OID/yield, and call protection,
- a covenant and document read flagging the most dangerous flexibility, including any drop-down or uptier exposure,
- a distress watch note with triggers and a recovery waterfall,
- and a one-line verdict — lend, pass, or lend-on-these-terms — with the single strongest argument for and against.

## Assessment Ideas
- Capital-structure mapping and subordination drill
- EBITDA-to-free-cash-flow walk with add-back critique
- Downside-case stress test (where does coverage break?)
- LBO sources-and-uses and debt-sizing lab
- Financing recommendation memo (structure, ratings, pricing)
- Credit-agreement read: spot the basket / add-back / leakage
- Liability-management case (trace a drop-down or uptier)
- Recovery-waterfall and fulcrum-security exercise
- Capstone credit packet and lend/pass verdict

## Research Notes
- Capital-structure priority, secured/unsecured/subordinated ranking, structural vs contractual subordination, and TLB-vs-high-yield trade-offs aligned with standard leveraged-finance references (Moody's/S&P methodology, LSTA primer concepts).
- Leverage, coverage, EBITDA-adjustment scrutiny, and the EBITDA-to-FCF walk reflect conventional credit-analysis practice; add-back inflation is a recognized recurring risk in sponsor deals.
- Cov-lite prevalence in the institutional term-loan market and the EBITDA-definition-as-master-variable point reflect post-2015 leveraged-loan market norms.
- Liability-management mechanics (drop-down / J.Crew and uptier / Serta exercises) reflect documented post-2020 restructuring developments; specifics of any individual case should be confirmed against current sources before teaching as settled law, as litigation outcomes continue to evolve.
- Recovery analysis, fulcrum security, absolute-priority rule, and out-of-court vs Chapter 11 framing follow standard distressed-debt and restructuring practice.
