# Equity Research Roadmap
**ID**: `equityResearchRoadmap` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Equity research sells one product: a defensible opinion about where a stock is going and why. A sell-side or buy-side analyst is paid not for facts (the filings are public and free) but for judgment — a view on the few variables that actually move a stock, an estimate that differs from consensus for a reason, and the discipline to be right often enough that people trade on the call. This course teaches the repeatable craft behind that opinion: understand the business and its industry, read what management reports and what they leave out, forecast the drivers that matter, value the result two ways, and write it up so a portfolio manager can act before the rest of the market does.

The emphasis is on variant perception and evidence, not hot takes. Anyone can echo the Street; the skill is knowing which line in the model the whole thesis hinges on, why a "beat" can still be bad news, how a target price is built rather than wished into existence, and when the facts have changed enough to change the rating. Learners practice the mechanics — segment revenue builds, margin bridges, EV/EBITDA versus P/E, a clean DCF — alongside the discipline that separates research from cheerleading: stating the consensus you are betting against, owning the downside case, and saying out loud what would prove the thesis wrong.

By the end, a learner should be able to initiate coverage on a company they had never heard of last week: map its drivers and true peer set, turn an earnings release and call into estimate revisions, build a forecast someone could disagree with on the merits, set a target price with a coherent multiple or cash-flow basis, frame the bull/bear debate honestly, and compress all of it into a note whose first paragraph already tells a busy investor the thesis, the catalyst, and the risk. The goal is not a longer report but a sharper call.

## Course Design Notes
The course follows the real arc of initiating and maintaining coverage: business and industry first, then disclosure, then forecast, then valuation and rating, then the note and the ongoing debate, then the discipline that keeps an analyst honest over time. Each chapter assumes the prior ones, mirroring how an analyst actually builds conviction. Route questions here when they test public-company research, business models, filings and earnings, forecasting, valuation, ratings and target prices, catalysts, note writing, or research compliance and conflicts. Keep every answer anchored to the chain that defines the discipline: evidence changes an estimate, the estimate changes the value, the value changes the rating, and the note explains why an investor should act now. Numerical examples use round figures so learners reason about mechanics, not arithmetic. Common traps are flagged in every chapter because in research, recognizing a flattering-but-wrong narrative is half the job.

## Chapter 1: Business Model, Industry, and the Investment Debate
**Key concepts**: revenue model and unit economics, segments and geographies, cost structure and operating leverage, market size and share, industry cycle and structure (suppliers, buyers, substitutes, rivalry), regulation, the true peer set, and the one or two debates that determine the stock.

**Core questions**
- How does this company actually make money, and which one or two drivers move the P&L most?
- What is the genuine peer set — competitors with similar growth, margins, and risk, not just the same sector tag?
- What is the central debate on this stock that the price is implicitly resolving?

**Applied skills**
- Write a one-page company snapshot: revenue drivers, segments, margin structure, peers, and the key bull/bear debate.
- Map the industry structure and identify the single variable (price, volume, mix, cycle, regulation) most worth tracking.

**Common traps**
- Treating a sector label as a peer set and comparing multiples across businesses with different growth and capital intensity.
- Cataloguing every fact about the company instead of isolating the few drivers that change the stock.
- Confusing a great business with a great stock — quality already in the price is not an opportunity.

## Chapter 2: Filings, Earnings, and Management Commentary
**Key concepts**: 10-K and 10-Q structure, the earnings release versus the call, guidance and how it is framed, GAAP versus non-GAAP and the adjustments that flatter, segment and KPI disclosure, backlog/bookings/deferred revenue, the MD&A, risk factors, and reading what management emphasizes versus what they bury.

**Core questions**
- What actually changed this quarter, and does it alter the multi-year thesis or just the noise?
- Is guidance credible given the company's track record, and is it raised, reiterated, or quietly walked back?
- Which non-GAAP adjustments are reasonable, and which are recurring "one-time" charges dressed up?

**Applied skills**
- Produce an earnings readout: beats/misses versus consensus, guidance change, the three drivers behind the result, and follow-up questions for management.
- Reconcile a non-GAAP figure to GAAP and judge whether the add-backs are defensible.

**Common traps**
- Reacting to a headline EPS beat without checking whether it came from operations, a lower tax rate, or buybacks.
- Taking adjusted EBITDA at face value when stock comp or restructuring is a permanent feature of the model.
- Mistaking confident tone on the call for credible guidance — track the gap between what was promised and what was delivered.

## Chapter 3: Forecasting and Estimate Discipline
**Key concepts**: driver-based revenue builds (price × volume, cohort/retention, units × ASP), the margin bridge, operating leverage and incremental margins, capex and depreciation, working capital, share count and buybacks, the path from revenue to EPS and free cash flow, and where your numbers sit versus consensus.

**Core questions**
- Is the forecast built from drivers tied to evidence, or a single growth rate painted across every line?
- Where, specifically, do your estimates differ from consensus, and what fact supports the gap?
- Which one assumption, if wrong, breaks the thesis — and how sensitive is EPS to it?

**Applied skills**
- Build a segment-level revenue forecast with a margin bridge from current to forecast operating margin.
- Run a sensitivity table showing EPS under high/base/low cases for the single most important driver.

**Common traps**
- Anchoring to consensus and nudging it by a few percent instead of building an independent number you can defend.
- Forecasting margin expansion with no operating-leverage or mix logic to support it.
- Forgetting that buybacks, dilution, and tax rate move EPS even when operating estimates are unchanged.

## Chapter 4: Valuation, Target Price, and Rating Logic
**Key concepts**: relative valuation (P/E, EV/EBITDA, EV/Sales, PEG, and matching the metric to enterprise versus equity value), DCF and when it earns its keep, sum-of-the-parts, the multiple-times-estimate target-price build, upside/downside and risk-reward skew, and the explicit conditions that would change a rating.

**Core questions**
- Which valuation method fits this business, and what justifies the multiple you are applying — growth, returns, or just the historical average?
- How is the target price actually constructed, and how much of it is the estimate versus the multiple?
- What is the risk-reward — upside to target versus downside to the bear case — and does it justify the rating?

**Applied skills**
- Build a target price as forward estimate × a justified multiple, and cross-check it against a simple DCF.
- Lay out a risk-reward: bull, base, and bear target prices with the probability-weighted skew.

**Common traps**
- Pairing an enterprise-value metric with a post-interest figure like net income, or comparing P/E across firms with very different leverage.
- Reverse-engineering a multiple to hit a target price the analyst already wanted.
- Issuing a Buy on modest upside while ignoring a fat downside case — rating the stock, not the risk-reward.

## Chapter 5: The Research Note — Thesis, Catalysts, and Variant Perception
**Key concepts**: the thesis statement, variant perception (what you believe that consensus does not), the catalyst path and timing, estimate-revision logic, channel checks and primary research, the risk section, the bear case stated fairly, and writing so the first paragraph carries the whole argument.

**Core questions**
- In one sentence, what is the thesis, the catalyst, and the risk?
- Why should an investor act now rather than in three months — what is the catalyst and when does it land?
- Have you stated the bear case strongly enough that a skeptic would recognize their own view in it?

**Applied skills**
- Write a one-page note: thesis up top, variant perception, estimate impact, valuation/target, catalysts with dates, and risks.
- Build a catalyst calendar mapping events (earnings, product, regulatory, capital allocation) to expected estimate or sentiment moves.

**Common traps**
- Burying the call under three pages of background so the reader cannot find the thesis or the catalyst.
- Presenting a one-sided story with a token risk section that no one believes the analyst takes seriously.
- "Variant perception" that is actually consensus — agreeing with the Street is not an edge.

## Chapter 6: Maintenance, Revisions, and Knowing When You Are Wrong
**Key concepts**: post-earnings model updates, estimate revisions and rating changes, thesis tracking versus position drift, falsification (what would prove the thesis wrong), reacting to new information versus to price, sunk-cost and confirmation bias, upgrade/downgrade discipline, and managing a coverage list over time.

**Core questions**
- Has the evidence changed, or just the price — and which one should change the rating?
- What did you say would prove the thesis wrong, and has any of it happened?
- Are you updating the model to follow the facts, or to defend a call you already made public?

**Applied skills**
- Run a post-earnings revision: update estimates, restate the target, and decide whether the rating still holds.
- Maintain a thesis tracker listing the original claims, the falsifiers, and their current status.

**Common traps**
- Holding a rating because changing it is embarrassing, not because the thesis still stands.
- Letting a falling price feel like new information when nothing fundamental has changed.
- Quietly moving the goalposts — redefining the thesis after the fact so it can never be wrong.

## Chapter 7: Compliance, Conflicts, and Research Ethics
**Key concepts**: material non-public information (MNPI) and insider-trading lines, Regulation FD and the level playing field, the research–banking wall and the legacy of the global research settlement, disclosure of conflicts and ownership, expert-network and channel-check boundaries, fair distribution of ratings changes, and CFA Institute standards on research integrity and independence.

**Core questions**
- Is this piece of information material and non-public — and if so, how must the analyst handle it?
- What conflicts (banking relationship, ownership, prior employment) must be disclosed, and does the structure protect independence?
- Is a channel check legitimate mosaic-theory research, or is it crossing into MNPI?

**Applied skills**
- Screen a research scenario for MNPI and Reg FD problems before publishing or trading.
- Draft the disclosure and rating-distribution language a note requires, and explain why each disclosure exists.

**Common traps**
- Treating a "tip" from a well-connected source as research rather than a potential insider-trading exposure.
- Assuming the mosaic theory blesses any data-gathering — it does not launder a single piece of material non-public information.
- Letting a banking relationship or a personal position shape a rating while the disclosure stays buried in fine print.

## Capstone: Initiating Coverage
Learners initiate coverage on one real or assigned public company and deliver a complete research package:
- a business-and-industry snapshot with drivers, true peer set, and the central debate;
- an earnings readout from the most recent quarter, reconciling GAAP and non-GAAP;
- a driver-based forecast model with a margin bridge and a clearly stated variant view versus consensus;
- a valuation page with a target price built from a justified multiple, cross-checked against a DCF;
- a risk-reward with bull/base/bear targets and the resulting rating;
- a one-page initiation note (thesis, catalyst, risk in the first paragraph) with a catalyst calendar;
- a thesis tracker listing the falsifiers that would change the call;
- and the required compliance disclosures.

## Research Notes
- Relative-valuation mechanics (P/E vs EV/EBITDA, enterprise-vs-equity matching, leverage sensitivity of P/E) and DCF/target-price construction confirmed via Corporate Finance Institute and Wall Street Prep valuation references.
- Earnings analysis, GAAP vs non-GAAP adjustments, and guidance interpretation aligned with SEC filing structure (10-K/10-Q, MD&A, Reg G on non-GAAP measures).
- Compliance content reflects Regulation FD, insider-trading/MNPI law, the 2003 Global Research Analyst Settlement legacy of the research–banking wall, and CFA Institute Standards of Practice on research independence and the mosaic theory.
- Workflow sequencing (coverage initiation, estimate revisions, catalyst-driven calls, variant perception) informed by sell-side and buy-side analyst training materials from CFI, Wall Street Prep, and Breaking Into Wall Street.
