# National Security Policy Roadmap
**ID**: `nationalSecurityPolicyRoadmap` - **Discipline**: Government - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course trains the working judgment of a national security professional: the action officer, desk analyst, or NSC staffer who has to turn a messy, classified, fast-moving problem into a defensible decision package. The skill being built is not memorizing doctrine but producing *usable judgment under uncertainty* — separating what is known from what is assumed, matching instruments to objectives, moving a decision through the interagency, and saying clearly what the government should do next and why.

The course is deliberately oriented around the artifacts of the trade — the threat brief, the intelligence-to-policy note, the DIME options matrix, the decision memo, the crisis update — because in this profession your judgment is only as good as the document that carries it. A learner who finishes should be able to read a raw situation, identify the interest at stake, calibrate the evidence, lay out real options with second-order effects, route the decision to the right table with the right legal lane, and communicate it without overrunning the facts.

It is also grounded in the law and process that constrain the work: the War Powers Resolution, the Title 10 / Title 50 divide, covert-action findings and Gang-of-Eight notification, classification authority under E.O. 13526, Leahy vetting, and OFAC/IEEPA sanctions authorities. National security work that ignores its legal architecture is not bold — it is a future inspector-general finding.

## Course Design Notes
Route learners here when they want a career path through strategic context, threat and intelligence assessment, the interagency process, the intelligence-policy boundary, instruments of national power, crisis management, and policy writing. Questions should reward the move a competent action officer would actually make: evidence-based, decision-focused, legally aware, and concise. Punish length-without-relevance, false certainty, single-instrument thinking, and mirror-imaging. Every chapter pairs a *concept* (deterrence, confidence labels, DIME, the NSC tiers) with a *craft output* (a brief, a note, a matrix, a memo, an update) so the learner is always converting knowledge into a deliverable. Keep distractors plausible — the failure modes here are professional ones, not cartoonish ones.

## Chapter 1: Strategy, Interests, and Threats
- **Core questions**: What U.S. interest is at stake, and how vital is it? Is this a threat of capability, intent, or both? What is known versus assumed, and how severe is the risk?
- **Key concepts**: national interests (vital / important / peripheral), the National Security Strategy, threat = capability × intent × opportunity, vulnerability, risk = threat × consequence, assumptions and indicators, ends-ways-means and strategic risk.
- **Applied skills**: write a threat-and-interest brief that ties facts to interests; surface fragile planning assumptions with watch indicators; distinguish a capability problem from an intent problem.
- **Common traps**: leading with background "to the horizon" and never stating the interest; treating capability as certainty (or dismissing it because intent is unproven); hiding the assumption a plan rests on; conflating vulnerability with intent.

## Chapter 2: Deterrence, Coercion, and Escalation
- **Core questions**: Are we trying to deter, compel, or reassure — and does the other side believe us? Is this deterrence by denial or by punishment? Where does this sit on the escalation ladder?
- **Key concepts**: deterrence by denial vs. by punishment, credibility and the commitment problem, extended deterrence and the nuclear umbrella, compellence vs. deterrence, escalation ladders and rungs, signaling and red lines, the security dilemma.
- **Applied skills**: classify a proposed action as deter/compel/reassure; assess whether a threat is credible to the intended audience; map an action onto an escalation ladder before recommending it.
- **Common traps**: confusing deterrence (stop them acting) with compellence (make them undo something); assuming a threat is credible because *we* find it serious; treating a red line as self-enforcing; ignoring how a "reassuring" move reads to an adversary.

## Chapter 3: Intelligence and Evidence
- **Core questions**: How strong is the evidence? What is fact, judgment, assumption, and gap? What would change the assessment, and what should policymakers *not* overread?
- **Key concepts**: ICD 203 analytic standards, words of estimative probability vs. confidence levels (never mixed in one sentence), source confidence and caveats, the intelligence cycle, indications & warning, Analysis of Competing Hypotheses and disconfirming evidence, mirror-imaging and groupthink, politicization risk.
- **Applied skills**: write an intelligence-to-policy note that separates evidence / judgment / confidence / gaps; apply ICD 203 likelihood and confidence language correctly; build an I&W product against a baseline with thresholds; use ACH to test a favored hypothesis.
- **Common traps**: presenting judgment as certainty because consumers dislike nuance; mixing a confidence level and a likelihood word in one sentence; treating vivid OSINT as verified; mirror-imaging the adversary; burying gaps in footnotes; tautological reasoning ("absence of proof = proof").

## Chapter 4: Instruments of National Power
- **Core questions**: Which instrument fits the objective? What is the likely second-order effect and partner/adversary reaction? What legal authority does the tool require?
- **Key concepts**: DIME / DIMEFIL, sanctions under IEEPA (50 USC 1701-1708) and OFAC designations, the SDN list and the 50 Percent Rule, general vs. specific licenses, Leahy vetting (22 USC 2378d / 10 USC 362), security assistance, alliances and burden-sharing, deterrent vs. assurance messaging.
- **Applied skills**: build a DIME options matrix with benefits/risks/constraints/reactions; assemble an evidentiary package for a sanctions designation; run a Leahy check; identify the legal authority a tool requires before recommending it.
- **Common traps**: single-instrument analysis (only the most forceful or only the cheapest option); nominating a sanctions target on suspicion without evidence; ignoring second-order spillover onto allies; skipping Leahy vetting as "paperwork"; assuming an entity is clean because it is not literally on the SDN list (50 Percent Rule).

## Chapter 5: Interagency Process and Decision Making
- **Core questions**: What decision is actually needed? Who must be at the table, and at what tier? Which agency owns execution? Which legal lane applies?
- **Key concepts**: the NSC system and its tiers (PC / DC / PCC), Summary of Conclusions and taskers, clearance and dissent, the Title 10 vs. Title 50 divide, covert-action Presidential Findings and Gang-of-Eight notification (50 USC 3093), Goldwater-Nichols and the combatant-command chain, the decision memo.
- **Applied skills**: route an issue to the correct NSC tier; write a decision memo with options, recommendation, agency positions, authorities, and an implementation owner; preserve meaningful dissent in the record; classify an activity as Title 10 or Title 50.
- **Common traps**: ending a meeting with agreement but no named owner or next decision point; scrubbing dissent to look unanimous; burying the recommendation in an appendix; picking an authority by convenience rather than statute; assuming the service chiefs command operational forces (they organize, train, and equip).

## Chapter 6: Law, Authorities, and Oversight
- **Core questions**: Is there a lawful basis for this action? Who must be notified, in what form, and by when? What classification and records obligations attach?
- **Key concepts**: War Powers Resolution (48-hour report, 60-day clock, +30-day withdrawal), Article II vs. statutory authority, classification levels and original classification authority under E.O. 13526, covert-action findings and timely notification, congressional oversight committees, the Federal Records Act, unauthorized-disclosure statutes (18 USC 793).
- **Applied skills**: identify the notification trigger and deadline for a use of force or covert action; apply the correct classification level and damage standard; recognize when an activity needs a Finding; preserve records that oversight will later demand.
- **Common traps**: treating the 60-day clock as a hard ceiling on presidential action (practice is contested) or ignoring the 48-hour report entirely; over- or under-classifying; running an activity "outside" both Title 10 and Title 50 as if that were an authority; deleting working records and calling it institutional memory.

## Chapter 7: Crisis Management and Decision Tempo
- **Core questions**: What changed since the last update? What decision cannot wait? What is the public line, and does it match classified reality?
- **Key concepts**: crisis rhythm and battle rhythm, the situation report, decision speed vs. completeness, the escalation ladder under time pressure, the public line, congressional and allied notification, after-action review and the Federal Records Act.
- **Applied skills**: write a crisis update that leads with what changed, implications, decisions needed, public line, and next review; bound uncertainty under press pressure (confirmed vs. unverified); run a lightweight crisis-record process that survives the adrenaline.
- **Common traps**: repeating yesterday's text; "the situation remains dynamic" as non-information; a confident casualty guess that forces a retraction; a public line that contradicts classified reporting; leaking to win one news cycle; AAR records scattered across chats.

## Chapter 8: Policy Writing for Decision Makers
- **Core questions**: What is the one decision this document asks for? Can a principal read it at decision speed? Does every sentence earn its place?
- **Key concepts**: bottom-line-up-front (BLUF), the decision question on page one, options-and-recommendation structure, separating evidence from judgment in prose, tailoring to the tier (PCC vs. DC vs. PC), concision and the cost of a reader's attention.
- **Applied skills**: front-load a packet with the decision question and recommendation; compress 40 pages of background into a one-page decision memo; write a public line that is true, brief, and durable.
- **Common traps**: background-to-the-horizon with no decision point; the recommendation hidden in a footnote; pseudo-precise percentages standing in for reasoned confidence; length as a substitute for relevance; tonal drama instead of analytic substance.

## Capstone
Produce an integrated national security policy package for a fictional fast-moving crisis. Deliverables, in a single coherent chain with shared assumptions and one explicit decision question: (1) a threat-and-interest brief; (2) an intelligence-to-policy note applying ICD 203 confidence language; (3) a DIME options matrix with second-order effects and required legal authorities; (4) an interagency decision memo routed to the correct NSC tier with a named implementation owner and Title 10 / Title 50 classification; and (5) a crisis update with a public line reconciled to the classified picture. The package is graded on coherence across the chain — a principal should be able to read any one product and trace it back to the interest and the evidence — and on legal awareness: the right authority, the right notification, the right classification.
