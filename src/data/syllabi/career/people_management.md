# People Management
**ID**: `peopleManagement` · **Discipline**: Leadership · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course builds the operating system a first-line manager actually runs day to day: setting expectations, delegating cleanly, diagnosing performance, coaching, running useful 1:1s, handling conflict, hiring well, and improving the work system instead of becoming its heroic bottleneck. It treats management as a learnable craft with named frameworks and observable behaviors, not as a personality trait or a reward for being the best individual contributor.

The throughline is the shift from doing the work to owning the outcomes the team produces. A manager succeeds through other people, which means their leverage comes from clarity (people know what good looks like and who decides), capability (people can do the work and grow), and conditions (the system, safety, and motivation that let people do their best work). When a team underperforms, a strong manager runs a diagnosis before reaching for a fix — is this a skill gap, a will gap, a clarity gap, or a system gap? — because the wrong diagnosis produces the wrong, often unfair, intervention.

The voice is adult and pragmatic. We name the real traps: avoiding the hard conversation until it festers, confusing being liked with being trusted, micromanaging in the name of quality, rescuing work instead of building capability, hiring on "vibe," and treating a single incident as a pattern. By the end, a learner should be able to run a humane, high-accountability team and explain *why* each move works using the underlying framework.

## Course Design Notes
The prior syllabus had solid practical bones — expectations, feedback, coaching, delegation, hiring, conflict, scale — but it left the frameworks implicit and folded "common traps" into prose. This rewrite makes the canonical models explicit and groundable for assessment: Situational Leadership (Hersey-Blanchard), the SBI feedback model (CCL), the GROW coaching model, Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan), Edmondson's four stages of psychological safety, RACI, OKR-vs-KPI, and the ADKAR change model (Prosci). Each chapter now carries explicit Common Traps so distractors and lessons can target the specific failure mode, and adds coverage the original lacked: performance calibration, fairness in formal action (PIP), remote/hybrid management, and motivation theory. Chapters move from the manager's role outward through individual capability, team conditions, hiring, and finally scale and change.

## Chapter 1: The Manager Job
**Core questions**
- What is a manager accountable for that an individual contributor is not?
- Which decisions belong to the manager, the team, or the company, and how do you make decision rights explicit?
- Where does helpful support quietly become bottleneck behavior?

**Key concepts**: outcome ownership vs. task execution; decision rights; RACI (Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed, with exactly one Accountable per item); the psychological contract; trust as reliability plus transparency; the manager operating charter.

**Applied skills**: write a manager operating charter (purpose, standards, decision rights, meeting norms, escalation rules); build a RACI for a cross-functional deliverable; reset a commitment transparently when you miss it.

**Common traps**: trying to stay the best IC instead of multiplying the team; leaving ownership ambiguous ("are you owning this or helping?"); assigning two Accountable parties (shared accountability becomes no accountability); breaking a commitment silently rather than re-contracting it.

## Chapter 2: Goals, Priorities, and Operating Rhythm
**Core questions**
- What matters most this week, this month, this quarter, and how would the team know if it changed?
- Which meetings create clarity and which hide confusion?
- What is the difference between an OKR and a KPI, and when do you use each?

**Key concepts**: specific measurable goals with a definition of done; OKRs (ambitious objective + measurable key results, drive change) vs. KPIs (ongoing health metrics); prioritization under finite capacity; operating cadence (1:1s, team meeting, async update, decision log); making priority changes visible.

**Applied skills**: rewrite a vague aim ("improve onboarding soon") into a dated, measurable goal with constraints; design a team operating rhythm; keep a decision log.

**Common traps**: goals that are tautological or "vibes, but professional"; status-theater 1:1s that just re-read the tracker; confusing activity metrics with outcomes; changing priorities without telling anyone.

## Chapter 3: Delegation and Ownership
**Core questions**
- What outcome (not just task) is being delegated, and who holds final authority?
- How much autonomy fits this person's skill and the work's risk?
- What check-in prevents drift without micromanaging?

**Key concepts**: delegating outcomes with context, decision boundaries, and escalation triggers; matching autonomy to demonstrated capability; Situational Leadership (Hersey-Blanchard) — Directing (S1), Coaching (S2), Supporting (S3), Delegating (S4) matched to follower readiness; the cost of rescuing.

**Applied skills**: write a delegation brief (outcome, why it matters, authority, constraints, support, timeline, review points); choose the right leadership style for a given person/task.

**Common traps**: "dumping" (no context or success criteria) vs. delegating; abandoning (delegate then disappear); reclaiming work the moment it gets messy, which teaches the team to wait instead of own; delegating with subjective acceptance criteria ("I'll know good when I feel it").

## Chapter 4: Feedback, Coaching, and Performance Diagnosis
**Core questions**
- Is this a skill gap, a will (motivation) gap, a clarity gap, or a system gap?
- What specific behavior needs to change, and what support is fair?
- When do you direct (give answers) versus coach (ask questions)?

**Key concepts**: performance diagnosis (skill / will / clarity / system); SBI feedback model (Situation, Behavior, Impact) from CCL, optionally SBI-I to inquire about intent; GROW coaching model (Goal, Reality, Options, Will/Way forward); coaching vs. directing as a function of skill, urgency, and risk; recognition as a deliberate practice.

**Applied skills**: deliver feedback using SBI with a clear next step; run a GROW conversation; diagnose a motivated person failing a new tool (skill gap, not attitude); document an expectation and schedule follow-up.

**Common traps**: labels instead of behavior ("you were weird in the meeting"); misdiagnosing a skill gap as an attitude problem; coaching when the situation actually needs clear direction (or vice versa); feedback with no follow-up; saving feedback for the annual review.

## Chapter 5: Difficult Conversations, Conflict, and Fair Process
**Core questions**
- What is the conflict about on the surface, and what interest is underneath?
- What needs to be said clearly but respectfully, and how do you separate facts from story?
- When does an issue require HR, legal, or senior escalation?

**Key concepts**: interests vs. positions; separating goals, facts, constraints, and decision authority; emotional regulation and repair; proportional response; documentation and fair process; when to escalate; the avoidance tax (problems grow and get less fair when ducked).

**Applied skills**: prepare a difficult-conversation plan (facts, desired outcome, likely reactions, boundaries, follow-up); mediate a two-lead priority conflict by clarifying shared goal and decision owner first; recognize escalation triggers (harassment, safety, legal exposure).

**Common traps**: treating volume as data ("pick the louder lead"); suppressing or "outlawing" conflict instead of resolving it; deciding privately with no explanation to either party; delaying because it feels awkward; using a single incident to justify formal action.

## Chapter 6: Performance Management and Fairness
**Core questions**
- Is there a documented *pattern*, and is the response proportionate to severity and context?
- What makes a performance improvement plan fair rather than a paper trail for exit?
- How do calibration sessions reduce rating bias across managers?

**Key concepts**: pattern vs. incident; proportionality; the performance improvement plan (PIP) as a genuine support-and-clarity tool — specific expectations, support, timeline, and review, not a foregone conclusion; documentation; calibration to align standards across managers and check for bias (recency, halo, leniency).

**Applied skills**: distinguish a coachable one-off from a pattern requiring formal action; structure a fair PIP; contribute evidence to a calibration discussion.

**Common traps**: launching a formal plan after one visible mistake; recency and halo bias in ratings; inconsistent standards between managers; using vague hints in group settings instead of direct private feedback; documenting nothing, then acting suddenly.

## Chapter 7: Hiring, Onboarding, and Team Design
**Core questions**
- What problem is this role meant to solve, and what evidence predicts success?
- What is interview theater versus job-relevant signal?
- How does a new hire become productive and connected?

**Key concepts**: role scorecard; structured interviews (same questions, same order, scored on a rubric) — roughly twice the predictive validity of unstructured interviews and a reducer of bias; behavioral evidence over impressions; structured debrief; 30/60/90 onboarding (context, first outcomes, key relationships, systems access, check-in rhythm); team topology and succession.

**Applied skills**: build a role scorecard and structured interview guide with a scoring rubric; run a debrief grounded in behavioral evidence; design a 30/60/90 plan.

**Common traps**: hiring on unnamed "vibe" (a vector for bias); rating buzzword frequency as competence; unstructured interviews that mostly measure interviewer comfort; link-dump onboarding with no context; explaining edge cases before basics.

## Chapter 8: Inclusion, Motivation, and Team Health
**Core questions**
- Who is being heard, interrupted, overloaded, or left out?
- What is actually draining motivation, and is it autonomy, competence, or relatedness?
- Where does the team need more psychological safety, and where does it need clearer accountability?

**Key concepts**: Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan) — autonomy, competence, relatedness as drivers of intrinsic motivation; Edmondson's psychological safety (shared belief the team is safe for interpersonal risk) and its four stages (inclusion, learner, contributor, challenger); safety is not the opposite of accountability — high-performing teams have both; burnout and workload signals; inclusive meeting design; recognition.

**Applied skills**: build a team-health view (engagement signals, workload risk, inclusion checks, actions); structure participation so hierarchy doesn't silence newer voices; diagnose a motivation drop against the three SDT needs.

**Common traps**: telling quieter people to "interrupt better" (puts the cost on the wrong party); treating psychological safety as lowered standards or "being nice"; reading politeness or quiet as agreement; addressing a meaning problem with a tools tutorial.

## Chapter 9: Managing Change and Scaling Yourself
**Core questions**
- What change are we asking people to make, and why would it be hard for them specifically?
- What should the manager stop doing as the team grows?
- What process should improve before adding more people?

**Key concepts**: ADKAR (Prosci) — Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement, where progress stalls at the first missing element (the barrier point); resistance as information; retrospectives and learning loops; managing managers via goals, rhythm, coaching, and calibration rather than direct task control; remote/hybrid management (outcomes over presence, deliberate communication, async clarity); delegation layers and succession.

**Applied skills**: diagnose an ADKAR barrier point and target the right intervention (e.g., low Desire is not fixed by more training); run a retrospective and convert lessons into one operating-system improvement; adapt 1:1s and visibility for remote/hybrid teams.

**Common traps**: pushing more Knowledge when the real gap is Desire; managing managers by reviewing every task (IC behavior that breaks at scale); equating remote presence/online status with productivity; adding people to a broken process; abandoning standards as scope grows.

## Capstone: Manager Operating System
Learners assemble and defend a coherent operating system for a real or realistic team:
- manager operating charter with decision rights and a RACI for one key deliverable
- team operating rhythm and a decision log template
- a delegation brief calibrated with Situational Leadership
- an SBI feedback example and a GROW coaching plan
- a difficult-conversation plan with escalation triggers
- a role scorecard, structured interview guide, and 30/60/90 onboarding plan
- a team-health view tied to SDT and psychological safety
- a change plan diagnosed with ADKAR and a retrospective-driven improvement memo

Assessment emphasizes diagnosis before prescription, fair and evidence-based process, and the ability to name *why* each move works.
