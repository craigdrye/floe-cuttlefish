# Project Management
**ID**: `projectManagement` · **Discipline**: Career · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Build the practical muscle to drive a project from a vague request to a delivered, accepted outcome. By the end you can define scope, align stakeholders, plan and sequence work, manage risk and change, read the numbers (critical path, float, earned value), choose a delivery method that fits the work, and recover credibly when the plan meets reality.

This is the working-professional sibling of a PMP course: fewer acronyms memorized for their own sake, more judgment you can defend in a status meeting. Where a concept has a real definition or formula — critical path, float, CPI/SPI, PERT, Sprint timeboxes, RACI's one-Accountable rule — the course teaches it precisely, because half of project failure is people using a term loosely and discovering too late that they meant different things.

The throughline is honesty under pressure: surfacing assumptions before they break, making tradeoffs visible before they are forced, and reporting status that tells leadership the truth in time to act. A green dashboard that hides a blocked dependency is not optimism; it is a deferred incident.

## Course Design Notes
This course spans initiation, scope, stakeholders, schedule, risk, change, delivery methods (predictive, agile, hybrid), quality and vendors, and recovery — closing with a capstone control room. It deliberately mixes two question styles: scenario-judgment items (what should the PM actually do?) and a smaller core of definitional and quantitative items (compute the critical path, interpret a CPI of 0.8, apply the PERT weighting) so learners leave with both instinct and vocabulary. Common Traps in each chapter name the specific way smart teams get this wrong in the field.

## Chapter 1: Project Thinking and Initiation
**Key concepts**: project vs operation, objective, deliverable, sponsor, stakeholder, constraints, assumptions, success criteria, charter, business case.

**Practical questions**
- What problem is this project solving?
- Who owns the outcome, and who can block the work?
- What does "done" mean in measurable terms?

**Workplace skill**
- Write a project charter with purpose, scope, success criteria, milestones, assumptions, risks, and decision rights.

**Common traps**: starting to schedule tasks before the deliverable is defined; adjectives ("make it amazing") posing as success criteria; no named sponsor with authority to unblock; assumptions left implicit so nobody owns checking them.

## Chapter 2: Scope, Requirements, and Work Breakdown
**Key concepts**: scope statement, requirements, acceptance criteria, WBS, work package, dependency, milestone, deliverable, non-goal, change request.

**Practical questions**
- What is included, excluded, uncertain, or negotiable?
- Which requirement is really a solution disguised as a need?
- What work packages make ownership visible?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a WBS and scope boundary statement for a messy cross-functional project.

**Common traps**: confusing requirements (the need) with solutions (one way to meet it); a WBS that lists activities instead of deliverables; no explicit non-goals, so "out of scope" is a verbal memory; gold-plating — helpful extra work that quietly consumes the testing buffer.

## Chapter 3: Stakeholders, Communication, and Operating Rhythm
**Key concepts**: stakeholder map, RACI, communication plan, meeting cadence, status report, decision log, action log, escalation, governance.

**Practical questions**
- Who needs to know, decide, approve, do, review, or be reassured?
- Which meetings create decisions, and which are just calendar fog?
- What status signal would change leadership behavior?

**Common concepts**: the RACI golden rule — exactly one Accountable per deliverable; multiple Responsible doers are fine, but shared accountability means no accountability.

**Workplace skill**
- Design a project operating rhythm: meetings, artifacts, updates, owners, and escalation rules.

**Common traps**: meetings spent reading status that is already in the tracker; action items with no owner, date, or definition of done; two Accountable owners (or zero) on a RACI; escalating with a red emoji instead of options, impact, and a recommended decision.

## Chapter 4: Schedule, Resources, and Critical Path
**Key concepts**: activity list, dependency, sequencing, critical path, total float/slack (a task's float is how long it can slip without delaying the project; critical-path tasks have zero float), PERT three-point estimate ((O + 4M + P) / 6), fast-tracking vs crashing, bottleneck, resource contention, buffer, milestone plan, Gantt chart, Kanban board, WIP limits.

**Practical questions**
- Which task actually determines the finish date, and how much slack do the others have?
- Where are we pretending people have infinite time?
- Which delay needs resequencing, de-scoping, fast-tracking, crashing, or escalation?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a dependency map and identify critical path, float, bottlenecks, and schedule recovery options.

**Common traps**: assuming the longest single task is the critical path (it is the longest dependency chain); treating parallel bars on a Gantt chart as parallel work when they share one person; padding every estimate instead of placing one transparent buffer against real uncertainty; confusing fast-tracking (overlap, adds risk) with crashing (add resources, adds cost).

## Chapter 5: Risk, Issues, and Change Control
**Key concepts**: risk vs issue (a risk has not happened yet; an issue has), risk register, probability, impact, qualitative risk score (probability x impact), trigger, owner, the four response strategies (avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept), contingency vs management reserve, issue log, root cause, change request, integrated change control, impact analysis.

**Practical questions**
- What might happen (risk), what already happened (issue), and what must be decided?
- Which risk deserves action now given probability and impact?
- What is the scope/schedule/cost/quality impact of this change, and who is authorized to approve it?

**Workplace skill**
- Create a risk register and change-request packet with options, tradeoffs, and recommendation.

**Common traps**: logging a risk with no owner or response, so the register becomes a museum; treating an active issue as a risk (and vice versa); accepting scope changes informally because the phrasing was casual ("while we're in there"); change control that bans changes instead of pricing them; buying down a risk with mitigation but never naming the trigger that activates the contingency.

## Chapter 6: Delivery Methods: Predictive, Agile, and Hybrid
**Key concepts**: predictive/waterfall, agile, the Agile Manifesto's four values, Scrum (Sprint of one month or less; the three accountabilities — Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers; the events — Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum at 15 minutes, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective; the artifacts — Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment, and their commitments), Kanban and WIP limits, Little's Law (cycle time = WIP / throughput), backlog, sprint, release, iteration, velocity vs throughput, hybrid governance, tailoring.

**Practical questions**
- Is the work stable and well-understood enough to plan deeply upfront, or volatile enough to demand iteration?
- Where do we need user feedback before committing the next increment?
- How do we keep governance and reporting without freezing learning?

**Workplace skill**
- Select a delivery approach for three project scenarios (stable construction, uncertain product, regulated-but-iterative) and defend the choice.

**Common traps**: choosing agile for the label while keeping a fixed scope, date, and budget (that is waterfall in costume); the Scrum Master acting as a status-collecting taskmaster instead of a coach who removes impediments; extending the Daily Scrum into a planning meeting; equating velocity across teams as if it were a universal productivity score; using Kanban with no WIP limit, which is just a to-do list on a wall.

## Chapter 7: Quality, Vendors, and Implementation
**Key concepts**: quality criteria, testing, acceptance, procurement, contract type, vendor management, implementation plan, training, rollout, adoption, handover.

**Practical questions**
- What does quality mean before someone complains?
- Which vendor dependency needs a contract, SLA, or inspection point?
- Who has to change behavior after delivery?

**Common concepts**: contract types and where risk sits — firm fixed price (seller carries cost risk), cost-plus/cost-reimbursable (buyer carries cost risk), time and materials (buyer carries risk, often capped with a not-to-exceed clause); SLA, escalation path, acceptance criteria, operational handover.

**Workplace skill**
- Build an implementation checklist covering testing, training, communications, support, vendor handoff, and acceptance.

**Common traps**: accepting "timely support" with no SLA, escalation path, or response-time numbers; signing cost-plus when scope is actually well-defined (giving away cost discipline); declaring go-live a success while support learns about new error codes from the first customer call; quality defined only by absence of complaints rather than explicit acceptance criteria; no named owner of the result after handover.

## Chapter 8: Status, Recovery, and Retrospectives
**Key concepts**: red/yellow/green status, watermelon status (green outside, red inside), variance, earned value basics — CPI = EV / AC and SPI = EV / PV, where below 1 is unfavorable; forecast/EAC; corrective action, decision memo, lessons learned, retrospective, after-action review, blameless culture.

**Practical questions**
- Is the project off track, or did the plan lie?
- What do the numbers say — is a CPI of 0.8 a rounding issue or a budget alarm?
- What decision is needed from whom by when?
- What should future projects learn from this one as a concrete process change?

**Workplace skill**
- Write an executive status update that distinguishes facts, risks, decisions, tradeoffs, and next actions.

**Common traps**: watermelon dashboards where aggregate green hides a blocked critical-path task; reporting activity volume (tasks completed) instead of delivery risk; reading SPI > 1 as "ahead" without noticing the work ahead is off the critical path; retrospectives that produce a list of villains or a vague "communicate better" instead of a named process change; recovering schedule by silently cutting testing.

## Capstone: Project Control Room
Learners manage a simulated project with shifting requirements, stakeholder pressure, vendor delay, quality issue, and timeline constraint. Final deliverables include:
- project charter
- WBS and scope statement
- stakeholder/RACI map
- communication rhythm
- schedule and critical path
- risk register
- change request
- status dashboard
- implementation checklist
- retrospective memo
