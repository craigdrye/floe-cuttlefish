# SHRM: People Ops Maze
**ID**: `shrmPeople` · **Discipline**: Leadership / Human Resources · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course prepares you to think and act like a credible HR professional — and, along the way, to pass the SHRM Certified Professional (SHRM-CP) or Senior Certified Professional (SHRM-SCP) exam — without pretending those are two different skills. The SHRM exams are unusual: roughly half the scored questions are situational judgment items that ask not "what is the rule" but "what is the *most effective* thing to do here," graded against what experienced practitioners actually chose. That format punishes the candidate who memorized definitions and rewards the one who has internalized a way of seeing the organization: people are not flowcharts, the business has to keep running, the law sets hard edges, and somebody has to make the defensible call with a paper trail behind it.

The spine of the course follows SHRM's own framework — the Body of Applied Skills and Knowledge (BASK) — but it refuses to teach the framework as a glossary. You will learn the eight behavioral competencies (organized into the Leadership, Interpersonal, and Business clusters) and the three HR knowledge domains (People, Organization, Workplace) that hold the 14 functional areas, and you will turn each into something an HR professional produces: a scenario triage note, a role scorecard, a change-readiness brief, an investigation memo, an HR dashboard, a board-ready people-risk read. The recurring discipline is the gap between an answer that is *technically correct* and one that is correct, lawful, humane, and aligned to the business at the same time — because the SHRM exam, and the job, both live in that gap.

By the end you should be able to take a messy people problem — an attrition spike, a harassment complaint, a manager demanding an immediate firing, an engagement score that dropped — and do four things a hiring manager and the exam both want: name what *kind* of problem it actually is (operational, strategic, legal, or interpersonal), identify the obligation and the missing facts, choose a proportionate action that protects people without becoming management's rubber stamp, and document it so a regulator, a plaintiff's lawyer, and a CEO would all read the same record the same way. Tools and HRIS platforms differ between employers; this reasoning, and the discipline of the defensible record behind every decision, is what travels with you.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when learners want the *generalist HR / SHRM-certification* track: the BASK behavioral competencies and the People, Organization, and Workplace knowledge domains, framed around the SHRM-CP and SHRM-SCP exams and the day-to-day judgment of an HR business partner. Keep it distinct from the deep specialist tracks elsewhere in Floe — detailed US employment-law litigation, payroll/comp modeling, or org-design consulting belong in their own courses; this one teaches the cross-cutting HR judgment and treats those as obligations the generalist must spot, coordinate, and escalate rather than litigate in depth.

The voice is a seasoned HR business partner mentoring a capable person moving from a coordinator or line-management role into the HR function — concrete (a real complaint, a real req, a real exam stem), plain-language about what each competency and rule is *for*, no acronym left undefined on first use, and relentlessly focused on the *defensible, humane decision and the evidence behind it*. Every chapter carries **Common traps**, because in HR the expensive error is almost never exotic: it is the manager who fires before the file supports it, the "culture fit" judgment that is really a disparate-impact problem, the engagement survey that measures activity instead of behavior, the disclosure that is correct but cruel, or the HR person who becomes either management's enforcer or the employees' lawyer instead of a partner with a spine. Keep facts current and frame the moving parts honestly. As of 2026, SHRM updated the BASK: it now defines **eight** behavioral competencies, having merged the former Diversity, Equity & Inclusion and Global & Cultural Effectiveness competencies into a single **Inclusive Mindset**, and it expanded AI-in-HR content across talent acquisition, learning, technology management, and analytics. Teach the durable principle (fair, consistent, documented, business-aware) and flag that competency labels, exam item counts, and fees are set by SHRM and change — point learners to the current BASK and handbook rather than to a number memorized here. Distractors should be plausible-but-flawed: the calls a real HR professional might mistakenly make (act without facts, over-promise, ignore the trend, escalate nothing or everything) — not silly.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The SHRM-CP and SHRM-SCP are computer-delivered exams of **134 scored multiple-choice questions plus a set of unscored field-test items**, taken over roughly **3 hours 40 minutes in two timed sections**, and reported on a **scaled score of 120–200**, with a passing standard set by SHRM (and set differently for the operational SHRM-CP and the strategic SHRM-SCP). Verify the exact counts, time, and fees against the current SHRM handbook — these are SHRM's to change.

Two facts about the *mix* should shape how you study. First, the scored items split into two formats: **knowledge items** (KBIs) that test mastery of HR knowledge and the foundational concepts behind the behavioral competencies, and **situational judgment items** (SJIs) that hand you a workplace scenario and ask which action is most (or least) effective. Roughly **40% of the exam is SJIs**, about **10% is foundational behavioral-competency knowledge**, and about **50% is knowledge items covering the 14 HR functional areas** — and overall, half the exam is allocated across the three behavioral-competency clusters and half across the three knowledge domains. Second, the SJIs are where line-experienced candidates lose points: they know the policy but pick the answer that is technically defensible yet second-best on effectiveness, escalation order, or stakeholder impact.

A workable rhythm: spend the first third building the *map* — the three knowledge domains, the 14 functional areas, and the eight competencies — until you can place any topic without hunting. Spend the middle third on the knowledge items, learning each functional area tied to a fact pattern rather than a definition. Spend the last third drilling SJIs against the clock: read every stem for *role, authority, urgency, and missing facts*, eliminate the extreme answers first, and after each miss write one sentence on *why the better answer was better*. The exam rewards the candidate who reasons from effectiveness and the SHRM lens, not the one who memorized the most.

## Chapter 1: SHRM Decision Logic and HR's Role
- **Core questions**: What *kind* of problem is this — an operational HR task, a strategic workforce issue, a legal risk, or an interpersonal judgment test? What answer best supports the organization while staying lawful, fair, and humane? What facts are missing before HR should act at all?
- **Key concepts**: the SHRM credentials (SHRM-CP for operational roles, SHRM-SCP for strategic ones) and the BASK as the exam blueprint; the "most effective action" logic of situational judgment versus the "what is correct" logic of knowledge items; HR as a business, legal, ethical, and human system simultaneously; evidence-based HR (decide from data and policy, not vibes); stakeholder balancing (employee, manager, organization, and the law all have a claim); the difference between HR as partner, as cop, and as employee advocate, and why the durable answer is partner-with-a-spine.
- **Applied skills**: write a scenario triage note that captures the issue, the affected people, the policy basis, the legal flag, the business impact, the missing facts, and the next action; classify a raw HR problem as operational, strategic, legal, or interpersonal and justify the call.
- **Common traps**: jumping to a remedy before naming what kind of problem it is; acting before gathering the facts the stem deliberately left out; picking the answer that protects HR rather than the one that resolves the situation; becoming either management's enforcer or the employee's lawyer instead of the organization's honest broker.

## Chapter 2: People Domain — Talent Acquisition and Onboarding
- **Core questions**: Does this selection process actually measure what the job requires, or does it measure who interviews well? Where in the hiring funnel does legal risk or a fairness problem enter? What does a new hire need in the first 90 days to become productive and stay?
- **Key concepts**: job and competency analysis as the foundation of every defensible hiring decision; workforce and succession planning; sourcing and the candidate experience; structured, job-related selection (validity, reliability, and the danger of unstructured "gut" interviews); the legal-defensibility lens on every screen (job-relatedness, consistency, and avoiding criteria that create adverse impact); onboarding as performance enablement, not paperwork; AI in talent acquisition — résumé screening and assessment tools that can encode bias and must be validated and monitored.
- **Applied skills**: turn a job analysis into a role scorecard and a structured interview plan tied to competencies, legal defensibility, and candidate experience; audit a hiring process to find the step where a neutral-looking criterion could screen out a protected group.
- **Common traps**: hiring for "culture fit" when the real criterion is "people like us"; unstructured interviews that feel insightful but predict nothing and aren't defensible; assuming an AI screening tool is neutral because it's a machine; treating onboarding as forms rather than the first 90 days of retention.

## Chapter 3: People Domain — Total Rewards, Performance, and Retention
- **Core questions**: Is this a pay problem, a manager problem, a workload problem, or a meaning problem — and how would I tell them apart? Does this reward or retention lever fit the actual business problem, or does it just sound generous? Is performance management driving improvement or just generating ratings?
- **Key concepts**: total rewards as a system (base pay, variable pay, benefits, recognition, and the non-cash value of growth and flexibility); internal equity and external competitiveness, and pay-transparency expectations; performance management designed for behavior change rather than score distribution; the difference between a retention problem rooted in compensation and one rooted in management or development; the cost and signal of attrition; recognition as a low-cost, high-signal lever; the legal edges around pay (equal-pay and pay-equity exposure).
- **Applied skills**: diagnose an attrition or engagement problem by separating the compensation hypothesis from the manager and growth hypotheses, naming the data that would distinguish them; recommend a reward or retention lever matched to the diagnosed cause and defend why a cheaper lever might beat a raise.
- **Common traps**: throwing money at a problem caused by a bad manager; assuming a raise fixes a meaning or workload problem; designing performance reviews that rank people instead of developing them; ignoring pay-equity exposure when adjusting compensation case by case.

## Chapter 4: Organization Domain — Strategy, Structure, and Change
- **Core questions**: What people risk could quietly block this strategy? Which change barrier is structural, cultural, managerial, or just bad communication? How will we *know* a training or engagement effort actually changed behavior rather than completion counts?
- **Key concepts**: organizational effectiveness and design (structure, roles, spans, and how they enable or strangle strategy); HR strategy and business acumen (reading the P&L and the operating model well enough to be in the room); change management frameworks and the human side of change (resistance as information, not insubordination); culture and engagement as measurable, movable conditions; leadership development and succession; learning transfer — the difference between training delivered and behavior changed (Kirkpatrick-style evaluation beyond "smile sheets").
- **Applied skills**: write a change-readiness brief that names stakeholders, the type of each barrier, a communication plan, the learning supports, and the behavioral success measures; tie a proposed people initiative to a specific business outcome a CFO would recognize.
- **Common traps**: treating resistance as a discipline problem instead of a signal about the change; measuring training by completion rate rather than behavior or business outcome; running a reorg on the org chart while ignoring the culture that will eat it; pitching HR initiatives in HR language no executive can act on.

## Chapter 5: Workplace Domain — Employment Law and Compliance Hygiene
- **Core questions**: Is this a policy matter, a legal risk, an employee-relations risk, or an urgent safety concern — and does mislabeling it change what I must do? What documentation has to exist *before* HR acts? Does a practice that looks neutral create disparate impact or an access barrier?
- **Key concepts**: the major US employment-law families the generalist must spot (anti-discrimination and protected classes; harassment and retaliation; reasonable accommodation and the interactive process; wage-and-hour and exempt/non-exempt classification; protected leave; employee privacy and recordkeeping); the distinction between **disparate treatment** (intentional) and **disparate impact** (a neutral practice with discriminatory effect); the interactive accommodation process as a duty, not a favor; retaliation as the claim that most often outlives the original complaint; the generalist's job as spotting the obligation and escalating, not litigating it.
- **Applied skills**: build a compliance checklist for a hiring, leave, accommodation, discipline, or termination scenario that lists the obligation, the required documentation, and the escalation point; classify a fact pattern as disparate treatment, disparate impact, or neither and name the missing evidence.
- **Common traps**: calling a problem "performance" when the timeline screams retaliation; skipping the interactive process and jumping to "we can't accommodate that"; treating a clean disparity statistic as proof or as nothing (it frames a question, it doesn't settle one); documenting after the decision instead of before it.

## Chapter 6: Workplace Domain — Inclusion, Safety, Risk, and the Global Lens
- **Core questions**: Is this practice expanding access and fairness or quietly narrowing it? Where does a workplace risk cross from a culture issue into a safety or legal obligation? When does a global workforce change which rules even apply?
- **Key concepts**: the 2026 BASK's **Inclusive Mindset** competency — the merger of the former diversity/equity/inclusion and global/cultural-effectiveness competencies — and what it asks of HR beyond representation metrics (equitable systems, belonging, and inclusive design of every people process); workplace safety and the duty to provide a safe environment; risk management and business continuity from the people angle; data privacy and the responsible use of employee data and AI monitoring tools; the global lens — jurisdiction, cultural difference, and the reality that a US-centric policy can be illegal or insulting elsewhere.
- **Applied skills**: review a hiring, promotion, or pay process for inclusive design and name the specific step where access narrows; flag where a workplace concern crosses from culture into a safety or legal duty that changes HR's obligations.
- **Common traps**: treating inclusion as a representation dashboard rather than the fairness of the underlying systems; exporting a US policy to a global workforce without checking local law and norms; deploying employee-monitoring or AI tools without a privacy and proportionality check; mistaking a recurring "personality clash" for the harassment or safety issue it actually is.

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Competencies in Practice
- **Core questions**: Which response de-escalates while preserving accountability? When should HR advise, coach, investigate, escalate, or stop the process entirely? What answer shows true partnership without becoming a rubber stamp?
- **Key concepts**: the eight BASK behavioral competencies and their clusters — **Leadership** (Leadership & Navigation, Ethical Practice, Inclusive Mindset), **Interpersonal** (Relationship Management, Communication), and **Business** (Business Acumen, Consultation, Analytical Aptitude) — plus the technical HR Expertise that underpins them; ethical practice as the willingness to be the friction when something is wrong; consultation as diagnosing before prescribing; communication that is simultaneously correct, clear, and humane; the "most effective action" logic of SJIs and why the best answer usually gathers facts, follows policy, protects people, and escalates serious risk rather than swinging to an extreme.
- **Applied skills**: write two versions of the same HR response — one technically correct but clumsy, and one that is correct, humane, clear, and business-aware — and explain what the second one does that the first doesn't; map a messy scenario to the competencies it actually tests.
- **Common traps**: confusing being "nice" with ethical practice (sometimes the ethical act is uncomfortable); prescribing a solution before diagnosing the problem; choosing the answer that avoids conflict over the one that resolves the situation; on SJIs, picking the dramatic action (fire, escalate to legal, do nothing) when the effective answer is the measured middle.

## Chapter 8: Data, Metrics, and Evidence-Based HR
- **Core questions**: Which metric actually answers the business question being asked? What data would distinguish a manager problem from a compensation problem? Where might a metric quietly incentivize the wrong behavior?
- **Key concepts**: core HR metrics (turnover and retention, time-to-fill, quality of hire, engagement, absenteeism) and the difference between **leading** and **lagging** indicators; the adverse-impact ratio (the four-fifths rule of thumb) as a fairness signal that frames a question rather than proving a violation; training evaluation beyond completion rates; HR dashboards that pair an activity number with a quality number so the story is honest; the analytical-aptitude competency and the responsible use of people analytics and AI (correlation is not cause, and a biased input makes a biased model).
- **Applied skills**: build a small HR dashboard with interpretation notes, stated limitations, and recommended actions; pick the single metric that answers a given business question and explain why the obvious alternative misleads.
- **Common traps**: reporting activity (calls handled, training completed) as if it measured impact; treating a correlation in people data as causation; setting a metric that drives gaming (fill the req fast, quality be damned); presenting a disparity statistic as a verdict instead of a prompt to investigate.

## Chapter 9: Employee Relations Lab
- **Core questions**: What, stated precisely, am I investigating — and what evidence would confirm *or* disprove it? Does the documentation support the action the manager wants, or only the manager's mood? Could an independent reviewer reach my conclusion from the file alone and call the process fair?
- **Key concepts**: workplace investigations (intake, scope, witness sequencing, credibility assessment, and the discipline of knowing when to stop); documentation that separates facts from allegations from conclusions; progressive and just-cause discipline applied consistently across seniority levels; coaching versus discipline; conflict resolution and psychological safety; confidentiality and its limits; retaliation prevention as an active duty after any complaint.
- **Scenario work** (run each against the framework above): a harassment complaint with unclear or conflicting witnesses; a manager demanding an immediate termination on weak documentation; an accommodation request that arrives *after* performance warnings; a recurring team conflict being relabeled as a "culture fit" problem.
- **Applied skills**: draft an investigation plan and a closeout memo that separate facts, findings, policy, credibility, and recommended action, and that an outside reviewer could follow; tell a manager "no, not yet, and here's what we need first" without losing the partnership.
- **Common traps**: anchoring on the first hypothesis and reading every fact as confirmation; recording the conclusion without the evidence that produced it; inconsistent discipline that protects the senior subject and punishes the junior one; failing to manage retaliation risk after a complaint closes; granting a termination because the manager is loud rather than because the file supports it.

## Chapter 10: Exam Survival and Scenario Judgment
- **Core questions**: What is this stem really asking — most effective, least effective, or what to do *first*? What does the role, authority, and urgency in the scenario tell me to rule out? Which answer reflects the SHRM lens (lawful, fair, documented, business-aware) rather than a personal hunch?
- **Key concepts**: situational-judgment item logic (effectiveness, not legality alone, is the grading dimension); knowledge-item discipline for the 14 functional areas; proportional response and correct escalation order; reading stems for role, authority, urgency, and missing facts; the "extremes are usually wrong" heuristic (ignore it / fire everyone / promise anything / act without facts); building a personal study map from your weakest domains.
- **Applied skills**: dissect an SJI stem into role, authority, urgency, and missing facts before reading the options; build a personal study map listing weak domains, recurring trap types, a review cadence, a practice-question log, and a retake-prevention plan.
- **Common traps**: answering the question you wish were asked (legality) when the stem asks effectiveness; on a "do first" item, picking a correct-but-later step; bringing a single employer's HR practice to a national exam (answer from the SHRM lens, not "how we do it here"); over-escalating every scenario to legal when a measured HR action is the intended best answer.

## Capstone: People Ops Maze Simulation
Take a single fictional employer and a messy, intertwined HR file — an attrition spike concentrated in one department, a cluster of manager complaints, a probable accommodation request that arrived after a performance warning, an engagement score that dropped, real compensation pressure, and a leadership demand for "quick, decisive action" — and work it from triage to a board-ready recommendation, producing the work product at each stage. Build: (1) an **issue triage memo** that classifies each thread as operational, strategic, legal, or interpersonal and names the missing facts; (2) a **stakeholder map** showing employees, managers, leadership, and legal, and what each is owed; (3) an **investigation or fact-finding plan** for the accommodation-after-warning thread that separates facts, policy, and credibility and protects against retaliation; (4) a **compliance-risk checklist** covering the hiring, leave, accommodation, discipline, and termination exposure in the file; (5) a small **HR dashboard** that diagnoses whether the attrition is a manager, compensation, or workload problem, pairing each activity metric with a quality metric; (6) a **business-aligned recommendation** that picks proportionate levers and ties each to a business outcome a CFO would recognize; and (7) a **two-audience communication plan** — one version for the manager, one for the affected employee — that is correct, clear, and humane. Close with one paragraph naming the single piece of evidence that, if it surfaced, would change your overall recommendation — the habit that separates an HR professional who gives a *defensible* answer from one who gives a merely *confident* one.
