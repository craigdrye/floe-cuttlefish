# Private Equity Roadmap
**ID**: `privateEquityRoadmap` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Private equity is not stock-picking with more leverage; it is the business of buying control of a company, owning it for three to seven years, and being paid only if it is worth meaningfully more when you sell than when you bought. That single fact reorders everything. A public investor can be wrong and walk away; a sponsor that buys a business outright cannot. So the discipline is ownership thinking under leverage: every dollar of return has to come from a source you can name and influence — buying well, paying down debt, growing earnings, or selling into a richer multiple — and every dollar of debt that funds the purchase is a fixed claim that does not care how the year went. This course teaches the craft of underwriting and owning a leveraged buyout the way a deal team and an investment committee actually do it: screen for fit, diligence the thesis, model the returns and the debt the business can carry, build a value-creation plan management will actually execute, and plan the exit before you sign.

The emphasis throughout is on the bridge between return math and operating reality. Anyone can build an LBO that shows a 25% IRR; the skill is knowing which assumptions are doing the work, what the business has to do every year to hit them, what happens in the downside when revenue misses and the cash sweep stalls, and whether the debt structure survives a bad year without tripping a covenant or starving the company of cash. You will work in the perspective of a sponsor that answers to limited partners and a credit market: capital is committed but called over years, fees and carry shape incentives, leverage cuts both ways, and an exit that slips two years can turn a good deal into a mediocre one through the simple arithmetic of time. The work is evidence-led — a thesis without a quality-of-earnings check is a story, a value-creation plan without management buy-in is a wish, and a return that depends entirely on multiple expansion is a bet on the market, not on the business.

By the end, a learner should be able to take a real or assigned target and produce an investment-committee-grade deal pack: a screening note that says why this is a PE asset and what the return must be, a diligence plan tied to the specific risks in the thesis, an LBO with a defensible base and a survivable downside, a value-creation plan with owners and timing and EBITDA impact, and an exit view that names the buyer and the multiple. Underneath all of it sits the chain that defines the discipline — entry, leverage, value creation, multiple, and exit — and the honest decomposition of which of those is actually expected to make the money. The goal is not a deal that looks good in the model; it is a deal whose returns you can attribute, defend in the downside, and exit on plan.

## Course Design Notes
The course follows the real arc of a deal: understand the PE model and what makes an asset ownable, screen and form the thesis, diligence it (commercial, financial, and operational), model the LBO and the debt capacity, build the value-creation plan, write the IC memo and run the process, then own and monitor the company and plan the exit — closing with the fund economics that govern everyone's incentives. Each chapter assumes the prior ones, mirroring how a deal team builds conviction. Route questions here when they test deal screening and investment criteria, commercial and financial diligence, quality of earnings, LBO mechanics and debt capacity, sources and uses, returns attribution (IRR/MOIC bridges), value creation and operating plans, IC memos and deal process, portfolio monitoring and the 100-day plan, exits, and fund-level economics (carry, J-curve, DPI/TVPI/IRR, GP/LP alignment). Keep numerical examples on round figures so learners reason about leverage, the returns bridge, and debt capacity rather than arithmetic. Anchor every answer to cash flow, the debt that has a fixed claim on it, and the source of the return. Common traps are flagged in every chapter, because in PE the expensive errors are quiet ones: the EBITDA add-back that does not survive contact with the auditor, the synergy that needs a CEO who is about to leave, the IRR that is really just the market re-rating, the covenant that looked loose until revenue dipped 10%.

## Deal Rhythm and How to Use This Course
A buyout is underwritten in layers, and skipping a layer is how sponsors lose money on a "great business." The working rhythm: decide whether the asset is even a PE deal and what return it must clear, then diligence the thesis hard enough to bet on it, then model the returns and the debt the business can actually carry, then build the plan that creates the value the model assumes, then write it up for IC and run a process, then own the company and plan the way out. Treat each chapter's "Applied skills" as a deliverable you could hand a deal partner or an investment committee, not a reading. A realistic cadence is one situation per chapter for practice and one full target for the capstone. Keep a running "deal file" across the course — the thesis, the gating diligence items, the return bridge, the value-creation initiatives, and the exit view — because in PE the discipline that separates a sponsor from a gambler is written down before the LOI, not rationalized in the exit memo.

| Layer | What you produce | Decision it supports |
|---|---|---|
| Model and criteria | Screening note, return hurdle, fit | Is this even a PE asset for us? |
| Diligence | Commercial/financial/operational findings, QoE | Is the thesis real and the earnings clean? |
| LBO and debt | Sources & uses, return bridge, downside, debt capacity | Does the math work and survive a bad year? |
| Value creation | 100-day and full-hold plan, owners, EBITDA impact | Can we actually create the value we underwrote? |
| Process and ownership | IC memo, exit view, monitoring plan | Should we buy it, and how do we get out? |

## Chapter 1: The PE Model and Investment Criteria
**Core questions**
- Why is this a private-equity asset at all — what about it rewards control, leverage, and a multi-year hold rather than a public-market position?
- What return does the deal have to clear, and where is that return supposed to come from: a cheap entry, debt paydown, EBITDA growth, or a richer exit multiple?
- What could break the thesis badly enough that no operating plan saves it — cyclicality, customer concentration, technological obsolescence, a regulated price?

**Key concepts**: the LBO as the core PE structure (buy with debt and equity, grow and de-lever, sell); sponsor, platform, and add-on (buy-and-build); control vs minority/growth equity; the hold period and why time erodes IRR; the four sources of return — entry multiple, leverage/debt paydown, EBITDA growth, and multiple expansion — and which a sponsor can actually control; IRR vs MOIC and why a fast 2x can beat a slow 3x; entry and exit multiples (EV/EBITDA); cash-generative, predictable businesses as the classic LBO target; the difference between a good business and a good deal.

**Applied skills**
- Write an investment-screen note: why the asset fits a PE thesis, the required return and rough entry/exit multiples, the initial return case, and the one or two risks that could kill it.
- Decompose a target return into its four sources and judge how much rests on multiple expansion (market timing) versus operational improvement (something you can drive).

**Common traps**
- Confusing a great business with a great deal — a wonderful company bought at a full multiple with no leverage room and no exit re-rating can still return nothing.
- Building a return that quietly depends on selling at a higher multiple than you paid, then calling it "operational value creation."
- Underwriting a long hold without modeling what the extra years do to IRR even when the company performs.

## Chapter 2: Commercial Diligence and the Investment Thesis
**Core questions**
- Is the revenue durable — where does it come from, how sticky is it, and what happens to it in a downturn or when a key customer leaves?
- What is the real source of the company's margin and pricing power, and is it defensible or about to be competed or regulated away?
- What specifically would have to be true for the thesis to hold, and what diligence work would confirm or kill each of those claims?

**Key concepts**: market size, growth, and structure (fragmented vs consolidated, cyclical vs secular); customer concentration and revenue durability; recurring vs one-time revenue and net revenue retention; pricing power and margin quality; competitive position, moats, and switching costs; management depth and the "is this team the team to back?" question; the thesis as a falsifiable claim with named risks; tying every diligence request to a specific risk and a decision point.

**Applied skills**
- Write a diligence plan: the thesis stated as testable claims, the commercial risks ranked, and the specific work (customer interviews, market study, churn analysis) that would confirm or break each claim.
- Build a revenue-durability view: concentration, retention, and recurring share, and what the downside case does to the top line.

**Common traps**
- Diligence that confirms the thesis you already love instead of hunting for the thing that breaks it — a request list with no falsifier is theater.
- Treating "the market is growing" as durability while ignoring that one customer is 40% of revenue and out for renewal next year.
- Backing a business whose margins are a moment in a cycle or a soon-to-end pricing window, not a durable structural advantage.

## Chapter 3: Financial Diligence and Quality of Earnings
**Core questions**
- Is reported EBITDA real, or is it propped up by add-backs and one-timers that will not repeat under your ownership?
- What is the true cash conversion of this business — how much of EBITDA actually becomes cash after working capital, capex, and taxes?
- Where are the hidden liabilities and the off-the-page risks: deferred capex, customer rebates, pending litigation, pension or earn-out obligations, aggressive revenue recognition?

**Key concepts**: quality of earnings (QoE) and normalized/adjusted EBITDA; legitimate vs aggressive add-backs (one-time vs recurring "one-timers"); working-capital normalization and the cash impact of growth; maintenance vs growth capex; cash conversion (EBITDA to free cash flow); net debt, debt-like items, and the cash-free/debt-free deal basis; revenue recognition and channel-stuffing risks; the role of the QoE provider and management's incentive to flatter the numbers; reconciling diligence EBITDA to the number in the model.

**Applied skills**
- Critique an adjusted-EBITDA bridge: which add-backs survive scrutiny, which are recurring costs in disguise, and what the defensible normalized EBITDA actually is.
- Translate EBITDA into free cash flow for the deal: subtract maintenance capex, the cash cost of working-capital growth, cash interest, and taxes, and show what is left to service debt.

**Common traps**
- Accepting management's adjusted EBITDA at face value — the add-back stack is where deals are quietly overpriced.
- Modeling off EBITDA as if it were cash and discovering, post-close, that growth consumes working capital and the cash sweep never materializes.
- Missing debt-like items (earn-outs, deferred comp, underfunded pensions) that turn a "clean" purchase price into a larger effective cost.

## Chapter 4: LBO Mechanics, Sources & Uses, and the Returns Bridge
**Core questions**
- How is the deal funded — what is the mix of debt and sponsor equity in sources and uses, and what does the cap structure cost?
- Across base and downside cases, what drives the return, and how much of it is leverage and de-levering versus EBITDA growth versus multiple?
- If the thesis disappoints — revenue flat, margins soft — does the model still service its debt, or does cash get squeezed toward a covenant breach?

**Key concepts**: sources and uses (purchase price, fees, refinanced debt, rollover equity, sponsor equity as the plug); the capital structure (revolver, term loans/TLB, second lien, mezzanine, the equity cheque) and the cost of each layer; entry and exit enterprise value and the bridge from EV to equity; free cash flow and the cash sweep that pays down debt; interest expense, fixed charges, and how leverage amplifies both gains and losses; the IRR/MOIC return bridge — decomposing return into entry, debt paydown, EBITDA growth, and multiple change; base, downside, and sensitivity cases; the levers IRR is actually most sensitive to (entry multiple, exit multiple, leverage, growth, hold length).

**Applied skills**
- Build a sources-and-uses table and a simple LBO case, then produce a returns bridge that attributes IRR/MOIC to leverage, EBITDA growth, and multiple change.
- Run a downside case (lower revenue/margin) and a sensitivity on the two biggest drivers, and identify the level at which the deal stops clearing the hurdle or the debt stops being serviceable.

**Common traps**
- An LBO that "works" only because the exit multiple is set above the entry multiple — paying yourself for a re-rating you have no reason to expect.
- Maximizing leverage to lift IRR while ignoring that the same leverage turns a 10% revenue miss into a covenant problem.
- Reading a single base-case IRR and never asking which assumption it depends on or how it behaves when that assumption is wrong.

## Chapter 5: Value Creation and the Operating Plan
**Core questions**
- Which value levers are genuinely controllable by an owner, and which require things outside your control (a market re-rating, a benign cycle)?
- What does management have to do, and by when, for the model's EBITDA path to be real rather than aspirational?
- Where does the first year of ownership actually move the needle, and what needs to be set up in the first 100 days to get there?

**Key concepts**: the value-creation toolkit — pricing, margin expansion, salesforce productivity, procurement/cost-out, working-capital release, and buy-and-build add-ons; the 100-day plan and governance (board seats, reporting cadence, KPIs); management alignment and equity incentive plans (the rollover and the management option pool); organic growth vs M&A-driven growth and multiple arbitrage in roll-ups; operational partners/operating teams; realistic timing — when initiatives hit the P&L, not when they are announced; tying each initiative to an owner, a milestone, and an EBITDA impact.

**Applied skills**
- Build a value-creation plan: ranked initiatives, each with an owner, a timeline, the operational change required, and its EBITDA impact, separating organic levers from add-on M&A.
- Write a 100-day plan: the governance, reporting, and quick wins that have to be in place early, and the management changes or incentives needed to execute.

**Common traps**
- A value-creation plan that is really a list of synergies with no owner, no timeline, and no one accountable for delivering them.
- Underwriting cost-out and growth simultaneously without noticing they fight each other, or that both depend on a management team you may be about to change.
- Assuming add-on multiple arbitrage scales forever, ignoring integration cost and the shrinking pool of cheap targets.

## Chapter 6: The IC Memo, Deal Process, and Negotiation
**Core questions**
- What must the investment committee believe to approve this deal, and which single diligence finding is gating — the one that, if wrong, ends it?
- How does the process protect you — what does the LOI, exclusivity, and the path to a signed purchase agreement actually lock in, and where does the risk sit between signing and closing?
- What are you really negotiating beyond price: reps and warranties, the indemnity and escrow, the earn-out, the rollover, and who carries which risk?

**Key concepts**: the deal funnel (teaser/CIM, IOI, management presentations, LOI, exclusivity, confirmatory diligence, SPA, close); the IC memo structure — thesis, valuation, diligence findings, risks, returns, value-creation plan, and exit; auction vs proprietary/bilateral deals and what each does to price and process; the purchase agreement essentials (purchase price mechanism, reps and warranties, indemnification, escrow, R&W insurance, MAC clauses, earn-outs); financing the deal (commitment letters, the debt package, financing-out risk); rollover equity and management incentives at signing; the difference between price and structure.

**Applied skills**
- Draft an IC memo excerpt: thesis, valuation and returns, the top three risks with mitigants, the gating diligence finding, and the recommended decision.
- Map the steps and risks from LOI to close, and identify where between signing and closing the buyer or seller carries the gap risk and how the SPA allocates it.

**Common traps**
- An IC memo that sells the upside and buries the risks instead of stating the one finding that could be deal-ending in plain terms.
- Treating the deal as won at LOI and underestimating financing risk, regulatory approval, or a diligence finding that re-trades the price.
- Negotiating only headline price while giving away structure — a weak indemnity or an aggressive earn-out can cost more than a turn of EBITDA.

## Chapter 7: Owning the Company, Monitoring, and the Exit
**Core questions**
- Once you own it, how do you know the thesis is on track — which KPIs, board cadence, and reporting tell you early whether value creation is happening or stalling?
- When the plan slips, what is the response — a management change, a refinancing, an add-on, a re-cut plan — and when do you accept the deal will under-deliver?
- How and to whom do you sell, and is the exit you assumed at entry — the buyer, the multiple, the timing — still realistic in the market you face?

**Key concepts**: portfolio monitoring (board governance, KPI dashboards, monthly/quarterly reporting, the value-creation tracker); the levers of an underperforming asset (refinance, recapitalize, add management, bolt-on, re-base the plan); dividend recapitalizations and returning capital before exit; exit routes — strategic sale, sponsor-to-sponsor (secondary buyout), IPO, and GP-led/continuation funds for assets a sponsor wants to hold longer; exit readiness and the sell-side QoE; multiple expansion vs deterioration at exit and why the exit market is not the entry market; holding-period IRR drag and the value of selling on plan.

**Applied skills**
- Build a portfolio-monitoring view: the KPIs that track the thesis, the board and reporting cadence, and the early-warning signals that a value lever is missing.
- Write an exit view: the most likely buyer type and why, the assumed exit multiple and its justification, the timing, and what has to be true in the company and the market for it to clear.

**Common traps**
- Underwriting a clean strategic exit at a premium multiple and then finding the only realistic buyer is another sponsor who will not pay it.
- Letting an underperforming asset drift while the hold clock runs, when a refinancing, a management change, or an earlier exit would protect the return.
- Assuming the exit market mirrors the entry market — multiples compress, debt gets expensive, and the IPO window closes, none of which the entry model knew about.

## Chapter 8: Fund Economics, the J-Curve, and GP/LP Alignment
**Core questions**
- How is a PE fund structured and paid, and how do management fees, carried interest, and the hurdle align — or misalign — the GP with its limited partners?
- Why does a fund show negative returns for years before it shows good ones, and how do you read DPI, TVPI, and IRR together without being fooled by any one of them?
- What does "alignment" actually require beyond the fee deck — GP commitment, the hurdle, the clawback, and honest mark-to-market on unrealized holdings?

**Key concepts**: the fund structure (GP/LP, the limited partnership, committed capital, the investment period, fund life ~10 years); capital calls and the drawdown of committed capital over time; management fee and carried interest (the classic "2 and 20"), the preferred return/hurdle, the catch-up, and the clawback; the J-curve (early fees and write-downs before realizations); the return metrics — DPI (cash actually returned), RVPI (unrealized value), TVPI (total value), and net IRR — and why DPI is the one that pays the pension; gross vs net returns and the drag of fees and carry; GP commitment and skin in the game; the secondaries market and continuation vehicles as liquidity tools; honest valuation of unrealized positions.

**Applied skills**
- Lay out a fund's economics — fee, carry, hurdle, catch-up — and explain where they align the GP with LPs and where they create perverse incentives (e.g., fee on committed capital, or marks that flatter unrealized value near fundraising).
- Read a fund snapshot of DPI/RVPI/TVPI and net IRR and judge how much of the "performance" is realized cash versus unrealized marks, and what the J-curve implies about its age.

**Common traps**
- Quoting a headline gross IRR while ignoring that fees and carry can take a large share of gross return — and that a high TVPI with low DPI is paper, not cash.
- Treating unrealized marks as realized performance, especially in a fundraising year when the GP is incentivized to carry holdings at flattering values.
- Assuming "2 and 20" aligns interests by itself, missing that a fee on committed capital can pay the GP regardless of returns unless the hurdle and clawback actually bite.

## Capstone: Full Private Equity Deal Pack
Take one target — a fictional or real company — and produce a complete, IC-ready deal pack:
- a screening note: why this is a PE asset, the required return and rough entry/exit multiples, and the one or two risks that could kill the deal;
- a diligence plan: the thesis as testable claims, the commercial and financial risks ranked, the gating items, and a quality-of-earnings view of normalized EBITDA;
- an LBO summary: sources and uses, a base and a downside case, a returns bridge attributing IRR/MOIC to leverage, EBITDA growth, and multiple, and the debt capacity the business can carry;
- a value-creation plan: ranked initiatives with owners, timing, and EBITDA impact, plus a 100-day plan and the management-alignment package;
- an IC memo excerpt: thesis, valuation and returns, the top risks with mitigants, the gating diligence finding, and a recommended decision;
- an exit view and monitoring plan: the likely buyer, the assumed exit multiple and timing, the KPIs that track the thesis, and the early-warning signals;
- a one-line verdict — pursue, pursue only at a lower price, or pass — with the single strongest argument for and against, and the one assumption the whole return rests on.

## Assessment Ideas
- Investment-screen note with a four-source return decomposition
- Commercial-diligence plan tying each request to a thesis risk and a falsifier
- Quality-of-earnings critique: which add-backs survive, and EBITDA-to-cash conversion
- LBO build with a sources-and-uses table and an IRR/MOIC returns bridge
- Downside-case and sensitivity lab (debt serviceability and covenant headroom)
- Value-creation plan with owners, timing, and EBITDA impact, plus a 100-day plan
- IC memo excerpt and gating-risk identification
- Signing-to-close risk map and purchase-agreement structure critique
- Portfolio-monitoring and exit-view exercise (buyer, multiple, timing)
- Fund-economics and DPI/TVPI/IRR reading drill (alignment and J-curve)
- Capstone deal pack and IC defense

## Research Notes
- The LBO structure, the four sources of return (entry multiple, leverage/de-levering, EBITDA growth, multiple expansion), and the IRR-vs-MOIC framing reflect standard private-equity and corporate-finance practice (CFA Institute alternative-investments curriculum; standard LBO modeling references).
- Diligence streams (commercial, financial/QoE, legal, operational), normalized/adjusted EBITDA, and the cash-free/debt-free deal basis follow conventional buy-side transaction practice; the "test the thesis, don't confirm it" framing is standard diligence discipline.
- Capital-structure layers (revolver, TLB, second lien, mezzanine), the cash sweep, and covenant mechanics reflect conventional leveraged-finance structures; specific tranche pricing and leverage multiples vary with the credit cycle and should not be taught as fixed.
- Deal-process steps (CIM/IOI/LOI/exclusivity/SPA), purchase-agreement essentials (reps and warranties, indemnity/escrow, R&W insurance, earn-outs), and exit routes (strategic, sponsor-to-sponsor, IPO, GP-led/continuation funds) reflect current market practice; the rise of continuation vehicles and dividend recaps is cycle- and rate-sensitive.
- Fund economics ("2 and 20," preferred return/hurdle, catch-up, clawback), the J-curve, and the DPI/RVPI/TVPI/net-IRR metric set reflect conventional limited-partnership terms; actual fee levels, hurdles, and GP commitments vary by fund and are negotiated, and the relative emphasis on DPI versus paper marks shifts with the fundraising and liquidity environment.
