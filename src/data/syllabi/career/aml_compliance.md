# AML / CAMS
**ID**: `amlCompliance` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course trains an adult learner to reason like a working anti-money-laundering professional and to pass the ACAMS Certified Anti-Money Laundering Specialist (CAMS) exam without treating those as two separate skills. Financial-crime work is, at bottom, a discipline of defensible judgment under incomplete information: a customer's behavior shifts, a name fuzzily matches a sanctions list, an alert fires at 2am, and someone has to decide — with a clock running — what the facts support, what gets escalated, what gets reported, and how to write it down so a regulator, a prosecutor, or a colleague two years from now can reconstruct exactly why. The goal is fluency in that translation from messy signal to proportionate, documented action.

The syllabus is deliberately cross-sector rather than bank-only, because money launderers are, and because CAMS is. Layering moves through securities accounts, casinos, real estate closings, money-services businesses, virtual-asset exchanges, and trade invoices — not just checking accounts. Across the chapters you will internalize the three classic stages (placement, layering, integration) and the modern typologies that blur them (trade-based laundering, mule networks, professional money-laundering organizations, crypto chain-hopping), the international architecture every program inherits (FATF's 40 Recommendations, the risk-based approach, Wolfsberg principles), and the U.S. machinery the exam and the job both lean on (the BSA, the AML Act of 2020, FinCEN, OFAC sanctions, and the FFIEC examination expectations). You will also learn the parts of the job no flashcard captures: why "suspicious" is a lower bar than "proven," why the second SAR matters as much as the first, and how a tuned-down alert threshold can make a program's metrics look healthy while it goes blind.

By the end you should be able to take an ambiguous customer or transaction and do four things well: assess its financial-crime risk with evidence rather than reflex, scope it across the customer relationship and the wider network, decide a proportionate response (clear, escalate, hold, file) against the right legal standard, and write a narrative a reviewer could act on. Evidence, scope, proportion, clarity — that combination is what distinguishes an analyst whose calls hold up from one who is merely busy, and it is also, not coincidentally, how the CAMS exam is scored.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests money-laundering and terrorist/proliferation-financing typologies; the FATF standards and the risk-based approach; customer due diligence, KYC, and beneficial ownership; sanctions and PEP screening; transaction monitoring and alert disposition; investigations and case-building; suspicious-activity reporting logic, timing, and confidentiality; AML program governance and independent testing; or emerging risk in crypto, trade finance, and correspondent banking. This is the certification-and-mastery track; for an entry-level on-ramp into bank compliance specifically, prefer the Bank Compliance and AML Roadmap.

The voice is a senior financial-crime investigator mentoring a sharp new analyst: concrete scenarios, no acronym left undefined on first use, and a relentless focus on the *next defensible action* and the *standard it must meet*. Every chapter surfaces Common traps, because the highest-value learning in AML is recognizing the seductive-but-wrong move — accepting a tidy explanation that the evidence does not support, confusing suspicion with proof, tipping off a subject, or letting a number stand in for a judgment. Keep facts current and U.S.-accurate where the exam expects U.S. detail. Prefer post-2020 framing: the AML Act of 2020 and its beneficial-ownership and whistleblower provisions; the Corporate Transparency Act and FinCEN's BOI registry as revised by the March 2025 interim final rule (U.S.-formed companies are exempt from federal BOI reporting; only foreign reporting companies file with FinCEN — distinct from the still-in-force BSA CDD beneficial-ownership rule banks apply); the SAR filing clock (30 days from initial detection, up to 60 if identifying a subject; continuing-activity review at 90 days); OFAC's 50% Rule and civil strict liability; and FATF Recommendation 16 (the "Travel Rule") applied to virtual-asset service providers. Distractors should be the plausible-but-flawed calls real analysts make, not absurd ones.

### Exam map (CAMS 6th edition, study guide v6.5)
The CAMS exam weights four domains; the chapters below are built to cover them in proportion rather than evenly. Map your effort accordingly:
- **Domain I — Risks and methods of ML/TF (~26%)**: Chapters 1, 2 (risk side), and 9.
- **Domain II — AML/CFT compliance standards (~25%)**: Chapters 2, 3, and the FATF/BSA threads throughout.
- **Domain III — AML/CFT and sanctions compliance programs (~28%, the heaviest)**: Chapters 3, 4, and 8.
- **Domain IV — Conducting and supporting investigations (~21%)**: Chapters 5, 6, and 7.

### Study rhythm
A working professional should plan four to eight weeks. Read one chapter, then immediately do its applied skill on a real or invented case rather than re-reading — recall and production beat highlighting. Drill the Common traps as their own deck; the exam loves the proportionate-second-best answer, and so does the job. Take a full timed mock at the two-thirds mark to expose weak domains while there is still time to fix them, then close the gap before sitting.

## Chapter 1: The Financial-Crime Landscape and ML/TF Stages
- **Core questions**: What is the suspected financial-crime risk here, and to whom? Does the pattern point to placement, layering, or integration — and does that distinction even hold for this typology? What facts are still missing before anyone calls it suspicious?
- **Key concepts**: the three classic stages (placement, layering, integration) and where they break down; predicate offenses; terrorist financing vs money laundering (small amounts, often legitimate source, reverse logic); proliferation financing; the economic and social harm AML exists to prevent; why "follow the money" cuts both ways for source and destination.
- **Applied skills**: map a fact pattern to the likely stage(s) and typology, name the red flags, and list the evidence still needed to move from "unusual" to "suspicious."
- **Common traps**: forcing every case into placement/layering/integration when terrorist financing or a structured trade scheme does not fit the mold; assuming a clean, documented source of funds means low risk; treating an exotic-sounding pattern as inherently suspicious without a benign baseline to rule out.

## Chapter 2: Customer Due Diligence, KYC, and Beneficial Ownership
- **Core questions**: Does this customer's profile actually support the activity we observe? Which ownership or control fact would change the risk rating? What documentation is *enough*, and enough for what decision?
- **Key concepts**: customer identification program (CIP); the four CDD pillars and ongoing monitoring as the "fifth pillar"; source of funds vs source of wealth; expected vs observed activity; risk rating and its drivers; enhanced due diligence (EDD) triggers; the BSA CDD rule's beneficial-ownership requirement (25% ownership prong plus a control prong) — and how it differs from the Corporate Transparency Act's FinCEN BOI registry.
- **Applied skills**: write a customer risk memo stating the profile, expected behavior, the gaps, the risk drivers, and a defensible risk-rating rationale; identify the beneficial owner through a layered ownership chain.
- **Common traps**: conflating source of funds with source of wealth; collecting documents without checking whether they actually resolve the risk question; assuming the FinCEN BOI registry now covers domestic LLCs (after the March 2025 rule, U.S.-formed entities are exempt from federal BOI reporting — yet banks still owe the separate BSA CDD beneficial-ownership obligation, which trips up learners working from stale guides).

## Chapter 3: Sanctions, PEPs, and Screening
- **Core questions**: Is this a true match or list noise? What evidence distinguishes our customer from the listed person? When must funds be blocked, rejected, or escalated — and on what authority?
- **Key concepts**: OFAC SDN and sectoral lists; blocking vs rejecting a transaction; the **50% Rule** (an entity 50%-or-more owned by blocked persons, individually or in aggregate, is itself blocked even if not listed); civil **strict liability** (no intent required to violate); politically exposed persons (PEPs) and source-of-wealth scrutiny; fuzzy matching, false positives, and disposition logic; geographic and secondary-sanctions risk.
- **Applied skills**: write a screening disposition note that lays out the match logic, the distinguishing evidence, and the recommended action; reason through whether a 50%-aggregated ownership structure pulls a non-listed entity into sanctions scope.
- **Common traps**: clearing a fuzzy match because the subject "doesn't seem like a criminal" rather than on identifying evidence; assuming no SDN hit means no sanctions exposure (the 50% Rule and crypto wallet listings say otherwise); treating sanctions like AML — sanctions are strict-liability and prohibitive, not risk-based-and-report.

## Chapter 4: Transaction Monitoring and Alert Disposition
- **Core questions**: Which activity is genuinely unusual *for this customer*? What baseline am I comparing against? What benign explanation is plausible, and does the evidence actually support it?
- **Key concepts**: structuring and the $10,000 CTR threshold it evades; velocity, round-dollar, and rapid in/out movement; funnel accounts and mule networks; cash-intensive businesses; wire and trade-based patterns; peer-group baselining; rules-based vs behavioral/scenario monitoring; alert tuning, suppression, and the false-positive economics of a program.
- **Applied skills**: complete an alert-review worksheet that cleanly separates observed behavior, corroborating evidence, plausible benign explanation, and conclusion — with the disposition justified against the right standard.
- **Common traps**: auto-clearing on a convenient story ("seasonal business") without corroboration; lowering a monitoring threshold to cut alert volume and calling the resulting quiet "improved performance"; confusing a CTR (a threshold-based currency report) with a SAR (a judgment-based suspicion report).

## Chapter 5: Investigations and Case-Building
- **Core questions**: What is the narrative the facts actually tell? Which facts raise suspicion, and which reduce it? Have I scoped beyond the single alerting account to the network around it?
- **Key concepts**: case chronology and timeline construction; link analysis across parties, accounts, and counterparties; adverse-media and open-source research with source weighting; evidence sufficiency and the gap between "unusual" and "suspicious"; investigative scope creep vs disciplined scoping; internal records as primary evidence.
- **Applied skills**: build an investigation summary with timeline, parties, activity, red flags, mitigating facts, and a disposition recommendation; decide when the network warrants pulling related accounts into scope.
- **Common traps**: building a one-sided case that only collects inculpatory facts; mistaking volume of evidence for weight of evidence; following the investigation so far it never closes; treating unverified adverse media as established fact.

## Chapter 6: Suspicious Activity Reporting — Logic, Timing, and Confidentiality
- **Core questions**: Does this meet the standard to file? What must the narrative say so a reader who never saw the case can act on it? Has the clock started, and is anyone at risk of tipping off the subject?
- **Key concepts**: the "knows, suspects, or has reason to suspect" filing standard; the **30-day** clock from initial detection (up to **60** if a subject must first be identified); **continuing-activity** review at 90 days with a follow-up SAR; SAR **confidentiality** and the prohibition on tipping off; the BSA **safe harbor** from civil liability for filing; the who/what/when/where/why/how narrative discipline; CTRs and other BSA reports as distinct obligations.
- **Applied skills**: draft a SAR-style narrative with a clear five-W-and-how structure, the supporting facts, and an explicit statement of why the activity is suspicious; calculate the correct filing deadline from a detection date.
- **Common traps**: overstating the narrative beyond what the evidence supports; waiting for proof of a crime when "suspicion" is the bar; missing the continuing-activity follow-up SAR; revealing the existence of a SAR to the customer or to an unauthorized party (a tipping-off violation, not a customer-service courtesy).

## Chapter 7: Quality Control and Examiner Readiness
- **Core questions**: Could an independent reviewer reconstruct this decision from the file alone? Where is the documentation thin enough that an examiner would push back? Is the *decision* defensible, separate from whether it was right in hindsight?
- **Key concepts**: maker-checker QA review; documentation sufficiency and audit trail; consistency across analysts; examiner expectations under the FFIEC BSA/AML manual; the difference between a defensible-at-the-time decision and a correct-in-hindsight one; remediation of identified file defects.
- **Applied skills**: run a QA checklist over a completed case file and flag the specific gaps that would not survive examination; rewrite a weak disposition rationale into a defensible one.
- **Common traps**: judging a past decision by information that only emerged later; treating a closed alert as a documented one; confusing a confident tone in the file with actual evidentiary support.

## Chapter 8: AML Program Governance and the Risk-Based Approach
- **Core questions**: Which control is supposed to catch this risk, and did it work? What metric would reveal that monitoring is degrading? Of the open issues, which gets remediated first and why?
- **Key concepts**: the enterprise-wide AML risk assessment; the BSA program pillars (designated officer, internal controls, training, independent testing, and risk-based CDD); the AML Act of 2020 (modernization, beneficial-ownership reform, expanded whistleblower incentives); FATF's risk-based approach and the Wolfsberg principles; model validation and monitoring-rule tuning; KPIs/KRIs and what they hide.
- **Applied skills**: build a control-gap tracker with owner, risk, evidence, remediation step, and due date; pair a volume metric (alerts closed) with a quality and coverage metric so the program cannot game itself.
- **Common traps**: celebrating a drop in alerts that actually came from a quietly raised threshold; treating independent testing as a formality rather than a control; applying the same intensity to every customer instead of risk-rating effort; assuming a vendor model is sound because it was expensive.

## Chapter 9: Emerging and Advanced Typologies
- **Core questions**: What makes this pattern harder to detect than classic cash laundering? Which data source would actually clarify the risk? How should the institution's risk appetite shape the response?
- **Key concepts**: virtual assets and chain analysis, mixers/tumblers, and FATF **Recommendation 16 (Travel Rule)** obligations on VASPs (sharing originator and beneficiary information); trade-based money laundering (over/under-invoicing, phantom shipments); correspondent banking and nested relationships; shell and shelf companies; human-trafficking and other vulnerable-sector finance; professional money-laundering networks; the proliferation-financing and sanctions-evasion overlap.
- **Applied skills**: write a one-typology briefing — chosen from crypto, trade finance, or correspondent banking — that states the mechanism, the red flags, the detection challenge, and the analyst questions it raises.
- **Common traps**: assuming crypto is anonymous (most public chains are pseudonymous and traceable); treating a correspondent relationship as low-risk because the respondent is a "real bank"; reading a single under-invoiced shipment as conclusive rather than as one red flag among the pattern needed.

## Capstone
Work a single financial-crime case end to end and produce the file an examiner would expect. Starting from a customer profile, KYC pack, and an account showing rapid in-and-out wires to a higher-risk jurisdiction with one fuzzy sanctions hit: rate the customer's risk and justify it; resolve the sanctions match with explicit match logic and the right action (clear, block, or escalate), reasoning through the 50% Rule if the ownership structure invites it; disposition the monitoring alert on an alert-review worksheet that separates observed behavior, corroboration, and a tested benign explanation; build the investigation summary with timeline, parties, red flags, and mitigating facts; decide whether the standard to file a SAR is met, draft the narrative with who/what/when/where/why/how, and state the correct filing deadline from the detection date; run a QA checklist over your own file and fix the weakest rationale; and close with a short reflection naming the one additional piece of evidence that would most change your decision. Deliverable: a defensible case file — risk memo, screening disposition, alert worksheet, investigation summary, SAR narrative, and QA notes — that another analyst could pick up cold and stand behind.
