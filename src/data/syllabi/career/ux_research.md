# UX Research: Evidence for Product Decisions
*Ask the right question, choose the right method, and turn what users do into decisions a team will actually act on.*

**ID**: `uxResearch` · **Discipline**: Product / Design · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
UX research is the discipline of reducing a product team's uncertainty about real people. It is not "asking users what they want," and it is not running a survey because someone asked for numbers. It is the practice of matching a clear decision to the lightest method that can credibly inform it, then defending the resulting evidence against the dozens of ways human studies quietly mislead us — leading questions, self-report that does not match behavior, samples that recruited only fans, themes invented to confirm what the PM already believed. This course teaches the craft and the judgment behind it.

You will learn to place any method on the landscape that working researchers carry in their heads — attitudinal versus behavioral, qualitative versus quantitative, and natural versus scripted context — and to explain *why* a generative interview, a tree test, an unmoderated benchmark, and a survey each answer a different kind of question. You will write screeners that do not telegraph the right answer, interview guides that ask about past behavior instead of hypothetical futures, and analyses that reach saturation honestly rather than cherry-picking the quote that lands. You will compute and interpret the metrics teams actually quote — SUS, NPS, task success, time on task, error rate — and know what each one does and does not prove.

The standard here is professional and adult: clear over clever, honest about limitations, and relentlessly focused on impact. A study that is methodologically perfect but changes no decision is a failed study. By the end you should be able to scope a research question, pick a defensible method, run it cleanly and ethically, synthesize without fooling yourself, and communicate findings so a non-technical executive knows exactly what to do next — and why.

## Course Design Notes
Almost every question is a concrete decision scenario: a PM hands you a vague ask, a stakeholder dismisses your sample size, a recruiter's screener is leaking, a survey result contradicts the analytics. The learner's job is to name the right move and, just as importantly, to see why the plausible-sounding alternatives fail. Distractors are deliberately built from real UXR mistakes and look-alike methods (open vs closed card sort, tree testing vs card sorting, concurrent vs retrospective think-aloud, attitudinal vs behavioral data), so wrong answers teach the distinctions a hiring manager actually probes.

Chapters move from research strategy and method selection, through recruiting and study design, interview and moderation craft, evaluative usability testing, quantitative UXR and survey design, synthesis and sensemaking, ethics and inclusive practice, and finally communication, influence, and the job hunt. The voice stays plain and practical, but the lesson notes always reinforce a transferable principle — the *why* behind the method — rather than a memorizable label. Numbers (SUS = 68 average, NPS = %promoters − %detractors, 5 users ≈ 85% of usability issues at a 31% problem-discovery rate) are grounded in canonical sources so they are exam-grade.

## Chapter 1: Research Strategy and Choosing a Method
**Core questions**
- Is this a "what should we even build" question or a "does this design work" question?
- Where does the method sit on attitudinal-vs-behavioral and qualitative-vs-quantitative?
- What is the lightest method that can credibly inform this specific decision?

**Key concepts**: generative vs evaluative (formative vs summative) research, the NN/g method landscape (attitudinal/behavioral × qualitative/quantitative × context of use), "what users say vs what users do," matching method to research question and decision, research questions vs interview questions.

**Common traps**: running evaluative tests before you know what to build; treating a survey as a discovery tool for unknown-unknowns; choosing a method by what is available rather than what the decision needs; confusing attitudinal self-report with behavioral evidence.

## Chapter 2: Recruiting, Sampling, and Screeners
**Core questions**
- Does this screener telegraph the answer that gets someone selected?
- Are we recruiting the actual users, or just whoever is easy and enthusiastic?
- How many participants does this method need, and why?

**Key concepts**: screener design with plausible distractor options, target criteria masking, recruiting for behavior not demographics, sample size by method (≈5 for qualitative usability; larger N for quantitative), self-selection and convenience-sample bias, professional-respondent/"survey farmer" risk, incentives sized to avoid coercion.

**Common traps**: stating the target criteria at the top of the screener; leading yes/no screening questions ("Do you love our app?"); over-long screeners that drive abandonment; equating a big survey N with a representative sample.

## Chapter 3: Interview and Moderation Craft
**Core questions**
- Is this question neutral, or does it assume its own answer?
- Am I asking about real past behavior or unreliable hypothetical futures?
- When do I probe, and when do I shut up and let silence work?

**Key concepts**: open vs closed and leading vs neutral questions, past behavior over hypotheticals, the "five whys" / laddering to root motivation, contextual inquiry and the master-apprentice stance, active silence and probing, avoiding confirmation and social-desirability bias, building rapport without priming.

**Common traps**: leading questions ("Don't you think this is faster?"); asking "would you pay $X" and trusting the answer; reacting to answers in ways that reward a "right" response; turning the five whys into an interrogation.

## Chapter 4: Evaluative Usability Testing
**Core questions**
- Is this a formative diagnose-and-fix test or a summative benchmark?
- Concurrent or retrospective think-aloud — and what does each cost?
- How do I rate and prioritize the issues I find?

**Key concepts**: moderated vs unmoderated and remote vs in-person testing, concurrent vs retrospective think-aloud (and the task-performance cost of thinking aloud), task scenarios vs instructions, severity rating, the Nielsen–Landauer model (≈5 users find ~85% of issues at a 31% discovery rate; iterate), heuristic evaluation (Nielsen's 10 heuristics) as an inspection method.

**Common traps**: writing tasks that tell the user where to click; defending "5 users is never enough" or "5 is always enough" as dogma instead of understanding the model's assumptions; reporting issues without severity; treating a one-off test as a summative benchmark.

## Chapter 5: Quantitative UXR, Surveys, and Metrics
**Core questions**
- What exactly does this metric measure, and what does it *not* prove?
- Is this survey question double-barreled, leading, or loaded?
- Is the difference real, or could it be sampling noise?

**Key concepts**: SUS (0–100, ~68 average; not a percentage), NPS (= %promoters[9–10] − %detractors[0–6]; passives 7–8 excluded; range −100 to +100), task success rate, time on task, error rate, SEQ/UMUX-Lite, Likert vs MaxDiff for forced trade-offs, double-barreled/leading/loaded question avoidance, confidence intervals and statistical vs practical significance, A/B testing basics.

**Common traps**: reading SUS 68 as "68% usable"; quoting NPS without excluding passives or knowing the range; double-barreled survey items; declaring a winner from a tiny, underpowered sample; conflating statistical significance with business importance.

## Chapter 6: Information Architecture Research
**Core questions**
- Am I trying to *learn* a structure or *validate* one?
- Open or closed card sort — what does each tell me?
- Where does tree testing fit relative to card sorting?

**Key concepts**: open card sorting (users create categories — reveals mental models), closed card sorting (users sort into your categories — validates a structure), hybrid sorts, tree testing for findability of a proposed IA, first-click testing, success/directness metrics in tree tests, labeling and terminology research.

**Common traps**: using a closed sort when you needed to discover categories; confusing card sorting (build/learn structure) with tree testing (validate findability); skipping tree testing and shipping an IA validated only by a sort; ignoring terminology mismatches.

## Chapter 7: Synthesis and Sensemaking
**Core questions**
- Did this theme emerge from the data, or did I impose it?
- Have I reached saturation, or just stopped early?
- Is this persona built on behavior or on demographics and wishful thinking?

**Key concepts**: affinity mapping (bottom-up, organic themes), thematic analysis and coding, thematic saturation, behavior-based personas, empathy/journey maps, jobs-to-be-done framing, separating observation from interpretation, triangulating across methods.

**Common traps**: grouping by interview-guide question or demographics instead of emergent behavior; discarding disconfirming data to fit the PM's hypothesis; building demographic personas ("35-year-old soccer mom") that do not predict behavior; reporting interpretation as if it were raw observation.

## Chapter 8: Research Ethics and Inclusive Practice
**Core questions**
- Does the participant actually understand what they are consenting to?
- Is this incentive an inducement that quietly becomes coercion?
- How is personal data stored, anonymized, and deleted?

**Key concepts**: informed and voluntary consent, right to withdraw without penalty, confidentiality/anonymization/pseudonymization and PII handling, data retention and deletion, incentives sized to avoid coercion, debrief and avoiding priming, inclusive and accessible recruiting, basic accessibility literacy (WCAG levels A/AA/AAA; POUR: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust).

**Common traps**: jargon-filled consent forms; outsized incentives that coerce reluctant participants; keeping raw PII longer than needed or sharing it broadly; excluding disabled or non-mainstream users and calling the sample "representative."

## Chapter 9: Communicating Findings and Driving Impact
**Core questions**
- What is the one decision this readout should change?
- How do I answer "you only talked to 5 people"?
- How do I prioritize issues a team can actually act on?

**Key concepts**: leading with the decision and the "so what," translating method into business impact for non-technical execs, defending qualitative rigor via saturation (not statistical significance), severity × effort / impact × effort prioritization, narrative and executive storytelling, addressing a missed confounder honestly, stakeholder alignment and research-as-a-team-sport.

**Common traps**: burying the recommendation under method detail; conceding qualitative findings are "not significant" instead of reframing the goal; prioritizing by personal annoyance or alphabetically; getting defensive when a stakeholder finds a real limitation.

## Chapter 10: ResearchOps, Career, and the Portfolio
**Core questions**
- How does a team make research repeatable and not lose its own insights?
- What makes a UXR portfolio case study strong?
- How do I show influence rather than just activity?

**Key concepts**: ResearchOps (recruiting pipelines, participant databases, repositories, governance), democratization with guardrails, the Problem → Approach → Impact case-study arc, demonstrating influence on shipped decisions, whiteboard/study-design interview challenges, collaborating with PM/design/data, knowing the limits of your own study.

**Common traps**: portfolios that narrate process (20 minutes on the affinity map) with no outcome; complaining that stakeholders ignored findings (signals weak influence); presenting research as a solo handoff rather than a collaboration; no repository, so insights are re-discovered every quarter.

## Capstone: From Question to Decision
Learners run an end-to-end mini-study and defend every choice:
- a one-paragraph research brief: the decision at stake, the research question, and why the chosen method fits the landscape
- a leak-proof screener with masked target criteria and a justified sample size
- an interview guide or test plan with neutral, behavior-focused tasks/questions
- a synthesis artifact (affinity themes or coded findings) that shows saturation and at least one disconfirming data point taken seriously
- the right metric(s) computed and correctly interpreted (e.g., SUS or NPS with its true meaning and limits)
- a one-slide executive readout: the decision it changes, severity/effort prioritization, and an honest statement of limitations

The goal is not a perfect study — it is a *useful, honest* one: the right question, a defensible method, clean and ethical execution, synthesis that does not fool the researcher, and a finding a team will actually act on.
