# Series 79: Investment Banking Without the Mythology
**ID**: `series79` · **Discipline**: Finance

## Course Aim
The Series 79 is FINRA's investment banking representative qualification — the narrowly-scoped license that lets a registered person advise on debt and equity capital raising, mergers and acquisitions, tender offers, financial restructurings, and Rule 144A/Reg S private placements. Unlike the Series 7, which sprawls across the retail product universe, the Series 79 deliberately *excludes* retail products and goes deep on the work IB analysts and associates actually do: pitching, modeling, drafting, underwriting, executing. Pass it and you can sign the offer letter to a bulge-bracket or middle-market IB; fail it and you cannot. Most firms put new analysts on a clock — usually 120 days from start date to passing this exam.

The Series 79 has 75 scored questions over 2 hours 30 minutes, divided among three job functions: data analysis (27%), underwriting and offerings (37%), and M&A/restructuring (36%). The arithmetic is harder than the SIE or 63 — DCF, accretion/dilution, EV-to-equity bridge, LBO returns, control premium math — but the exam is also less about recall than the others and more about deal-execution literacy. It expects you to think like a junior banker: walking a fact pattern from initial pitch through fairness opinion, identifying which underwriting structure fits which issuer, distinguishing a 144A from a Reg S placement on instinct, knowing when a tax-free reorganization changes the deal.

What you leave with is the working vocabulary of a deal team. You will recognize the rhythm of a sell-side process (teaser → CIM → IOI → LOI → DD → SPA → close), know which document gets signed at which step, understand which fairness opinion methodologies a board will rely on, and read a covenant package well enough to spot a maintenance covenant from an incurrence one. The exam will not make you a banker — your first staffing will do that — but it will close the gap between "finance major" and "billable hour 1."

The Floe Series 79 voice is technical but unstuffy. We assume you already speak corporate finance — three-statement modeling, WACC, EBITDA adjustments — and we use realistic deal scenarios with named sponsors, multiples, EBITDA figures, and capital structure breakdowns. The exam expects you to model with intent; we drill that intent.

## Course Design Notes
Route any question on valuation methodology (DCF, trading comps, transaction comps, LBO, sum-of-the-parts), capital raising (IPO mechanics, follow-on, debt issuance, PIPEs, 144A/Reg S), M&A (auctions, fairness opinions, lockups, deal protection, hostile defenses), restructuring (Chapter 11, exchange offers, 363 sales), or registration/disclosure (S-1, S-3, S-4, 13D, 14D-9, Schedule TO) here.

Where SIE is "what is a security," Series 79 is "what is the right offering structure for this issuer." Where Series 7 is "is this product suitable for this retail client," Series 79 is "how do we sell this deal to a Tier 1 syndicate." Where Series 63 is "what state filings apply," Series 79 is "what Section 5 exemption are we relying on, and is it 506(b) or 506(c)."

Use named sponsors ("TPG-backed Falcon Industries, $620M revenue, 18% EBITDA margin"), specific multiples ("10.5x LTM EBITDA"), and full deal mechanics. Distractors should mirror the kind of green-analyst errors that show up in modeling tests (forgetting to add minority interest in the EV bridge, mis-timing terminal value to year 6 instead of year 5).

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The Series 79 has **75 scored questions plus 10 unscored pretest items in 2 hours 30 minutes (150 minutes)**. Passing is 73%. FINRA's outline divides the exam into three job functions:

| Topic | Exam Weight | Scored Qs | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Collection, Analysis, and Evaluation of Data | 27% | 20 | Financial statements, three-statement linkages, accounting policy, valuation math |
| Underwriting/New Financing Transactions, Types of Offerings, Registration | 37% | 28 | The largest unit — IPO, follow-on, debt, 144A/Reg S, Reg D, Reg A+, registration timeline |
| M&A, Tender Offers, Financial Restructuring | 36% | 27 | M&A process, fairness opinions, Williams Act, defenses, Chapter 11 |

**Study cadence: 6-8 weeks at 8-12 hours per week** is realistic for a candidate already at an IB firm. The most common pace is 6 weeks because the start-date clock is usually 90-120 days from hire.

- **4-week sprint (full-time study, finance background)**: Week 1 — three-statement / valuation / accretion-dilution drills; Week 2 — IPO / follow-on / debt / 144A; Week 3 — M&A process + tender offers + restructuring; Week 4 — two full-length practice exams, error log, edge-case mop-up.
- **6-week standard**: Add a dedicated week for financial statement deep dive and a dedicated week for fairness opinion / restructuring scenarios.
- **8-week deep prep**: Build (or rebuild) a working LBO model and a 3-statement model from scratch in the first two weeks. The act of building beats reading every time for Series 79's quantitative items.

Open the question bank in week 1. Drill valuation math *daily* — accretion/dilution and LBO returns decay the fastest. Schedule the actual exam at the start of prep; the deadline forces honesty.

## Chapter 1: Financial Statement Analysis for Bankers
**Core questions:** What does a target's financials actually tell a banker about valuation, capacity to pay, and deal structure?

**Key concepts**
- Three statement linkages: net income (IS) → retained earnings (BS) and cash from ops (CFS); CapEx (CFS) → PP&E (BS) → D&A (IS); debt issuance (CFS) → debt balance (BS) → interest expense (IS)
- Quality of earnings: non-recurring items (restructuring, litigation, gain/loss on sale), stock-based compensation (cash vs non-cash, dilution effect), working capital normalization, one-time tax benefits
- EBITDA → adjusted EBITDA → run-rate EBITDA → pro forma EBITDA (synergies, full-year acquisitions)
- Net working capital (NWC), net debt, **enterprise value to equity value bridge**: EV = Equity + Debt + Preferred + Noncontrolling Interest − Cash and equivalents
- Accounting policies: ASC 606 revenue recognition (5-step model: identify contract → performance obligations → transaction price → allocate → recognize); ASC 842 lease accounting (right-of-use asset, lease liability on balance sheet); ASC 805 acquisition accounting

**Applied skills**
- Build an EV-to-equity bridge from a target's balance sheet, in both directions
- Adjust EBITDA for one-time and non-cash items
- Identify red flags in working capital trends (DSO expanding faster than revenue = collections deteriorating; DPO expanding = stretching vendors)

**Common traps**
- The EV bridge: **EV = Equity + Debt + Preferred + Minority Interest − Cash**. Forgetting minority interest (NCI) is the most common modeling error in S79 candidates and in actual modeling tests.
- Stock-based comp is a *non-cash* expense added back in cash from ops — but it is *dilutive* and many sponsors require it to be deducted from EBITDA for valuation purposes. "Adjusted EBITDA" methodology must be specified.
- ASC 842 (effective 2019) put operating leases on the balance sheet. EV-to-equity comparisons across pre- and post-2019 financials must reconcile lease debt.
- Pro forma EBITDA with synergies is *not* historical EBITDA. Banker model run-rate carefully — buy-side and sell-side will fight over this number.

**Authoritative sources:** ASC 606, ASC 842, ASC 805 (FASB); Damodaran, *Investment Valuation*; Pignataro, *Financial Modeling and Valuation*.

## Chapter 2: Valuation — DCF, Comps, Precedents, LBO
**Core questions:** Which methodology fits the deal, and what does the football field tell the board about negotiating range?

**Key concepts**
- DCF: unlevered free cash flow (UFCF) = EBIT × (1 − tax) + D&A − CapEx − ΔNWC; discount at WACC; terminal value via Gordon Growth (TV = FCF × (1+g) / (WACC − g)) or Exit Multiple (TV = EBITDA × exit multiple)
- WACC = (E/V × Re) + (D/V × Rd × (1 − t)); Re from CAPM (Rf + β × MRP); unlevered beta and re-levering for capital structure
- Trading comps: peer selection (industry, size, growth, profitability), multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E, EV/EBIT)
- Precedent transactions: control premium typically 25-40%, synergies built in; deal-time differences require adjustment for market conditions
- LBO: sources and uses, IRR (typically 20%+ targeted), MOIC (typically 2-3x over 5 years), debt capacity (5-7x EBITDA leverage is common)
- Football field: combines DCF, comps, precedents, LBO floor, premiums paid analysis
- Accretion/dilution: pro forma EPS vs standalone EPS; deal is accretive when target's earnings yield > acquirer's cost of acquisition financing

**Applied skills**
- Compute WACC from a target's capital structure and beta (unlever target's beta with peer median, re-lever with deal capital structure)
- Decide when a precedent transaction is too old to use (typically 2-3 years; sector volatility shortens that)
- Run accretion/dilution on a stock-for-stock deal (use exchange ratio to compute new shares issued, recompute EPS)

**Common traps**
- Gordon Growth terminal value: g must be **less than WACC**, and typically less than long-term GDP growth (2-3%). Higher g produces infinite or negative TV.
- Discount terminal value back the **full forecast period** to PV. TV computed at year 5 must be discounted by (1+WACC)^5.
- Precedent transactions reflect *paid* control premiums. Comparing them directly to trading comps undercounts the premium.
- An all-cash deal financed with cash on hand can still be dilutive if the acquirer loses interest income greater than the target's earnings yield.

**Authoritative sources:** Rosenbaum & Pearl, *Investment Banking* (Wiley); Damodaran online; CFI valuation modules.

## Chapter 3: Equity Capital Markets and IPO Process
**Core questions:** How does a company go public, what is the role of each underwriter, and what disclosure rules apply at each step?

**Key concepts**
- Registration: **S-1** filing (initial IPO), **S-3** (shelf, large mature issuers, $75M public float), **F-1/F-3** (foreign private issuers)
- SEC review: comment letters typically 2-4 rounds over 90-180 days
- Roadshow, book building, pricing meeting, allocation, stabilization
- **Quiet period**: pre-filing (no offers), waiting period (preliminary prospectus / "red herring" permitted, no firm offers), post-effective (final prospectus required for confirmation)
- Underwriter types: firm commitment (most common, syndicate bears risk), best efforts, all-or-none, mini-max (minimum threshold required)
- **Greenshoe (over-allotment) option**: up to 15% of shares, exercisable within 30 days, allows underwriters to cover short syndicate position
- Syndicate structure: lead left book runner, joint book runners, co-managers
- Lock-ups: typically **180 days** for insiders and pre-IPO holders
- **JOBS Act** Emerging Growth Company status (revenue under $1.235B): confidential S-1 filing, fewer disclosures (2 years audited financials vs 3), testing the waters with QIBs/IAIs before filing
- **Regulation M** (anti-manipulation during distribution); **Rule 105** (no shorting within 5 business days of a follow-on if covering with the offering)

**Applied skills**
- Identify what underwriters can say in each phase of the quiet period (pre-filing: nothing; waiting: red herring + roadshow; post-effective: final prospectus)
- Compute syndicate economics from a gross spread (typically 7% for IPOs; broken into management fee, underwriting fee, and selling concession)
- Apply Reg M restrictions to a stabilizing bid (only on the day-of and limited to the offering price)

**Common traps**
- The greenshoe is a *call option* held by underwriters. They exercise if the stock rises above the offering price; they do not exercise if it falls (and instead cover their short by buying in the open market, which stabilizes).
- "Testing the waters" under JOBS Act is limited to QIBs (qualified institutional buyers) and IAIs (institutional accredited investors) — *not* retail.
- Rule 105 prohibits covering a follow-on subscription with shares sold short during a 5-business-day restricted period before pricing. Penalty is disgorgement plus civil fines.
- Lock-up early release requires lead underwriter consent. Lock-ups are contractual, not regulatory.

**Authoritative sources:** Securities Act of 1933 §§5, 6, 10; SEC Regulation M; SEC Rule 105; JOBS Act of 2012; FINRA Rule 5110 (corporate financing).

## Chapter 4: Private Placements and Exempt Offerings
**Core questions:** When does a deal stay private, and what restrictions apply to resale?

**Key concepts**
- **Reg D Rule 504**: up to $10M in 12 months; states retain registration authority
- **Rule 506(b)**: unlimited offering size; up to 35 non-accredited *sophisticated* investors + unlimited accredited; no general solicitation; restricted securities
- **Rule 506(c)**: unlimited offering size; accredited only, must *verify* (not self-certify); general solicitation permitted; restricted securities
- **Rule 144** resale: holding period — 6 months for reporting issuers, 12 months for non-reporting; volume limits — greater of 1% of outstanding or average weekly trading volume over 4 weeks; manner-of-sale and notice requirements (Form 144 if > 5,000 shares or > $50K)
- **Rule 144A**: resale to QIBs ($100M securities owned/invested); fungible with later registered shares via A/B exchange offer (registration rights)
- **Reg S**: offshore offering exemption; Category 1, 2, 3 with varying distribution compliance periods (40 days, 6 months, 1 year)
- **Reg A+** (Tier 1 up to $20M, Tier 2 up to $75M as of 2021): mini-IPO with ongoing reporting (Tier 2) but state preemption for Tier 2
- **PIPEs** (private investment in public equity): typically Reg D placement to accredited; registration rights to register resale within 60-180 days
- **Form D**: filed with SEC within **15 days** of first sale of securities

**Applied skills**
- Choose the right exemption for a $20M raise targeting institutional investors (likely Rule 506(b) or 506(c) depending on solicitation strategy)
- Compute Rule 144 dribble-out volume limit for a control person (greater of 1% or avg weekly volume)
- Identify when a Reg S offering can flow back to US investors (after applicable distribution compliance period, depending on Category)

**Common traps**
- **506(b) vs 506(c)**: 506(b) prohibits general solicitation but allows up to 35 sophisticated non-accredited; 506(c) requires verification but allows general solicitation and is accredited-only. Reverse-engineering the right one for the fact pattern is the gotcha.
- Rule 144 holding period **does not apply to non-affiliates** of reporting issuers after 12 months — they can sell freely without volume or manner restrictions.
- **144A is a resale exemption**, not an offering exemption. The initial sale to the IB / placement agent must rely on §4(a)(2) or another exemption; 144A handles the resale to QIBs.
- Reg A+ Tier 1 still requires state blue-sky review (coordinated review program). Tier 2 preempts state registration.

**Authoritative sources:** Securities Act §§4(a)(2), 4(a)(7); Regulation D (17 CFR §§230.500-506); Rule 144 (§230.144); Rule 144A (§230.144A); Regulation S; Regulation A+; SEC 2020 accredited investor amendments.

## Chapter 5: Debt Capital Markets
**Core questions:** What debt instrument matches the issuer's rating, use of proceeds, and covenant tolerance?

**Key concepts**
- Investment grade (BBB-/Baa3 and above) vs high yield (BB+/Ba1 and below); split-rated; fallen angels
- 144A bond offerings with registration rights (A/B exchange — issue 144A, register identical bonds, exchange to make freely tradable)
- **Covenants**: incurrence (tested only at the time of a specific action — issuing more debt, paying dividend) vs maintenance (tested quarterly — leverage ratio, coverage ratio); restricted payments (RP) baskets; debt baskets; restricted vs unrestricted subsidiaries
- Bank debt: revolver (committed line, undrawn fee), Term Loan A (amortizing, bank-held), Term Loan B (institutional, bullet, repriced often, lower covenant); second lien
- **Mezzanine** debt: subordinated, often with warrants; **PIK** (payment-in-kind) and PIK-toggle; **unitranche** (single facility blending senior and subordinated)
- Make-whole call: present-value-based redemption premium (typically Treasury + 50 bps); call schedule; equity clawback (typically up to 35% of issuance in first 3 years at par + coupon)
- Convertible bonds: coupon, conversion ratio, conversion price, contingent conversion, anti-dilution adjustments

**Applied skills**
- Stack a capital structure by priority and cost (senior secured → senior unsecured → subordinated → preferred → common)
- Identify the right instrument for an LBO financing (typically Term Loan B + senior unsecured notes + sponsor equity)
- Read a covenant package and distinguish restrictive vs permissive terms

**Common traps**
- **Incurrence vs maintenance**: high yield bonds typically have *only* incurrence covenants (tested at action); bank debt typically has *both*. "Cov-lite" loans have incurrence-only — most loans post-2017 are cov-lite.
- A make-whole call discount rate is typically Treasury + 50 bps — a small spread. The present value math results in a *premium*, not a discount; that premium is what makes the make-whole expensive for the issuer.
- Equity clawback typically allows the issuer to call up to **35%** of the issuance at a price equal to par plus the coupon, funded by an equity offering. Used when the issuer wants to deleverage after an IPO.
- 144A "for life" bonds (no registration rights) trade at a yield premium to the registered alternative. Registration rights agreements typically require the issuer to register within 90-180 days or pay step-up interest.

**Authoritative sources:** Standard & Poor's / Moody's rating definitions; Pratt's Capital Markets Manual; LMA / LSTA loan market guides.

## Chapter 6: M&A Process and Documentation
**Core questions:** What happens between engagement letter and closing, and what is the banker's role at each step?

**Key concepts**
- Sell-side process types: **broad auction** (many buyers, maximizes price, reveals data), **targeted auction** (10-20 buyers), **negotiated sale** (1-2 buyers, faster, lower disclosure)
- Process documents in order: **engagement letter → teaser (no-name profile) → NDA → CIM (confidential information memo) → management presentations → data room → IOI (indication of interest, non-binding) → LOI (letter of intent, often exclusivity) → due diligence → SPA (stock purchase agreement) or APA (asset purchase agreement) or merger agreement → closing**
- Indications of interest (IOIs): preliminary, non-binding, valuation range based on CIM
- Letters of intent (LOIs): often include exclusivity (30-60 days), valuation, structure, key conditions
- Definitive agreement: representations and warranties, indemnification baskets and caps, escrow, **MAC clause** (material adverse change/effect), reverse termination fee, breakup fee, go-shop period, no-shop with fiduciary out
- Antitrust: **HSR Act** filing if size-of-transaction ≥ $119.5M (2024) and size-of-person tests met; initial 30-day waiting period (15 days for cash tender offers); second request extends period until 30 days after substantial compliance

**Applied skills**
- Sequence the documents in a sell-side process from teaser to closing
- Identify deal-protection provisions in a merger agreement (no-shop, break fee, force-the-vote)
- Determine whether a go-shop is appropriate for the target's board (typically yes when there was no pre-signing auction — Revlon duties suggest a market check)

**Common traps**
- **MAC clauses** are rarely successfully invoked. Most US courts require the change to be (1) durationally significant, (2) substantial in magnitude, and (3) outside the buyer's knowledge or risk allocation at signing. Akorn v. Fresenius is the major case where a MAC was upheld.
- A **go-shop** allows the target to solicit competing offers for a window (typically 30-50 days) after signing. Different from a no-shop (forbids solicitation but allows responses to unsolicited bids under fiduciary out).
- Reverse termination fee = buyer pays if buyer walks (or financing fails). Breakup fee = target pays if it accepts a superior proposal. Different parties, different triggers.
- HSR size-of-transaction threshold adjusts annually for GDP. The 2024 figure is **$119.5M**.

**Authoritative sources:** Hart-Scott-Rodino Act (15 USC §18a); Delaware General Corporation Law §251; Revlon v. MacAndrews & Forbes (1986); Akorn v. Fresenius (Delaware Chancery 2018).

## Chapter 7: Tender Offers, Proxy Fights, and Hostile M&A
**Core questions:** How does a bidder reach shareholders directly, and what disclosures and timelines control the contest?

**Key concepts**
- **Williams Act of 1968**: amended Exchange Act to regulate tender offers
- **Schedule 13D**: 5% beneficial ownership; filed within **10 days** of crossing 5%; "passive" investors may file 13G instead
- **Schedule TO**: filed by bidder upon commencement of tender offer
- **Schedule 14D-9**: filed by target with recommendation (recommend, oppose, neutral) within **10 business days** of TO commencement
- Tender offer mechanics: **20 business day minimum** open period; **all-holders rule** (open to all holders of class); **best-price rule** (highest price paid is paid to all)
- Tender extension: **10 business days** if material change to terms; price changes (other than de minimis) reset the 20-day clock
- Defenses: **poison pill** (rights plan diluting acquirer if they cross threshold), **staggered board** (only 1/3 of directors elected per year, slows takeover), **white knight** (friendly competing bidder), **Pac-Man** (target bids for acquirer), **golden parachute** (rich executive severance), **scorched earth** (sell off crown jewels), **greenmail** (pay raider to go away — taxed heavily post-1987)
- Going-private transactions: **Rule 13e-3** (filing requirements, fairness disclosure, often appraisal rights)
- Two-step transactions: tender + back-end merger via Section 251(h) DGCL (short-form merger at 50% acceptance — replacing the old 90% top-up option)

**Applied skills**
- Time a 13D filing and amendment after a material change (10 days for initial; "promptly" for amendment)
- Identify when a tender offer must be extended (material change → 10 business days)
- Apply Rule 14e-5 anti-fraud restrictions during a tender (no off-tender purchases by bidder during the open period, with limited exceptions)

**Common traps**
- **Best-price rule** under Rule 14d-10: highest consideration paid to *any* tendering shareholder must be paid to *all*. Compensation arrangements with management can be challenged as "consideration" if not bona fide employment-related.
- A **poison pill** is triggered when an acquirer crosses a threshold (typically 10-20%) without board approval. Pills can be redeemed by the board — making them a negotiation tool, not a permanent barrier.
- **13D vs 13G**: 13D is for active investors who may seek to influence control; 13G is for passive (under 20%, no control intent). Activists almost always file 13D.
- A **two-step tender** under DGCL §251(h) (added 2013) allows a back-end merger at 50% + 1 acceptance, eliminating the old 90% top-up requirement.

**Authoritative sources:** Williams Act §§13(d)-14(f); Regulation 14D; Regulation 14E; Rule 13e-3; Delaware General Corporation Law §251(h); Moran v. Household International (1985, poison pills upheld).

## Chapter 8: Restructuring, Bankruptcy, and Fairness Opinions
**Core questions:** When a company can't pay, who gets what and in what order — and what does a banker actually deliver to the board?

**Key concepts**
- **Chapter 11** reorganization: debtor-in-possession (DIP); DIP financing typically primes existing liens with priority; plan of reorganization; voting by class (impaired classes vote, ⅔ in amount + ½ in number); **cramdown** of dissenting class if "fair and equitable" + does not discriminate unfairly
- **Chapter 7** liquidation: trustee appointed, no reorganization, assets liquidated
- **Absolute priority rule**: secured (to collateral value) → administrative claims → priority unsecured (taxes, wages up to cap) → general unsecured → subordinated → preferred → common. No class can recover until senior classes paid in full (with limited exceptions for new value).
- Out-of-court restructuring: **exchange offer** (offer existing bondholders new bonds with revised terms), **consent solicitation** (modify covenants if a supermajority consents), tender + consent
- **363 sale**: sale of assets in bankruptcy under §363; **stalking horse bid** sets floor with break fee protection; competing bids in auction
- **Fairness opinion**: banker's opinion to the board that consideration is fair from a financial point of view; scope, methodologies (DCF, comps, precedents, premiums paid), conflicts disclosure, FINRA Rule 5150 procedural requirements
- **Solvency opinion**: distinct from fairness — opines that company is solvent before/after a transaction (especially dividend recaps and LBOs)

**Applied skills**
- Order creditor claims under the absolute priority rule
- Identify when a 363 sale is preferable to a plan of reorganization (faster, free-and-clear sale, particularly good for distressed assets that lose value over time)
- Evaluate the methodologies disclosed in a fairness opinion (DCF, trading comps, precedent transactions, premiums paid)

**Common traps**
- **DIP financing** typically has super-priority and primes pre-petition liens with court approval under §364(d). This is why DIP lenders charge premium rates — they get the safest seat at the table.
- **Cramdown** requires the plan to satisfy the "absolute priority rule" against dissenting classes. The "new value" exception (LaSalle case) allows equity holders to contribute new value and retain interest, but only in narrow circumstances.
- **Fairness opinion**: FINRA Rule 5150 requires written disclosure of (1) prior material relationships, (2) compensation arrangements (especially contingent fees), (3) committee or independent review of the opinion. The opinion is *not* a recommendation to vote; it is a fairness-from-a-financial-point-of-view assessment.
- **Stalking horse** bids typically receive a break fee (1-3% of bid value) and expense reimbursement as protection for setting the auction floor. Without protection, no one wants to be the stalking horse.

**Authoritative sources:** Bankruptcy Code §§363, 364, 1129; FINRA Rule 5150 (fairness opinions); In re LaSalle Street Partnership (1999); In re Lehman Brothers Holdings (DIP precedent).

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
150 minutes for 75 scored items is **120 seconds each** — Series 79 gives you more time per question than the SIE or 63, but the questions are denser and the math is harder. Calculator is on-screen four-function only; scratch paper and pencil provided.

- **Use scratch paper for every valuation question.** Do not try to compute WACC, accretion/dilution, or EV-to-equity in your head. The most common Series 79 failure mode is rushed arithmetic, not weak conceptual understanding.
- **When two answers look equally correct**, choose the one a senior banker would defend to a client. The exam rewards judgment, not just recall. If one answer is technically right but "the banker would never say that to a client," pick the other one.
- **The 27/37/36 split**: Data analysis questions cluster early; offerings in the middle; M&A and restructuring at the end. If you are running long on data analysis, flag a question and move — momentum compounds across 75 questions.
- **The accretion/dilution trap**: a stock-for-stock deal at a P/E above your own = accretion if the target's earnings yield (E/P) > the cost of issuing your stock; dilution otherwise. Mixed cash/stock deals require allocating financing cost across components.
- **Day before**: review the EV bridge, WACC formula, Rule 144 volume formula, 506(b)/506(c) distinction, Williams Act timeline, absolute priority order. Light review only. Sleep.

If your last two full-length practice exams averaged 78%+ across all three job functions, you are ready. If valuation/data analysis is your weak area, dedicate the day-before window to drilling 25 valuation items only.
