# Financial Modeling
**ID**: `financialModeling` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Build financial models that are useful, readable, auditable, and decision-ready. Learners practice driver-based forecasting, three-statement integration, DCF valuation, scenario analysis, and model communication without turning Excel into a haunted mansion.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus identified the right core: assumptions, statement logic, sensitivities, and cash flow. This rewrite adds model architecture, historical analysis, revenue builds, working capital, debt schedules, valuation, error checks, outputs, presentation, and a capstone model-review workflow.

## Chapter 1: Modeling Standards and Architecture
**Key concepts**: model purpose, input/processing/output structure, color coding, audit trail, sign convention, hardcode discipline, checks, version control, scenario toggles.

**Practical questions**
- What decision will this model support?
- Can another analyst follow the logic without a flashlight?
- Where could a small formula error change the recommendation?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a clean model shell with assumptions, historicals, forecast schedules, outputs, checks, and documentation.

## Chapter 2: Historical Financials and Operating Drivers
**Key concepts**: income statement, balance sheet, cash flow statement, revenue drivers, cost structure, margins, KPIs, seasonality, nonrecurring items, common-size analysis.

**Practical questions**
- What actually drives revenue and margins?
- Which historical data should be normalized?
- What ratios explain business performance rather than merely decorating it?

**Workplace skill**
- Spread three years of historical financials and calculate operating metrics, margins, growth, and sanity checks.

## Chapter 3: Revenue, Expense, and Working Capital Forecasts
**Key concepts**: volume/price build, bookings, churn, utilization, headcount, COGS, opex, DSO, DIO, DPO, deferred revenue, working capital turns.

**Practical questions**
- Is the forecast driver-based or just a growth-rate blanket?
- Which assumptions are controllable, market-driven, or structurally constrained?
- Does working capital support the revenue story?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a revenue and cost forecast with at least two driver cases and working-capital schedules.

## Chapter 4: Three-Statement Integration
**Key concepts**: depreciation, amortization, capex, debt schedule, interest, taxes, retained earnings, cash sweep, circularity, balance sheet balancing, cash flow linkage.

**Practical questions**
- How does one assumption move through all three statements?
- Why does the balance sheet not balance?
- Which formula should be a plug, and which should never be a plug?

**Workplace skill**
- Build an integrated three-statement model with working checks and a debt schedule.

## Chapter 5: Free Cash Flow and DCF Valuation
**Key concepts**: unlevered free cash flow, WACC, terminal value, perpetuity growth, exit multiple, enterprise value, equity value, net debt, discounting.

**Practical questions**
- What cash flow is available to capital providers?
- Which terminal value method fits the business and assumption confidence?
- What valuation conclusion survives reasonable sensitivity ranges?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a DCF with WACC assumptions, terminal value, enterprise-to-equity bridge, and sensitivity table.

## Chapter 6: Comparables, Scenarios, and Sensitivities
**Key concepts**: trading comps, precedent transactions, multiples, football field, scenario analysis, data tables, tornado chart, breakeven, downside case.

**Practical questions**
- Which peers are actually comparable?
- Which assumptions drive most of the valuation movement?
- What does the downside case reveal about risk?

**Workplace skill**
- Create a valuation output page with DCF, comps, scenarios, sensitivities, and concise interpretation.

## Chapter 7: Model Review, Error Detection, and Stress Testing
**Key concepts**: formula audit, circular reference, hardcode scan, balance check, cash check, sign check, reasonableness check, sensitivity check, peer review.

**Practical questions**
- What would a skeptical reviewer test first?
- Which numbers look precise but are assumption-heavy?
- Can the model fail gracefully when inputs change?

**Workplace skill**
- Perform a model audit and produce a review notes log with severity, location, issue, fix, and owner.

## Chapter 8: Communicating the Model
**Key concepts**: executive summary, assumption bridge, output page, recommendation, limitation, caveat, source note, chart design, decision memo.

**Practical questions**
- What should a decision maker remember after closing the workbook?
- Which caveats are honest and which are hedging?
- How do we explain uncertainty without sounding useless?

**Workplace skill**
- Write a one-page investment or operating decision memo from model outputs.

## Capstone: Three-Statement + DCF Case
Learners build and present a model for one company or case:
- historical financial spread
- driver-based forecast
- three-statement integration
- debt/capex/working capital schedules
- DCF valuation
- comparable multiples
- scenario and sensitivity analysis
- model checks
- recommendation memo
- model review log

## Assessment Ideas
- Model shell standards check
- Historical spread and ratio analysis
- Revenue build lab
- Three-statement integration test
- DCF valuation review
- Sensitivity/scenario output page
- Model audit
- Capstone model and memo
