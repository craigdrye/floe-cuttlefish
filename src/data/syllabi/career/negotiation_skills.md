# Negotiation Skills
**ID**: `negotiation` · **Discipline**: Communication · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course teaches negotiation as structured problem-solving under tension, not as a battle of wills or a personality trait you either have or lack. The core claim is that most outcomes are decided before anyone speaks: by preparation, by a clear-eyed read of each side's alternatives, and by whether you can expand the deal before you divide it. Learners build a repeatable process they can run in compensation talks, vendor renewals, client discounts, cross-functional priority fights, and multi-party partnerships.

The spine of the course is the distinction between **creating value** (making the pie bigger through trades, packages, and contingent terms) and **claiming value** (deciding who gets which slice through anchoring, concessions, and leverage). These two activities are in permanent tension — the very moves that win you a bigger slice can shrink the pie, and vice versa. This is the negotiator's dilemma, and managing it deliberately is the skill that separates a competent negotiator from a lucky one. Learners leave able to do both, and to know which one a given moment calls for.

The course is grounded in the canonical literature — Fisher and Ury's principled negotiation, Lax and Sebenius on value creation and claiming, Raiffa on process, and modern practitioner work on tactical empathy and multiple-offer strategies — but it is relentlessly practical. Every chapter ends in a work product a learner could carry into a real negotiation the next morning: a prep sheet, a package plan, a tactic-response memo, an agreement summary. The voice is adult and direct. We do not promise that preparation guarantees a good outcome; we show that the absence of preparation nearly guarantees a bad one.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus had the right building blocks: interests, BATNA, ZOPA, anchoring, conversation control, tradeoffs, and closing. This rewrite keeps those and sharpens them into a single arc — prepare, create, claim, communicate, defend, align, close — so that each chapter feeds the next rather than standing alone. Every chapter now carries an explicit **Common traps** section, because in this domain the most valuable learning is diagnosing why a plausible-sounding move backfires (anchoring without a walkaway, splitting the difference reflexively, sharing information that destroys leverage, signing a deal nobody internal will implement). Named frameworks are introduced precisely (principled negotiation's four moves, logrolling, MESO, decreasing-concession patterns, tactical empathy, the winner's curse) so the question bank can test them at exam grade. The labs and capstone remain, now anchored to the same vocabulary.

## Chapter 1: Negotiation Fundamentals
**Core questions**
- What happens to each side if no deal is reached?
- What do we want, why do we want it, and what might they actually need?
- Is there a real zone of possible agreement, or are we wasting our time?

**Key concepts**: interests vs. positions, BATNA (best alternative to a negotiated agreement), reservation point / walkaway, target / aspiration, ZOPA (zone of possible agreement), leverage, relationship vs. substance, process.

**Applied skills**
- Build a prep sheet with goal, BATNA, reservation point, target, the counterpart's likely interests, and an opening plan.
- Estimate a ZOPA from each side's reservation points and decide whether a deal is even possible.

**Common traps**
- Confusing your *position* (the number you state) with your *interest* (why you want it) and arguing the number instead of the need.
- Treating BATNA as a threat to wield rather than a private benchmark that sets your reservation point.
- Entering with a target but no walkaway, so any concession feels acceptable and you drift past your own floor.

## Chapter 2: Creating Value Before Claiming It
**Core questions**
- What issues besides price actually matter to each side?
- Which trade costs me little but matters a lot to them?
- What information can I share safely that expands the pie rather than handing away leverage?

**Key concepts**: integrative negotiation, value creation, issue expansion, logrolling (trading across issues by relative priority), multi-issue packages, contingent agreements, objective criteria, information exchange, mutual gains.

**Applied skills**
- Design three multi-issue packages that trade across price, timing, risk, scope, quality, support, or relationship value.
- Identify a difference in priorities or forecasts and turn it into a logrolling trade or a contingent contract.

**Common traps**
- Negotiating a single issue (price) when the real value lives in the bundle, leaving joint gains on the table.
- Assuming the other side values everything the way you do, so no profitable trade is ever discovered.
- "Splitting the difference" on every issue, which is fast but systematically destroys integrative value.

## Chapter 3: Claiming Value and Bargaining Tactics
**Core questions**
- Should I anchor first, invite their first offer, or gather more information before either?
- What concession pattern protects value and signals where my limit is?
- When is firmness real leverage, and when is it just theater?

**Key concepts**: distributive bargaining, anchoring and adjustment (Tversky–Kahneman), first offer, midpoint pull, concession patterns, decreasing/tapering concessions, MESO (multiple equivalent simultaneous offers), the negotiator's dilemma, the winner's curse, silence, deadlines, counteroffers.

**Applied skills**
- Plan an opening anchor justified by a rationale, with a pre-set concession ladder that shrinks each step.
- Construct a MESO: three offers of equal value to you but differing structure, to read the counterpart's priorities without conceding.

**Common traps**
- Anchoring aggressively with no support, so the anchor is dismissed instead of pulling the midpoint.
- Making equal or *increasing* concessions, which signals there is always more to give.
- Falling to the winner's curse: an offer accepted instantly is often a sign you left money on the table or misjudged value.

## Chapter 4: Communication, Emotion, and Trust
**Core questions**
- What question would reveal the constraint hiding behind their stated position?
- How do I lower the emotional temperature without surrendering substance?
- What trust signal is actually credible rather than performative?

**Key concepts**: active listening, diagnostic / calibrated questions, paraphrasing and mirroring, labeling emotions, tactical empathy, face-saving, tone, accusation audit, psychological safety, cultural context, credibility.

**Applied skills**
- Run a difficult conversation using inquiry, summary, reframing, and a conditional proposal.
- Use a calibrated "how" or "what" question to shift problem-solving onto the counterpart without conceding.

**Common traps**
- Reacting to the emotion as if it were the substance, and conceding to make discomfort stop.
- Mistaking agreement for understanding — "I hear you" without demonstrating you actually grasped their interest.
- Confusing empathy with capitulation; naming a feeling is not the same as accepting a demand.

## Chapter 5: Power, Ethics, and Difficult Tactics
**Core questions**
- Which moves are hard but legitimate bargaining, and which are unethical or relationship-destroying?
- How should I respond to a threat, a lie, or a take-it-or-leave-it ultimatum?
- When should the negotiation stop entirely?

**Key concepts**: hard bargaining vs. bad faith, deception and misrepresentation, bluffing, threats and ultimatums, false deadlines, the nibble, power imbalance, confidentiality, fairness norms, reputation, escalation, walking away.

**Applied skills**
- Write a response plan for five difficult tactics: extreme anchor, false deadline, missing authority, personal attack, and hidden constraint.
- Distinguish a hard-but-fair tactic from one that crosses an ethical or legal line, and choose a proportionate response.

**Common traps**
- Matching a bad-faith tactic with your own bad faith, escalating instead of naming and neutralizing it.
- Caving to a deadline or ultimatum without testing whether it is real.
- Conceding to a post-agreement "nibble" because the deal feels too close to risk, training the counterpart to keep asking.

## Chapter 6: Internal and Multi-Party Negotiation
**Core questions**
- Are we negotiating externally before we have aligned internally?
- Who can quietly veto implementation after the deal is signed?
- Which coalition or sequence changes the bargaining range?

**Key concepts**: principal–agent problem, mandate and authority, internal alignment, stakeholder mapping, coalitions, sequencing, caucus, consensus vs. majority, implementation risk, the two-table problem (internal and external).

**Applied skills**
- Build an internal stakeholder map and a mandate memo before opening an external negotiation.
- Sequence approaches to multiple parties to build a coalition that shifts leverage.

**Common traps**
- Granting concessions externally that you have no internal authority or mandate to deliver.
- Ignoring a low-profile internal veto holder until they block implementation.
- Treating a multi-party deal as a series of identical two-party deals, missing coalition dynamics.

## Chapter 7: Closing, Documentation, and Implementation
**Core questions**
- What exactly did we agree to, and who will do what, by when?
- Which ambiguity in this deal will become next quarter's conflict?
- How will the relationship function the day after the signature?

**Key concepts**: term sheet, durable vs. fragile agreement, ambiguity vs. deliberate flexibility, contingencies, conditions precedent, follow-up and handoff, post-settlement settlement, relationship repair, post-negotiation review.

**Applied skills**
- Draft a plain-language agreement summary with obligations, conditions, dates, owners, open issues, and an escalation path.
- Run a post-settlement settlement: after agreement but before signing, probe for trades that improve the deal for both sides.

**Common traps**
- Celebrating "agreement in principle" while leaving the operative details ambiguous, guaranteeing a later dispute.
- Optimizing the signed terms while neglecting the working relationship that has to deliver them.
- Skipping the post-negotiation review, so the same mistakes repeat in the next deal.

## Chapter 8: Negotiation Labs
**Simulation set**
- salary / compensation negotiation
- vendor renewal under a deadline
- cross-functional prioritization fight
- customer discount request
- manager / employee workload conflict
- multi-party partnership

**Reflection discipline (after every lab)**
- What did I prepare, and what did I miss?
- Where did I create value? Where did I claim it?
- What did the other side teach me about their interests?
- What would I do differently with the same hand?

## Capstone: Negotiation Playbook
Learners assemble a reusable playbook containing:
- a prep template
- a BATNA / ZOPA analysis
- a counterpart map
- an issue-package and logrolling plan
- a communication script (diagnostic questions, labels, conditional proposals)
- a difficult-tactic response plan
- an internal alignment and mandate memo
- a plain-language agreement summary
- a post-negotiation retrospective

The capstone is assessed on coherence — whether the prep, the value-creation plan, the claiming strategy, and the closing documents reinforce one another rather than contradict — and on whether the learner can defend each choice against the common traps named above.

## Assessment Ideas
- Negotiation prep sheet with explicit BATNA, reservation point, and target
- Multi-issue package design exercise (with at least one logrolling trade)
- Single-issue distributive bargaining simulation with concession-pattern analysis
- Difficult-conversation roleplay scored on inquiry and reframing
- Difficult-tactic response memo
- Multi-party negotiation simulation with stakeholder map
- Plain-language agreement summary
- Negotiation Playbook capstone
