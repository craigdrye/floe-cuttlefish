# IQ & Reasoning Tests: The Aptitude Toolkit
**ID**: `iqReasoningTests` · **Discipline**: Self & Society

## Course Aim
Reasoning tests are the closest thing modern workplaces and admissions systems have to a universal benchmark. They show up as Wonderlic, SHL, Kenexa, Talent Q, Cubiks Logiks, the WAIS subtests behind clinical IQ scores, and the cognitive sections of GMAT/GRE/LSAT/MCAT. They also show up in unfortunate dinner-party arguments about what IQ actually measures.

This course gives you the practical pattern fluency to perform well on those tests — number series, verbal analogies, spatial rotations, Raven's-style matrices, syllogisms, deductive reasoning, working memory tasks — alongside the psychometric and historical literacy to interpret the scores. You will learn what crystallized vs fluid intelligence really are, why the Flynn effect happened, where g (general intelligence) holds up empirically, and where it doesn't.

This is a Fundamentals course for adults 20+ preparing for employer aptitude assessments, graduate admissions cognitive sections, or simply wanting to understand the science behind a contested public concept. It pairs well with Brain Burners and Quant Interview Core.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test (a) a specific reasoning subtype (verbal analogy, number series, abstract matrix, spatial), (b) the psychometrics of cognitive tests (reliability, g loading, score interpretation, percentile rank), or (c) the workplace/admissions use cases (Wonderlic, SHL Verify, Talent Q, GRE/GMAT cognitive sections). Voice should be coaching, calm, and confident about the parts that are empirically settled.

## Chapter 1: What IQ Measures (and Doesn't)
**Core questions:** What is g? What's the difference between fluid and crystallized intelligence? Why does Spearman's discovery still matter?

**Key concepts**
- Spearman's g (1904): positive manifold — cognitive tests correlate with each other
- Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) three-stratum model: g at top, broad abilities, narrow abilities
- Fluid intelligence (Gf) — novel-problem reasoning, peaks early adulthood, declines from 30s
- Crystallized intelligence (Gc) — accumulated knowledge, peaks later 50s-70s
- IQ scales (standardized mean 100, SD 15)
- What IQ predicts (job performance, educational outcomes) — and what it doesn't (wisdom, creativity beyond a threshold, character)

**Applied skills**
- Interpret an IQ score in standard-deviation units
- Recognise when "g" is doing more or less of the predictive work

**Common traps**
- Treating IQ as fixed — it shifts with development and across measurement instances
- Conflating IQ with intelligence — the concept is broader
- Forgetting that g loading varies by test (Raven's high, vocabulary moderate)

## Chapter 2: Number Series & Numerical Reasoning
**Core questions:** How do you spot the rule of a number series fast? What numerical-reasoning tricks come up on employer tests?

**Key concepts**
- Arithmetic progressions: constant difference (+5, +5, +5)
- Geometric progressions: constant ratio (×3, ×3, ×3)
- Mixed (alternating two patterns: +2, ×3, +2, ×3)
- Quadratic / triangular / square / Fibonacci-style series
- Differences-of-differences technique
- Numerical word problems: percentages, ratios, rates, units conversions
- Reading data tables and charts under time pressure

**Applied skills**
- First-glance trick: take differences. If constant, arithmetic; if doubling-style, geometric
- Spot interleaved patterns by separating odd-position and even-position terms
- Estimate confidently — most employer tests reward speed + accuracy, not perfection

**Common traps**
- Forcing arithmetic when the rule is geometric or alternating
- Reading "11, 22, 33, 44, ..." as +11 when the rule may also be ×concat-digit
- Spending >2 min on one item when others are easier

## Chapter 3: Verbal Analogies & Vocabulary
**Core questions:** What are the canonical analogy relations? How do you eliminate wrong choices?

**Key concepts**
- Standard relations: synonym, antonym, category-instance, part-whole, function, cause-effect, sequence, degree
- Wrong-answer patterns: reversed relation, same word in different context, "feels related" but wrong
- Vocabulary depth as a Gc proxy
- Eponym and Greek/Latin root strategies

**Applied skills**
- Articulate the relation in a phrase ("X is a kind of Y") before choosing
- Eliminate by testing the reverse direction
- Use root knowledge to break unfamiliar pairs

**Common traps**
- Choosing on association rather than the actual relation
- Reversing direction in answer (apple:fruit :: chair:?  → wood vs. furniture — pick the same direction)
- Picking the rare word as "obviously right" because it sounds clever

## Chapter 4: Logical Sequences & Syllogisms
**Core questions:** When is an argument valid? How do you spot a quantifier trap?

**Key concepts**
- Conditional reasoning: P→Q, contrapositive ~Q→~P, denying the antecedent and affirming the consequent fallacies
- Categorical syllogisms (All A are B; Some A are B; No A are B)
- Diagram methods (Venn for syllogisms; arrows for conditionals)
- Necessary vs sufficient conditions
- LSAT-style stems (must be true, could be true, must be false)

**Applied skills**
- Convert prose into formal P→Q and contrapositives
- Diagram a syllogism in under 30 seconds
- Spot the quantifier shift in "all" vs "some" vs "most"

**Common traps**
- Treating "most" as "all"
- Affirming the consequent (P→Q, Q observed, infer P — invalid)
- Mistaking necessary for sufficient

## Chapter 5: Abstract / Matrix Reasoning
**Core questions:** What rules govern Raven's matrices? How do you scan a 3×3 grid efficiently?

**Key concepts**
- Raven's Progressive Matrices (Standard, Advanced, Coloured) and Raven's 2
- Rules within Raven's: distribution-of-3, constant-in-rows, addition/subtraction of figures, progression
- High g loading (typically 0.7+) — why this test is used as a g proxy
- APM (Advanced Progressive Matrices) for above-average populations

**Applied skills**
- Scan rows first, then columns, then diagonals
- Articulate the rule before choosing
- Eliminate by checking the rule applies to all 3 rows

**Common traps**
- Choosing on surface similarity instead of rule consistency
- Missing the second rule (most APM items use 2-3 rules combined)
- Running out of time on the last 3-5 items where rules stack hardest

## Chapter 6: Spatial Reasoning
**Core questions:** What's the difference between mental rotation and paper folding? How do you not get fooled by mirror images?

**Key concepts**
- Mental rotation (Shepard & Metzler 1971): 3D figures, varying angles
- Paper folding / hole punching
- Cubes and nets (unfolding a cube; matching opposite faces)
- Cross-section problems
- Sex-difference research and its caveats

**Applied skills**
- Track one fixed feature through the rotation
- For cubes, fix a 'top' face and infer the rest from adjacency
- Eliminate mirror images by chirality (clockwise vs counterclockwise feature ordering)

**Common traps**
- Choosing a mirror image — looks similar but chirality differs
- Misreading a 2D projection as a different solid
- Running out of working memory tracking three transformations at once

## Chapter 7: Working Memory & Processing Speed
**Core questions:** What WAIS subtests measure these? Why do they affect overall IQ scores?

**Key concepts**
- WAIS-IV subtests: Digit Span (forward, backward, sequencing), Arithmetic, Letter-Number Sequencing
- Processing speed: Symbol Search, Coding, Cancellation
- Working memory ≠ short-term memory — it includes manipulation
- Aging effects: processing speed declines from mid-20s onward
- Practice effects and ceiling effects

**Applied skills**
- Chunk long digit spans (e.g. groupings of 3-4)
- Pace symbol-search style tasks with rhythm, not max speed bursts
- Train spaced retrieval to lift working memory operationally (modest gains)

**Common traps**
- Believing working-memory training transfers to general IQ (mixed evidence, mostly task-specific)
- Confusing fast typing with processing speed in the WAIS sense

## Chapter 8: Wechsler & Stanford-Binet
**Core questions:** What is the modern Wechsler structure? When is Stanford-Binet preferred?

**Key concepts**
- WAIS-IV / WAIS-5: Verbal Comprehension Index, Perceptual Reasoning, Working Memory, Processing Speed → Full Scale IQ (FSIQ)
- WAIS used age 16+; WISC for ages 6-16; WPPSI for 2:6-7:7
- Stanford-Binet 5 (SB5): fluid + knowledge + quantitative + visual-spatial + working memory
- Norming and cultural adaptation
- Clinical use: ADHD profiles, learning disability identification, cognitive impairment

**Applied skills**
- Read a WAIS index pattern (e.g. high VCI / low PSI = classic profile)
- Recognise when an FSIQ summary masks meaningful sub-index variation

**Common traps**
- Reporting a single IQ when the indices diverge by >15 points
- Treating WAIS as job-performance instrument — it's clinical/diagnostic, not selection

## Chapter 9: Workplace Aptitude Tests
**Core questions:** Which tests do employers actually use? How are they validated?

**Key concepts**
- Wonderlic Personnel Test (50 items, 12 min) — famously short, broad g
- SHL Verify Numerical / Verbal / Inductive
- Kenexa / IBM
- Talent Q Elements
- Cubiks Logiks
- General Aptitude Test Battery (GATB) — older, US government
- Civil service tests (UK, US, India)
- Validation expectations: criterion validity, adverse impact analysis (4/5ths rule), local norms
- Legal frameworks: US Title VII + ADA; UK Equality Act 2010; EU employment law

**Applied skills**
- Recognise which test type fits which job (numerical for finance; verbal for legal; inductive for engineering)
- Anticipate score reporting (percentile rank vs raw score vs SHL "Norm Group" comparison)

**Common traps**
- Practicing the wrong test type — most candidates over-prepare numerical and under-prepare inductive
- Treating coaching effects as small — moderate practice gives ~5-10 point lifts on most workplace tests

## Chapter 10: Score Interpretation & Limits
**Core questions:** What does a percentile rank actually mean? What's the SEM around a score? How does the Flynn effect work?

**Key concepts**
- IQ as standardized score (M=100, SD=15)
- Percentile rank vs standard score (130 = 98th percentile)
- Standard error of measurement (SEM ~3-5 points on WAIS) — a single score is a range, not a point
- Flynn effect (~3 IQ points per decade gain through 20th century in industrialized countries; recent reversal in some samples)
- Ceiling effects on standard instruments above ~135-140
- Practice effects on retest (5-10 point inflation common)
- Test bias controversies and modern psychometrics responses

**Applied skills**
- Translate a raw score to percentile rank
- Report a confidence interval, not just a point estimate
- Anticipate the score difference your audience expects (e.g. test publisher norms vs population norms)

**Common traps**
- Reading a single score without its confidence interval
- Comparing raw scores across two different tests
- Forgetting that any single test session is a sample, not a fixed measurement

## Capstone: Practical Strategy
Drill timed Raven's-style and number series for 20 minutes per day for 2 weeks. Take one full SHL Verify or Wonderlic to calibrate. Note your error patterns — most candidates have one weak subtype (commonly inductive or spatial). The goal is fluency, not perfection; on most employer tests, 80-85% accuracy at speed beats 100% at half-speed.
