# Venture Capital Roadmap
**ID**: `ventureCapitalRoadmap` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Venture capital looks like picking winners; it is actually managing a portfolio of nearly-certain failures in search of the handful of outliers that pay for everything else. A seed or early-stage fund will write the great majority of its checks into companies that return little or nothing, and earn its entire return from the one or two that return the fund many times over. That single fact — the power law — bends every decision in the job. It means the question is never "is this a good business?" but "could this plausibly be a fund-returner, and do I have a differentiated reason to believe it?" It means a diligence process tuned to avoid losses is the wrong process, because the cost of missing the outlier dwarfs the cost of the failures you correctly screen out. This course teaches the repeatable craft of early-stage venture judgment: building a thesis about where value will accrue, sourcing into it, separating real traction from a good story, pricing a deal whose downside is total and whose upside is the only thing that matters, and writing the memo that makes the partnership act.

The emphasis throughout is on evidence and asymmetry, not founder mystique or pattern-matched vibes. At pre-seed and seed the data is thin and the narrative is loud, so the skill is knowing which scarce signals actually carry information — who the customers are and how badly they need the product, whether the founder is compounding their understanding faster than anyone else in the space, whether usage is pulling or being pushed — and which impressive-sounding metrics are noise dressed as proof. You will work in the perspective of someone who answers to limited partners and to a partnership: capital is finite, ownership is the thing that determines whether a win matters, reserves have to be allocated before you know which companies deserve them, and a fund is judged a decade later on DPI, not on how exciting its first board decks looked. The work is structured around a hard discipline borrowed from the best investors — name the bet, name the evidence, name what would prove you wrong, and size the position so the inevitable losses are survivable and the outliers are owned in size.

By the end, a learner should be able to take a real or assigned company and produce an investor-grade view: a thesis and market map that says where they are hunting and why now, a sourcing rationale for why this lead is worth conviction, a diligence pack that separates reported claims from confirmed evidence, a fund-math note that ties check size and ownership to the exit that would make the deal matter, and a one-page investment recommendation a partnership could act on — with the core risk, the falsifier, and the reserve plan stated plainly. The goal is not enthusiasm; it is enthusiasm that is sized, priced, evidence-led, and honest about how it loses.

## Course Design Notes
The course follows the real life of a venture investment, from thesis to exit. It opens with the fund mechanics and the power-law math that govern every other decision, because an analyst who does not understand how the fund makes money will diligence the wrong things. It then moves through sourcing and market mapping, the traction and unit-economics diligence that tests the numbers, the founder/product/customer evidence that tests everything the numbers cannot, the deal terms and memo where a decision is actually made, and finally portfolio support, reserves, and exits, where most of a fund's eventual return is actually decided. Each chapter assumes the prior ones, mirroring how an investor builds toward conviction and then lives with it for ten years. Route questions here when they test venture career skills — thesis and market mapping, sourcing, startup metrics and diligence, founder and customer evidence, deal terms and valuation, the investment memo, fund mechanics and power-law math, reserves and follow-on, board support, and exits. Send deep accounting and modeling drills to Financial Modeling, public-market valuation craft to Equity Research, and the lender's view to Credit and LevFin; this course owns the early-stage investor's decision.

Keep every answer anchored to the chain that defines the discipline: a thesis about where value accrues, a differentiated reason to believe this company, evidence that separates real traction from story, ownership and terms that make a win matter, and an honest falsifier and reserve plan. Numerical examples use round figures so learners reason about the mechanics — ownership, fund-returner math, CAC payback, dilution across rounds — rather than arithmetic. Common traps are flagged in every chapter, because in venture the expensive mistakes are subtle and asymmetric: the loss you correctly avoided that cost you nothing, set against the outlier you passed on that would have returned the fund; the metric that grew because it was subsidized; the term that looked founder-friendly and quietly capped the investor's upside.

## Venture Rhythm and How to Use This Course
A venture decision is built in layers, and skipping a layer is how funds pay seed prices for companies with no path to a fund-returning outcome. The working rhythm: understand the fund's math and what an outcome has to look like to matter, then form the thesis and source into it, then diligence the numbers and then the things the numbers cannot show, then price the deal and write the memo, then live with the position through follow-ons and an exit. Treat each chapter's "Applied skills" as a deliverable you could hand an investment committee or a partner, not a reading. A realistic cadence is one company per chapter for practice and one full deal for the capstone. Keep a running "conviction tracker" across the whole course — the thesis, the evidence, the falsifiers, the ownership and reserve plan — because in venture the discipline that separates investors from tourists is written down before the check clears, not narrated after the markup.

| Layer | What you produce | Decision it supports |
|---|---|---|
| Fund math and power law | Ownership target, fund-returner math, reserve frame | Could this deal ever matter to the fund? |
| Thesis and sourcing | Market map, "why now," sourcing rationale | Are we even hunting in the right place? |
| Diligence — the numbers | Traction and unit-economics read, evidence-vs-claim list | Is the traction real or subsidized? |
| Diligence — the rest | Founder, product-pull, and customer-reference read | Is the team and the pull good enough to win? |
| Terms and memo | Valuation, ownership, term-sheet read, IC memo | Invest, pass, or invest only on these terms? |
| Portfolio and exit | Board support, reserve/follow-on call, exit view | Double down, hold, or write it off? |

## Chapter 1: The Venture Model, Fund Math, and the Power Law
**Core questions**
- How does this fund actually make money — what does a single deal have to return for it to matter to the whole fund?
- What ownership do you need to enter with, and hold after dilution, for a good outcome to move the fund's number?
- How much of the fund is reserved for follow-on, and what does that leave for new bets?

**Key concepts**: the power law and the fund-returner concept (one or two deals carrying the entire return); fund size, the number of checks, and portfolio construction math; ownership targets and why ownership, not just being "in," determines whether a win matters; dilution across subsequent rounds and the gap between entry ownership and exit ownership; reserves and the split between initial checks and follow-on; the GP/LP structure, capital calls, the management fee and the 10-year fund life; carry, the hurdle, and how the GP is actually paid; DPI (cash returned), TVPI (total value), MOIC, and IRR, and why DPI is the number that finally judges a fund; the gross-to-net gap created by fees and carry.

**Applied skills**
- Write a fund-math note: given a fund size, ownership target, and a plausible exit value, compute what MOIC the deal returns and whether it is a fund-returner.
- Reverse the question — given a fund size, work out the exit value a deal must reach, at your entry-and-diluted ownership, to return the fund.

**Common traps**
- Diligencing a deal to avoid losing money when the fund's return depends entirely on not missing the outlier — a process that screens out failures and outliers together is worse than useless.
- Forgetting dilution: an attractive entry ownership that bleeds to a few points by exit may make even a large outcome irrelevant to the fund.
- Confusing TVPI markups with DPI — paper value from later-round prices is not cash returned, and a fund that never realizes distributions has not made anyone money.

## Chapter 2: Thesis, Market Mapping, and Sourcing
**Core questions**
- Where do you believe value will accrue over the next several years, and why is now the moment rather than two years ago or two years from now?
- In a given category, who are the players, where is the wedge, and what does a winner have to look like?
- Of all the companies you could meet, what makes this specific lead worth real conviction rather than a polite first meeting?

**Key concepts**: the investment thesis (a forward view of where value will be created and captured); "why now" and the enabling shift (technology, regulation, behavior, cost curve) that makes a market suddenly investable; market mapping — segmenting a category into players, incumbents, and white space; the wedge and the path from a narrow beachhead to a large market; TAM honestly sized (bottom-up from real buyers, not top-down from a giant number times one percent); proprietary sourcing, founder networks, and warm versus cold inbound; deal flow as a funnel (sourced → met → diligenced → termsheet → closed) and the conversion math; the difference between a thesis and a theme.

**Applied skills**
- Build a market map for a category: the segments, the notable startups and incumbents, the white space, and the open questions a thesis would need to resolve.
- Write a sourcing rationale for one lead: the thesis it fits, the "why now," and the specific differentiated reason it merits conviction over the dozen adjacent companies.

**Common traps**
- A "thesis" that is just a hot theme everyone already names — agreeing that AI or fintech is big is not a differentiated view and gives no edge in a competitive round.
- Top-down TAM theater: a trillion-dollar market times one percent proves nothing about whether this company can reach real buyers with urgent pain.
- Mistaking deal flow for judgment — seeing every company in a space is worthless if you cannot say which one is worth conviction and why.

## Chapter 3: Traction, Metrics, and Unit-Economics Diligence
**Core questions**
- Which of these metrics is real evidence of pull, and which is a number that grew because it was subsidized or selectively framed?
- Do the unit economics work — does each customer eventually pay back what it cost to acquire and serve them?
- What would a customer or the raw data confirm, and where could the reported figure be misleading?

**Key concepts**: ARR/MRR and the difference between recurring and one-time or pilot revenue; growth rate and the quality behind it; gross retention and net revenue retention (and why NRR above 100% signals genuine pull); logo versus dollar churn; cohort analysis and whether later cohorts behave better or worse than early ones; CAC, LTV, and the CAC-payback period; gross margin and whether the "software" business is actually a services business; burn rate, runway, and the burn multiple (capital consumed per dollar of new ARR); the gap between vanity metrics (registered users, GMV, pipeline) and metrics that survive scrutiny (paying retention, payback, margin).

**Applied skills**
- Build a diligence checklist for a company's metrics that separates each reported claim from the specific evidence (cohort data, a customer call, the raw export) that would confirm it.
- Take a metrics deck and re-derive the numbers that matter — net retention, CAC payback, burn multiple — and flag which headline figures are being flattered.

**Common traps**
- Accepting top-line growth that is bought: heavy discounting, paid pilots counted as ARR, or a single anchor customer inflating the whole number.
- Reading high gross retention as health while net retention is falling because nobody expands — flat-to-shrinking accounts behind a low-churn headline.
- Treating GMV, signups, or "pipeline" as traction when none of them is recurring paying revenue that survived a renewal.

## Chapter 4: Founder, Product Pull, and Customer Evidence
**Core questions**
- Is this founder compounding their understanding of the market faster than anyone else competing for it — are they learning, not just selling?
- Are customers urgent and pulling the product into their business, or merely polite about a nice-to-have?
- What would make adoption stall — the implementation friction, the switching cost, the roadmap dependency that has to come true?

**Key concepts**: founder-market fit and why "earned insight" beats credentials; the difference between a founder who is learning fast and one who has a fixed pitch; product pull versus push (inbound demand and organic expansion versus heroic sales); reference calls and how to run them so customers tell you the truth rather than the polite version; implementation friction, time-to-value, and switching costs; "must-have" versus "nice-to-have" and how to detect urgency; roadmap risk and the features the whole thesis silently depends on; the team beyond the founder and the early hiring signal; channel checks and the mosaic of evidence at a stage where hard data is thin.

**Applied skills**
- Write a customer-call plan and run (or script) reference calls that surface urgency, switching cost, and renewal intent rather than fishing for compliments.
- Write a founder assessment: the earned insight, evidence the founder is compounding their understanding, and the single biggest gap in the team or the founder's blind spots.

**Common traps**
- Mistaking a polished founder for a learning one — the best pitch is not the best signal, and a fixed, unchanging story under questioning is a warning, not a strength.
- Reading politeness as pull: a happy reference who would not be upset if the product vanished is telling you it is a nice-to-have.
- Pattern-matching to a previous winner ("looks like the next X") instead of doing the work to see whether this specific market and team actually hold up.

## Chapter 5: Valuation, Terms, and the Investment Memo
**Core questions**
- Are the price and ownership consistent with the fund math — can this entry ever produce a fund-returner at a believable exit?
- Which terms actually protect or impair the investor's economics and control, beyond the headline valuation?
- What does the partnership have to believe for this to work, what is the single core risk, and what would prove the thesis wrong?

**Key concepts**: pre-money and post-money valuation and how the option pool shuffles dilution onto founders or investors; the SAFE and the convertible note (cap, discount, and how they convert) versus a priced round; ownership math and how the round size and price set your stake; the key economic and control terms — liquidation preference (1x non-participating as the clean norm, participating as the red flag), pro rata rights, board seats and information rights, protective provisions, anti-dilution; the IC (investment committee) memo as the document that forces and records the decision; thesis, evidence, risks, terms, ownership, reserve plan, and a clear recommendation as its spine; the explicit falsifier — what fact, if learned, would have changed the decision.

**Applied skills**
- Read a term sheet and flag the economics and control terms that matter, translating each into its effect on the investor's payoff and the founder's incentives.
- Write a one-page investment memo: thesis, the strongest evidence, the core risk and falsifier, the proposed terms and ownership, the reserve plan, and a plain recommendation.

**Common traps**
- Fixating on the valuation headline while ignoring a participating liquidation preference or a stacked preference that quietly reorders who gets paid in a modest exit.
- Letting a low price justify a weak company — a cheap entry into a business with no path to an outlier is still a write-off, just a smaller one.
- Writing a memo with no falsifier, so every later development gets reinterpreted as confirmation and the position can never be honestly reassessed.

## Chapter 6: Portfolio Support, Reserves, and Exits
**Core questions**
- What help actually moves a portfolio company's outcome, and what is just board theater that consumes the founder's time?
- Which companies deserve the next follow-on dollar, and is this a markup worth doubling into or a bridge to nowhere?
- How does this position actually return cash — what is the realistic exit path, and what dilutes or blocks it along the way?

**Key concepts**: board roles, governance, and the difference between useful support (hiring, customer intros, the next round) and noise; reserves and the follow-on decision as a fresh investment, not loyalty to a past one; the up round, the flat or down round, the bridge, and the inside round as signals; markups and why a paper TVPI gain is not yet a return; signaling risk when an existing investor does or does not follow on; pro rata and maintaining ownership through dilution; the exit landscape — acquisition, secondary sale, and IPO — and the realistic distribution of outcomes; the long fund clock and why DPI, eventually, is the only score that counts.

**Applied skills**
- Write a portfolio update for one company: the key metrics, the founder's asks, the live risks, and a defended follow-on view (lead, follow pro rata, or pass).
- Make a reserve-allocation call across a small mock portfolio — decide where the next follow-on dollars go and justify it on outcome potential, not on which company is currently most exciting.

**Common traps**
- Following on out of loyalty or to defend a markup, rather than judging the follow-on as a fresh investment on its own merits.
- Reading every up round as success — a markup driven by a frothy market, not by the business, can reverse and is not realized cash.
- Treating the exit as automatic: most companies that raise venture never reach a large exit, and ignoring the realistic outcome distribution overstates every position.

## Capstone: The Full Venture Investment Pack
Take one company — fictional or real, at pre-seed or seed — and produce the complete pack an investor would carry into an investment committee:
- a thesis-and-market-map note: where value accrues, "why now," the category map, and where this company sits;
- a sourcing rationale: the differentiated reason this lead merits conviction over its neighbors;
- a fund-math note: entry and diluted ownership, the exit value required to return the fund, and whether this can plausibly be a fund-returner;
- a diligence pack: a metrics read separating real traction from subsidized growth, with an explicit evidence-vs-claim list;
- a founder-and-customer read: the earned insight, the product-pull evidence from reference calls, and the biggest gap;
- a terms read: the valuation, ownership, and the key economic and control terms, flagged for their effect on the payoff;
- a one-page investment memo with thesis, evidence, the core risk and falsifier, terms, a reserve plan, and a clear recommendation;
- and a one-line verdict — invest, pass, or "invest only on these terms" — with the single strongest argument for and against.

## Research Notes
- The power law, fund-returner logic, and portfolio-construction math (fund size, ownership targets, reserves) reflect standard early-stage venture practice as described in widely used references such as the work on venture portfolio math by practitioners and the CFA Institute's alternative-investments material; treat specific portfolio-construction ratios as illustrative, since they vary by fund stage and strategy.
- Fund economics (GP/LP structure, management fee, carry, hurdle, the ~10-year fund life) and the performance metrics (DPI, TVPI, MOIC, IRR, the gross-to-net gap) follow conventional limited-partnership fund terms; exact fee and carry levels vary by fund and should be confirmed against current sources rather than taught as fixed.
- Startup metrics and unit economics (ARR/MRR, net revenue retention, CAC payback, cohort analysis, burn multiple, gross margin) align with standard SaaS and growth-investing references (e.g., a16z and Bessemer metric frameworks); specific benchmark thresholds drift with market conditions and are best taught as directional, not absolute.
- Deal-structure content (pre/post-money, SAFE and convertible-note mechanics, option-pool shuffle, liquidation preference, pro rata, board and protective provisions, anti-dilution) reflects conventional U.S. early-stage financing practice (e.g., Y Combinator SAFE documents and standard venture term sheets); specific legal terms and standard documents are periodically updated and should be verified against current versions before teaching as settled detail.
- Sourcing, founder-market-fit, product-pull, reference-call, and portfolio-support framing follows conventional early-stage investor practice; these are judgment heuristics rather than settled fact and are framed as such throughout.
