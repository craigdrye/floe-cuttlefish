# Clinical Research Roadmap
**ID**: `clinicalResearchRoadmap` - **Discipline**: Healthcare - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This is the on-ramp for someone walking into a clinical trial site for the first time — a new coordinator, a research nurse picking up study duties, a regulatory associate, or a clinician curious about how a protocol becomes patient visits. A trial site is a place where medicine and paperwork are the same act: the dose you give and the line you write about giving it are equally part of the study, and if the second one is wrong the first one effectively didn't happen. This course teaches the shape of that work — who does what, in what order, with what written down — so that a beginner can step onto a site and understand the machine they've joined rather than just following a checklist they don't yet trust.

The spine of the course is the daily loop of trial conduct: a participant is screened and consented, made eligible, seen on a schedule, measured against a protocol, and watched for harm — and every one of those steps produces a record that a monitor or an FDA inspector can later use to reconstruct exactly what happened. We build that loop on the framework that actually governs it: Good Clinical Practice as set out in ICH E6, now in its R3 revision (adopted by FDA in September 2025 and the EU in July 2025), plus the US scaffolding of 21 CFR Parts 11, 50, 56, and 312. You will learn the roles (sponsor, CRO, investigator, coordinator, monitor, IRB), the documents (protocol, source, CRF, the regulatory binder and TMF), the safety vocabulary (AE, SAE, the reporting clock), and the two questions that sit under every decision a site makes: **is the participant safe, and is the record defensible?**

By the end you should be able to prepare a visit, confirm consent and eligibility, document to a standard that survives verification, recognize a reportable safety event and start the clock, classify a protocol deviation honestly, and keep a regulatory file an inspector could open cold. This is the practical foundation. For the next layer — decoding sponsor and monitor shorthand, the precise reporting timelines, and the modern risk-based-monitoring vocabulary an experienced CRC is expected to know cold — graduate to the **Clinical Research Jargon Buster** track.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests entry-level clinical-research operations: the roles and handoffs on a trial site; the informed-consent process; screening, eligibility, and visit-window logic; source documentation and source-to-CRF data flow; the basics of adverse-event and serious-adverse-event recognition and escalation; protocol deviations and corrective action; investigational-product accountability; and what makes a site monitor- and inspection-ready. This is the foundational roadmap; for jargon decoding, exact safety-reporting clocks, RBQM/QbD, and decentralized-trial detail at certification depth, prefer the Clinical Research Jargon Buster.

The voice is a calm senior coordinator orienting a sharp new hire: concrete, unhurried, every acronym tied to a real site task the first time it appears. Keep the bar at *understanding the system and doing the next defensible thing*, not memorizing trivia — but where a fact appears it must be current and correct (ICH E6(R3), 21 CFR, FDA guidance), because a beginner who learns a wrong number carries it for years. Distractors should be the genuine beginner mistakes (re-consent everyone after any change, fix a value to clear a query, sit on a "maybe-SAE" overnight, confuse the binder being thick with the file being complete), each encoding one specific failure mode. When a scenario is ambiguous, the right answer is the one that keeps the participant safe and the record reconstructable.

### Where to start
There is no single exam behind this roadmap; it is the practical floor under several credentials (ACRP CCRC/CCRA, SOCRA CCRP) and, more importantly, under day-one competence. Work the chapters in order, because the trial loop is sequential — you cannot reason about data quality before you understand what counts as source, or about deviations before you know what the protocol's windows are. After each chapter, do its applied skill on a real or invented case rather than re-reading; the work of a site is production (writing the note, building the checklist), not recognition. Drill the Common traps as their own set, because nearly every finding a monitor writes maps to one of them.

## Chapter 1: The Trial Ecosystem and Who Owns What
- **Core questions**: Who are the players on a trial, and where does each one's responsibility begin and end? Who can perform a given study task, and how is that authorization recorded? When something is unclear, who do I escalate to?
- **Key concepts**: sponsor, CRO, principal investigator (PI), sub-investigator, clinical research coordinator (CRC), monitor/CRA, IRB/IEC; Good Clinical Practice and ICH E6(R3) as the standard the whole system runs on; the protocol as the contract; the delegation-of-authority log; PI oversight as a duty that cannot be delegated away.
- **Applied skills**: draw a role map showing handoffs and escalation paths from coordinator to PI to sponsor; read a delegation log and confirm every person performing a study task is actually authorized for it.
- **Common traps**: assuming a CRO contract moves the sponsor's accountability to the CRO (it does not); treating GCP as optional best practice rather than the expected standard; letting someone perform a study task they were never delegated to do; confusing the PI's duties with the coordinator's.

## Chapter 2: Informed Consent as a Process
- **Core questions**: Is consent valid, current, and documented before anything study-related happens? What version did the participant sign, and is it the one the IRB currently approves? When does someone need to be re-consented, and who exactly?
- **Key concepts**: informed consent as an ongoing conversation, not a signature event; the IRB-approved current version of the consent form; documenting the consent discussion contemporaneously; re-consent triggers (new risk information, an amendment that changes risk or benefit); assent and legally authorized representatives; the firm rule that no study procedure happens before consent is complete.
- **Applied skills**: verify that the signed consent matches the currently approved version and predates every study procedure; scope a re-consent to only the affected, enrolled participants; write a consent note that captures what was discussed, by whom, and when.
- **Common traps**: starting a screening procedure before consent is signed and calling it "just intake"; treating the signature as the whole of consent; re-consenting the entire site (or no one) after an amendment instead of the affected participants; using an expired or superseded consent version.

## Chapter 3: Screening, Eligibility, and the Visit Schedule
- **Core questions**: Does this participant actually meet every inclusion criterion and fail every exclusion? Is the screening or visit happening inside its window? What is due at this visit, and what gets done first?
- **Key concepts**: inclusion vs exclusion criteria and why both must be satisfied; the screening window; the schedule of assessments as the visit's to-do list; visit windows and what counts as in vs out of window; protocol-required order of procedures; the difference between a screen failure and an enrolled participant.
- **Applied skills**: confirm eligibility against the protocol criterion by criterion with documented source for each; build a visit-prep checklist from the schedule of assessments; flag a visit drifting toward its window edge before it breaches.
- **Common traps**: enrolling someone who misses a criterion and treating it as a rounding error; performing a visit outside its window and not documenting it as a deviation; working from memory instead of the schedule of assessments; missing a required assessment because nothing prompted it.

## Chapter 4: Source Documentation and the CRF
- **Core questions**: Can this data be verified back to an original source? What changed, who changed it, and when? Does the record satisfy ALCOA+?
- **Key concepts**: source documents vs the case report form (CRF/eCRF); eSource; the audit trail; ALCOA+ (Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate — plus Complete, Consistent, Enduring, Available); the proper way to correct an entry (single line-through, initial, date, reason — never overwrite); data queries and how to resolve one from real source; why a late or back-dated entry is a red flag.
- **Applied skills**: reconcile source against the CRF and explain any gap; resolve a monitor's query by going back to the source rather than inventing or editing data; make a corrective entry that preserves the original.
- **Common traps**: treating "the CRF is the source" (transcribing with nothing behind it); changing a value to make a query disappear; overwriting rather than correcting an entry; writing notes a day late and not marking them as late entries.

## Chapter 5: Recognizing and Reporting Safety Events
- **Core questions**: Is this an adverse event (AE) or a serious adverse event (SAE)? Who must be told, and how fast? What facts do I capture now versus what's still pending?
- **Key concepts**: AE vs SAE and the seriousness criteria (death, life-threatening, hospitalization or its prolongation, persistent disability, congenital anomaly, important medical event); preliminary causality and expectedness; the reporting clock (the protocol-defined 24-hour site-to-sponsor SAE notification, and the sponsor's downstream expedited timelines of 7 and 15 calendar days for qualifying reports); the formal safety workflow as the only legitimate channel; the Investigator's Brochure as the expectedness reference.
- **Applied skills**: classify an event as AE or SAE against the criteria; start the reporting clock and escalate through the formal workflow rather than a hallway mention; record known vs pending facts so the report is honest and updatable.
- **Common traps**: defaulting a possible SAE to "non-serious" to avoid the paperwork; reporting a safety event over email or chat instead of the safety workflow; letting a "maybe-SAE" sit overnight while the clock runs; deciding causality privately and never reporting it.

## Chapter 6: Protocol Deviations and Corrective Action
- **Core questions**: Did the conduct depart from the protocol? Is this deviation important — does it touch participant safety/rights or data reliability? What caused it, and what stops it from happening again?
- **Key concepts**: protocol deviation vs important (major) deviation; the test for "important" (affects subject rights, safety, or well-being, or the completeness/accuracy/reliability of the data); reporting obligations for deviations; root-cause analysis; corrective action (fixes this instance) vs preventive action (stops the next one); deviation trends as a quality signal.
- **Applied skills**: classify a missed-window visit or eligibility breach honestly rather than minimizing it; write a deviation note with the impact, the reporting path, and a CAPA that separates the fix from the prevention.
- **Common traps**: euphemizing a deviation as a "scheduling accommodation" or "operational texture"; logging a deviation with no owner and no recurrence control; confusing a corrective action with a preventive one; hiding a deviation rather than reporting it.

## Chapter 7: Investigational Product Accountability
- **Core questions**: Does the IP trail balance? Can dosing, storage, and disposition be reconstructed per participant? What happens when a storage condition goes out of range?
- **Key concepts**: the investigational product (IP) accountability log; receipt, dispensing, return, and destruction; the reconciliation identity (received = dispensed + returned + destroyed + on hand); temperature and storage excursions, quarantine, and the assessment decision; blinding and emergency unblinding; the Investigator's Brochure as the dosing/safety reference.
- **Applied skills**: reconcile an IP log so the totals balance and the per-participant dosing is reconstructable; document a temperature excursion and the quarantine/use decision that follows.
- **Common traps**: reconciling only the grand totals while ignoring per-subject dosing; treating a storage excursion as cosmetic and dosing anyway; unblinding for convenience rather than medical necessity; an accountability log with gaps no one can explain.

## Chapter 8: Monitoring, the Regulatory File, and Inspection Readiness
- **Core questions**: What will the monitor or inspector ask for, and is it there? What is missing or unsigned? Could a stranger reconstruct this entire trial from the file alone?
- **Key concepts**: monitoring visit types (site initiation, interim/routine, close-out); source data verification and the shift toward risk-based, centralized monitoring; essential documents; the Trial Master File (TMF/eTMF) and the Investigator Site File (ISF); the delegation/signature log; PI engagement as something an inspector tests; the monitor follow-up letter as a list of polite findings; "inspection-ready" meaning evidence, not vibe.
- **Applied skills**: turn a vague monitor follow-up letter into owned, dated action items; verify the regulatory file is complete and current (consents, delegation log, IRB approvals, IP records); test whether the live site records actually match the binder.
- **Common traps**: mistaking a thick binder for a complete file; reading "an opportunity to strengthen contemporaneous documentation" as praise rather than a finding; closing a monitor visit with action items that have no owner or deadline; assuming source data verification is the only kind of monitoring.

## Capstone
Run one simulated study week at your site, start to finish, and produce the documentation it generates. Prepare for an upcoming participant visit using the schedule of assessments; before any procedure, confirm consent is the current approved version and complete; verify eligibility against the protocol criterion by criterion. During the visit a participant reports a symptom that meets a seriousness criterion — classify it, start the reporting clock, and route it through the safety workflow with known and pending facts separated. You also discover Visit 3 happened a day outside its window — write the deviation note with impact, reporting path, and a CAPA. Reconcile the IP log for the participant. Finally, a monitor's follow-up letter lands with soft language about "limited contemporaneous documentation" and "confirming the PI's continued engagement" — translate it into a prioritized, owned, dated action list and update the regulatory file accordingly. The deliverable is a single ordered worklog — safety first, then consent/data, then deviation, then IP, then oversight. The grade is simple: would this week's records keep the participant safe and survive an FDA inspection?
