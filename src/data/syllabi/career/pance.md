# PANCE: The Clinical Vignette Engine
**ID**: `pance` · **Discipline**: Medicine

## Course Aim
The PANCE is a five-hour, 300-question argument that the candidate is ready to practice medicine as a generalist physician assistant. Each item is a small clinical story: a patient walks in, the history hints, the exam confirms or contradicts, the labs and imaging refine, and a single best next step is requested. The exam does not reward exotic recall. It rewards a clinician who can hold the whole body in mind, walk a vignette from chief complaint to disposition in under a minute, and pick the textbook first move from a list of plausible alternatives. This syllabus drills the underlying engine — pattern recognition across organ systems crossed with NCCPA task areas — until the vignettes read fast and the next step picks itself.

The voice here is reassuring on purpose. You finished rotations. You have seen most of what the exam asks. What you need now is consolidation: a confident map of the high-yield diagnoses by system, a confident map of the first-line drugs and the contraindications that flip them to the second-line, and the discipline to keep moving when a vignette is unfamiliar. By the time you sit the exam, your inner monologue should sound less like "I don't know" and more like "the next best step is…"

PANCE differs from USMLE Step 2 CK in three ways worth naming. First, the **breadth is wider but the depth is shallower**: you will see more systems, more screening guidelines, more vaccinations and preventive medicine, with less weight on rare zebras. Second, **pharmacology is heavier**: nearly one in five questions touches pharmaceutical therapeutics, and the test rewards knowing first-line drugs cold, including the ones that get swapped for comorbidities. Third, **professional practice is its own task area**: ethics, informed consent, scope of practice, mandatory reporting, and basic statistics show up as their own items, not just as flavoring.

A word on the test itself: 300 multiple-choice items, five hours, five blocks of 60 items with optional breaks between blocks. You cannot return to a block once submitted, but within a block you can flag, return, and change answers. NCCPA scales scores; the passing score is roughly 350 (with the scale running 200-800), but the cutoff has drifted up over the years. Plan for ~1 minute per item with a small buffer; you will have it.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test NCCPA blueprint content: organ-system pathology across the fifteen system slices, the eight NCCPA task areas (History and PE, Diagnostic Studies, Diagnosis, Health Maintenance/Patient Education/Preventive, Clinical Intervention, Pharmaceutical Therapeutics, Applying Basic Science, Professional Practice), generalist screening guidelines, and vignette-pattern recognition for the "most likely diagnosis" and "next best step" prompts. The voice should make a sprawling generalist exam feel coverable without flattening medicine into trivia.

This course differs from the USMLE banks in scope (broader, less deep), from the NCLEX banks in role (provider decisions, not nursing scope/safety judgments), and from specialty board reviews in posture (generalist first, subspecialty only where the generalist must triage). When in doubt, route to PANCE if the item asks for diagnosis, first-line therapy, or next best step in management of a common adult condition; route elsewhere if the item asks about nursing scope, hospital operations, or rare/zebra pathophysiology that only the specialist would manage.

**Study cadence.** Three to six months of focused preparation starting near the end of rotations. Sixty to eighty vignette questions daily with annotated rationales; one weekly mixed mock of 120 items. ## Chapter 1: Cardiovascular Medicine
**Core questions:** Is this chest pain ACS, PE, aortic dissection, pericarditis, or musculoskeletal? Which murmur is this, and what is the next imaging step? Which heart-failure exacerbation gets which diuretic and which afterload reducer?

**Key concepts**
- ACS spectrum: STEMI (ST elevation ≥1 mm in 2 contiguous leads; door-to-balloon 90 min, door-to-needle 30 min if no PCI capability), NSTEMI (positive troponin, no ST elevation), unstable angina (negative troponin, dynamic symptoms); troponin rises 3-6 h, peaks 12-24 h, normalizes 7-14 d
- ECG localization: anterior V1-V4 (LAD), inferior II/III/aVF (RCA — watch for bradycardia and RV involvement; nitrates may cause hypotension), lateral I/aVL/V5-V6 (LCX), posterior tall R/ST depression in V1-V3 (posterior leads V7-V9 confirm)
- Heart failure: HFrEF (EF ≤40%) vs HFpEF (EF ≥50%); GDMT for HFrEF — ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan, preferred over ACEi when tolerated) + beta-blocker (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol) + diuretic (loop for volume) + SGLT2 inhibitor (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) + MRA (spironolactone if K+ and Cr allow); hydralazine + isosorbide for self-identified Black patients on top of GDMT, or as ACEi/ARB alternative
- Acute decompensation: LMNOP — Lasix, Morphine (only if dyspnea-driven anxiety, sparingly), Nitrates, Oxygen, Position (upright)
- Dysrhythmias: atrial fibrillation — rate (metoprolol, diltiazem) vs rhythm (cardioversion if hemodynamically unstable or first-time; amiodarone, flecainide); CHA2DS2-VASc ≥2 in men, ≥3 in women → anticoagulation (DOAC preferred; warfarin INR 2-3 if mechanical valve or moderate-to-severe MS); HAS-BLED for bleeding risk
- Valvular disease: aortic stenosis — late-peaking systolic ejection murmur, decreased S2, syncope/angina/HF (SAD); aortic regurgitation — early diastolic decrescendo blowing murmur, water-hammer pulse, Quincke pulse; mitral regurgitation — holosystolic murmur radiating to axilla; mitral stenosis — opening snap and mid-diastolic rumble, usually rheumatic
- Hypertension staging (ACC/AHA 2017): normal <120/80, elevated 120-129/<80, stage 1 130-139/80-89, stage 2 ≥140/90; treat stage 2 with two-drug therapy; first-line classes thiazide, ACEi/ARB, CCB; compelling indications — ACEi/ARB in CKD or DM with proteinuria, beta-blocker in CAD/HFrEF
- HTN urgency vs emergency: emergency is >180/120 + end-organ damage (encephalopathy, AKI, pulmonary edema, MI, stroke, eclampsia) — IV nicardipine, labetalol, or nitroprusside; lower MAP by no more than 25% in the first hour
- Hyperlipidemia: ASCVD risk calculator drives statin intensity; high-intensity for clinical ASCVD, LDL ≥190, DM age 40-75 with high risk; ezetimibe and PCSK9 inhibitors for intolerance or insufficient LDL reduction

**Applied clinical skills**
- Triage chest pain in under two minutes by red flags and pretest probability
- Choose initial antihypertensive by comorbidity (DM, CKD, HF, pregnancy, Black/non-Black)
- Read a 12-lead well enough to localize an MI and call cath lab

**Common traps**
- Inferior STEMI with RV involvement: nitrates can precipitate profound hypotension. Volume resuscitate first; confirm RV involvement with V4R.
- A new murmur in a fever + IVDU patient is endocarditis until proven otherwise — blood cultures × 3, TTE then TEE.
- Sensitivity vs specificity: troponin sensitive but not specific; D-dimer sensitive but not specific; positive likelihood ratio = sensitivity / (1 − specificity).
- A-fib with rapid response in a patient with WPW is dangerous — avoid AV nodal blockers (no adenosine, diltiazem, beta-blockers); use procainamide or cardioversion.

**Authoritative sources**
ACC/AHA guidelines (most recent); ESC guidelines where convergent; PANCE Prep Pearls; Rosh Review; Smarty PANCE; UpToDate.

## Chapter 2: Pulmonary and Critical Care
**Core questions:** Is this an asthma exacerbation, COPD exacerbation, PE, or pneumonia? Which CURB-65 score sends this patient home and which to the ICU? When does pneumothorax need a needle vs a chest tube vs nothing?

**Key concepts**
- Asthma stepwise therapy (GINA): SABA reliever (or ICS-formoterol as both reliever and controller in mod-severe), step up with low-dose ICS, ICS+LABA, ICS+LABA+LAMA, biologics (omalizumab for IgE, mepolizumab for IL-5)
- COPD GOLD stages by post-bronchodilator FEV1; group A/B/C/D by symptoms and exacerbations; LABA + LAMA for moderate-severe; add ICS for blood eosinophils or frequent exacerbations; oxygen for SpO2 <88% or PaO2 <55 — long-term oxygen improves mortality
- Pneumonia coverage: outpatient CAP without comorbidities — amoxicillin or doxycycline or macrolide (resistance-dependent); outpatient with comorbidities — beta-lactam + macrolide/doxy or respiratory fluoroquinolone; inpatient non-ICU — ceftriaxone + azithromycin or respiratory fluoroquinolone; ICU — ceftriaxone + macrolide or fluoroquinolone, add anti-pseudomonal/MRSA coverage if risk factors
- CURB-65: Confusion, Urea >19 mg/dL, RR ≥30, BP <90/60, age ≥65. 0-1 outpatient, 2 admission, ≥3 ICU
- PE: Wells score → low-probability + negative D-dimer rules out; moderate/high → CTA pulmonary angiogram; treatment — anticoagulation (DOAC first-line in most), thrombolytics for massive PE with hemodynamic instability; IVC filter if anticoagulation contraindicated
- TB: latent vs active workup; PPD interpretation by risk class (5 mm immunocompromised/close contacts/CXR changes; 10 mm high-risk groups; 15 mm low-risk); IGRA preferred in BCG-vaccinated; RIPE for active (rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol) × 2 mo intensive, then RI × 4 mo
- ARDS Berlin criteria: acute (≤7 days), bilateral opacities, not explained by HF (PCWP <18), PaO2/FiO2 ≤300 (mild ≤300, moderate ≤200, severe ≤100); low tidal volume ventilation 6 mL/kg PBW, plateau pressure ≤30, PEEP titration, prone positioning if severe
- Pneumothorax: small spontaneous primary — observation and oxygen; large or symptomatic — needle aspiration or chest tube; tension — needle decompression at 2nd intercostal midclavicular line (adults) or 5th intercostal anterior axillary line (newer guidance), do not wait for imaging

**Applied clinical skills**
- Interpret PFTs (obstructive vs restrictive vs mixed) in 30 seconds — FEV1/FVC <0.70 obstructive; reduced TLC restrictive; DLCO low in emphysema and ILD, high in asthma
- Choose between BiPAP (COPD, CHF), CPAP (OSA), and intubation in acute respiratory failure
- Workup hemoptysis: CXR first, then CT and bronchoscopy

**Common traps**
- COPD blue bloater = chronic bronchitis (cough, sputum, cyanosis, edema); pink puffer = emphysema (barrel chest, pursed-lip breathing, hyperinflation).
- ICS is first-line CONTROLLER for asthma, but NOT first-line for COPD — LABA + LAMA is, with ICS added for specific phenotypes.
- A patient with asthma who appears "calm" with a silent chest is decompensating, not improving.
- Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis — never wait for the X-ray if hemodynamically unstable.

**Authoritative sources**
GINA asthma report; GOLD COPD report; IDSA/ATS CAP guidelines; Berlin ARDS definition; PANCE Prep Pearls; Rosh.

## Chapter 3: Gastrointestinal, Hepatobiliary, and Nutrition
**Core questions:** Which acute abdomen quadrant pattern is this, and what imaging first? Is this UC or Crohn's? Which hepatitis serology pattern matters now?

**Key concepts**
- Acute abdomen patterns: appendicitis (RLQ McBurney's, Rovsing, psoas, obturator; CT confirms; appendectomy), cholecystitis (RUQ + Murphy + fever; US first, HIDA if equivocal; lap chole), pancreatitis (epigastric pain radiating to back, lipase >3× ULN — Atlanta criteria: 2/3 of pain + lipase + imaging; Ranson at 0/48 h, BISAP), diverticulitis (LLQ, fever; CT confirms; outpatient antibiotics if uncomplicated, admit if abscess/perforation), SBO/LBO (vomiting, distension, obstipation; CT and decompression; surgery if strangulated), mesenteric ischemia (pain out of proportion to exam; CTA; surgical emergency)
- IBD: ulcerative colitis (continuous from rectum, mucosal only, bloody diarrhea, primary sclerosing cholangitis association) vs Crohn's (skip lesions, transmural, fistulas, perianal disease, can affect mouth to anus, terminal ileum favored); 5-ASA for mild, steroids for flares, immunomodulators and biologics (anti-TNF infliximab/adalimumab, anti-integrin, anti-IL-23) for maintenance
- GERD/PUD: H. pylori test-and-treat (urea breath, stool antigen, or biopsy; triple/quadruple therapy); PPI for symptom control; alarm symptoms (dysphagia, weight loss, bleeding, anemia, age >60 new onset) → endoscopy; Barrett surveillance
- Upper GI bleed: melena, NG aspirate; PPI infusion, endoscopy within 24 h, urea breath/biopsy for H. pylori; variceal bleed needs octreotide + ceftriaxone (SBP prophylaxis) + endoscopic band ligation
- Lower GI bleed: hematochezia; colonoscopy preferred; CTA if active and brisk
- Hepatitis serology: HBsAg = current infection; anti-HBs = immunity (vaccination or recovery); anti-HBc IgM = acute; anti-HBc IgG = past or chronic; HBeAg/HBV-DNA = high replication; combinations matter — "vaccinated only" = anti-HBs alone; "past resolved" = anti-HBs + anti-HBc IgG
- Hepatitis C: screen all adults once; cure with direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) achieving SVR12
- Cirrhosis complications: variceal bleed (above), ascites (sodium restriction + spironolactone + furosemide; SBP prophylaxis if recurrent), spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (PMN >250 in ascitic fluid → ceftriaxone), hepatic encephalopathy (lactulose, rifaximin), hepatorenal syndrome; MELD-Na drives transplant priority
- Pancreatitis (acute): gallstone and alcohol are top causes; ERCP for gallstone pancreatitis with cholangitis; supportive care with aggressive IV hydration (LR preferred); chronic — steatorrhea, calcifications, pancreatic enzyme replacement
- Malabsorption: celiac (anti-tTG IgA, total IgA to rule out deficiency; duodenal biopsy if positive; gluten-free diet), lactose intolerance, small intestinal bacterial overgrowth, tropical sprue
- Vitamin/mineral deficiencies: B12 (macrocytic anemia + neuropathy), folate (macrocytic without neuro), vitamin D (osteomalacia, falls), vitamin K (coagulopathy), thiamine (Wernicke-Korsakoff in alcoholics)

**Applied clinical skills**
- Pick imaging modality by suspected pathology (US for RUQ, CT for LLQ, etc.)
- Decode hepatitis B serology in five patterns
- Recognize colon cancer red flags and order the right screening (colonoscopy 45-75 q10y, FIT annual, CT colonography q5y)

**Common traps**
- Sensitivity vs specificity vs PPV/NPV: high sensitivity rules out (SnNout); high specificity rules in (SpPin); positive LR = sens/(1-spec).
- Pancreatic enzyme replacement requires acid suppression (PPI) for activity in the duodenum.
- A patient with cirrhosis and new-onset confusion needs lactulose; a patient with cirrhosis and fever + abdominal pain + ascites needs a diagnostic paracentesis.
- Mesenteric ischemia: the labs and physical look benign relative to the pain — pain out of proportion is the cue.

**Authoritative sources**
ACG guidelines; AASLD hepatitis; Atlanta criteria for pancreatitis; PANCE Prep Pearls; UpToDate.

## Chapter 4: Endocrine and Renal
**Core questions:** Is this DKA, HHS, or hypoglycemia? Hyper- or hypothyroid, and is this thyroid storm? Which electrolyte changes my next move?

**Key concepts**
- Diabetes: T1 (autoimmune, insulin-dependent, prone to DKA) vs T2 (insulin resistance, can develop DKA but classically HHS) vs LADA; A1c targets <7% for most, <6.5% in select, <8% in elderly/short life expectancy; ADA stepwise — metformin first-line, add SGLT2i if HF/CKD or GLP-1 RA if ASCVD/obesity, then sulfonylurea/DPP4/TZD/basal insulin
- DKA management: IV fluids first (NS bolus, then NS/half-NS based on Na), insulin drip 0.1 U/kg/h AFTER K+ ≥3.3, replace K+ before insulin if K <3.3 (insulin drives K+ into cells and can precipitate arrhythmia), bicarbonate only if pH <6.9, glucose dropdown trigger to add D5 when glucose <250 to allow insulin to clear ketones, transition to SC insulin when anion gap closed and patient eating
- HHS: glucose typically >600, profound dehydration, minimal ketones, AMS more common; fluids are even more central, slower insulin
- Thyroid: hypothyroid — TSH↑, T4↓ → levothyroxine; subclinical (TSH abnormal, T4 normal) — treat if symptoms, TSH >10, or pregnant; hyperthyroid — Graves (TRAb positive, diffuse goiter, ophthalmopathy) — beta-blocker for symptoms, methimazole (first-line outside first trimester) or PTU (first trimester pregnancy, thyroid storm), radioiodine or surgery; thyroid storm — PTU + propranolol + steroids + iodine (1 h after PTU) + cooling
- Adrenal: Addison (primary insufficiency — fatigue, hyperpigmentation, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, low cortisol with high ACTH; cosyntropin stim no response; hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone); Cushing (excess cortisol; dexamethasone suppression test, 24-h urinary free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol); pheochromocytoma (episodic HTN, palpitations, headache, diaphoresis; plasma free metanephrines; surgical resection AFTER alpha-block then beta-block)
- Calcium: hypercalcemia of malignancy or primary hyperparathyroidism; symptoms "stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones"; treatment IV fluids + calcitonin + bisphosphonates; hypocalcemia — Chvostek, Trousseau, prolonged QT; check magnesium (refractory hypocalcemia often hypomagnesemia)
- AKI: prerenal (BUN:Cr >20:1, FENa <1%, urine Na <20), intrinsic (ATN — muddy brown casts; AIN — eosinophils, drug history; glomerular — RBC casts), postrenal (obstruction — bladder scan, US)
- CKD: stages by eGFR; complications anemia (EPO), bone disease (vitamin D, phosphate binders), acidosis, hyperkalemia; dialysis indications AEIOU
- Kidney stones: calcium oxalate most common; uric acid radiolucent; struvite from urease-producing organisms (Proteus); cystine in cystinuria; size <5 mm typically passes, ≥10 mm or proximal usually needs intervention (ureteroscopy, shockwave lithotripsy)

**Applied clinical skills**
- Manage DKA in priority order (fluids → K → insulin → bicarbonate rarely)
- Differentiate primary vs secondary vs tertiary hyperparathyroidism by PTH and calcium
- Recognize SIADH (euvolemic hyponatremia + concentrated urine) vs cerebral salt wasting (hypovolemic hyponatremia + concentrated urine)

**Common traps**
- Never start an insulin drip in DKA with K+ <3.3 — replace K+ first.
- Subclinical hypothyroidism in pregnancy: treat with levothyroxine, even if asymptomatic — fetal neurodevelopment.
- Pheochromocytoma surgery requires alpha-blockade FIRST (phenoxybenzamine), then beta-blockade — unopposed beta can precipitate hypertensive crisis.
- Hyperkalemia in AKI/CKD: calcium gluconate stabilizes, insulin/D50 shifts, kayexalate/loop/dialysis removes. Calcium does NOT lower the level.

**Authoritative sources**
ADA Standards of Care; KDIGO; ATA thyroid; Endocrine Society; PANCE Prep Pearls; Rosh.

## Chapter 5: Neurology and Musculoskeletal
**Core questions:** Stroke or stroke mimic, and is the patient within the tPA window? Migraine, tension, cluster, or red-flag headache? Which fracture goes home with a splint and which goes to the OR today?

**Key concepts**
- Stroke: ischemic vs hemorrhagic — non-contrast CT first to rule out bleed; tPA within 4.5 h of last known well (strict criteria), thrombectomy within 24 h selected large-vessel occlusion; NIHSS scoring; secondary prevention — antiplatelet, statin, BP control, A-fib anticoagulation; TIA — same workup, ABCD2 risk score
- Seizures: focal (motor, sensory, autonomic, with/without awareness) vs generalized (tonic-clonic, absence, myoclonic, atonic); status epilepticus — IV lorazepam or diazepam, then fosphenytoin/valproate/levetiracetam; first-line by type — focal carbamazepine or levetiracetam; generalized valproate (avoid in childbearing-age women — teratogenic), lamotrigine, levetiracetam; absence ethosuximide
- Headaches: red flags SNOOP (Systemic symptoms, Neuro deficit, Onset thunderclap, Older age new, Pattern change); migraine — abortive triptans (CONTRAINDICATED in CAD/uncontrolled HTN/recent vasoactive use), NSAIDs, antiemetics; prophylaxis — topiramate, propranolol, amitriptyline, CGRP antagonists; cluster — high-flow O2 + subcutaneous sumatriptan
- Dementias: Alzheimer (insidious, memory, hippocampal atrophy; cholinesterase inhibitors donepezil/rivastigmine, memantine for moderate-severe), vascular (stepwise after strokes), Lewy body (fluctuating cognition, parkinsonism, visual hallucinations — AVOID typical antipsychotics, severe sensitivity), frontotemporal (behavior and language change, younger)
- Delirium vs dementia: delirium acute, fluctuating, attention deficit, reversible cause (UTI, drugs, dehydration, post-op)
- Neuropathy: diabetic peripheral (glycemic control, gabapentin/pregabalin/duloxetine), Bell's palsy (acute peripheral CN VII; oral steroids within 72 h, antivirals controversial), Guillain-Barré (ascending paralysis after URI/GI illness; LP — albuminocytologic dissociation; IVIG or plasmapheresis; watch respiratory)
- Parkinson's: bradykinesia + rest tremor + rigidity + postural instability; carbidopa-levodopa first-line for symptomatic; dopamine agonists, MAO-B inhibitors
- Multiple sclerosis: relapsing-remitting most common; optic neuritis often first; MRI brain/spine with multiple lesions disseminated in time and space; LP oligoclonal bands; disease-modifying therapy
- MSK: RA (symmetric small joints, morning stiffness >1 h, RF, anti-CCP, erosions; methotrexate first-line) vs OA (asymmetric, weight-bearing, no morning stiffness, Heberden/Bouchard) vs gout (monosodium urate crystals, podagra; NSAIDs/colchicine/steroids acute; allopurinol/febuxostat chronic, NOT during acute attack) vs pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate, chondrocalcinosis) vs septic arthritis (single hot swollen joint, fever; arthrocentesis WBC >50k with PMN predominance; IV antibiotics)
- Common fractures: scaphoid (snuffbox tenderness; thumb spica even with negative X-ray and re-image in 2 weeks; avascular necrosis risk), Boxer's (5th metacarpal neck, ulnar gutter), Colles (distal radius dorsally angulated, fall on outstretched hand), Jones (5th metatarsal proximal diaphysis; nonunion risk; non-weight-bearing), open fractures (IV antibiotics, tetanus, OR for irrigation and debridement)
- Low back pain red flags: cauda equina (saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder, bilateral leg weakness — SAME DAY MRI and surgical decompression), cord compression in cancer, fracture, infection

**Applied clinical skills**
- Localize a stroke from exam findings to vascular territory (MCA, ACA, PCA, basilar, lacunar)
- Choose SSRI, SNRI, mirtazapine, or bupropion by side-effect profile (insomnia → mirtazapine; sexual side effects → bupropion; pain → SNRI)
- Recognize cauda equina and septic arthritis as same-day emergencies

**Common traps**
- Triptans are CONTRAINDICATED in coronary artery disease, uncontrolled HTN, recent ergot/MAOI; use NSAIDs or antiemetic-dopamine antagonists instead.
- Lewy body dementia + typical antipsychotic = malignant sensitivity; use quetiapine cautiously if antipsychotic needed.
- Never start allopurinol DURING an acute gout flare — start after the flare resolves; can continue if already on it.
- Anti-CCP is more specific than RF for RA.

**Authoritative sources**
AAN guidelines; ACR rheumatology; AHA/ASA stroke; ILAE seizures; PANCE Prep Pearls; Rosh.

## Chapter 6: Psychiatry and Behavioral Health
**Core questions:** Major depression vs bipolar II vs persistent depressive disorder? Which antipsychotic side effect is dangerous and which is annoying? When does substance withdrawal need ICU monitoring?

**Key concepts**
- Major depressive disorder: SIG E CAPS, ≥5 for ≥2 weeks; SSRI first-line (sertraline, escitalopram, fluoxetine), 4-6 weeks for full effect; switch class or augment if non-response; ECT for refractory or psychotic features
- Bipolar I (mania ≥1 week or hospitalization-required) vs II (hypomania + major depression, never full mania); mood stabilizers — lithium (level 0.6-1.2, narrow window, kidney/thyroid monitoring), valproate (NOT in pregnancy — neural tube defects), lamotrigine (depression-predominant; titrate slowly for SJS risk), atypical antipsychotics
- Anxiety: GAD — SSRI/SNRI first-line + CBT; panic disorder — SSRI; OCD — SSRI at higher doses; PTSD — SSRI + trauma-focused CBT
- Schizophrenia: positive (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech) and negative (flat affect, avolition, alogia); atypicals first-line (risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole); clozapine for treatment-resistant (agranulocytosis — weekly CBC; myocarditis; seizures)
- Antipsychotic side effects: EPS (acute dystonia within hours — diphenhydramine/benztropine; akathisia — beta-blocker; parkinsonism — anticholinergic or switch; tardive dyskinesia — late, often irreversible, VMAT2 inhibitors valbenazine/deutetrabenazine); NMS (high fever, lead-pipe rigidity, AMS, autonomic instability, elevated CK — stop drug, cool, dantrolene/bromocriptine); metabolic syndrome especially with olanzapine/clozapine; hyperprolactinemia with risperidone/haloperidol
- Serotonin syndrome: cognitive + autonomic + neuromuscular (clonus, hyperreflexia); discontinue, supportive, cyproheptadine
- Substance use: alcohol — CIWA-Ar scoring, benzodiazepines, thiamine BEFORE glucose (Wernicke prevention); long-term naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram; opioids — clonidine, buprenorphine, methadone; benzodiazepines — life-threatening withdrawal, taper slowly
- Eating disorders: anorexia (BMI <17.5 by older criteria; refeeding syndrome — phosphate, K, Mg fall, start slow); bulimia (purging, dental erosion, Russell sign, hypokalemic alkalosis); fluoxetine for bulimia; SSRIs avoided in anorexia until weight restored

**Applied clinical skills**
- Distinguish bipolar from unipolar before prescribing — SSRIs can precipitate mania
- Recognize serotonin syndrome vs NMS by timing and triggers
- Manage acute alcohol withdrawal with structured CIWA-driven benzodiazepines

**Common traps**
- Never start an SSRI in unrecognized bipolar disorder — precipitates mania. Ask about past hypomanic episodes.
- Thiamine BEFORE glucose in alcohol withdrawal — IV glucose first can precipitate Wernicke's.
- Lithium toxicity: dehydration, low-sodium diets, NSAIDs, and thiazides raise levels.
- Acute dystonia is an emergency — IM diphenhydramine or benztropine, not "wait."

**Authoritative sources**
DSM-5-TR; APA guidelines; SAMHSA TIP series; PANCE Prep Pearls.

## Chapter 7: Reproductive, Genitourinary, and Women's Health
**Core questions:** Which contraception is contraindicated in this patient? Which abnormal uterine bleeding pattern needs biopsy now? Which prenatal complication is preeclampsia, and what is the next step?

**Key concepts**
- Contraception: efficacy ranking IUD/implant > injectable > pill/patch/ring > barrier; combined hormonal contraindicated in migraine WITH aura, smoking >35, breast cancer history, uncontrolled HTN, history of VTE; progestin-only options if estrogen contraindicated
- Pregnancy care: prenatal visits q4 wks to 28, q2 wks to 36, weekly thereafter; routine labs (CBC, type/Rh/screen, rubella, syphilis, HIV, Hep B, urine culture, GBS at 35-37 wks); GDM screening 24-28 wks; anti-D immunoglobulin at 28 wks for Rh-negative
- Preeclampsia: BP ≥140/90 + proteinuria after 20 wks OR ≥140/90 with severe features (BP ≥160/110, thrombocytopenia, transaminitis, AKI, pulmonary edema, neurologic symptoms); magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis (4-7 mEq/L therapeutic); delivery is definitive treatment; eclampsia adds seizures
- Postpartum hemorrhage: massage → empty bladder → oxytocin → methylergonovine (Methergine — AVOID in HTN/preeclampsia) → carboprost (Hemabate — AVOID in asthma) → misoprostol → balloon → surgery
- STIs: gonorrhea/chlamydia — empiric ceftriaxone 500 mg IM + doxycycline 100 mg PO BID × 7 d (or single-dose azithromycin if pregnancy/intolerance); syphilis — penicillin G (benzathine IM for primary/secondary/early latent; IV for neurosyphilis); HSV — acyclovir/valacyclovir; trichomonas — metronidazole
- PID: abdominal pain + cervical motion tenderness + adnexal tenderness; ceftriaxone + doxycycline + metronidazole; admit if pregnant, septic, abscess, or failed outpatient
- Abnormal uterine bleeding: PALM-COEIN; postmenopausal bleeding → ENDOMETRIAL BIOPSY (rule out endometrial cancer); pregnancy test in any reproductive-age woman
- Cervical cancer screening: Pap q3y for 21-29; q5y co-test (Pap + HPV) or HPV alone 30-65; HPV vaccine 9-26 (catch-up to 45 case-by-case)
- Breast cancer screening: mammography q2y for 40-74 (USPSTF 2024 lowered start to 40); high-risk start earlier with MRI
- Benign prostatic hyperplasia: LUTS, AUA symptom score; alpha-blockers (tamsulosin) for symptoms, 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride) for prostate ≥40 g; combination for severe
- Prostate cancer: DRE + PSA shared decision-making 55-69; biopsy if elevated; Gleason score; active surveillance for low-risk
- Erectile dysfunction: PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) — CONTRAINDICATED with nitrates
- UTI: uncomplicated cystitis — nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX, fosfomycin; pyelonephritis — fluoroquinolone outpatient or ceftriaxone inpatient; complicated/men/pregnant — culture-guided
- Nephrolithiasis (see Chapter 4)
- PCOS: oligomenorrhea + hyperandrogenism + polycystic ovaries (Rotterdam 2/3); OCP + spironolactone for symptoms; metformin if insulin resistance; clomiphene/letrozole for fertility

**Applied clinical skills**
- Pick contraception by comorbidity and preference in under a minute
- Recognize postmenopausal bleeding as endometrial cancer until biopsy
- Triage preeclampsia by severe features and gestational age

**Common traps**
- Sildenafil + nitrates = profound hypotension, contraindicated.
- Trichomonas requires partner treatment too; metronidazole + alcohol = disulfiram-like reaction.
- Methergine in HTN/preeclampsia is contraindicated — go to carboprost (unless asthma) or misoprostol.
- Any positive pregnancy test in a woman of childbearing age with abdominal pain — rule out ectopic; transvaginal US, serial hCG.

**Authoritative sources**
ACOG practice bulletins; CDC STI Treatment Guidelines; USPSTF; AUA guidelines; PANCE Prep Pearls.

## Chapter 8: Infectious Disease, Hematology, and Dermatology
**Core questions:** Is this sepsis and what is the source? Microcytic, normocytic, or macrocytic anemia, and what next? Is this rash benign, infectious, or dangerous?

**Key concepts**
- Sepsis: SIRS/qSOFA → Sepsis-3 (life-threatening organ dysfunction from infection, SOFA ≥2); Hour-1 bundle — measure lactate, blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics, broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 h, 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥4, vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line) if MAP <65 after fluids
- Empiric antibiotics by source: CAP (above); UTI/pyelo (above); skin/soft tissue — cellulitis cephalexin or clindamycin; if MRSA risk doxycycline/TMP-SMX or vancomycin IV; abscess — I&D ± antibiotics; meningitis — vancomycin + ceftriaxone (+ ampicillin if neonate/elderly/immunocompromised for Listeria) + dexamethasone before/with first antibiotic dose for adult bacterial; pseudomonas coverage — pip-tazo or cefepime
- HIV: screen all 13-64 once; ART for all regardless of CD4 — typical regimen integrase inhibitor + 2 NRTIs (bictegravir/TAF/FTC, dolutegravir + TDF/3TC); PrEP for high-risk (tenofovir/emtricitabine daily); PEP within 72 h; opportunistic infection prophylaxis by CD4 — PCP (TMP-SMX) <200, MAC <50, toxoplasmosis <100
- Anemias by MCV: microcytic (iron deficiency — low ferritin, high TIBC; thalassemia — normal ferritin, target cells, Mediterranean/Asian; anemia of chronic disease — high ferritin, low TIBC, low iron; sideroblastic — ringed sideroblasts); normocytic (acute blood loss, hemolysis with high reticulocytes, chronic disease, renal anemia with low EPO); macrocytic (B12 — low B12, high methylmalonic acid, neuropathy; folate — low folate, normal MMA, no neuro; hypothyroid; alcohol; drugs)
- Coagulation: PT/INR (extrinsic — warfarin, factor VII); aPTT (intrinsic — heparin, hemophilia A/B, vWD); vWD most common inherited bleeding disorder (mucocutaneous, normal PT/aPTT or slightly elevated aPTT, abnormal ristocetin cofactor); hemophilia A (factor VIII) and B (factor IX) — X-linked recessive, hemarthrosis; DIC (consumption — high PT/aPTT/D-dimer, low platelets and fibrinogen)
- HIT: heparin-induced thrombocytopenia type II at days 5-10 with platelet drop ≥50%; PARADOXICAL thrombosis; stop ALL heparin (including flushes), start argatroban or bivalirudin
- Transfusion reactions: acute hemolytic (ABO mismatch — fever, flank pain, hemoglobinuria — STOP, NS, return blood to bank), febrile non-hemolytic (most common, leukocyte-mediated — stop, acetaminophen, restart if mild), allergic/anaphylactic (rash to anaphylaxis — antihistamine to epinephrine), TRALI (acute lung injury within 6 h — supportive), TACO (volume overload — diuretic)
- Leukemia/lymphoma overview: AML (Auer rods, adults), ALL (children, often respond well), CLL (smudge cells, indolent elderly), CML (Philadelphia/BCR-ABL, imatinib); Hodgkin (Reed-Sternberg, bimodal age, contiguous spread) vs non-Hodgkin
- Dermatology: ABCDE for melanoma (Asymmetry, Border irregular, Color variation, Diameter >6 mm, Evolution); basal cell (pearly papule, telangiectasias, slow growth, Mohs for face); squamous cell (scaly, ulcerated, sun-exposed, can metastasize); actinic keratosis pre-malignant
- Common rashes: psoriasis (well-demarcated, scaly, extensor surfaces; topical steroids, vitamin D analogs, biologics for severe); eczema/atopic (flexural, itchy; emollients, topical steroids); contact dermatitis (linear, exposure history); pityriasis rosea (Christmas tree, herald patch, self-limited); lichen planus (purple polygonal pruritic papules, Wickham striae)
- Life-threatening dermatoses: SJS/TEN (drug reaction — sulfa, anticonvulsants, allopurinol; mucosal involvement; <10% SJS, >30% TEN; ICU/burn unit, IVIG); necrotizing fasciitis (pain out of proportion, crepitus, rapid progression; surgical emergency, broad-spectrum + clindamycin for toxin suppression); SSSS (toxin-mediated; supportive)

**Applied clinical skills**
- Start sepsis bundle within the first hour
- Read a CBC and choose the next two labs without overordering
- Recognize melanoma from a description without seeing the photo

**Common traps**
- Antibiotics BEFORE blood cultures = lost diagnostic opportunity. Draw cultures first if it does not delay antibiotic by >45 min.
- HIT is a clotting problem; transfusing platelets is contraindicated unless actively bleeding.
- Cellulitis with rapid expansion + systemic toxicity + pain out of proportion = necrotizing fasciitis; do not wait for imaging.
- Vancomycin trough 15-20 (not 10-15 for serious infections); follow MIC.

**Authoritative sources**
Surviving Sepsis Campaign; IDSA guidelines; CDC HIV guidelines; AAD dermatology; PANCE Prep Pearls.

## Chapter 9: EENT, Health Maintenance, and Professional Practice
**Core questions:** Red eye — which one is the emergency? Which screening is due at this visit? When does the ethics question hide inside a procedure?

**Key concepts**
- Red eye differentiation: bacterial conjunctivitis (purulent discharge, glued shut) vs viral (watery, often URI, very contagious) vs allergic (bilateral itching) vs iritis/uveitis (photophobia, ciliary flush) vs acute angle closure glaucoma (sudden pain, fixed mid-dilated pupil, halos, nausea — EMERGENCY, ophthalmology, topical and systemic IOP-lowering) vs corneal abrasion (foreign body sensation, fluorescein uptake)
- Otitis media: bulging erythematous TM with effusion; first-line amoxicillin (high-dose) if treating; consider observation in mild older children; recurrent — tubes; AOM vs OME (effusion without acute inflammation)
- Otitis externa ("swimmer's ear"): tragal/auricular pain, debris; topical antibiotic drops (ciprofloxacin/dexamethasone); malignant otitis externa in diabetics (Pseudomonas) — systemic
- Pharyngitis: Centor criteria for streptococcal probability (fever, no cough, tonsillar exudates, tender anterior cervical nodes, age 3-14); rapid antigen ± culture; penicillin or amoxicillin (cephalexin or azithromycin/clindamycin if penicillin allergy); rheumatic fever prevention drives treatment
- Peritonsillar abscess: trismus, uvular deviation, "hot potato" voice — drainage + antibiotics
- Epistaxis: anterior (Kiesselbach plexus — direct pressure 10-15 min, then silver nitrate or packing); posterior (admit, balloon pack, ENT)
- Sinusitis: viral most; bacterial if >10 days, biphasic worsening, or severe — amoxicillin-clavulanate first-line
- USPSTF Grade A/B screening highlights (see also Chapter 9 NCLEX): BP, colorectal 45-75, mammography (updated 2024 to start at 40 q2y), cervical (Pap q3y 21-29, q5y co-test 30-65), AAA one-time 65-75 ever-smoker men, osteoporosis women 65+, lung LDCT 50-80 with 20-pack-year, lipid 40-75, depression/anxiety/PHQ-2 in adults and pregnancy, HCV all adults once, HIV 13-64 once
- Immunizations: childhood + adult schedules; Tdap q10y; annual flu; COVID per current; pneumococcal — PCV15 + PPSV23 sequence or PCV20 alone for adults ≥65 (and younger high-risk); shingles ≥50 (RZV); HPV through 26 (case-by-case to 45)
- Evidence basics: sensitivity (true positive rate; SnNout); specificity (true negative rate; SpPin); PPV (probability disease given positive — falls with low prevalence); NPV; likelihood ratios (+LR = sens/(1−spec); −LR = (1−sens)/spec); NNT and NNH; absolute vs relative risk reduction; study design (RCT > cohort > case-control > case series), bias (selection, recall, attrition, confounding), confidence intervals
- Ethics: informed consent (disclosure + capacity + voluntariness); capacity vs competence (capacity is decision-specific and clinically assessed; competence is a legal status); confidentiality and HIPAA exceptions (mandatory reporting — child/elder abuse, certain communicable diseases, gunshot wounds, imminent threat to self/others; subpoena; minors with exceptions for STI, mental health, substance use in many states)
- Advance directives: living will (specific instructions); durable power of attorney for healthcare (surrogate decision-maker); DNR/POLST; surrogate hierarchy varies by state but commonly spouse → adult children → parents → siblings

**Applied clinical skills**
- Differentiate the four red-eye patterns from history alone
- Build an age-appropriate health-maintenance plan for adults 18-75
- Hold a difficult conversation (bad news, code status, AMA discharge) in the right order

**Common traps**
- Acute angle closure glaucoma is an emergency; do NOT dilate, do NOT give anticholinergics; topical pilocarpine + timolol + apraclonidine + acetazolamide + ophthalmology.
- Strep pharyngitis is treated to prevent rheumatic fever, not to shorten illness; rheumatic fever prevention windows are days, glomerulonephritis is not prevented by antibiotics.
- Capacity is decision-specific: a patient with dementia may still have capacity for a low-stakes decision.
- A capacitated adult can refuse life-saving care; respect autonomy.

**Authoritative sources**
USPSTF recommendations; CDC immunization schedule; AAFP; AOA ophthalmology; PANCE Prep Pearls.

## Chapter 10: Capstone — Exam-Day Discipline and Task-Area Triangulation
**Core questions:** How do I budget five hours and 300 questions without crashing? When a vignette is ambiguous, which task area is being tested? Which professional-practice questions hide ethics inside a procedure?

**Key concepts**
- NCCPA task areas and their stem signatures: "most likely diagnosis" (Diagnosis), "next best step" or "best initial test" (Diagnostic Studies or Clinical Intervention), "first-line treatment" or "most appropriate medication" (Pharmaceutical Therapeutics), "when should this patient be screened" (Health Maintenance), "what is the underlying mechanism" (Applying Basic Science), "what would you ask next" (History and PE), "what is the next ethical step" (Professional Practice)
- Pacing: 300 items in 5 hours = 1 minute per item with 0 buffer; budget ~50 seconds per item to leave 10 minutes per block to flag and return; never spend >2 minutes on a single item — flag and move
- Block strategy: read the last sentence first (the actual question), then the chart; if confused, identify the task area first — the question often telegraphs what kind of answer to look for
- When stuck, eliminate first; second-best answers are usually wrong because they treat a complication or are second-line
- Ethics layer: many "professional practice" items test the SEQUENCE of communication — acknowledge first, gather information second, then plan together
- Bayesian humility: a low-pretest probability + positive screening test = still likely false positive; if a vignette gives you a strong pretest signal, weight it

**Applied clinical skills**
- Identify the task area before computing an answer
- Apply Bayesian thinking when a test result conflicts with pretest probability
- Hold a difficult conversation (bad news, code status, AMA discharge) in the right order

**Common traps**
- Changing an answer on review: only do it if you find a new fact in the stem. First-instinct is usually right when you do not have a new reason.
- Two correct answers, pick the one that addresses the priority: in emergent vignettes, stabilization > diagnosis; in ambulatory vignettes, diagnosis > treatment.
- A "best initial test" item rewards low-cost, high-yield first move (often US, X-ray, basic labs); a "most accurate test" item rewards the gold standard.
- Take the breaks. Mid-block fatigue is the silent contributor to most score drops in mock exams.

**Authoritative sources**
NCCPA candidate guide; Rosh Review strategy; UWorld test-taking primers; PANCE Prep Pearls.
