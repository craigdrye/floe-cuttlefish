# Pregnancy: What to Expect, Explained
*A calm, plain-language map of the nine months (and the fourth trimester) — so the mystery becomes understanding.*

**ID**: `pregnancyBasics` · **Discipline**: Health Literacy / Maternal & Newborn Education · **Level**: Career / Adult Enrichment

## Course Aim
Pregnancy comes wrapped in a fog of advice — from relatives, the internet, half-remembered movies, and a stack of pamphlets nobody fully reads. This course clears the fog. It walks a curious adult, expecting or just learning, through what actually happens across the trimesters: how the baby develops month by month, what prenatal checkups are for, how to eat well and what to skip, which symptoms are ordinary and which deserve a phone call, how labor unfolds, what birth and pain-relief options exist, and what the early postpartum and newborn weeks look like. The goal is confident, plain-language understanding, not a medical degree.

This is educational health literacy, not medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment. Pregnancy is wonderfully variable, and every body and baby is different. Nothing here replaces an OB-GYN or midwife, who know your specific situation. Throughout the course we deliberately point back to that relationship: when a question is about a worrying symptom, the right answer is almost always "talk to your provider." We aim to make you a sharper, calmer participant in your own care — someone who knows what questions to ask and when something is worth a call.

The tone is warm and reassuring, never alarmist. Pregnancy can feel medicalized and scary, and a lot of fear comes from not knowing what is normal. So we name the common, harmless stuff plainly (morning sickness, fatigue, Braxton Hicks, lochia, mild jaundice) while being equally clear about genuine warning signs (bleeding, preeclampsia symptoms, decreased fetal movement, postpartum red flags) so you know exactly when to act. Mental health is treated as part of health: the baby blues and postpartum depression are covered honestly and without stigma, with a clear path to help.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want grounded, non-clinical understanding of pregnancy: someone newly expecting, a partner or family member, or just a curious adult who wants the real picture instead of folklore. The course is scenario-driven — built around moments people actually face ("is this swelling normal?", "when do I go to the hospital?", "what does dilated to 6 cm mean?") — and every distractor names a specific misconception so a wrong answer teaches something real. Common myths (eat for two, one glass of wine is fine, heartburn means a hairy baby) are addressed head-on with evidence.

Safety and the limits of the material are non-negotiable, not garnish. At least one item makes explicit that this is education, not medical advice, and that decisions belong with an OB or midwife. Warning-sign content is woven through several chapters rather than buried in one, because recognizing red flags is a core literacy skill. The postpartum chapter includes mental-health recognition and the path to professional help, including the US 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline for crisis situations and a clear "when in doubt, call" default. Content stays compassionate, destigmatizing, and free of graphic detail or prescriptive dosing.

## Chapter 1: Trimesters & Fetal Development
- **Core questions**: How is pregnancy dated? What are the three trimesters? When do organs form and when is movement felt? What is "full term"?
- **Key concepts**: dating from last menstrual period, ~40 weeks, trimesters as roughly equal thirds, embryo vs. fetus, organogenesis (weeks 3-8), quickening, full-term definition.
- **Common traps**: dating from conception, thinking trimesters are uneven, assuming organs form late, expecting movement in the first weeks.

## Chapter 2: Prenatal Care & Checkups
- **Core questions**: Why does prenatal care matter? How often are visits? What happens at one? OB vs. midwife? Screening vs. diagnostic tests?
- **Key concepts**: monitoring blood pressure/weight/growth/labs, visit frequency ramping toward term, OB vs. certified midwife scope, screening estimates risk while diagnostic tests confirm.
- **Common traps**: thinking care is just for dating or photos, believing midwives are untrained, confusing a screen with a diagnosis.

## Chapter 3: Nutrition & What to Avoid
- **Core questions**: Why folic acid? Is "eating for two" real? What about fish, alcohol, caffeine, and risky foods?
- **Key concepts**: folate and neural tube defects, modest extra calories (not double), lower-mercury fish for omega-3s, no safe alcohol level, moderate caffeine limit, listeria/toxoplasma food risks.
- **Common traps**: doubling calories, avoiding all fish, believing a daily drink is safe, assuming caffeine is fully banned, missing unpasteurized-dairy risk.

## Chapter 4: Common Symptoms — What Is Normal
- **Core questions**: What is morning sickness, fatigue, heartburn? What are Braxton Hicks? When does swelling cross from normal to concerning?
- **Key concepts**: first-trimester nausea, hormonal fatigue, the relaxed sphincter behind heartburn, irregular practice contractions, normal edema vs. preeclampsia-pattern swelling.
- **Common traps**: panicking over normal symptoms, mistaking Braxton Hicks for true labor, ignoring sudden severe swelling, believing the hairy-baby heartburn myth.

## Chapter 5: Warning Signs — When to Call a Provider
- **Core questions**: When is bleeding a concern? What suggests preeclampsia? What about decreased fetal movement, preterm fluid leakage, or feeling unsure?
- **Key concepts**: report bleeding beyond light spotting, preeclampsia symptom cluster (headache, vision changes, upper-belly pain, severe swelling), the "know your baby's movement pattern" rule, "when in doubt, call."
- **Common traps**: waiting on bleeding, dismissing decreased movement as sleeping, self-diagnosing online instead of calling, treating urgent signs as routine.

## Chapter 6: Stages of Labor
- **Core questions**: How many stages of labor are there? What is each one? What does dilation mean? When do you head to the hospital?
- **Key concepts**: three stages (dilation, pushing/birth, placenta), effacement and dilation to ~10 cm, the 5-1-1 rule of thumb, wide variation in pace.
- **Common traps**: thinking dilation measures the baby or a countdown clock, confusing the second and third stages, misreading the 5-1-1 rule.

## Chapter 7: Birth Options & Pain Relief
- **Core questions**: What is a C-section? How does an epidural work? What non-medication options exist? What do doulas and birth plans do?
- **Key concepts**: cesarean as surgical birth, epidural numbing the lower body while awake, non-drug coping (breathing, movement, water, support), doula as non-medical support, birth plan as flexible preferences.
- **Common traps**: thinking an epidural means going to sleep, calling a doula a clinician, treating a birth plan as a binding script, framing one birth method as the only "right" one.

## Chapter 8: Postpartum Basics
- **Core questions**: What is the fourth trimester? What is lochia? Baby blues vs. postpartum depression? When to seek help? What are postpartum red flags?
- **Key concepts**: ~12-week recovery period, normal tapering lochia, baby blues (mild, brief) vs. postpartum depression (intense, lasting, treatable), pelvic-floor recovery, hemorrhage/infection/clot warning signs.
- **Common traps**: neglecting parent recovery, mistaking heavy bleeding for normal lochia, stigmatizing postpartum depression as a failing, ignoring red flags.

## Chapter 9: Newborn Care Intro
- **Core questions**: What is safe sleep? How often do newborns feed? Breast vs. formula? What about jaundice and cord care? When to call the pediatrician?
- **Key concepts**: back-to-sleep on a firm, bare surface, frequent on-cue feeding, "fed and healthy" feeding philosophy, common mild jaundice, dry cord care, fever in under-3-month-olds as a red flag.
- **Common traps**: tummy sleeping or soft bedding, expecting adult-style meal schedules, framing formula as dangerous, scrubbing the cord with alcohol, ignoring infant fever.

## Capstone
Walk through a full pregnancy as an informed, calm participant. Place a scenario in the right trimester and explain what is developing. Describe what a routine prenatal visit checks and why, and distinguish a screening result from a diagnosis. Sort a list of everyday symptoms into "usually normal" versus "call the provider," correctly flagging bleeding, preeclampsia signs, and decreased fetal movement. Explain the three stages of labor and what "dilated to 6 cm" and the 5-1-1 rule mean. Compare birth and pain-relief options without declaring a single "best." Finally, map the fourth trimester: name normal recovery (lochia, fatigue, baby blues) versus postpartum red flags, distinguish baby blues from postpartum depression with a clear path to help (including 988 for crises), and list newborn-care basics — safe sleep, feeding, and when to call the pediatrician. End by stating, in your own words, why all of this supports but never replaces your OB or midwife.

## Assessment Ideas
- Symptom-sorting drills graded on correctly separating "usually normal" from "contact a provider," with no missed red flags.
- Vocabulary-in-context tasks (dilation, effacement, lochia, quickening, full term) graded on plain-language accuracy.
- Myth-vs-fact items (eating for two, alcohol, fish, heartburn folklore) graded on evidence-based reasoning.
- Capstone graded on safe triage instinct, correct deferral to providers, and destigmatizing, accurate framing of mental health.
