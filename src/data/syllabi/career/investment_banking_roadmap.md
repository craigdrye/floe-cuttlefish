# Investment Banking Roadmap
**ID**: `investmentBankingRoadmap` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Investment banking is, underneath the spreadsheets, an advisory business: a bank is hired to help a board or a sponsor make and execute a single large decision — sell the company, buy a competitor, raise debt, go public — and to be right and credible enough to be hired again. The analyst sits at the bottom of that engagement, but the work that flows up is the engagement: the model the managing director quotes to the CEO, the page the board actually reads, the diligence answer that decides whether a buyer raises or walks. This course trains the craft of producing that work — accurately, on deadline, in a form a client can act on — and the judgment to know what the deal is really turning on while you build it.

The throughline is the deal process, not valuation theory. Valuation, accounting, and modeling are tools borrowed from adjacent crafts (and from Floe's Financial Modeling, Equity Research, and Credit and LevFin courses); banking's own discipline is sequencing and execution — knowing whether you are on a pitch or a live mandate, who owns which workstream, what a buyer will object to before they say it, why a number on page 12 has to tie to the number on page 3, and how a process actually moves from teaser to signed agreement to a closed wire. An analyst who can value a company but cannot run a clean comment turn, manage a diligence tracker, or anticipate the question the materials must answer is not yet useful. One who can do both becomes the person the deal team trusts with the version that goes out.

By the end, a learner should be able to take a fictional mandate and act like the analyst on it: map the process and its deliverables, build a defensible valuation range and explain what drives it, assemble a transaction page that survives a sponsor's scrutiny, run diligence and quality control so nothing embarrasses the team in front of the client, and frame a recommendation a banker could deliver in a boardroom. The goal is not a thicker pitch book — it is output the senior team can put in front of a client without rechecking every cell.

## Course Design Notes
The course follows the real arc of a deal: origination and process context first, then the analytical core (accounting, valuation, financing) that every workstream relies on, then the client-facing materials those analytics feed, then execution and quality control, and finally the soft mechanics — comments, diligence, and communication — that decide whether an analyst is trusted. Each chapter assumes the prior ones, mirroring how a junior actually builds toward owning a workstream. Route questions here when they test banking analyst workflow, deal-process sequencing, pitch versus live-deal context, M&A and capital-markets execution, transaction materials (pitch books, CIMs, board decks), accretion/dilution and sources and uses, diligence management, model and document quality control, or client-ready communication. Send pure valuation-method drills to Financial Modeling, the investor-opinion craft to Equity Research, the lender's view to Credit and LevFin, and live-deal slang to the IB Jargon Buster — this course owns the process and the deliverable, and treats those tools as means to it.

Keep every answer concrete and output-focused: the test is always "what does the analyst hand up, and is it right." Numerical examples use round figures so learners reason about mechanics — accretion, debt capacity, a football-field range — rather than arithmetic. Common traps are flagged in every chapter, because in banking the costly errors are rarely the hard ones; they are the untied number, the comp that does not belong, the diligence answer sent before it was checked, the page that buries the decision the client has to make.

## Desk Rhythm and How to Use This Course
A deal is built in layers and on other people's deadlines, and the analyst's reputation is made or lost in the gap between "asked at 6pm" and "right by 8pm." The working rhythm: understand what the client is deciding and where the process stands, then make the underlying numbers true, then turn them into the page or the model the senior team needs, then control quality before it leaves your hands. Treat every chapter's "Applied skills" as a deliverable you could hand a vice president, not a reading assignment. A realistic cadence is one mock company per chapter for practice and one full mandate for the capstone. Keep a running tie-out discipline from the first chapter on — every number in a document should trace to a source — because the difference between a trusted analyst and a watched one is whether the team has to recheck your work.

## Chapter 1: The Deal Process and Client Context
**Key concepts**: coverage versus product groups, the pitch-to-mandate-to-close arc, sell-side versus buy-side processes, broad versus targeted auctions, the deliverable chain (teaser, NDA, CIM, management presentation, data room, IOI, LOI, definitive agreement), signing versus closing, workstreams and who owns them, and the fee structure (retainer, success fee) that shapes incentives.

**Core questions**
- What is the client actually trying to decide, and is this a pitch for work or a live mandate already underway?
- Where in the process are we, and which document or milestone comes next?
- Which workstream owns the answer to the question on the table — and who is the right person to ask?

**Applied skills**
- Build a deal-process map for a sell-side mandate: stages, deliverables, owners, deadlines, and dependencies.
- Place a given request (a teaser, a CIM section, a buyer list) in the correct stage and identify what must already be done for it.

**Common traps**
- Treating pitch work — speculative, unpaid, often never executed — with the same certainty as a signed, live deal.
- Confusing signing with closing, and assuming a deal is done when the agreement is executed rather than when conditions clear and the wire moves.
- Doing a task without knowing which workstream or decision it feeds, and so building something technically correct but useless to the team.

## Chapter 2: Reading the Company — Accounting and Financial Statement Links
**Key concepts**: the three statements and how they link, revenue quality, EBITDA and its add-backs, normalized versus reported earnings, working capital and its swings, capex versus depreciation, the net-debt build, nonrecurring items, and the adjustments a buyer will and will not accept.

**Core questions**
- Do the three statements actually connect — does net income flow to cash, does the balance sheet balance?
- Which adjustment or add-back materially changes the earnings a buyer will pay a multiple on?
- What in this company's numbers will a sophisticated buyer push back on in diligence?

**Applied skills**
- Produce a company financial summary: normalized EBITDA with defensible add-backs, a net-debt bridge, and quality-of-earnings flags.
- Reconcile reported to adjusted figures and label which adjustments are clean and which are recurring costs dressed as one-time.

**Common traps**
- Accepting management's adjusted EBITDA at face value when "one-time" charges recur every single year.
- Ignoring working-capital seasonality, so a normalized number is taken from a flattering point in the cycle.
- Confusing enterprise-level performance with equity value by forgetting the net-debt bridge between them.

## Chapter 3: Valuation in a Deal Context
**Key concepts**: trading comps, precedent transactions, DCF, the enterprise-to-equity bridge, control premiums and synergies in precedents, the multiple that fits the business, the football field, and sensitivity to the one or two assumptions that move the range.

**Core questions**
- Which peer set and which precedent deals are genuinely defensible for this company, and which are reaches?
- What drives the valuation range — the multiple, the estimate, or the synergy assumption — and where is it fragile?
- What does the football field tell the client, and which method should carry the most weight here?

**Applied skills**
- Build a valuation football field from comps, precedents, and a DCF, with the assumption set and a one-line rationale for the range.
- Defend a peer set: explain why each comp belongs on growth, margin, and risk, and why a tempting one was excluded.

**Common traps**
- Padding a comp set with same-sector names that have different growth and capital intensity to flatter the range.
- Forgetting that precedent transactions embed a control premium, so they read high versus trading comps for a reason.
- Presenting a wide range with no view on which method matters, leaving the client to do the analyst's job of judgment.

## Chapter 4: M&A Execution — Rationale, Accretion/Dilution, and Synergies
**Core questions**
- Why does this deal make strategic sense, and what is the equity story a buyer's board needs to hear?
- Is the transaction accretive or dilutive to EPS, and what is actually driving that — the financing mix or the operating combination?
- Which synergy and integration assumptions is the whole rationale resting on, and how credible are they?

**Key concepts**: strategic rationale and the equity story, accretion/dilution mechanics, the cash-versus-stock financing mix and its effect on EPS, purchase-price allocation and goodwill, synergies (cost versus revenue) and integration risk, and the contribution analysis behind an exchange ratio.

**Applied skills**
- Build a simple accretion/dilution analysis and explain whether the deal is accretive because of operations or merely because debt is cheaper than the target's earnings yield.
- Draft a transaction-rationale page: strategic logic, the combined equity story, key synergies, and the principal risks.

**Common traps**
- Calling a deal "accretive" as if that proves it is good, when accretion driven by cheap debt can still destroy value.
- Treating revenue synergies — the hardest to realize — with the same confidence as cost synergies in the model.
- Selling the rationale while burying integration and execution risk that the buyer's board will ask about first.

## Chapter 5: Capital Markets — Financing, Sources and Uses, and the Investor Story
**Key concepts**: equity (IPO, follow-on) versus debt financing, sources and uses, debt capacity and leverage limits, the equity story and the IPO narrative, the league-table and underwriting context, the relevant investor audience for each instrument, and the objections each financing must pre-empt.

**Core questions**
- How is this transaction or company funded — what are the sources, the uses, and does the structure balance?
- How much debt can this business actually carry before the structure stops working, and who buys the rest?
- What is the equity story, and what is the single objection the financing materials must answer to get the deal done?

**Applied skills**
- Build a sources-and-uses table for an acquisition or recapitalization and explain how the financing mix was chosen.
- Draft the spine of an equity story or IPO narrative: the growth thesis, the use of proceeds, and the investor question it must defeat.

**Common traps**
- Building a sources-and-uses table that does not balance, or that quietly ignores fees and minimum cash.
- Assuming a capital structure the market will not actually buy because leverage or coverage breaches what investors tolerate.
- Writing an equity story that lists features instead of answering the one objection a skeptical investor will raise.

## Chapter 6: Transaction Materials — Pitch Books, CIMs, and Board Decks
**Key concepts**: the purpose and audience of each document (pitch book to win work, CIM to market an asset, board deck to support a decision, fairness materials), narrative arc, the executive summary that carries the page, exhibit design, the difference between a marketing document and a decision document, and consistency of message across pages.

**Core questions**
- Who reads this document, what decision are they making, and does the first page already give them what they need?
- Is this a marketing piece (sell the story) or a decision piece (support a judgment) — and is the tone right for that?
- Does every page advance the argument, or are there slides that exist only because they were in the last deck?

**Applied skills**
- Build a transaction page that stands alone: rationale, valuation, financing, and key risks, with the takeaway up top.
- Critique a pitch-book section for audience fit, narrative flow, and whether the recommendation is findable in ten seconds.

**Common traps**
- Burying the recommendation or the ask three pages deep so the client cannot find what they are being asked to decide.
- Mixing a marketing tone into a board or fairness document, where overstatement creates real liability.
- Inconsistent numbers across pages — a multiple on page 4 that contradicts the football field on page 11 — which destroys credibility instantly.

## Chapter 7: Diligence, Execution, and Quality Control
**Core questions**
- Does every number in this document tie to a source, and could you defend each one if the client asked?
- Which open comment or diligence item is the current bottleneck blocking the next deliverable?
- What is most likely to embarrass the team in front of the client, and have you checked it before it goes out?

**Key concepts**: turns and markups, version control, the comment log, tie-outs and source-referencing, footnotes, the diligence tracker and data-room management, the QoE and confirmatory diligence, the management presentation, and the closing checklist.

**Applied skills**
- Run a tie-out: trace every figure in a one-pager to its source and produce a clean comment log resolving each markup.
- Maintain a diligence tracker — open items, owners, status, the next bottleneck — that a VP could read in thirty seconds.

**Common traps**
- Sending a deliverable before tying it out, so a senior banker finds the error in front of the client instead of the analyst finding it first.
- Mishandling version control and circulating an old or unmerged draft after comments have changed the analysis.
- Treating a comment as a typo fix when it actually changes the underlying analysis, and patching the page without re-running the model.

## Chapter 8: Communication, Judgment, and Working the Deal Team
**Key concepts**: the up-front clarifying question, managing up and flagging issues early, the recommendation framed for a decision-maker, escalation versus quiet failure, time and deadline management across competing asks, and the analyst's reputation as a function of trust rather than hours.

**Core questions**
- Before building, do you understand the task well enough that the output will be right the first time?
- When something is going to be wrong or late, are you flagging it early or hoping it resolves itself?
- Can you state the recommendation in two sentences a managing director could repeat to a client?

**Applied skills**
- Turn a vague late-night request into a precise task list with one well-aimed clarifying question, then deliver against it.
- Write a client-ready recommendation: the call, the two or three reasons behind it, and the principal risk, in plain language.

**Common traps**
- Guessing at an ambiguous instruction and building the wrong thing rather than asking one sharp question up front.
- Sitting on a problem — a number that will not tie, a deadline that will slip — until it becomes the team's emergency.
- Confusing volume with value: a long, hedged answer that never states the recommendation the senior banker actually needs.

## Capstone: The Analyst Packet for a Live Mandate
Learners take one fictional sell-side mandate and produce the full analyst packet a deal team would carry into a client meeting:
- a deal-process map with stages, deliverables, owners, and the current milestone;
- a company financial summary with normalized EBITDA, a net-debt bridge, and quality-of-earnings flags;
- a valuation football field from comps, precedents, and a DCF, with a defended peer set and a stated view on the range;
- a transaction-rationale page with strategic logic, an accretion/dilution read, synergies, and risks;
- a sources-and-uses table and a one-paragraph financing recommendation;
- a clean, tied-out transaction page suitable for a board deck, with the takeaway up top;
- a diligence tracker and comment log showing the next bottleneck and that the packet ties out;
- and a two-sentence client-ready recommendation a banker could deliver in the room.
