# MBE: Seven Subjects, One Calm Pencil
**ID**: `mbe` · **Discipline**: Law (United States)

## Course Aim
The Multistate Bar Examination is 200 multiple-choice questions across two 3-hour sessions, with 175 scored items distributed equally — 25 each — across Civil Procedure, Constitutional Law, Contracts (including UCC Article 2), Criminal Law and Procedure, Evidence, Real Property, and Torts, plus 25 unscored pretest items the candidate cannot identify. Average pace is approximately 1 minute 48 seconds per question. The MBE is scaled jointly with the rest of the Uniform Bar Examination to a 400-point total (or counted per-jurisdiction); a scaled score near 140 is roughly the passing threshold in most UBE jurisdictions, and a 125 corresponds to the long-term 70th percentile of test-takers.

This course trains the bar-level analyst. The MBE will not ask you what the law should be. It will ask you, given a fact pattern with named parties, a jurisdictional anchor, and four polished answer choices, which choice states the legal outcome — and which choice states it for the right reason. Each answer choice on a real MBE contains both a result and a rationale. The wrong answers fail on the rationale even when they accidentally reach the right result, and the right answer states a rationale grounded in black-letter doctrine that controls.

This is not bar-essay prep. Bar essays reward IRAC structure, multi-issue spotting, and policy. The MBE rewards the opposite: a single dispositive doctrine, applied deductively to a tightly drawn fact pattern, with no extraneous reasoning. Daydreams about what the law ought to be lose points. Memorized doctrine, applied without sentiment, wins them.

The aim of this course is operational fluency in seven subjects at the level of an entry-level associate who has just left a bar-prep course. By the end, you should be able to read a 100-word vignette, identify the controlling subject and sub-doctrine, eliminate two distractors on the rationale alone, and choose between the remaining two by the dispositive fact — in under two minutes, two hundred times in a row.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions to this track when they test any of the seven MBE subjects under the NCBE subject-matter outlines, with realistic fact patterns, named parties, and bar-style answer choices that each include a rationale. This is distinct from bar-essay preparation: the MBE is multiple-choice, the call asks for "best argument," "most likely outcome," "best ground for objection," or "what additional fact would best support," and the answer choices must be evaluated on both result and reason. Each question should reward issue-spotting first, doctrine second, and policy almost never.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
NCBE distributes the 175 scored items equally across the seven subjects (25 scored items each), plus 25 unscored pretest items. Subject-matter outlines are published by NCBE and refreshed periodically.

| Subject | Scored Items | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| Civil Procedure | 25 | Federal rules; SMJ, PJ, Erie, joinder, preclusion; careful reading of forum facts |
| Constitutional Law | 25 | Scrutiny tiers, individual rights, federalism; many questions are about the level of review |
| Contracts | 25 | Common law vs. UCC Article 2; identify the goods/services line in the first sentence |
| Criminal Law and Procedure | 25 | Substantive crimes (common law and MPC) plus 4th, 5th, 6th Amendment |
| Evidence | 25 | Federal Rules of Evidence; hearsay rules dominate; FRE numbers matter |
| Real Property | 25 | Estates and future interests, recording acts, mortgages, landlord-tenant |
| Torts | 25 | Intentional torts, negligence, strict liability, products, defamation, vicarious |

Suggested cadence: 8-12 weeks of dedicated bar prep, post-graduation, roughly 6 hours per day. Use AdaptiBar, Themis, BarBri, or Kaplan as a doctrinal scaffold; complete 2,500-3,500 NCBE-style practice questions before exam day; sit at least three full timed 200-question simulated MBEs in the last four weeks. Maintain a per-subject error log indexed to the NCBE subtopic codes, not just the broad subject — your weakness is rarely "Evidence" generically; it is the difference between 803(6) business records and 803(8) public records.

## Chapter 1: Civil Procedure
**Core questions:** Where can the case be heard, who can be joined, what governs the law applied, and when is judgment final?

**Key concepts**
- Subject matter jurisdiction: federal question (28 U.S.C. § 1331, well-pleaded complaint rule); diversity (§ 1332, complete diversity, amount in controversy > $75,000 exclusive of interest and costs); supplemental (§ 1367, same case or controversy, with § 1367(b) limitations in diversity); removal and remand (§§ 1441, 1446, 1447); home-state defendant bar on removal in diversity (§ 1441(b)(2))
- Personal jurisdiction: International Shoe "minimum contacts" plus traditional bases; specific (purposeful availment + claim arises out of or relates to those contacts, Ford Motor); general/all-purpose (Goodyear, Daimler — essentially at home: domicile/incorporation/PPB); transient presence (Burnham); consent
- Venue (§ 1391), transfer (§§ 1404, 1406, 1407 MDL), forum non conveniens
- Erie doctrine: Hanna v. Plumer two-track analysis; state substantive law in diversity; Federal Rules apply if on point and valid under Rules Enabling Act and Constitution
- Pleadings: FRCP 8 plausibility (Twombly/Iqbal), FRCP 9 particularity, FRCP 11 sanctions (safe harbor 21 days), FRCP 12 motions and waiver (12(h)(1) party-process, 12(h)(2) failure-to-state, 12(h)(3) SMJ never waived)
- Joinder: FRCP 13 counterclaims/crossclaims, 14 impleader, 18 multiple claims, 19 required parties, 20 permissive, 24 intervention, 23 class action (numerosity, commonality, typicality, adequacy; 23(b)(1)/(2)/(3))
- Discovery: FRCP 26 scope ("relevant to any party's claim or defense and proportional"); 26(b)(3) work product; 30 depositions; 33 interrogatories; 34 requests for production/inspection; 36 admissions; 37 sanctions
- Adjudication: FRCP 50 JMOL (renewed JMOL after verdict), 56 summary judgment (no genuine dispute of material fact), 59 new trial, 60 relief from judgment, jury trial right (7th Amendment for legal claims), appeal final-judgment rule and exceptions
- Preclusion: claim preclusion (same claim, final judgment on merits, same parties or privies); issue preclusion (issue actually litigated and decided, essential to judgment; mutuality largely abandoned)

**Applied skills**
- Compute amount in controversy with claims aggregation rules (single plaintiff vs. multiple plaintiffs vs. class action)
- Diagram a specific-PJ scenario from purposeful availment through relatedness through reasonableness
- Decide whether a Federal Rule is "on point" and "valid" before reaching the Erie balance

**Common traps**
- Treating partial diversity as adequate (§ 1332 requires COMPLETE diversity)
- Forgetting that an SMJ defect is never waivable and can be raised at any time (12(h)(3))
- Confusing specific PJ (claim must arise out of or relate to contacts) with general PJ (essentially at home)
- Applying state substantive law in diversity but state procedural law also (procedural questions usually take a Federal Rule)
- Forgetting that work product is qualified protection (showing of substantial need + undue hardship), while attorney-client privilege is absolute

**Authoritative sources:** Federal Rules of Civil Procedure; 28 U.S.C. §§ 1331, 1332, 1367, 1391, 1404, 1441; International Shoe, Daimler, Ford Motor, Burnham, Bristol-Myers Squibb, Twombly, Iqbal, Hanna v. Plumer, Erie, NCBE MBE Subject Matter Outline.

## Chapter 2: Constitutional Law
**Core questions:** Does the government have the power, and is the exercise of that power consistent with individual rights?

**Key concepts**
- Justiciability: Article III standing (injury-in-fact, causation, redressability); ripeness; mootness with capable-of-repetition-yet-evading-review exception; political question (Baker v. Carr); advisory opinions barred; Flast v. Cohen Establishment Clause taxpayer exception (narrow)
- Federal legislative powers: Commerce Clause (channels, instrumentalities, substantial effects on interstate commerce — economic activity may be aggregated under Wickard/Raich; non-economic activity may not, Lopez/Morrison); taxing and spending (general welfare; conditions germane, unambiguous, not coercive — NFIB v. Sebelius); Necessary and Proper Clause; 14th Amendment Section 5 (congruent and proportional, Boerne)
- State powers and limits: Dormant Commerce Clause (discriminatory laws strict; incidental burdens Pike balancing); Privileges and Immunities (Article IV, fundamental rights of out-of-staters); preemption (express, field, conflict)
- Separation of powers: executive privilege (qualified, Nixon); executive immunity (absolute for official acts, Nixon v. Fitzgerald); legislative immunity (Speech or Debate); Chadha bicameralism + presentment (no legislative veto); Clinton v. New York (no line-item veto); appointments and removal; war powers (commander-in-chief plus War Powers Resolution)
- 14th Amendment Equal Protection: strict scrutiny (race, national origin, alienage by state, fundamental rights); intermediate (sex, illegitimacy); rational basis (everything else); state action (entanglement, public function, encouragement)
- Substantive due process: fundamental rights (marriage Loving/Obergefell, family, contraception, refusal of medical treatment Cruzan); Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health (overruling Roe; abortion now state-law analysis); right to die Glucksberg (no constitutional right)
- Procedural due process: Mathews v. Eldridge balancing (private interest, risk of erroneous deprivation, government interest)
- First Amendment speech: content-based regulation strict scrutiny; content-neutral intermediate; symbolic conduct O'Brien; commercial speech Central Hudson; obscenity Miller (SLAPS, prurient interest, patently offensive); true threats; incitement Brandenburg (directed to + likely to produce imminent lawless action); fighting words (narrow); defamation NYT v. Sullivan (public officials/figures actual malice) and Gertz (private figure on public concern, at least negligence); prior restraints; public forums (traditional/designated/limited/non); government speech doctrine
- First Amendment religion: Free Exercise (Smith — neutral, generally applicable laws permissible; hybrid claims and Sherbert pre-Smith; RFRA at federal level — Hobby Lobby); Establishment (Lemon and Endorsement/Coercion tests historically; Kennedy v. Bremerton 2022 — historical practice and understanding)
- Takings: per se physical (Loretto); categorical regulatory (Lucas — total economic wipeout); Penn Central balancing (partial regulatory); just compensation
- Contracts Clause; Ex Post Facto; Bills of Attainder; § 1983 actions

**Applied skills**
- Identify the level of scrutiny first; many questions are about correctly tiering the right
- Distinguish state action from purely private conduct
- Apply Brandenburg vs. true threats vs. fighting words to expressive-speech fact patterns

**Common traps**
- Treating commercial speech as content-neutral (it is content-based but evaluated under Central Hudson intermediate)
- Reading Sullivan to require actual malice for private figures (only public officials/figures; Gertz governs private figures on matters of public concern)
- Forgetting that the Dormant Commerce Clause does not apply when Congress has authorized the state regulation (or when the state acts as a market participant)
- Confusing procedural due process (Mathews) with substantive due process (fundamental rights review)

**Authoritative sources:** U.S. Constitution; Lopez, Morrison, Raich, NFIB v. Sebelius, Mathews v. Eldridge, NYT v. Sullivan, Gertz, Brandenburg, Miller v. California, Smith, Kennedy v. Bremerton, Lucas, Penn Central, Dobbs.

## Chapter 3: Contracts and UCC Article 2
**Core questions:** Is there a contract, what are its terms, has it been performed or breached, and what remedy follows?

**Key concepts**
- Source of law: common law (services, real estate, employment) vs. UCC Article 2 (goods — movable, tangible at time of identification); mixed contracts use predominant-purpose test (Bonebrake)
- Formation: offer (objective manifestation, definite terms); acceptance (mirror image at common law; UCC 2-207 battle of the forms — additional terms become part of contract between merchants unless materially alter, expressly limit, or are objected to; different terms knock-out in many courts); consideration (bargained-for legal detriment); promissory estoppel; modification (common law requires new consideration; UCC 2-209 does not — only good faith)
- Defenses to formation/enforcement: incapacity (minors, mentally incompetent, intoxicated), mistake (mutual material vs. unilateral); misrepresentation; duress; undue influence; unconscionability; illegality; statute of frauds (MYLEGS — Marriage, > 1 Year, Land, Executor's promise, Goods > $500 under UCC, Suretyship); parol evidence rule (integration analysis: final and complete excludes prior agreements and contemporaneous oral agreements as to terms within the writing; partial integration permits consistent additional terms)
- Performance and breach: conditions (express vs. constructive; substantial performance for constructive); divisible vs. entire contract; anticipatory repudiation (unequivocal); demand for adequate assurance (UCC 2-609 and Rest. 2d § 251); perfect tender in UCC sale of goods (2-601); cure (2-508); installment-contract substantial impairment (2-612)
- Third-party rights: assignment (rights), delegation (duties); intended vs. incidental beneficiaries (Lawrence v. Fox; vesting cuts off modification)
- Remedies: expectation damages (benefit of bargain — direct, consequential foreseeable Hadley v. Baxendale, incidental); reliance; restitution; specific performance (real estate; unique goods); injunctions; liquidated damages (reasonable forecast at formation, actual damages difficult to determine); UCC seller remedies (resale 2-706, market 2-708, lost-volume profit 2-708(2), incidental); UCC buyer remedies (cover 2-712, market 2-713, consequential foreseeable to seller); mitigation duty
- Warranties (UCC): express (2-313), implied warranty of merchantability (2-314, merchant in goods of that kind), implied warranty of fitness for particular purpose (2-315); disclaimer (2-316)
- Risk of loss (UCC 2-509, 2-510); shipment vs. destination contracts; sale on approval and sale or return (2-326)

**Applied skills**
- Identify goods/services line within the first read of the fact pattern; everything else follows
- Apply UCC 2-207 step-by-step to merchant-vs.-merchant battle of the forms
- Compute expectation damages with foreseeability filter and mitigation reduction

**Common traps**
- Fee simple defeasible vs. life estate — durational language ("so long as," "until," "while") = fee simple determinable + possibility of reverter; conditional language ("provided that," "on condition that," "but if") = fee simple subject to condition subsequent + right of entry/re-entry must be exercised. (This is property, but the MBE often blends estates into contracts via land sale contracts — keep the markers straight.)
- Treating an acceptance with new terms as a counter-offer under UCC (it usually is not — 2-207(1) creates a contract unless acceptance is expressly conditional on assent to additional terms)
- Forgetting that UCC modification does NOT require new consideration (2-209(1))
- Applying perfect tender to non-UCC contracts (substantial performance governs at common law)
- Treating consequential damages as automatically recoverable (Hadley foreseeability filter)

**Authoritative sources:** Restatement (Second) of Contracts; UCC Article 2 (especially §§ 2-201, 2-204, 2-207, 2-209, 2-313 to 2-316, 2-601, 2-609, 2-706, 2-708, 2-712, 2-713, 2-715); Hadley v. Baxendale; Bonebrake v. Cox.

## Chapter 4: Criminal Law and Procedure
**Core questions:** Did the defendant commit the crime, and were the police and prosecutors constitutionally clean in their investigation and adjudication?

**Key concepts**
- Mens rea: common-law specific intent (with mistake-of-fact defense to any honest mistake, intoxication relevant), general intent (mistake reasonable), strict liability (no defense from mistake); MPC purposely/knowingly/recklessly/negligently
- Inchoate crimes: attempt (specific intent + substantial step or dangerous proximity); conspiracy (agreement; modern unilateral; overt act in MPC and many jurisdictions; Wharton's rule; Pinkerton liability for foreseeable crimes in furtherance); solicitation
- Homicide: common-law murder (intent to kill, intent to inflict serious bodily harm, depraved heart, felony murder — inherently dangerous BRAKES: Burglary, Robbery, Arson, Kidnapping, Escape, Sexual assault/Rape; res gestae; agency vs. proximate-cause approaches); first-degree murder typically requires premeditation and deliberation; voluntary manslaughter (adequate provocation, heat of passion, no cooling-off); involuntary manslaughter (criminal negligence or misdemeanor manslaughter)
- Accomplice liability: aid, abet, encourage with intent that the crime succeed; accomplice liable for the crime and reasonably foreseeable additional crimes (split among jurisdictions)
- Defenses: self-defense (reasonable force, proportional, imminent threat; deadly force only against deadly threat; retreat rule varies, stand-your-ground in some); defense of others; defense of property (no deadly force); duress (not for homicide in most jurisdictions); necessity; insanity (M'Naghten — didn't know nature/quality or wrongness; MPC — substantial capacity test; irresistible impulse; Durham product test — rare); intoxication (voluntary may negate specific intent; involuntary fuller defense)
- 4th Amendment search/seizure: government action; reasonable expectation of privacy (Katz); search warrant requirement and probable cause; exceptions — search incident to lawful arrest (Chimel, Gant for vehicle), automobile (Carroll — probable cause + readily mobile), plain view (lawful presence + immediately apparent), exigent circumstances (hot pursuit, evanescent evidence, emergency aid), consent (voluntary, including third party with apparent authority Rodriguez), Terry stop and frisk (reasonable suspicion of crime + armed and dangerous), special needs/administrative; standing (own privacy interest required); exclusionary rule and fruit of the poisonous tree (Wong Sun); good-faith Leon exception; independent source; inevitable discovery; attenuation
- 5th Amendment: Miranda (custodial interrogation; warnings before; invocation rights — silence honored Mosley, counsel cuts off all interrogation Edwards; voluntariness separately required); privilege against self-incrimination; double jeopardy (attaches when jury sworn or first witness sworn in bench trial; Blockburger same-elements test)
- 6th Amendment: right to counsel attaches at first formal charging (Massiah, deliberate elicitation post-charge); right to jury trial when authorized imprisonment > 6 months; right to confront witnesses (Crawford — testimonial statements barred unless declarant unavailable and prior cross opportunity); right to compulsory process; effective assistance Strickland (deficient + prejudice)
- Sentencing and Eighth Amendment proportionality; collateral attack on convictions

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish Miranda from voluntariness — a confession can satisfy Miranda and still be coerced
- Apply the Blockburger same-elements test for double jeopardy
- Walk a vehicle stop through Terry vs. probable cause vs. Carroll vs. consent

**Common traps**
- Treating Miranda as required for all police questioning (only custodial interrogation)
- Forgetting that 6th Amendment right to counsel is offense-specific (Texas v. Cobb); 5th-Amendment-Edwards rule is not
- Applying "fruit of the poisonous tree" without checking attenuation/independent-source/inevitable-discovery
- Confusing felony murder (no separate intent to kill) with first-degree murder (premeditation and deliberation)
- Treating duress as a defense to homicide (most jurisdictions: no)

**Authoritative sources:** Model Penal Code; common-law crimes; Miranda v. Arizona, Edwards v. Arizona, Massiah v. United States, Brewer v. Williams, Strickland v. Washington, Katz v. United States, Terry v. Ohio, Carroll v. United States, Chimel v. California, Arizona v. Gant, Riley v. California, Crawford v. Washington, Mapp v. Ohio.

## Chapter 5: Evidence
**Core questions:** Is the item relevant, is it admissible, and which Federal Rule of Evidence lets it in or keeps it out?

**Key concepts**
- Relevance (FRE 401, 402); FRE 403 exclusion when probative value substantially outweighed by unfair prejudice, confusion, misleading the jury, undue delay, waste of time, or needlessly cumulative
- Character evidence (FRE 404, 405): generally inadmissible to prove conduct in conformity; criminal-defendant exception (defendant may open door; prosecution rebuttal); reputation/opinion methods (FRE 405(a)); specific instances on cross only or in essential-element cases (FRE 405(b)); FRE 404(b) other-acts admissible for non-propensity purposes (MIMIC: Motive, Intent, Mistake/absence, Identity, Common plan)
- Habit/routine practice (FRE 406)
- Subsequent remedial measures (FRE 407): inadmissible to prove negligence, culpable conduct, defective design; admissible for ownership/control/feasibility (if disputed) and impeachment
- Compromise offers (FRE 408); medical expenses (FRE 409); plea negotiations and withdrawn pleas (FRE 410); liability insurance (FRE 411)
- Witnesses: competence (FRE 601, federal rule generally permits all witnesses; state law in diversity for state-law claims); impeachment — prior inconsistent statements, character for truthfulness (FRE 608 reputation/opinion; FRE 609 prior convictions — crimes of dishonesty automatic; other felonies probative-value balanced for non-defendant; for criminal defendants reverse 403), bias, contradiction, sensory/mental capacity
- Hearsay (FRE 801-807): definition — out-of-court statement offered for the truth of the matter asserted; non-hearsay purposes (effect on listener, state of mind of speaker, verbal acts, impeachment); FRE 801(d) exclusions — prior statements by declarant-witness (prior inconsistent under oath; prior consistent rebutting fabrication; identification); party-opponent admissions including vicarious (agent/employee within scope, FRE 801(d)(2)(D)), adoptive, authorized, co-conspirator in furtherance; FRE 803 — admissible regardless of availability (present sense impression; excited utterance; then-existing mental/emotional/physical state; statement for medical diagnosis; recorded recollection — read but not received; business records 803(6); public records 803(8) with criminal-case limitations; learned treatise; ancient documents; market reports); FRE 804 — declarant unavailable (former testimony; dying declaration in homicide and civil cases; statement against interest; statement of personal/family history; forfeiture by wrongdoing); FRE 807 residual
- Confrontation Clause (Crawford): testimonial out-of-court statements barred against criminal defendant unless declarant unavailable and prior cross opportunity; non-testimonial (excited utterances during ongoing emergency, Davis v. Washington) outside Crawford
- Privileges: attorney-client (federal common law); spousal — adverse testimony (only married at trial, witness-spouse holds) and confidential communications (either spouse holds); psychotherapist-patient (Jaffee v. Redmond); clergy; physician-patient (state-law creature); journalist (limited); work-product (qualified)
- Expert testimony (FRE 702): Daubert reliability standard (testing, peer review, error rate, general acceptance); Kumho Tire (non-scientific experts also); FRE 703 (bases of opinion); FRE 704 (ultimate-issue, with mental-state caveat in criminal cases); lay opinion FRE 701
- Authentication (FRE 901, 902 self-authenticating); best evidence rule (FRE 1001-1008): original required to prove contents of writing, recording, photograph; duplicates admissible unless genuine question of authenticity; secondary evidence permissible with adequate justification
- Judicial notice (FRE 201): adjudicative facts generally known in jurisdiction or accurately and readily determinable

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish hearsay (truth of matter asserted) from non-hearsay (effect on listener, verbal acts)
- Apply 803(6) business records (regular practice + made at or near time + person with knowledge + custodian or qualified witness)
- Walk a confrontation-clause challenge through testimonial/non-testimonial analysis

**Common traps**
- Treating 404(b) as "character evidence is always inadmissible" (other-acts admissible for non-propensity MIMIC purposes; only the propensity inference is barred)
- Forgetting that FRE 408 bars settlement offers for liability but admits them for bias or to negate a delay claim
- Treating present-sense impression and excited utterance as interchangeable (different timing/triggering requirements)
- Admitting business records that lack the regularity-of-practice foundation
- Applying Crawford to non-testimonial statements (it does not apply)

**Authoritative sources:** Federal Rules of Evidence; Crawford v. Washington; Davis v. Washington; Daubert v. Merrell Dow; Kumho Tire; Jaffee v. Redmond; Bullcoming/Melendez-Diaz.

## Chapter 6: Real Property
**Core questions:** Who owns what interest, can they convey it, who wins in a recording dispute, and what duties run between landlord and tenant?

**Key concepts**
- Present possessory estates: fee simple absolute; fee simple determinable ("so long as," "until," "while" — automatic termination, possibility of reverter); fee simple subject to condition subsequent ("provided that," "on condition that," "but if" — must be exercised, right of entry / power of termination); fee simple subject to executory limitation (cuts off in favor of third party, executory interest); life estate (waste doctrine: voluntary, permissive, ameliorative)
- Future interests: reversion; possibility of reverter; right of entry; remainder (vested — ascertained, no condition precedent; vested subject to open — class gift, members may be added; vested subject to complete defeasance; contingent — unascertained or condition precedent); executory interest (shifting — cuts off another grantee; springing — cuts off grantor)
- Rule Against Perpetuities (common-law): no interest is valid unless it must vest, if at all, within 21 years of a life in being at the creation of the interest; charity-to-charity exception; many states adopt USRAP wait-and-see 90-year alternative
- Concurrent estates: tenancy in common (default; no survivorship; freely transferable); joint tenancy (four unities — time, title, interest, possession; right of survivorship; severance by unilateral conveyance, mortgage in title-theory states, contract); tenancy by the entirety (spouses; cannot be severed unilaterally; survivorship; creditors of one spouse generally cannot reach)
- Landlord-tenant: term of years; periodic tenancy (notice equal to period, capped at six months for year-to-year); tenancy at will (terminable any time); tenancy at sufferance (holdover); implied warranty of habitability (residential; cannot be waived); constructive eviction (substantial interference + tenant gives notice and vacates within reasonable time); duty to mitigate (modern majority); assignment vs. sublease (assignment: entire remaining term; sublease: less than entire); privity of estate vs. privity of contract
- Easements: express (writing, statute of frauds); implication by prior use (apparent + continuous + reasonably necessary at severance); easement by necessity (strict necessity, landlocked); easement by prescription (adverse + continuous + open and notorious + statutory period); termination by release, merger, abandonment with affirmative act, estoppel, prescription against the easement; license (revocable unless estoppel)
- Covenants running with land (legal remedy — damages): touch and concern + intent + notice + horizontal privity (between original parties — shared interest) + vertical privity (between original and successor — entire estate transferred for burden, lesser for benefit)
- Equitable servitudes (equitable remedy — injunction): touch and concern + intent + notice; no privity required; common-scheme implied reciprocal negative servitude
- Adverse possession: continuous, hostile (claim of right), open and notorious, actual, exclusive, for the statutory period (CHOAN-E); color of title may shorten; tacking with privity
- Conveyancing: contract of sale (statute of frauds; marketable title implied; risk of loss — equitable conversion majority puts on buyer at signing, Massachusetts/UPCRA on seller); deeds (general warranty — six covenants; special warranty — grantor's acts only; quitclaim — none); delivery and acceptance; merger doctrine (deed merges contract obligations on closing)
- Recording acts: race (first to record wins); race-notice (subsequent BFP without notice who records first wins); notice (subsequent BFP without notice wins regardless of recording); BFP requires value and no notice (actual, record, inquiry); shelter rule
- Mortgages: lien theory (majority — mortgagor retains title) vs. title theory (minority); foreclosure judicial vs. non-judicial power of sale; equitable redemption pre-sale; statutory redemption post-sale in some states; deficiency judgment; due-on-sale clauses; equitable subrogation; mortgage assumption vs. taking subject to
- Zoning: variance (undue hardship + no diminution to neighbors); nonconforming use; spot zoning impermissible

**Applied skills**
- Identify "so long as" vs. "provided that" in the first read; the language drives the estate
- Diagram chain of conveyances and recordings to apply the recording act
- Compute RAP validity by finding a life in being who measures the vesting

**Common traps**
- Fee simple defeasible vs. life estate — durational language (so long as, until, while) = fee simple determinable + possibility of reverter; conditional language (provided that, on condition that, but if) = fee simple subject to condition subsequent + right of entry/re-entry must be exercised.
- Treating joint tenancy as indestructible (it can be severed unilaterally; tenancy by the entirety cannot)
- Forgetting that a mortgage in a title-theory state severs a joint tenancy when granted (lien theory: only on foreclosure)
- Treating a quitclaim grantee as never a BFP (they can be; the deed type does not control BFP status)
- Confusing equitable redemption (before foreclosure sale) with statutory redemption (after, only in some states)

**Authoritative sources:** Restatement (Third) of Property; uniform recording acts; state-by-state landlord-tenant statutes; NCBE MBE Subject Matter Outline.

## Chapter 7: Torts
**Core questions:** Whose fault is it, whose harm was caused, and which doctrine assigns the loss?

**Key concepts**
- Intentional torts (intent = purpose or substantial certainty; transferred intent across the original five — battery, assault, false imprisonment, trespass to land, trespass to chattels): battery (harmful or offensive contact with plaintiff or extension); assault (reasonable apprehension of imminent battery); false imprisonment (confinement to bounded area + plaintiff aware or harmed); IIED (extreme and outrageous conduct + intent or recklessness + severe emotional distress; third-party variant if present and immediate family, or any plaintiff with bodily harm); trespass to land; trespass to chattels (intermeddling); conversion (severe interference — replevin or full value)
- Defenses to intentional torts: consent (express or implied; capacity; scope); self-defense (reasonable belief, proportional force, no retreat required from home generally); defense of others; defense of property (no deadly force absent threat to person); recapture of chattels (fresh pursuit, proper demand absent futility); necessity — public (absolute, no liability) and private (privileged entry but liability for actual damages)
- Negligence: duty (general reasonable-person standard with adjustments — children of similar age and intelligence except for adult activities, physicians local-then-national custom, common carriers, common-law no-duty rules with exceptions like special relationships, voluntary undertaking, contractual, statute-imposed); breach (reasonable care; negligence per se where statute violated and plaintiff is in protected class and harm is the type intended to prevent — with excuses); causation — actual (but-for; substantial factor for concurrent causes; alternative liability Summers v. Tice; market-share for fungible products); proximate (foreseeability of type and manner of harm; eggshell-skull plaintiff doctrine; intervening but foreseeable causes do not break chain, intervening superseding criminal acts may); damages — actual harm required for negligence; pure economic loss generally barred without physical impact; NIED (zone-of-danger or physical-manifestation rule; bystander Dillon variant in some states)
- Special duties: landowners under traditional categorization — trespasser (refrain from willful/wanton); licensee (warn of known dangers); invitee (reasonable inspection); attractive nuisance to child trespassers (foreseeable + dangerous condition + child unable to appreciate + utility low + reasonable care would eliminate); modern reasonable-care unified approach in some states
- Strict liability: abnormally dangerous activities (Restatement factors — high risk, severity, inability to eliminate by reasonable care, uncommon use, location, value to community); wild animals; domestic animals with known dangerous propensity
- Products liability: four theories — strict liability (commercial seller in chain + product unreasonably dangerous + reaches user without substantial change + causes harm), negligence (manufacturer/seller breach), warranty (express, implied merchantability, implied fitness for particular purpose), misrepresentation; defect categories — manufacturing (deviation from intended), design (Restatement 2d 402A consumer-expectation OR Restatement 3d risk-utility with reasonable alternative design), warning (foreseeable risk + adequate warning would have reduced — heeding presumption)
- Defamation: false statement of fact about plaintiff + publication to third party + fault (Sullivan actual malice for public officials/figures; Gertz at least negligence for private figure on matter of public concern; common-law strict for private figure on private matter); libel/slander (slander per se if profession, criminal conduct, loathsome disease, unchastity); defenses — truth, absolute privilege (judicial, legislative, spousal), qualified privilege (lost by malice or excess)
- Privacy torts: appropriation (commercial use of name/likeness); intrusion upon seclusion; false light (with NYT v. Sullivan overlay for public concern); public disclosure of private facts
- Misrepresentation: intentional/fraudulent misrepresentation (scienter, intent, reliance, damages); negligent misrepresentation limited (commercial setting, foreseeable user)
- Interference with contract; interference with prospective economic advantage
- Vicarious liability: respondeat superior (scope of employment, including frolic vs. detour, intentional torts within scope rare); independent contractor general non-liability with exceptions for non-delegable duties (e.g., inherently dangerous activities, ultrahazardous, common-carrier passenger safety); partnership/joint enterprise; parents not vicariously liable at common law except statutory
- Damages: compensatory (economic and non-economic); punitive (egregious conduct; constitutional Gore guideposts — reprehensibility, ratio, comparable penalties; State Farm v. Campbell roughly single-digit ratio); single satisfaction; collateral source rule (traditional bar; eroded by statutes)
- Apportionment: contributory negligence (Maryland/Virginia/Alabama/N.C./D.C. — complete bar); pure comparative (any percentage); modified comparative (50% or 51% bar); assumption of risk (express; implied — primary/secondary)

**Applied skills**
- Walk a single fact pattern through all four products-liability theories
- Apply NYT v. Sullivan vs. Gertz vs. common-law defamation based on plaintiff status and subject matter
- Identify the four causation steps: actual + proximate + foreseeable type + intervening cause analysis

**Common traps**
- Treating "intent" as intent to harm (intent is purpose or substantial certainty as to contact/confinement/etc.; harm is not required)
- Forgetting transferred intent works across the five traditional torts but not IIED
- Mixing up consumer-expectation (Restatement 2d 402A) and risk-utility (Restatement 3d) for design defects — many bar questions use either
- Applying actual-malice standard to a private figure on a private matter (common-law strict liability still applies; Gertz only governs public-concern matters)
- Treating private necessity as full defense (it privileges entry but does not bar liability for actual damages — Vincent v. Lake Erie)

**Authoritative sources:** Restatement (Second) and (Third) of Torts; Restatement (Second) of Torts § 402A; Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability; NYT v. Sullivan; Gertz v. Welch; State Farm v. Campbell; Vincent v. Lake Erie.

## Chapter 8: Cross-Subject Issue Spotting
**Core questions:** When two doctrines collide in one fact pattern, which controls — and which fact tells you?

**Key concepts**
- Civil procedure plus Erie meeting state substantive law (e.g., is the state rule substantive enough to apply in diversity?)
- Criminal law plus criminal procedure: substantive crime alongside suppression issue
- Contracts plus real property: land sale contracts, marketable title, statute of frauds writing requirements
- Evidence plus constitutional law: Crawford confrontation clause interacting with hearsay exceptions
- Torts plus evidence: subsequent remedial measures (FRE 407) in a negligence suit
- Real property plus contracts: equitable conversion, risk of loss before closing

**Applied skills**
- Spot the second issue without losing time on the first
- Recognize when a "best argument" question requires you to compare two valid arguments (the better one is the answer)

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
**Pacing.** 200 questions in two 3-hour sessions = ~1:48 per question average. Plan 1:45 per question with a small buffer for review at session end. Never skip a question — choose your best guess and move on; flag for review only if time. Review only flagged items at the end of each session.

**Reading the call.** "Most likely to prevail" tests the doctrinal winner. "Best defense" tests the strongest defense among the choices, not necessarily a winning one. "What additional fact, if proven, would best support" requires you to pick the fact that completes a missing element of the underlying claim — not a fact that strengthens an already-complete claim.

**Eliminating answer choices.** A wrong rationale invalidates a wrong answer even if the outcome is correct. Two answers that say "the seller wins" can both be wrong if their reasons are wrong. Read all four; identify the result-plus-rationale pair that survives doctrinal scrutiny.

**Heuristics that work.** Look for the jurisdictional anchor in the stem ("modern majority," "common law," "UCC," "in this jurisdiction, the recording act provides..."). Answers that ignore the anchor are usually wrong. Answers that introduce a fact not in the stem are wrong.

**Heuristics that fail.** "Whichever feels fair." "Policy reasons." "What the law should be." None of these win MBE points.
