# Priya's Brutal Surgeon's Quiz
**ID**: `priyasBrutalSurgeonsQuiz` · **Discipline**: Healthcare / Surgery · **Level**: Expert consultant, department lead, surgical educator

## Course Aim
This is an intentionally brutal expert surgical quiz. It is not medical-school recall, residency trivia, or generic clinical reasoning. It tests whether a senior surgeon can recognize dangerous operative anatomy, read misleading postoperative signals, and pick the safest move when the easy-looking answer is the one that kills the patient. The ideal item feels like a hostile oral-board case, a morbidity-and-mortality conference trap, or a consultant phone call at 2:17 a.m. where the registrar has already chosen the wrong plan.

Every case demands disciplined prioritization: what is killing the patient right now, what anatomy is not yet proven, which test is falsely reassuring, which source is uncontrolled, which complication must be assumed until excluded, and what action is safest in the next ten minutes. Difficulty comes from multi-step reasoning and colliding constraints — not from obscure vocabulary or longer wording. A medical student should be unable to answer from a single remembered fact; a strong attending should still hesitate over at least two of the distractors.

Priya is the course voice: exacting, brilliant, clinically serious, and funny. The silliness (courtroom metaphors, noir suspects, treacherous drains, smug vessels, haunted checklists) makes cases memorable, but it never obscures the decision and never substitutes for difficulty. The clinical judgment underneath each joke is real, defensible in an oral-board setting, and safe in actual practice.

## Course Design Notes
- This syllabus replaces a generic `medical` track that was too broad and too junior — it covered triage, differential diagnosis, and general safety suitable for clinical reasoning, not an expert surgical gauntlet.
- Chapters are organized by specialty-specific surgical domains, not generic healthcare topics. Each chapter carries the same spine: Core questions, Key concepts, Applied skills, and Common traps.
- "Common traps" per chapter are the heart of the course. They name the specific false reassurance, the specific overconfident shortcut, and the specific sequencing error that the chapter exists to punish.
- Difficulty must come from multi-step reasoning, colliding constraints, and misleading clues — never from longer wording, rarer vocabulary, or hidden facts. The prompt must contain enough information to solve the case without guessing.
- The generation scaffolding at the end (Difficulty Standard, Course Voice, Assessment Design Rules, Coverage Goals, Capstone) governs how individual items are written and balanced. Treat chapter "Core questions" as the syllabus anchors and the scaffolding as the style guide.
- Cancer judgment is deliberately split across one focused oncology chapter plus oncology threads inside the anatomy, HPB, and head-and-neck chapters, so the quiz does not collapse into a pure-oncology or pure-sepsis exam.

## Chapter 1: Hostile Operative Anatomy
Anatomy that punishes overconfidence, and the discipline of operating from proven landmarks rather than assumption.

**Core questions**
- When is the anatomy not yet proven, and what does the surgeon owe the patient before the next cut, clip, or burst of energy?
- How do you recognize a named danger zone from partial intraoperative clues?
- When is bailout (subtotal cholecystectomy, conversion, fundus-first, leaving tissue, calling for help) the expert move rather than the cowardly one?

**Key concepts**
- Critical view of safety in cholecystectomy; the infundibular trap and false common-bile-duct identification; Rouvière's sulcus, the R4U line, the cystic plate, and safe-plane discipline.
- Moynihan's hump, replaced/accessory right hepatic artery, and the geometry of vasculobiliary injury.
- Berry's ligament and recurrent laryngeal nerve danger zones; external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve in superior-pole thyroid dissection.
- Facial nerve trunk landmarks in parotid surgery and the mimics that lead to wrong-plane dissection.
- Obturator fossa contents and traction hazards; pelvic autonomic nerves, ureter, lateral ligament, and internal iliac branches.
- Retroperitoneal vascular zones; SMA/SMV/portal vein/uncinate process and pancreaticoduodenal branch hazards — and what not to open.

**Applied skills**
- Convert partial visual cues into a stop-or-proceed decision with an explicit safety rationale.
- Choose the specific bailout that matches the specific danger rather than pressing on for completion.
- Plan dissection so that vascular and ductal structures are proven before division, not after.

**Common traps**
- Good-looking anatomy is treated as a critical view; it is not until both structures are positively identified.
- Visual nerve continuity is taken as proof of nerve function.
- Blind clipping, blind clamping, broad energy application near unproven structures, and using traction as navigation.
- Assuming standard arterial anatomy when a replaced or accessory vessel is in play.

## Chapter 2: Source Control and Postoperative Sepsis
Antibiotics are not plumbing. The chapter is about uncontrolled sources hiding behind quiet drains and reassuring scans.

**Core questions**
- What is the actual source, and is it controlled — or merely being treated with drugs while it keeps soiling?
- When does physiology override weak or negative imaging and force action now?
- What level of source control does this patient need: antibiotics, percutaneous drainage, transanal drainage, washout, diversion, or takedown?

**Key concepts**
- Contained vs free anastomotic leak after colorectal surgery; low pelvic leaks with incomplete or false-negative CT.
- Drains that are quiet because they are blocked, misplaced, or not communicating with the collection.
- Abscesses, infected collections, gas–fluid signals, and the escalation ladder of source control.
- Septic physiology: lactate, oliguria, confusion, tachycardia, leukopenia, rising vasopressor need.
- Stable-but-drainable vs unstable-and-must-operate decision-making.

**Applied skills**
- Separate drain management from source control and act on the difference.
- Escalate before collapse rather than after, using physiology as the trigger.
- Identify the single dangerous miss inside a crowded postoperative picture.

**Common traps**
- Low drain output is read as "no leak" when the drain is simply not communicating.
- IV-only or under-protocolled CT is treated as excluding a low pelvic leak.
- Absent fever, steroids, immunosuppression, or the early postoperative state are mistaken for reassurance.
- Repeat imaging is ordered as prudence when it is actually fatal delay.

## Chapter 3: HPB Catastrophes
High-consequence hepatobiliary and pancreatic traps where small signals precede large disasters.

**Core questions**
- After a difficult cholecystectomy, is this a simple cystic-stump leak or a vasculobiliary injury with ischemia hiding underneath?
- After pancreatectomy, what does a small sentinel bleed mean, and what must happen before the herald becomes the hemorrhage?
- When is local repair a trap, and when must the patient be drained, stabilized, and transferred to HPB expertise?

**Key concepts**
- Bile leak localization: cystic-duct stump, duct of Luschka, lateral common hepatic duct injury, major duct transection.
- Right hepatic artery injury with sectoral ischemia; the integrated picture of bile leak plus arterial injury plus sepsis.
- Roles and limits of ERCP, MRCP, CTA, biloma drainage, and cholangiography.
- Post-pancreatectomy fistula (clinically relevant grades), GDA stump erosion, hepatic arterial pseudoaneurysm; covered stent vs embolization.
- Perihilar cholangiocarcinoma resectability: future liver remnant, drainage strategy, portal vein embolization.

**Applied skills**
- Treat sentinel bleeding as the emergency it is, with angiography and ICU-level control rather than observation.
- Sequence drainage, imaging, and transfer instead of attempting premature definitive repair.
- Integrate vascular, biliary, septic, and physiologic data into one plan.

**Common traps**
- A negative single-phase CTA is taken to exclude intermittent sentinel hemorrhage.
- Transient hemodynamic stability is mistaken for resolution.
- Local bile-duct repair is attempted without proven anatomy, vascular status, or specialist expertise.
- The "small" herald bleed is observed instead of investigated.

## Chapter 4: Vascular, Trauma, and Damage Control
Sequencing under hemorrhage and physiologic failure, where definitive surgery in an exhausted patient is the mistake.

**Core questions**
- Does this patient's physiology choose damage control over definitive reconstruction?
- Should this hematoma be opened, packed, or left alone — and is proximal control established first?
- How do hemorrhage, thrombosis, perfusion, and source control get balanced in the same patient?

**Key concepts**
- Proximal vascular control before opening central hematomas; Zone I/II/III retroperitoneal logic.
- Pelvic fracture hemorrhage: binder, external fixation, preperitoneal packing, IR embolization.
- Damage control laparotomy: pack, control contamination, temporary closure, planned return.
- Open abdomen and abdominal compartment syndrome: bladder pressure, ventilator pressures, oliguria, lactate.
- Limb ischemia and reperfusion; compartment syndrome despite palpable pulses; mesenteric ischemia in low-flow, septic, or acute-on-chronic states.

**Applied skills**
- Trigger damage control on physiology (acidosis, hypothermia, coagulopathy) rather than anatomy alone.
- Choose hematoma management by zone and mechanism, not reflex.
- Resuscitate coagulopathy (fibrinogen, calcium, temperature, acidosis, platelets, targeted reversal) alongside surgical control.

**Common traps**
- Preserved pulses are taken to exclude compartment syndrome.
- A soft abdomen is taken to exclude mesenteric ischemia.
- High CVP is read as volume overload when it reflects abdominal compartment syndrome.
- Definitive reconstruction is pursued in a patient who needs to be packed and closed.

## Chapter 5: Anticoagulation, Coagulopathy, and Competing Catastrophes
Situations where every available option can hurt the patient, and the only safe answer is a two-phase plan.

**Core questions**
- What is reversible, what is time-critical, and what can safely wait?
- How do you survive the bleeding now while preventing the thrombosis that is also trying to kill the patient?
- Is this diffuse coagulopathic ooze or a discrete surgical bleeder — and does that change the operation?

**Key concepts**
- Mechanical mitral valve or recent PE colliding with emergency abdominal sepsis or a suspected anastomotic leak.
- Warfarin reversal with PCC and vitamin K; DOAC reversal principles and institutional protocols; LMWH and neuraxial timing conflicts.
- Coagulopathy of trauma/sepsis: fibrinogen depletion, hypocalcemia, hypothermia, acidosis, platelet dysfunction.
- Restart strategy: short-acting, titratable anticoagulation after hemostasis is secured.
- When prophylactic anticoagulation is inadequate and when therapeutic anticoagulation is dangerous.

**Applied skills**
- Build explicit two-phase plans: stabilize and control now, then re-anticoagulate deliberately.
- Distinguish coagulopathic bleeding (correct the milieu) from a surgical bleeder (return to control it).
- State the reversal agent, the timing, and the restart trigger rather than reaching for all-or-nothing decisions.

**Common traps**
- All-or-nothing thinking: either fully anticoagulate or fully reverse, ignoring the bridge.
- Restarting full-dose anticoagulation before hemostasis is proven.
- Treating diffuse ooze with reoperation, or treating a surgical bleeder with more product.
- Forgetting that the patient who is bleeding today still has a valve, clot, or rhythm that can kill them tomorrow.

## Chapter 6: Endocrine, Head-and-Neck, and Airway Traps
Small anatomical spaces where delay turns a manageable problem into an asphyxiating one.

**Core questions**
- When does airway or nerve function override completion bias and cosmetic concern?
- After signal loss or hematoma, do you stop, stage, decompress, or call for immediate help — and in what order?
- What looks intact but is functionally unsafe?

**Key concepts**
- Loss of recurrent laryngeal nerve signal and the case for staging a planned total thyroidectomy; bilateral vocal fold paralysis risk.
- Post-thyroidectomy neck hematoma and the threatened airway; why decompression precedes transfer to theatre.
- Superior-pole dissection and the external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve; parathyroid preservation and postoperative hypocalcemia.
- Compartment-oriented neck dissection vs node berry-picking.
- Parotidectomy: facial nerve trunk landmarks, retromandibular vein, duct and branch preservation.

**Applied skills**
- Act on evolving airway compromise before saturation falls.
- Convert intraoperative nerve-monitoring data into a stage-or-proceed decision.
- Decompress a threatening neck hematoma at the bedside rather than waiting for ideal conditions.

**Common traps**
- Normal oxygen saturation is treated as reassuring during evolving airway compression.
- Visual nerve continuity is treated as proof of function.
- Completion of the second side proceeds after first-side signal loss, risking bilateral paralysis.
- Cosmetic or completion bias overrides functional safety.

## Chapter 7: Surgical Oncology and MDT Sequencing
Operations where margin, lymph basin, biology, and sequencing matter far more than technical speed.

**Core questions**
- Is this disease oncologically sensible to remove, or merely technically removable?
- What is the right sequence — biopsy, staging, neoadjuvant therapy, drainage, embolization, resection, palliation, or referral — and what happens if the surgeon skips a step?
- Does the operation improve survival or quality of life, or does it mainly satisfy the surgeon?

**Key concepts**
- Establish tissue diagnosis, clinical stage, biology, resectability, and fitness before committing; respect tumor planes (no capsular rupture, tumor violation, piecemeal excision, or shaving malignant adhesions).
- Margin language: R0/R1/R2, circumferential/radial vs distal/proximal margins, and when width changes management.
- Colorectal/anal: T4b en bloc multivisceral resection; obstructing left-sided cancer (stent bridge, diverting stoma, emergency resection, damage control); synchronous liver metastases sequencing; rectal MRI risk features (threatened CRM, EMVI, lateral nodes, low level); total neoadjuvant therapy; watch-and-wait; Lynch/FAP extent-of-colectomy decisions.
- HPB/upper-GI: perihilar cholangiocarcinoma (Bismuth pattern, vascular involvement, caudate resection, remnant drainage, PVE); pancreatic adenocarcinoma (resectable/borderline/locally advanced, venous reconstruction, arterial encasement, CA19-9, occult metastases); HCC and the failing-liver trap; colorectal liver metastases (FLR, two-stage hepatectomy, disappearing metastases); gastric/GEJ staging laparoscopy and peritoneal cytology; esophageal conduit and anastomotic planning.
- Endocrine/H&N/breast/skin/sarcoma: thyroid lobectomy vs total and compartmental nodal dissection; medullary thyroid (calcitonin/CEA, RET, pheochromocytoma exclusion); breast conservation vs mastectomy and SLN vs axillary dissection; soft-tissue sarcoma biopsy-tract planning and avoiding unplanned excision; melanoma Breslow/ulceration/SLNB/margin logic.
- Metastatic/palliative: oligometastatic local therapy vs widespread disease; recurrent pelvic disease and exenteration selection; malignant bowel obstruction options; defining the symptom target before any palliative operation.

**Applied skills**
- Force an explicit sequence and justify each step against margin, biology, nodal basin, remnant-organ function, and metastatic pattern.
- Reason as a multidisciplinary team, not a solo operator: when to delay for staging, nutrition/prehabilitation, drainage, embolization, or genetic testing.
- Reject impressive operations that worsen survival in occult metastatic, biologically explosive, or physiologically impossible disease.

**Common traps**
- Radiographic resectability is mistaken for biological resectability.
- A negative staging scan is taken to exclude peritoneal disease in high-risk gastric, pancreatic, or ovarian-pattern cancers.
- A small future liver remnant turns an R0 resection fatal.
- A single biopsied node is treated as permission for node berry-picking.
- A low rectal tumor's distal margin distracts from the CRM that is the real threat.
- Excellent neoadjuvant response breeds false confidence; tumor stuck to an organ is malignant until proven otherwise in theatre.
- Emergency obstruction eclipses hereditary risk, synchronous disease, and proper metastatic staging.

## Difficulty Standard
Written for adult experts, roughly 35+: attending surgeons, senior fellows, department-leading consultants, and surgical educators.

**Medium** — at least two reasoning steps; tests a known safety principle in a specific scenario; wrong answers include at least one plausible near-miss. Shape: recognize unsafe anatomy, then choose bailout over continued dissection.

**Hard** — two reasoning steps plus a misleading clue or tempting near-miss option; the correct answer is defensible at oral boards; at least one wrong answer is what a competent but hurried surgeon might pick. Shape: connect a postoperative finding to a complication, then choose stabilization and referral over premature repair.

**Very Hard** — at least three reasoning steps; fair diagnostic ambiguity or misleading reassurance; tests sequencing (resuscitation, diagnosis, source control, escalation) and what not to do. Shape: identify a sentinel bleed, connect it to fistula-related arterial erosion, reject false reassurance from a negative CTA, then choose angiography and ICU-level control.

## Course Voice
Strange, crisp, and memorable.
- Priya is exacting, brilliant, clinically serious, and funny.
- Vary the silliness: courtroom metaphors, noir suspects, tiny bureaucrats, cursed theatre, melodramatic organs, treacherous drains, smug vessels, chaotic interns, suspicious noodles, haunted checklists.
- The joke must never obscure the decision. Avoid gratuitous gore — clinical urgency is enough.
- Do not make wrong answers purely silly; at least two should be plausible near-misses.
- Do not make the correct answer the longest, most detailed, or only sober option. At least one wrong answer should be the longest and should read like a tempting but flawed expert plan.

## Assessment Design Rules
Every item should include: a named surgical domain; a difficulty tag (medium/hard/very hard); a clear decision hinge; at least two reasoning steps (three for very hard); one clearly correct answer; three plausible wrong answers with at least two detailed near-misses; at least one wrong answer longer than the correct one; for hard/very hard, one "almost correct" distractor that would become correct if a single stated fact changed; at least one sequence-only trap where the ingredients are reasonable but the order is unsafe; for very hard, a collision of at least two domains (e.g., oncology + vascular anatomy, cancer + ICU physiology, colorectal cancer + anticoagulation, HPB cancer + liver-remnant physiology); some missing-data traps where the safest step is to obtain a specific fact first; a teaching note explaining the principle; and enough facts to solve the case without hidden assumptions.

Reject an item if: a medical student could answer from one fact; the correct answer is only "call senior help" without naming the problem; the prompt is long but not difficult; wrong answers are silly rather than plausible; the correct answer is signposted by length or sobriety; no wrong answer is longer than the correct one; every distractor is obviously inferior at a glance; a very-hard item tests one domain or one-step recognition; the scenario hinges on a controversial guideline detail without enough context; or the "correct" answer would be unsafe in real practice.

## Coverage Goals
Across a mature 100-item version, aim for approximate balance:
- HPB and biliary/pancreatic complications: 20–25%
- Colorectal leak, pelvic sepsis, and colorectal/rectal oncologic judgment: 15–20%
- Vascular, trauma, and damage control: 15–20%
- Endocrine/head-and-neck/airway traps: 10–15%
- Surgical critical care and postoperative collapse: 10–15%
- Anticoagulation/coagulopathy tradeoffs across domains: 10–15%
- Broader surgical oncology and MDT sequencing: 10–15%

One item may count toward several domains, but the quiz must not collapse into only-HPB or only-sepsis.

## Capstone
A consultant-level oral-board gauntlet: a linked sequence of cases threading operative anatomy, postoperative collapse, anticoagulation, oncology, and false reassurance. At each stage the learner must state three things explicitly — the unsafe assumption hiding in the case, the next safest action, and the principle that generalizes. The gauntlet should escalate: a hostile cholecystectomy with an unproven pulsatile vessel; the same patient returning with bile leak and suspected right-liver ischemia; a Whipple neighbour with a pancreatic fistula and a small sentinel bleed who is also on anticoagulation for a mechanical valve; and a final oncologic-sequencing dilemma where the technically impressive operation is the wrong one. Passing requires never being lured by transient stability, quiet drains, preserved pulses, normal saturations, or a single negative scan.

Use as syllabus anchors, not as exact prompt text.

- Whipple patient with pancreatic fistula and a small sentinel bleed.
- Low anterior resection with pelvic pain, low drain output, and a weak CT protocol.
- Ruptured AAA repair followed by abdominal compartment syndrome.
- Open-book pelvic fracture in an anticoagulated patient with a Zone III hematoma.
- Mechanical mitral valve plus perforated diverticulitis needing urgent source control.
- Thyroidectomy with recurrent laryngeal nerve signal loss on the first side.
- Post-thyroidectomy neck hematoma with normal saturation but evolving airway symptoms.
- Septic postoperative patient on vasopressors with pain out of proportion and possible mesenteric ischemia.
- T4b colon cancer where en bloc multivisceral resection collides with a marginal future plan.
- Perihilar cholangiocarcinoma where the question is what remains, not what leaves.
