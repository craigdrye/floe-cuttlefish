# Priya's Brutal Surgeon's Quiz
**ID**: `priyasBrutalSurgeonsQuiz` · **Discipline**: Healthcare / Surgery · **Level**: Expert consultant, department lead, surgical educator

## Course Philosophy
This syllabus is for an intentionally brutal expert surgical quiz. It is not medical-school recall, residency trivia, or generic clinical reasoning. The course tests whether a senior surgeon can recognize dangerous operative anatomy, misleading postoperative signals, and management tradeoffs when the easy-looking answer is unsafe.

The ideal question feels like a hostile oral-board case, a morbidity-and-mortality trap, or a complex consultant phone call at 2:17 a.m. The answer should require disciplined prioritization: what is killing the patient, what anatomy is not yet proven, what test is falsely reassuring, what source is uncontrolled, what complication must be assumed until excluded, and what action is safest now.

## Design Critique Incorporated
- The generic `medical` syllabus was too broad and too junior; it covered triage, differential diagnosis, and safety in a way suitable for general clinical reasoning, not an expert surgical gauntlet.
- The course needs specialty-specific surgical domains rather than generic healthcare topics.
- Difficulty must come from multi-step reasoning, not longer wording or obscure vocabulary.
- Silly tone should make cases memorable, but the clinical decision must remain crisp.
- The syllabus must guide generation toward consultant-level traps: false-negative imaging, quiet drains, preserved pulses, transient stability, incomplete anatomy, anticoagulation tradeoffs, and source-control failures.

## Difficulty Standard
Questions should be written for adult experts, roughly 35+, who may already be attending surgeons, senior fellows, department-leading consultants, or surgical educators.

### Medium
- Requires at least two reasoning steps.
- Usually tests a known safety principle in a specific scenario.
- Wrong answers include at least one plausible near-miss.
- Example reasoning shape: recognize unsafe anatomy, then choose bailout rather than continued dissection.

### Hard
- Requires at least two reasoning steps plus a misleading clue or tempting near-miss management option.
- The correct answer should be defensible in an oral-board setting.
- Wrong answers should include options that a competent but hurried surgeon might choose.
- Example reasoning shape: connect postoperative finding to a complication, then choose stabilization and referral rather than premature repair.

### Very Hard
- Requires at least three separate reasoning steps.
- Must include fair diagnostic ambiguity or misleading reassurance.
- Should test sequencing: resuscitation, diagnosis, source control, specialist escalation, and what not to do.
- Example reasoning shape: identify a sentinel bleed, connect it to fistula-related arterial erosion, reject false reassurance from negative CTA, then choose angiography and ICU-level control.

## Module 1: Hostile Operative Anatomy
Focus on anatomy that punishes overconfidence.

- Critical view failure in cholecystectomy
- Infundibular trap and false duct identification
- Rouviere's sulcus, R4U line, cystic plate, and safe-plane discipline
- Moynihan hump, replaced right hepatic artery, accessory hepatic arteries, and vasculobiliary injury risk
- Berry's ligament and recurrent laryngeal nerve danger zones
- External branch of superior laryngeal nerve during superior pole thyroid dissection
- Parotid facial nerve landmarks and false nerve mimics
- Obturator fossa anatomy: obturator nerve, vessels, lymphatics, and traction hazards
- Pelvic autonomic nerves, ureter, lateral ligament, and internal iliac branches
- Retroperitoneal vascular zones and what not to open
- SMA, SMV, portal vein, uncinate process, and pancreaticoduodenal branch hazards

Question targets:
- Identify when anatomy is not proven.
- Choose bailout rather than heroic dissection.
- Recognize named danger zones from partial clues.
- Reject blind clipping, blind clamping, broad energy use, and traction-as-navigation.

## Module 2: Source Control and Postoperative Sepsis
Focus on the fact that antibiotics are not plumbing.

- Contained anastomotic leak after colorectal surgery
- Low pelvic leaks with false-negative or incomplete CT
- Drains that are quiet because they are blocked, misplaced, or not communicating
- Abscesses, infected collections, and gas-fluid signals
- Source control scaling: antibiotics, percutaneous drainage, transanal drainage, operative washout, diversion, or takedown
- Septic physiology: lactate, oliguria, confusion, tachycardia, leukopenia, vasopressor need
- Steroids, immunosuppression, early postoperative state, and absent fever as false reassurance
- When repeat imaging is delay rather than prudence
- When a stable patient can be drained and when instability demands operation

Question targets:
- Make physiology override weak negative imaging.
- Distinguish drain management from source control.
- Require escalation before collapse.
- Test whether the learner can identify the dangerous miss in a crowded postoperative picture.

## Module 3: HPB Catastrophes
Focus on high-consequence hepatobiliary and pancreatic traps.

- Vasculobiliary injury after difficult cholecystectomy
- Bile leak localization: cystic duct stump, duct of Luschka, lateral common hepatic duct injury, major duct transection
- Right hepatic artery injury with sectoral ischemia
- When ERCP is helpful and when it is inadequate
- Biloma drainage, cholangiography, MRCP, CTA, and transfer to HPB expertise
- Post-pancreatectomy pancreatic fistula and clinically relevant leak
- Delayed post-pancreatectomy hemorrhage and sentinel bleeding
- GDA stump erosion, hepatic arterial pseudoaneurysm, covered stent vs embolization decisions
- Post-Whipple collections, fistula sepsis, and source-control sequencing
- Perihilar cholangiocarcinoma: future liver remnant, drainage strategy, portal vein embolization, and resectability

Question targets:
- Treat small sentinel bleeding as dangerous.
- Avoid premature local bile duct repair without anatomy and expertise.
- Integrate bile leak, hepatic ischemia, sepsis, and vascular injury.
- Reject false reassurance from transient stability or negative single-phase imaging.

## Module 4: Vascular, Trauma, and Damage Control
Focus on sequencing under hemorrhage and physiology failure.

- Proximal vascular control before opening central hematomas
- Zone I, II, and III retroperitoneal hematoma logic
- Pelvic fracture hemorrhage: binder, external fixation, preperitoneal packing, IR embolization
- Anticoagulated trauma: factor Xa inhibitors, warfarin, platelets, fibrinogen, calcium, temperature, acidosis
- Damage control laparotomy: pack, control contamination, temporary closure, planned return
- Open abdomen, abdominal compartment syndrome, bladder pressure, ventilator pressures, oliguria, and lactate
- Vascular repair after limb ischemia and reperfusion
- Compartment syndrome despite pulses
- Mesenteric ischemia in low-flow, septic, vasopressor, or acute-on-chronic vascular disease states
- Massive PE after surgery: reperfusion choices under bleeding risk

Question targets:
- Recognize when physiology chooses damage control.
- Reject definitive reconstruction in exhausted patients.
- Know when not to open a hematoma.
- Balance hemorrhage, thrombosis, perfusion, and source control.

## Module 5: Anticoagulation, Coagulopathy, and Competing Catastrophes
Focus on situations where every option can hurt the patient.

- Mechanical mitral valve and emergency abdominal sepsis
- Recent PE with suspected anastomotic leak
- Atrial fibrillation anticoagulation after sentinel bleeding
- Warfarin reversal with PCC and vitamin K
- DOAC reversal principles and institutional protocols
- LMWH and neuraxial/epidural timing conflicts
- Diffuse coagulopathic bleeding vs surgical bleeder
- Fibrinogen depletion, hypocalcemia, hypothermia, acidosis, platelet dysfunction
- Restart strategy: short-acting titratable anticoagulation after hemostasis
- When prophylactic anticoagulation is inadequate and when therapeutic anticoagulation is dangerous

Question targets:
- Require two-phase plans: survive now, prevent thrombosis next.
- Avoid all-or-nothing thinking.
- Make learners state what is reversible, what is time-critical, and what can wait.

## Module 6: Endocrine, Head-and-Neck, and Airway Traps
Focus on small spaces where delay becomes catastrophe.

- Loss of recurrent laryngeal nerve signal and staged thyroidectomy decisions
- Bilateral vocal fold paralysis risk
- Post-thyroidectomy neck hematoma and threatened airway
- Why normal oxygen saturation can be falsely reassuring in evolving airway compression
- Superior pole dissection and external branch of superior laryngeal nerve
- Parathyroid preservation and postoperative hypocalcemia risk
- Compartment-oriented neck dissection vs berry-picking nodes
- Parotidectomy: facial nerve trunk landmarks, retromandibular vein, duct, and branch preservation
- Head-and-neck oncologic margins, nodal basins, and reconstructive consequences

Question targets:
- Make airway and nerve function override cosmetic or completion bias.
- Test when to stop, stage, decompress, or call for immediate help.
- Reject visually intact but functionally unsafe assumptions.

## Module 7: Oncologic Surgical Judgment
Focus on operations where the margin, lymph basin, and sequencing matter more than speed.

### Cross-Cutting Cancer Principles
- Establish tissue diagnosis, clinical stage, biology, resectability, and patient fitness before committing to an operation
- Distinguish technically removable disease from oncologically sensible disease
- Know when neoadjuvant therapy improves margin, nodal, sphincter, organ-preservation, or systemic-control outcomes
- Respect tumor planes: avoid capsular rupture, tumor violation, piecemeal excision, shaving malignant adhesions, and contaminated margins
- Understand R0, R1, and R2 implications, circumferential/radial margins, distal/proximal margins, and when margin width changes management
- Separate diagnostic excision, definitive oncologic resection, cytoreduction, palliation, bypass, diversion, and damage-control surgery
- Make nodal strategy anatomical and disease-specific rather than casual node-picking
- Recognize when an operation should be delayed for MDT planning, nutrition/prehabilitation, biliary drainage, embolization, genetic testing, or systemic therapy
- Avoid making survival worse by performing a heroic operation in occult metastatic, biologically explosive, or physiologically impossible disease
- Balance oncologic purity against functional outcomes: voice, swallowing, continence, sexual/urinary function, limb function, liver remnant, pancreatic fistula risk, and quality of life

### Colorectal and Anal Cancer
- T4b colon cancer and en bloc multivisceral resection
- Locally advanced colon cancer: en bloc resection vs dangerous peeling from abdominal wall, duodenum, bladder, uterus, ileum, or retroperitoneum
- Obstructing left-sided colon cancer: bridge-to-surgery stent, diverting stoma, emergency resection, or damage-control strategy in sepsis/perforation
- Synchronous liver metastases: staged, liver-first, colon-first, combined resection, conversion chemotherapy, and when not to chase everything at once
- Peritoneal metastases: cytoreductive surgery and HIPEC selection vs palliative systemic therapy
- Rectal cancer MRI risk features: threatened CRM, EMVI, lateral pelvic nodes, low tumor level
- Total neoadjuvant therapy and MDT sequencing
- Low rectal cancer tradeoffs: sphincter preservation vs oncologic margin, watch-and-wait after complete clinical response, APR, intersphincteric resection, and pelvic sidewall disease
- Anal squamous cancer: chemoradiation-first logic and salvage APR selection
- Hereditary risk: Lynch syndrome, FAP, synchronous lesions, and extent of colectomy decisions

### HPB, Pancreatic, and Upper GI Cancer
- Compartment-oriented nodal dissection in thyroid cancer
- Perihilar cholangiocarcinoma resectability: what remains matters as much as what leaves
- Perihilar cholangiocarcinoma: Bismuth pattern, vascular involvement, caudate resection, future liver remnant drainage, portal vein embolization, cholangitis control, and transplant-protocol exceptions
- Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma: resectable vs borderline vs locally advanced disease, venous reconstruction, arterial encasement, neoadjuvant strategy, CA19-9 interpretation, and occult metastatic risk
- Distal cholangiocarcinoma, ampullary cancer, and pancreatic head cancer: when Whipple is appropriate and when physiology, biology, or metastatic disease should stop the operation
- Hepatocellular carcinoma: portal hypertension, liver function, transplant criteria, resection vs ablation vs embolization, and why removing tumor from a failing liver can be a trap
- Colorectal liver metastases: future liver remnant, two-stage hepatectomy, portal vein embolization, ablation adjuncts, chemotherapy-associated liver injury, and disappearing liver metastases
- Gastric and GE junction cancers: staging laparoscopy, peritoneal cytology, neoadjuvant therapy, D2 lymphadenectomy, and margin strategy
- Esophageal cancer: endoscopic therapy for early lesions, neoadjuvant chemoradiation/chemotherapy, conduit planning, anastomotic level, and salvage operation hazards

### Endocrine, Head-and-Neck, Breast, Skin, and Sarcoma
- Thyroid cancer: lobectomy vs total thyroidectomy, central/lateral neck compartments, recurrent laryngeal nerve invasion, radioactive iodine implications, and when berry-picking nodes is wrong
- Medullary thyroid carcinoma: calcitonin/CEA, RET testing, pheochromocytoma exclusion, compartmental dissection, and family screening logic
- Parathyroid carcinoma suspicion: en bloc resection and avoiding capsular rupture
- Head-and-neck squamous cancer: airway, nutrition, margin, nodal basin, carotid involvement, and salvage after radiation
- Breast cancer: breast conservation vs mastectomy, sentinel node vs axillary dissection, neoadjuvant therapy for downstaging, inflammatory breast cancer, and margin/re-excision logic
- Sarcoma and melanoma margin logic where appropriate
- Soft-tissue sarcoma: biopsy tract planning, compartment anatomy, limb-sparing surgery, radiation sequencing, and avoiding unplanned excision
- Melanoma: Breslow depth, ulceration, sentinel node biopsy, wide local excision margin, in-transit disease, immunotherapy sequencing, and nodal basin management

### Metastatic, Recurrent, and Palliative Cancer Surgery
- Oligometastatic disease where local therapy may matter vs widespread disease where surgery only adds harm
- Recurrent pelvic cancer: exenteration selection, sidewall involvement, ureteric/vascular reconstruction, and functional devastation
- Malignant bowel obstruction: resection, bypass, stoma, venting gastrostomy, systemic therapy, or comfort-focused care
- Bleeding, perforated, or obstructing tumors: emergency control without losing sight of stage and biology
- Cytoreduction: ovarian/peritoneal disease selection, completeness of cytoreduction, and when heroic debulking is futile
- Palliative operations: define the symptom target and reject operations that satisfy the surgeon more than the patient

### Cancer-Specific Diagnostic Traps
- Radiographic resectability does not equal biological resectability
- A negative staging scan does not exclude peritoneal disease in high-risk gastric, pancreatic, or ovarian-pattern cancers
- A small liver remnant can make an R0 resection fatal
- A single biopsied node is not permission for nodal berry-picking
- Tumor stuck to an organ is malignant until proven otherwise in the operating room
- A low rectal tumor's distal margin is not the only margin that matters; CRM can be the real trap
- Excellent response to neoadjuvant therapy can create both organ-preservation opportunities and false confidence
- Emergency obstruction can distract from hereditary risk, synchronous disease, and metastatic staging

### Cancer Question Targets
- Force the learner to choose sequencing: biopsy, staging, neoadjuvant therapy, drainage, embolization, resection, palliation, or referral
- Make the decision hinge involve margin, biology, nodal basin, remnant organ function, or metastatic pattern
- Include near-miss wrong answers that are technically possible but oncologically poor
- Test MDT reasoning, not solo-surgeon swagger
- Make the learner reject operations that are impressive but wrong for the disease stage or patient physiology

Existing anchor topics:
- T4b colon cancer and en bloc multivisceral resection
- Rectal cancer MRI risk features: threatened CRM, EMVI, lateral pelvic nodes, low tumor level
- Total neoadjuvant therapy and MDT sequencing
- Compartment-oriented nodal dissection in thyroid cancer
- Perihilar cholangiocarcinoma resectability: what remains matters as much as what leaves
- Sarcoma and melanoma margin logic where appropriate
- Avoiding tumor violation, shaving malignant adhesions, and inadequate local excision
- When local control requires specialist referral or multidisciplinary planning

Question targets:
- Reject shortcut resections.
- Distinguish technical resectability from oncologic strategy.
- Make staging, margins, and nodal strategy drive the answer.

## Module 8: Diagnostic Traps and False Reassurance
These traps should appear repeatedly across modules.

- Normal early swallow study after bariatric surgery does not exclude leak
- IV-only CT does not exclude low pelvic leak
- Low drain output does not exclude abscess or leak
- Negative CTA does not exclude intermittent sentinel hemorrhage
- Preserved pulses do not exclude compartment syndrome
- Lack of fever does not exclude sepsis
- Soft abdomen does not exclude mesenteric ischemia
- Stable hemoglobin does not exclude evolving vascular catastrophe
- High CVP does not prove volume overload in abdominal compartment syndrome
- Good-looking anatomy does not prove critical view
- Visual nerve continuity does not prove nerve function

Question targets:
- Force the learner to name the false reassurance.
- Make the correct answer depend on the misleading clue being interpreted correctly.
- Include overconfident junior interpretations that are plausible but dangerous.

## Style and Tone
The course voice should be strange, crisp, and memorable.

- Priya is exacting, brilliant, clinically serious, and funny.
- Silliness should vary: courtroom metaphors, noir suspects, tiny bureaucrats, cursed theatre, melodramatic organs, treacherous drains, smug vessels, chaotic interns, suspicious noodles, haunted checklists.
- Goblins are allowed but should not dominate.
- The joke must never obscure the decision.
- Avoid gratuitous gore. Clinical urgency is enough.
- Do not make wrong answers purely silly; at least two wrong answers should usually be plausible near-misses.
- Do not make the correct answer obviously longer, more detailed, or more sober than the distractors.
- At least one wrong answer should be the longest answer option, and it should sound like a tempting but flawed expert plan.

## Question Construction Rules
Every new question should include:

- A named surgical domain
- A difficulty level: medium, hard, or very hard
- A decision hinge
- At least two reasoning steps; at least three for very hard
- One clearly correct answer
- Three plausible wrong answers
- At least two detailed near-miss wrong answers
- At least one wrong answer longer than the correct answer
- For hard and very hard questions, one "almost correct" distractor that would become correct if one key fact in the prompt changed
- At least one sequence-only trap, where the answer ingredients are reasonable but the order is unsafe or oncologically wrong
- For very hard questions, a collision between at least two domains, such as oncology plus vascular anatomy, cancer plus ICU physiology, endocrine cancer plus airway risk, colorectal cancer plus anticoagulation, or HPB cancer plus liver-remnant physiology
- At least some missing-data traps where the safest next step is to obtain a specific staging, anatomy, physiology, pathology, or organ-reserve fact before treatment
- A teaching note or hint that explains the core principle
- Enough facts to solve the case without hidden assumptions

Reject questions if:

- A medical student could answer from one remembered fact.
- The correct answer is only “call senior help” without saying what problem is being escalated.
- The prompt is long but not actually difficult.
- The wrong answers are obviously silly rather than plausible.
- The correct answer is clearly signposted by being the longest, most detailed, or only serious answer.
- No wrong answer is longer than the correct answer.
- The wrong answers are all obviously inferior after one glance.
- No distractor would tempt a strong surgeon under pressure.
- A very hard question tests only one domain or one-step recognition.
- The scenario depends on a controversial guideline detail without giving enough context.
- The answer would be unsafe in real surgical practice.

## Representative Question Seeds
Use these as syllabus anchors, not as exact prompt text.

- Hostile cholecystectomy with large pulsatile vessel and no critical view
- Post-cholecystectomy bile leak plus right liver ischemia
- Whipple patient with pancreatic fistula and small sentinel bleed
- Low anterior resection with pelvic pain, low drain output, and weak CT protocol
- Ruptured AAA repair followed by abdominal compartment syndrome
- Open-book pelvic fracture in an anticoagulated patient with Zone III hematoma
- Mechanical mitral valve plus perforated diverticulitis needing urgent source control
- Thyroidectomy with recurrent laryngeal nerve signal loss on the first side
- Post-thyroidectomy neck hematoma with normal oxygen saturation but evolving airway symptoms
- Septic postoperative patient on vasopressors with pain out of proportion and possible mesenteric ischemia

## Coverage Goals
Over a mature 100-question version of the course, aim for approximate balance:

- HPB and biliary/pancreatic complications: 20-25%
- Colorectal leak, pelvic sepsis, and oncologic rectal/colon judgment: 15-20%
- Vascular, trauma, and damage control: 15-20%
- Endocrine/head-and-neck/airway traps: 10-15%
- Surgical critical care and postoperative collapse: 10-15%
- Anticoagulation/coagulopathy tradeoffs across domains: 10-15%
- Surgical oncology and MDT sequencing: 5-10%

The same question can count toward multiple domains, but the quiz should not become only HPB or only goblin-flavored postoperative sepsis.
