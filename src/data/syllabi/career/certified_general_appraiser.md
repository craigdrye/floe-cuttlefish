# Certified General Appraiser
**ID**: `certifiedGeneralAppraiser` - **Discipline**: Real Estate - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
The Certified General Real Property Appraiser credential is the top rung of the appraisal ladder: it authorizes you to value any property type, of any complexity, at any value — the office tower, the agricultural operation, the hotel, the half-built subdivision, the contaminated industrial site. That breadth is exactly why the work is hard. A residential appraiser can lean on a deep well of comparable home sales; a general appraiser routinely faces a property where the only honest answer comes from building income models, reconciling thin sales data, and defending judgment calls in writing to a lender, a court, or a peer reviewer. This course trains that judgment, not just the formulas.

Two things govern everything you do. The first is the discipline of the assignment: who needs the value, for what decision, as of what date, on what interest, and under what conditions. Get that wrong and a flawless calculation answers the wrong question. The second is USPAP — the Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice — which sets the ethics, competency, scope-of-work, and reporting rules that make a value opinion *credible* rather than merely a number. We treat USPAP not as a memorization chore but as the operating system the rest of the work runs on, and we keep returning to it: every approach, every adjustment, every report decision has a standards dimension.

You will finish able to set up a commercial assignment correctly, run the sales comparison, income, and cost approaches with the math automatic and the assumptions defensible, reconcile three indications into one supportable opinion, and write a report a reviewer can follow without phoning you. That is the standard the credential exam measures, and it is the standard a working file has to survive when someone disputes the number.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test USPAP-style professional judgment, assignment conditions, property rights, highest and best use, commercial market and feasibility analysis, the three approaches to value (sales comparison, income capitalization, cost), cap rates, DCF, depreciation and obsolescence, adjustment logic, reconciliation, and appraisal reporting. Keep scenarios grounded in commercial, industrial, agricultural, and special-purpose property — not generic single-family house-price guessing, which belongs to the residential classifications below this one.

This syllabus mirrors the body of knowledge behind the AQB-developed National Uniform Licensing and Certification Examination for the Certified General classification, organized into six chapters that move from assignment setup and standards, through the three approaches, to reconciliation and reporting. The exam is closed-book and includes a working knowledge of math and statistics (compound interest, mortgage-equity, regression awareness, units of comparison), so chapters carry the relevant quantitative skills rather than hiding them. USPAP requirements appear throughout because the real exam — and real practice — never separates technique from standards.

**On the standards edition:** USPAP is now maintained as a permanent, continuously updated edition rather than the old biennial cycle, and your state may have an effective-date lag. Learn the *concepts and structure* (the Ethics, Competency, Scope of Work, and Record Keeping rules, and Standards 1 and 2 for real-property development and reporting) rather than page numbers, and always confirm the edition in force for your jurisdiction and exam date. Every chapter includes **Common traps** drawn from how candidates and working appraisers actually lose points: answering the wrong assignment, double-counting adjustments, mismatching cap-rate to income, and writing conclusions the file does not support.

**Suggested study rhythm:** spend the first third getting assignment setup and USPAP automatic, the middle third drilling the three approaches until the math needs no scratch paper, and the final third on highest and best use, reconciliation, and report critique — because those integrative skills are where strong candidates separate from passers. Do at least one full mixed-approach problem each week so the approaches stay connected rather than siloed.

## Chapter 1: Assignment Setup and Professional Standards
- **Core questions:** What exactly is being valued, for whom, for what use, as of what date, and under what conditions? What does USPAP require before you may proceed, and what makes the result *credible*?
- **Key concepts:** the problem-identification elements — client and intended users, intended use, type and definition of value (market value, investment value, use value), effective date, relevant property characteristics, and assignment conditions; scope of work and what makes it acceptable; the USPAP Ethics, Competency, Scope of Work, and Record Keeping rules; extraordinary assumptions versus hypothetical conditions; jurisdictional exception and limiting conditions; the workfile and what must be retained.
- **Applied skills:** Draft an assignment-definition checklist that pins down every problem element; classify a given condition as an extraordinary assumption versus a hypothetical condition and state the reporting consequence; write a competency memo for an unfamiliar property type that says how you will become competent; decide an acceptable scope of work for a stated intended use.
- **Common traps:** Confusing market value with investment or use value and answering the wrong question. Calling something a hypothetical condition when it is an extraordinary assumption (the test is whether the premise is *contrary to fact* now versus *uncertain* and assumed). Accepting an assignment outside your competency without disclosure and a plan to gain it. Treating the workfile as optional — in a dispute, the file is the proof.

## Chapter 2: Property Rights and Market Analysis
- **Core questions:** Which bundle of rights is being appraised, and what would real market participants actually pay attention to? What is the highest and best use, and how does it drive everything downstream?
- **Key concepts:** fee simple, leased fee, leasehold, life estates, easements, deed restrictions, and partial/fractional interests; the four agents of production and the economic principles (anticipation, substitution, supply and demand, contribution, balance, conformity, externalities); market-area and neighborhood analysis; supply and demand indicators — vacancy, absorption, new supply, rent trends, exposure time, and marketing period; the four tests of highest and best use (legally permissible, physically possible, financially feasible, maximally productive) applied both *as vacant* and *as improved*; interim use and the concept of an excess versus surplus land.
- **Applied skills:** Build a property-rights comparison table and identify which interest the assignment requires. Run the four-test highest-and-best-use screen for a site as vacant and the same site as improved, and explain when demolition is indicated. Assemble a market snapshot with vacancy, rent, absorption, and competing supply, and state what it implies for value.
- **Common traps:** Appraising fee simple when the assignment is the leased fee (or vice versa) and ignoring the lease's effect on value. Skipping the *as vacant* highest-and-best-use test and so missing that the existing improvement is not the optimal use. Confusing exposure time (retrospective, before the effective date) with marketing period (prospective). Treating excess land (separately marketable) and surplus land (not) the same way.

## Chapter 3: Sales Comparison Approach
- **Core questions:** Which sales are genuinely comparable, what needs adjusting and in which direction, and which adjusted indications deserve the most weight?
- **Key concepts:** comparable selection and verification; the standard sequence of adjustments — property rights conveyed, financing terms, conditions of sale, expenditures immediately after purchase, market conditions (time), then physical and locational elements; the difference between sequential (the order matters) and additive adjustments; units of comparison (price per square foot, per unit, per acre, per buildable area); paired-sales analysis, grouped-data and qualitative ranking, bracketing, and reconciliation of the adjusted set; data-source reliability and the limits of unverified online listings.
- **Applied skills:** Build a sales grid with at least five adjustment categories applied in the correct sequence and signed in the correct direction. Run a paired-sales mini-lab to extract a single adjustment. Bracket the subject between superior and inferior comps and write the reconciliation paragraph that explains why one sale carries the most weight.
- **Common traps:** Applying market-conditions (time) and other adjustments in the wrong order so the percentages compound incorrectly. Getting adjustment *direction* backwards — a comparable superior to the subject adjusts *down*. Over-adjusting a poor comp instead of giving more weight to a better-bracketed one. Treating an unverified asking price or auto-valuation as a verified sale.

## Chapter 4: Income Approach
- **Core questions:** What income belongs to the interest being appraised, what expenses are truly the property's, and how does that income become value at a rate the market supports?
- **Key concepts:** potential gross income, vacancy and collection loss, other income, effective gross income, operating expenses (fixed, variable, reserves for replacement), and net operating income — and what is *excluded* from NOI (debt service, depreciation, capital items, income tax); contract rent versus market rent and the value impact of below- or above-market leases; direct capitalization and the IRV relationship (Value = Income / Rate); overall cap rate extraction from comparable sales and band-of-investment/mortgage-equity derivation; yield capitalization and discounted cash flow with explicit growth, reversion, and terminal cap rate; lease structures (gross, net, NNN), reimbursements, concessions, and expense normalization.
- **Applied skills:** Build an NOI worksheet from a rent roll and operating statement, normalizing owner-stated expenses to market. Extract an overall cap rate from comparable sales and reconcile it with a band-of-investment estimate. Construct a multi-year DCF with a reversion and run a sensitivity table over rent growth, vacancy, discount rate, and terminal cap rate.
- **Common traps:** Putting debt service, capital expenditures, depreciation, or income tax into operating expenses and crushing NOI. Capitalizing contract rent when the assignment calls for market rent (or failing to value the leasehold/leased-fee difference a below-market lease creates). Mismatching the rate to the income — applying an overall cap rate to pre-vacancy or pre-expense income. Picking a terminal cap rate equal to the going-in rate without a reason, or letting tiny rate changes swing value without acknowledging the sensitivity.

## Chapter 5: Cost Approach and Depreciation
- **Core questions:** When does cost actually help, and how do the three forms of depreciation reduce a new-building cost to a supportable value?
- **Key concepts:** the cost-approach formula (land value + cost new − depreciation + entrepreneurial incentive/profit); reproduction cost versus replacement cost new and why the choice changes which obsolescence you must measure; the three forms of depreciation — physical deterioration (curable and incurable), functional obsolescence (curable, and incurable from a deficiency or a superadequacy), and external/economic obsolescence (locational or market-wide); age-life and modified age-life methods, the breakdown method, and cost-to-cure; deriving land value separately (sales comparison, extraction, allocation, ground-rent capitalization).
- **Applied skills:** Build a cost-approach packet that values land separately, estimates cost new, and subtracts each form of depreciation. Classify a list of building problems into physical, functional, and external obsolescence and decide which are curable. Estimate external obsolescence by paired sales or capitalized rent loss and allocate it correctly.
- **Common traps:** Double-counting depreciation — measuring the same loss as both functional and physical, or once in the cost approach and again in a market-derived land value. Using replacement cost but then deducting for a functional issue the replacement design already eliminated. Leaning on the cost approach for an old property where depreciation is large and poorly supported (it is strongest for new or special-purpose property). Forgetting entrepreneurial incentive where the market would require it.

## Chapter 6: Reconciliation and Reporting
- **Core questions:** How does the appraiser turn three value indications into one defensible opinion, and how does the report prove that the opinion is credible?
- **Key concepts:** reconciliation as a weighing of approach *relevance, quantity, and quality of evidence* — not an averaging; consistency checks across approaches (the same highest and best use, the same property rights, the same market story); reasonable exposure-time and value-range judgments; USPAP Standard 2 reporting — the required elements of an appraisal report, the Appraisal Report option, certification, assumptions and limiting conditions, and the prohibition on misleading reports; the reviewer's trail and what makes a file reproducible; common review findings — unsupported adjustments, unclear property rights, weak market support, math errors, and conclusions the analysis does not back.
- **Applied skills:** Write a reconciliation memo that weights three indications by evidence quality and explains the choice rather than averaging. Run a consistency check that catches an approach contradicting the stated highest and best use. Assemble the required reporting elements and a reviewer checklist, and produce an error log sorted by standards, property rights, math, market evidence, and reporting support.
- **Common traps:** Averaging the three approaches instead of reconciling by relevance and reliability. Letting the approaches disagree on highest and best use or property rights and not noticing. Burying an unsupported adjustment or assumption that a reviewer will pull first. Writing a report that omits a required element or quietly leans on an undisclosed extraordinary assumption — which can make the whole report misleading under USPAP.

## Capstone
Produce a full commercial appraisal file for a fictional property complex enough to demand all three approaches — for example, a mixed-use building with ground-floor retail and upper-floor offices on a site whose highest and best use is arguably *not* the current improvement. You receive a client and intended use, an effective date, a rent roll with one clearly below-market lease, an owner-provided operating statement that mixes in capital and debt items, a set of five raw comparable sales with mismatched units and dates, land sales for the site, and a cost manual extract.

Deliver: (1) a complete assignment definition with the property interest, value type, conditions, and any extraordinary assumption or hypothetical condition stated correctly; (2) a highest-and-best-use analysis as vacant and as improved with a conclusion; (3) a sales comparison grid with adjustments in the correct sequence and direction, bracketing, and a reconciled indication; (4) an income approach with a normalized NOI, an extracted-and-reconciled cap rate, and a DCF with a sensitivity table — and an explicit treatment of the below-market lease's effect on the interest appraised; (5) a cost approach with land valued separately and all three forms of depreciation supported and not double-counted; (6) a final reconciliation that weights the three indications by evidence quality with a written rationale, not an average; and (7) a USPAP-compliant report skeleton with certification, assumptions and limiting conditions, and a one-page reviewer checklist showing where your file would survive challenge and where it is genuinely limited. The standard is the working standard: another appraiser should be able to pick up your file, follow it without calling you, and reach a value within a defensible range.

## Assessment Ideas
- Assignment-setup and USPAP drills graded on correct problem identification, the extraordinary-assumption-versus-hypothetical-condition call, and scope-of-work adequacy.
- Approach calculation sets (adjusted sales grid, NOI and cap-rate extraction, DCF, cost-approach with depreciation) graded on setup, units, correct adjustment/rate matching, and conclusion — not just the final number.
- Highest-and-best-use cases graded on the four tests applied as vacant and as improved, and on whether the conclusion is consistent with the rest of the analysis.
- Reconciliation and report critique exercises graded on weighting by evidence quality, internal consistency across approaches, and catching the planted unsupported adjustment or missing required reporting element.
- Capstone graded on assignment correctness, three-approach execution, defensible reconciliation, USPAP reporting compliance, and the honesty of the stated limitations.
