# CFP: Whole-Life Financial Planning
**ID**: `cfp` · **Discipline**: Finance

## Course Aim
The Certified Financial Planner exam is not a securities exam, not an insurance exam, not a tax exam — it is all three plus a fiduciary conversation, glued together by the financial planning process. The CFP Board tests whether you can sit across from a real person, listen to a messy life, and produce a coherent recommendation that respects regulation, taxes, insurance, investments, retirement, and estate transfer at the same time. Most candidates know the topics in isolation; the exam, and the work, demand integration.

The credential gates client-facing planning roles at RIAs, broker-dealer wirehouses, bank wealth platforms, and independent practices. Carrying the CFP marks tells a prospective client (and a compliance officer) that you have demonstrated competency across the eight Principal Knowledge Topics, you have passed the experience and ethics gates, and you are bound by the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct — including a **fiduciary duty at all times when providing financial advice**, which has been the Board's standard since the 2020 update. This is a higher bar than securities suitability and broader than the Investment Advisers Act fiduciary standard for investment advice.

This syllabus is built around the eight Principal Knowledge Topics plus the planning process and the psychology of financial planning. The goal is to leave you fluent in numbers and in plain English, equally comfortable explaining a Roth conversion, a 1035 exchange, a special-needs trust, and why your client's panicky 2 a.m. email needs a measured 9 a.m. reply. You will leave with a working planner's toolkit — the integration habits, the tax-and-estate vocabulary, the behavioral diagnosis skills, and the judgment to know when the right answer is "we should talk before deciding."

The voice of this track should match what is being tested: a senior planner reviewing junior work. Warm, fiduciary, allergic to product-pushing, rigorous about which assumption drives the recommendation, and direct when the client is making a mistake. The exam rewards "best of three reasonable" recommendations over "obviously correct" formulas — write distractors accordingly.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test the planning process, professional conduct and regulation, education planning, insurance and risk management, investment planning at the planner level, tax planning, retirement savings and income planning, estate planning, or behavioral and communication skills with clients. The voice should feel like a senior planner who reviews work: warm, fiduciary, allergic to product-pushing, and rigorous about which assumption drives the recommendation.

The signature CFP voice is **whole-life planner balancing tax, insurance, investments, estate, retirement, education, and behavioral finance**. Every question should resemble a client situation (one named couple or individual, 1-3 financial facts, then the question) rather than a definition. Distractors should be plausibly good — the right answer is the **best** of three defensible recommendations, not the only legal answer.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The CFP Board's current Principal Knowledge Topics weighting (effective for 2022+ exams) is below. The exam is **170 multiple-choice questions** delivered as two 3-hour sessions over two days, including standalone questions and **case-based item sets** (multiple questions tied to a single comprehensive client scenario).

| Topic | Exam Weight | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| Professional Conduct and Regulation | 8% | Memorize Code of Ethics and Standards; expect duty traps |
| General Principles of Financial Planning | 15% | Process, statements, debt, economics, communication |
| Psychology of Financial Planning | 7% | Behavioral biases, crisis events, client communication |
| Risk Management and Insurance Planning | 11% | Property, casualty, life, health, disability, LTC |
| Investment Planning | 17% | Risk, return, portfolio construction, taxes on investments |
| Tax Planning | 14% | Income, gain, AMT, business entities, charitable, basis |
| Retirement Savings and Income Planning | 18% | Qualified plans, IRAs, Social Security, distributions |
| Estate Planning | 10% | Wills, trusts, gifting, transfer taxes, special needs |

**Realistic prep:** 250-300 hours over 3-6 months for candidates with finance background; 350-450 hours for career-changers. The hardest part is **integration** — the case-based item sets pull simultaneously from tax, retirement, insurance, and estate. Topic-by-topic mastery is necessary but not sufficient.

**Sample 16-week plan (~270 hours):**
- **Weeks 1-2** (25h): Professional Conduct + General Principles + the seven-step planning process. Memorize the Code of Ethics duties.
- **Weeks 3-4** (35h): Investment Planning — MPT, CAPM, performance metrics, taxation of investments.
- **Weeks 5-6** (35h): Tax Planning — individual tax, basis, AMT, business entities, charitable.
- **Weeks 7-8** (35h): Retirement Savings & Income Planning — qualified plans, IRAs, Social Security, distribution rules, SECURE Act 2.0.
- **Week 9** (20h): Risk Management & Insurance Planning — life, disability, LTC, health, P&C.
- **Week 10** (15h): Estate Planning — wills, trusts, gifting, transfer taxes, basis at death.
- **Week 11** (10h): Education Planning + Psychology of Financial Planning.
- **Week 12** (20h): First full mock + review.
- **Weeks 13-14** (30h): **Case practice** — full multi-topic client cases (this is the integration step).
- **Week 15** (20h): Targeted weak-area work + second mock.
- **Week 16** (15h): Light review, third mock, Ethics re-read.

**Weekly cadence:** Mon-Wed reading and topic questions; Thu-Fri **case-style questions** (multi-topic); Sat full-topic timed block; Sun review error log. Most candidates fail because they study topics in isolation — case practice forces integration.

## Chapter 1: Professional Conduct and Regulation
**Core questions:** What duties does a CFP professional owe a client, and where do regulation, firm rules, and the Code of Ethics overlap?

**Key concepts**
- CFP Board Code of Ethics (six principles: integrity, objectivity, competence, fairness, confidentiality, professionalism, diligence) and Standards of Conduct
- **Fiduciary duty at all times** when providing **financial advice** to a client — broader than ERISA fiduciary or 1940 Act IA fiduciary
- Duties owed to clients (loyalty, care, follow client instructions), to firms (compliance with internal policies, attest to firm compliance with CFP Board rules), and to the profession
- Material conflicts of interest: identify, disclose, manage, obtain informed consent
- Regulatory environment: Investment Advisers Act of 1940, broker-dealer regulation, ERISA basics, state insurance regulation
- CFP Board disciplinary process: investigation, hearing, sanctions (private censure, public censure, suspension, revocation)

**Applied skills**
- Identify whether an engagement constitutes financial advice (broad — any recommendation that influences a financial decision) or financial planning (integrated, multi-domain, ongoing)
- Draft a clear conflict-of-interest disclosure that survives scrutiny
- Decide when a duty to escalate, recommend a specialist, or terminate an engagement is triggered

**Common traps**
- Believing the fiduciary duty applies only during a "comprehensive financial planning" engagement — it applies to **any** financial advice
- Believing a disclosed conflict is automatically cured — disclosure plus informed consent is required, and some conflicts must be eliminated
- Confusing the CFP Board sanction process with FINRA, SEC, or state insurance disciplinary processes — they are independent and can run in parallel
- Forgetting that the **stricter** standard binds when CFP Board rules and other regulation differ
- Treating "best interest" (Reg BI for brokers) as identical to CFP Board fiduciary — Reg BI is a transaction-level standard; CFP is ongoing

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board *Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct* (current edition); Dalton *Personal Financial Planning Foundations and Practice*; Keir *Education for Personal Financial Planning*.

## Chapter 2: General Principles and the Planning Process
**Core questions:** How does a planner move from "tell me about yourself" to a written, monitored plan?

**Key concepts**
- **Seven-step planning process**: (1) understand circumstances, (2) identify goals, (3) analyze, (4) develop recommendations, (5) present recommendations, (6) implement, (7) monitor and update
- Financial statements: personal balance sheet (use **fair market value** for assets, not cost basis), cash flow statement, net worth
- Cash management, budgeting, emergency reserve (typically 3-6 months expenses; longer for variable income), debt management (consumer, student, mortgage)
- Education planning: **529 plans** (federal income tax-free growth and distributions for qualified education expenses; recent SECURE 2.0 expansion allows $35K lifetime rollover to a Roth IRA for the beneficiary), Coverdell ESAs ($2K/yr limit), UTMA/UGMA (custodial, includes financial aid impact), prepaid tuition; federal aid impact (parental assets weighted at 5.64% in EFC, student assets at 20%)
- Economic concepts: time value of money (PV, FV, annuities, perpetuities), monetary and fiscal policy basics, business cycle, inflation
- Education tax credits: AOTC (40% refundable, up to $2,500/yr for first 4 years), LLC (nonrefundable, up to $2,000/yr, lifetime)

**Applied skills**
- Build a personal balance sheet and statement of cash flow from raw client data
- Compare 529, Coverdell, and taxable savings for a specific family situation (529 wins for tax-advantaged growth; UTMA gives the kid control at majority)
- Calculate required savings rates to fund overlapping goals using TVM

**Common traps**
- Listing assets at cost on the balance sheet — use FMV
- Forgetting that AOTC is **partially refundable** (up to 40%) and LLC is not refundable
- Recommending UTMA for college savings without warning about the kiddie tax (unearned income above ~$2,500 in 2024 taxed at parent rate) and the loss of control at majority
- Confusing 529 owner = parent (asset of parent, low aid impact) with 529 owner = grandparent (distribution counted as student income in older FAFSA rules — now relaxed under FAFSA Simplification)
- Forgetting the 529-to-Roth IRA rollover requires a 15-year-old account and is capped at $35K lifetime

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board topic list; Dalton chapters on planning process and education; Keir GP module.

## Chapter 3: Psychology of Financial Planning
**Core questions:** How do clients actually behave around money, and how does the planner adjust the plan without abandoning it?

**Key concepts**
- Behavioral biases: loss aversion (≈ 2× pain of gain), recency, anchoring, mental accounting, herding, status quo, overconfidence
- Money scripts (Klontz): money avoidance, money worship, money status, money vigilance
- Financial trauma, couples and family money dynamics, intergenerational transmission
- Crisis events: job loss, divorce, death, disability, sudden wealth syndrome
- Communication skills: active listening, motivational interviewing, open-ended vs closed-ended questions

**Applied skills**
- Re-frame a panicked client conversation without violating fiduciary duty (acknowledge → clarify → revisit IPS, not "just stay the course")
- Detect when a client's stated risk tolerance contradicts behavior (financial behavior questionnaires, prior reactions to drawdowns)
- Coach a couple with different money scripts (one money-vigilant saver, one money-status spender) toward a shared plan

**Common traps**
- Treating risk tolerance as a single number — willingness ≠ ability; assessing one without the other is incomplete
- Confronting a client's bias directly in a downturn — works in a textbook, backfires in real conversations; **re-anchor to goals and the IPS**, not market views
- Forgetting that **sudden wealth** clients often need a 6-12 month decision-pause buffer, not a complete plan in week 1
- Recommending "don't sell" without giving the client a partial action that reduces anxiety (rebalance into bonds, build cash buffer)

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board Psychology of Financial Planning Domain; Klontz *Money Scripts*; Dalton/Keir behavioral sections.

## Chapter 4: Risk Management and Insurance Planning
**Core questions:** Which risks does this household actually need to insure, and how is each policy priced and structured?

**Key concepts**
- Risk identification and treatment: avoid, retain, reduce, transfer
- **Property and casualty**: homeowners forms (HO-3 most common, open-perils dwelling + named-perils contents; HO-5 open-perils on both; HO-6 condo unit-owners); auto liability and physical damage; umbrella policies (typically $1-5M layered above primary)
- **Life insurance**: term (level, decreasing, renewable, convertible), whole, universal (UL), variable, variable-universal; policy provisions (incontestability, suicide clause, dividend options); **1035 exchange** (tax-free swap of like-kind insurance/annuity contracts, no recognition of gain); **MEC** (modified endowment contract — taxed LIFO with 10% penalty before 59.5); life insurance need calculation (human-life value vs needs analysis vs capital-retention)
- **Disability income**: definition of disability (own-occ vs any-occ vs modified); elimination period; benefit period; partial vs residual disability; tax treatment (employer-paid premiums = taxable benefits; employee-paid = tax-free benefits)
- **Long-term care (LTC)**: stand-alone vs hybrid (life+LTC); LTC Partnership Programs (asset protection equal to benefits received when applying for Medicaid); ADL triggers (2 of 6) and cognitive impairment trigger
- **Health insurance**: Medicare Part A (hospital, premium-free if 40 quarters), Part B (medical, means-tested premium), Part C (Medicare Advantage), Part D (drug); Medigap (Plans G, N most common after 2020 elimination of Plan F for new enrollees); ACA premium tax credits (income 100-400% FPL, modified by IRA Inflation Reduction Act through 2025)
- Business insurance: key person, buy-sell funding (cross-purchase vs entity-purchase), business overhead expense

**Applied skills**
- Calculate life insurance need using human-life value (PV of future earnings) and needs-analysis (immediate cash + income replacement − existing assets)
- Compare disability policies on **definition of disability** (own-occ is best), elimination, benefit period, and renewability (non-cancellable, guaranteed renewable, conditionally renewable)
- Recommend property-coverage limits using **replacement cost** (not market value or actual cash value)

**Common traps**
- Recommending term life for a young family without checking conversion options
- Forgetting that **employer-paid** disability premiums → benefits are **taxable**; employee-paid → tax-free
- Confusing **1035 exchange** (life-to-life or annuity-to-annuity, tax-free) with a regular surrender (taxable gain)
- Treating a hybrid LTC policy as identical to stand-alone — hybrids return premiums (or death benefits) if unused, but at a price
- Recommending Medigap Plan F for a new Medicare enrollee — Plan F is no longer available for those who became Medicare-eligible after Jan 1, 2020; Plan G is the closest substitute

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board RM&I Domain; Rejda *Principles of Risk Management and Insurance*; Dalton/Keir insurance modules.

## Chapter 5: Investment Planning
**Core questions:** What portfolio fits this client, and how do taxes, fees, and behavior change the answer?

**Key concepts**
- Risk and return, **Modern Portfolio Theory** (efficient frontier, optimal risky portfolio, capital allocation line), **CAPM** (E(R) = Rf + β(Rm − Rf))
- Asset allocation, diversification, rebalancing, dollar-cost averaging, lump-sum investing tradeoffs
- Fixed-income basics: duration (price sensitivity to yield), modified duration, convexity, yield to maturity, credit quality, bond ladders
- Pooled vehicles: mutual funds (open-end), ETFs (intraday liquidity, tax-efficiency, redemption-in-kind), closed-end funds (discount/premium to NAV), separately managed accounts, unit investment trusts
- **Performance metrics**: **Sharpe** (excess / total risk), **Treynor** (excess / β), **Jensen's alpha** (CAPM residual), **information ratio** (active return / active risk), **Sortino** (uses downside deviation only)
- Taxation of investments: short-term vs long-term capital gains (1 year holding), qualified dividends, wash-sale rule (61-day window — 30 days before + 30 days after), basis tracking (specific ID vs FIFO vs avg cost), bond OID and ABP

**Applied skills**
- Build a model allocation for a client with multiple goals and tax wrappers (asset location: bonds in tax-deferred, qualified-dividend stocks in taxable)
- Apply tax-loss harvesting within wash-sale rules (replace with a similar but **not substantially identical** position for 31+ days)
- Evaluate whether an actively managed fund's fees are likely to be recovered (active share, expense ratio, IR)

**Common traps**
- Confusing Sharpe (total risk in denominator) with Treynor (β in denominator) — use Treynor when comparing **diversified** portfolios; Sharpe when one is more diversified than the other
- Forgetting that wash-sale rule applies across **all accounts the taxpayer controls**, including IRAs (loss is permanently disallowed if replacement is in an IRA)
- Treating ETF tax-efficiency as automatic — leveraged and inverse ETFs distribute heavy short-term gains; only the in-kind redemption mechanism makes traditional equity ETFs tax-efficient
- Computing Jensen's alpha without recomputing the CAPM expected return — alpha is the **residual** against the CAPM line, not return minus risk-free
- Confusing modified duration (continuous compounding adjustment) with Macaulay duration (weighted-average time to cash flows)

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board Investment Planning Domain; Reilly & Brown *Investment Analysis and Portfolio Management*; Dalton/Keir investment modules.

## Chapter 6: Tax Planning
**Core questions:** How do you legally minimize lifetime taxes across the household, not just this year?

**Key concepts**
- Individual income tax: filing status, standard vs itemized deductions, above-the-line vs below-the-line, tax credits vs deductions, AMT (parallel system with its own exemption and rate structure), NIIT (3.8% on investment income above MAGI thresholds: $200K single / $250K MFJ)
- **Capital gains and losses**: short-term (≤ 1 year, ordinary rates), long-term (> 1 year, 0%/15%/20% based on income), Section 1202 QSBS exclusion (up to $10M or 10× basis exclusion if held 5+ years), wash-sale rule, basis tracking
- **Equity compensation tax**:
  - **NSOs** (non-qualified stock options): ordinary income on exercise (FMV − strike), basis = FMV at exercise, no AMT
  - **ISOs**: no regular tax on exercise but **AMT preference item** (bargain element added to AMTI); long-term capital gains treatment if both 2-year-from-grant and 1-year-from-exercise holding satisfied
  - **RSUs**: ordinary income at vesting (FMV at vest), basis = FMV at vest
- Business entity taxation: sole proprietorship (Schedule C), partnership/LLC (pass-through), S-corp (pass-through with reasonable salary requirement), C-corp (double taxation)
- Charitable contributions: cash (up to 60% AGI), appreciated long-term securities (FMV up to 30% AGI), DAF, CRT (income beneficiary first, charity at termination), CLT (charity first, family at termination)
- Tax credits: child tax credit, dependent care credit, retirement savers credit, education credits (AOTC, LLC), foreign tax credit
- **2024 thresholds (key numbers candidates should know):**
  - Gift tax annual exclusion: **$18,000** per donee per year (2024)
  - Estate/gift lifetime exemption: **$13.61M** per person (2024)
  - Kiddie tax unearned-income threshold: $2,600 (2024)
  - 401(k) elective deferral limit: $23,000 (2024); catch-up at 50+: additional $7,500
  - IRA contribution limit: $7,000 (2024); catch-up at 50+: $1,000

**Applied skills**
- Project a multi-year tax picture to find Roth-conversion or harvesting windows (low-income gap years before SS or RMDs)
- Choose between bunching deductions and standard deduction
- Optimize a charitable gift using appreciated stock vs cash (donor avoids capital gains and gets FMV deduction up to 30% AGI)

**Common traps**
- Forgetting AMT triggers on ISO exercise — large ISO exercises can generate huge AMT liabilities even when no cash changes hands; mitigate by exercising small lots or exercising-and-selling same-year (disqualifying disposition)
- Confusing the **wash-sale rule** (capital loss disallowed) with the **constructive sale rule** (mark-to-market on a hedged appreciated position)
- Treating NIIT and AMT as the same — NIIT is a 3.8% surtax on investment income above MAGI thresholds; AMT is a parallel tax system
- Recommending S-corp election without flagging the **reasonable salary** requirement (the IRS scrutinizes too-low salaries)
- Confusing the gift-tax **annual exclusion** ($18K per donee) with the **lifetime exemption** ($13.61M per donor) — exclusion is per-recipient per-year and is **not** counted against the lifetime exemption

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board Tax Planning Domain; Dalton/Keir tax modules; IRS Pub 17.

## Chapter 7: Retirement Savings and Income Planning
**Core questions:** Can this household retire when and how it wants, and what does the income plan look like?

**Key concepts**
- Qualified plans: defined benefit, defined contribution (401(k), 403(b), 457), profit-sharing, money-purchase, ESOPs; ERISA fiduciary basics, vesting schedules (cliff vs graded), nondiscrimination testing
- IRAs: traditional (deductible if no workplace plan, or limited based on income), Roth (income limits for direct contributions; back-door Roth via non-deductible traditional + conversion), SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA
- **SECURE Act 2.0 (signed Dec 2022) key changes:**
  - **RMD age 73** for those reaching 72 after 2022; rises to **75 in 2033**
  - **Catch-up contributions** for high earners ($145K+ wages, indexed) must be **Roth** starting 2026 (originally 2024, delayed)
  - IRA catch-up at 50+ indexed for inflation starting 2024 ($1,000 indexed)
  - 401(k) "super catch-up" at ages 60-63: greater of $10K or 150% of regular catch-up, starting 2025
  - 529-to-Roth IRA rollover (up to $35K lifetime, 15-year-old account)
  - 10-year rule for non-spouse beneficiaries (most must drain inherited IRA in 10 years)
- Social Security: PIA based on top 35 years of indexed earnings, FRA 66-67 depending on birth year, early claim at 62 (permanently reduces benefit ~30%), delayed retirement credits to 70 (~8%/yr), taxation of benefits (up to 85% taxable based on provisional income)
- Distribution rules: RMDs (current age 73), 72(t) substantially equal periodic payments (SEPP) to avoid 10% penalty before 59.5, Roth ordering rules (contributions → conversions → earnings; conversions have separate 5-year clocks per conversion), inherited IRA rules
- Retirement-income strategies: systematic withdrawal (4% rule and its critics), bucket strategy (cash + intermediate + long-term), floor-with-upside (essential needs covered by guaranteed income, discretionary from risky assets), annuities (SPIA, DIA, QLAC)

**Applied skills**
- Model a Roth conversion ladder against expected future tax brackets and IRMAA brackets
- Compare Social Security claiming ages with breakeven and longevity analysis (FRA → delay each year adds ~8%; breakeven typically late 70s to early 80s)
- Build a retirement-income plan that survives a sequence-of-returns shock in the first 5 years of retirement

**Common traps**
- Confusing the **RMD age** under SECURE 2.0 — currently 73, rising to 75 in 2033; do not say 70.5 or 72 on the exam
- Forgetting that **Roth 401(k)** is subject to RMDs during owner's lifetime under pre-SECURE 2.0 rules — SECURE 2.0 eliminates Roth 401(k) RMDs **starting 2024**
- Recommending a non-spouse inherited IRA stretch — most non-spouse beneficiaries must drain in **10 years** (eligible designated beneficiaries — spouse, minor child, disabled, chronically ill, person <10 years younger — are exceptions)
- Confusing the **5-year clock for Roth contributions** (one clock for all contributions) with **5-year clock per Roth conversion** (each conversion has its own clock for the 10% penalty exemption)
- Forgetting that **Social Security taxation** uses provisional income (AGI + tax-exempt interest + 50% of SS benefit), not AGI alone

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board Retirement Domain; Dalton/Keir retirement modules; SECURE Act 2.0 summaries; IRS Pub 590-A/B; SSA *POMS*.

## Chapter 8: Estate Planning
**Core questions:** Where does this client's wealth go, when, and with how much friction?

**Key concepts**
- Probate vs non-probate transfers: wills, intestacy (state-specific), will substitutes (POD, TOD, beneficiary designations, joint tenancy with right of survivorship, tenancy by the entirety)
- **Powers of attorney**: **durable POA** (survives incapacity), springing POA (effective on incapacity), healthcare POA / healthcare proxy, advance directive / living will, HIPAA authorization
- Trusts:
  - Revocable living trust (probate avoidance, no creditor protection or estate tax benefit)
  - Irrevocable trusts: bypass (credit-shelter), marital, **QTIP** (qualified terminable interest property — surviving spouse income for life, remainder to specified beneficiaries), **ILIT** (irrevocable life insurance trust — removes death benefit from estate), **Crummey trust** (permits annual-exclusion gifts to be present-interest via withdrawal right), dynasty trust, **QPRT** (qualified personal residence trust — gifts a residence at discounted value), GRAT, special-needs trust (preserves SSI/Medicaid eligibility)
- Gifting and transfer taxes:
  - **Annual exclusion: $18,000 per donee per year (2024)**, indexed
  - **Lifetime exemption: $13.61M per person (2024)**, scheduled to sunset to ~$7M (inflation-adjusted) after 2025 unless Congress acts
  - Portability (DSUE — deceased spouse unused exemption — must be elected on a timely Form 706)
  - **GST tax** (40% on transfers skipping a generation; GST exemption tracks the lifetime exemption)
- **Step-up in basis at death** (carried over for inherited assets at FMV on date of death — converts unrealized gain to zero for income tax)
- Estate planning for non-traditional families, business owners (buy-sell agreements, family limited partnerships), and charitable intent

**Applied skills**
- Recommend a trust structure for a blended family with minor children from prior marriages (QTIP for surviving spouse income + remainder to children of first marriage)
- Use annual-exclusion and lifetime-exemption gifts in a coordinated plan (gift appreciating assets first to remove future appreciation from the estate)
- Spot beneficiary-designation errors that override the will (life insurance, IRAs, 401(k)s, TOD accounts) — the **beneficiary designation controls**, regardless of the will

**Common traps**
- Believing a will controls IRA or life insurance disposition — beneficiary designations control
- Confusing the **annual exclusion** ($18K per donee per year) with the **lifetime exemption** ($13.61M per donor) — they are separate; gifts within the annual exclusion don't reduce the lifetime exemption
- Forgetting that **ILIT** life insurance proceeds remain in the **gross estate** if the insured had any incidents of ownership in the prior 3 years (3-year lookback)
- Treating a revocable living trust as creditor-protection or estate-tax-shelter — neither; it only avoids probate
- Forgetting **step-up in basis** when planning appreciated-asset gifts — gifting an appreciated stock during life **carries over** the donor's basis to the donee, while inheritance **steps up** to FMV at death

**Authoritative sources:** CFP Board Estate Planning Domain; Dalton *Estate Planning*; Keir estate modules; IRS Pub 559.

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline and Case Integration
The CFP exam loves integrated cases. A retirement question is often actually a tax question wearing a Social Security costume, with an estate-planning footnote and a behavioral wrinkle. The math:

- **Timing:** 170 questions × ~2 minutes per question across two 3-hour sessions. **Roughly 2 minutes per question** is the budget. Case-based item sets share a fact pattern so per-question time is closer to 1.5 minutes once you have read the case.
- **Read every fact pattern twice** — once for the gist, once for the specific numbers and dates. The trap distractor often hinges on a date or filing status detail.
- **State your assumption** — when a question is borderline (e.g., "should the client convert to Roth?"), the right answer often depends on a future tax-rate assumption. Identify it before answering.
- **Best of three reasonable:** CFP distractors are often **plausible** recommendations. The right answer is the **best** — usually the one that is most fiduciary, most tax-efficient, or most aligned with the client's stated goals.
- **Watch the verb:** "Recommend" requires a single recommendation. "Identify" requires a list. "Evaluate" requires a balanced assessment.
- **Worst-section discipline:** If you blank on a question, flag and move on; come back if time permits. Do not let one stuck question cost three others.
- **Calculator (BA II Plus):** **DEC = 9**, **P/Y = 1, C/Y = 1**, clear TVM between problems. Verify settings before exam start. Practice the cash-flow worksheet for IRR/NPV-style questions.
- **Ethics last hour:** Save the Professional Conduct and Regulation questions for late in the exam, when fatigue is highest but judgment can still recall the Code.

Strong CFP candidates leave the exam having reasoned like planners, not like product specialists. Practice integrated case studies, write down the assumption you are using before answering, read every fact pattern twice, and remember the fiduciary duty governs every recommendation, not just the conclusion.
