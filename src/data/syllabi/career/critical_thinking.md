# Critical Thinking: Think Clearly
*Spot the flaw, weigh the evidence, and change your mind for the right reasons.*

**ID**: `criticalThinking` · **Discipline**: Reasoning · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Most bad decisions are not caused by a lack of intelligence. They are caused by a handful of predictable thinking traps that everyone falls into: believing a confident voice, mistaking a coincidence for a cause, defending an opinion because we already hold it. This course is a friendly, practical tour of those traps and how to step around them. No philosophy degree required.

You will learn to take any claim apart the way a mechanic opens a hood: find the conclusion, find the reasons, and check whether the reasons actually hold the conclusion up. You will get a working vocabulary for the most common logical fallacies and cognitive biases, so that the next time someone uses one on you (or you catch yourself using one), you can name it and respond. The tone is warm and plain-language, but the standards are real: clear thinking means being accurate, not just confident.

By the end you should be able to read a headline, hear a sales pitch, or sit in a meeting and quietly ask the right questions: What is the claim? What is the evidence? Compared with what? What would change my mind? Those questions, used habitually, are most of what "critical thinking" actually is.

## Course Design Notes
The course is built around the core skill of "spot the flaw": almost every question hands the learner a short, concrete scenario (a sales pitch, a news headline, an office email, a study) and asks them to identify the reasoning error or evaluate the strength of the argument. Distractors are themselves named fallacies or biases, so wrong answers teach the distinctions between look-alike errors (e.g., ad hominem vs straw man, anchoring vs availability, post hoc vs cherry-picking).

Chapters move from structure (what an argument is) to validity and soundness, then through the fallacy families, the major cognitive biases, the correlation-causation-statistics cluster, evidence and source evaluation, and finally the constructive habits (steelmanning, falsifiability, calibration). Two later chapters are pure mixed practice and capstone-style integration, where fallacies appear stacked and unlabeled, the way they do in real life. The voice stays beginner-friendly throughout, but every lesson note reinforces an accurate, transferable principle rather than a memorized label.

## Chapter 1: Anatomy of an Argument
**Core questions**
- Which sentence is the conclusion and which are the premises?
- What unstated assumption does this argument secretly depend on?
- Is this an actual argument, or just a confident assertion?

**Key concepts**: premise, conclusion, inference, assertion vs argument, hidden assumptions, premise and conclusion indicator words ("because", "since", "therefore", "so").

**Common traps**: treating a loud opinion as an argument; mistaking a reason ("because...") for the main point; missing the load-bearing assumption that quietly connects premise to conclusion.

## Chapter 2: Validity, Soundness, and Strong Reasoning
**Core questions**
- If the premises were true, would the conclusion have to be true?
- Is this argument valid but still unsound because a premise is false?
- Is this everyday argument strong even if it is not airtight?

**Key concepts**: validity, soundness, modus ponens, modus tollens, affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, deductive vs inductive strength.

**Common traps**: confusing validity (structure) with truth of premises; running a true if-then statement backward; judging an argument by the speaker's confidence or vocabulary instead of its support.

## Chapter 3: Fallacies That Attack the Person or the Argument
**Core questions**
- Is this objection engaging the claim, or just attacking the claimant?
- Is the opponent's real position being represented fairly?
- Does where a claim came from actually determine whether it is true?

**Key concepts**: ad hominem, tu quoque (appeal to hypocrisy), straw man, steelmanning as the antidote, genetic fallacy.

**Common traps**: confusing ad hominem with straw man; treating a source's flaws as proof a specific claim is false; dismissing hypocrites' correct advice.

## Chapter 4: Fallacies of Structure and Framing
**Core questions**
- Are these really the only two options, or has the middle been erased?
- Why must each step in this predicted chain actually follow?
- Is this "authority" an expert in the relevant field — and do other experts agree?

**Key concepts**: false dilemma, slippery slope, circular reasoning (begging the question), equivocation, appeal to authority (valid vs fallacious), appeal to popularity (bandwagon).

**Common traps**: accepting an either/or frame that hides options; treating a feared cascade as inevitable without a mechanism; assuming all appeals to authority are fallacies (relevant expert consensus is good evidence).

## Chapter 5: Cognitive Biases
**Core questions**
- Am I noticing only the evidence that fits what I already believe?
- Is a first number or vivid memory secretly steering my estimate?
- Am I making a decision based on money or effort I can never get back?

**Key concepts**: confirmation bias, anchoring, availability heuristic, sunk cost fallacy, hindsight bias, survivorship bias.

**Common traps**: mistaking confirmation bias (filtering evidence) for availability (ease of recall); letting an anchor price define "a good deal"; honoring sunk costs; learning only from the visible survivors.

## Chapter 6: Correlation, Causation, and Statistics Traps
**Core questions**
- Could a third factor explain why these two things move together?
- Did B really cause A, or did B just happen after A?
- "X% improved" — compared with what?

**Key concepts**: correlation vs causation, confounding variables, post hoc ergo propter hoc, control/comparison groups, regression to the mean, base rate neglect, misleading averages (mean vs median).

**Common traps**: reading any correlation as causation; crediting an intervention for a rebound that was regression to the mean; ignoring base rates when interpreting a positive test; trusting an average skewed by outliers.

## Chapter 7: Evaluating Evidence and Sources
**Core questions**
- Can I trace this claim back toward a primary source?
- Who funded or benefits from this evidence, and how should that change my reading?
- Is this a single anecdote standing in for real data?

**Key concepts**: primary vs secondary sources, conflict of interest, anecdote vs statistical evidence, cherry-picking, lateral reading.

**Common traps**: trusting a polished website on its own terms instead of reading laterally; either dismissing or ignoring funding bias rather than scrutinizing carefully; letting one vivid story outweigh population data.

## Chapter 8: Reasoning Well — Steelmanning and Good Habits
**Core questions**
- Have I stated the opposing view in its strongest, fairest form?
- Could any possible observation count as evidence against this claim?
- Who carries the burden of proof here?

**Key concepts**: steelmanning, falsifiability, burden of proof and appeal to ignorance, Occam's razor, the principle of charity, updating beliefs in proportion to evidence.

**Common traps**: beating a weak caricature instead of the best argument; defending an unfalsifiable claim; reversing the burden of proof ("you can't disprove it"); multiplying unsupported assumptions when a simpler explanation fits.

## Chapter 9: Spot the Flaw (Mixed Practice)
**Core questions**
- Given an unlabeled scenario, which named fallacy is at work?
- When two errors are stacked, can I separate and name both?
- What is the genuine concern hiding behind the rhetorical trick?

**Key concepts**: rapid fallacy identification, distinguishing look-alike fallacies, recognizing stacked fallacies (e.g., bandwagon plus weak appeal to authority).

**Common traps**: matching a scenario to the wrong but similar fallacy; seeing only the most obvious flaw and missing a second; letting an emotionally charged scenario override careful identification.

## Chapter 10: Putting It Together
**Core questions**
- What is the first thing a careful thinker checks in this full argument?
- Which of several competing claims is best supported by evidence rather than rhetoric?
- How confident should I be, given the strength of the evidence?

**Key concepts**: integrating fallacies, biases, and evidence checks; ranking claims by support quality; calibrating confidence; naming your own biases in real time.

**Common traps**: jumping to a verdict before checking for confounders; ranking claims by emotional pull rather than evidence quality; treating preliminary findings as proven or dismissing them entirely.

## Capstone: The Clear-Thinking Toolkit
Learners assemble a personal, reusable toolkit and demonstrate it on a real claim of their choosing:
- a one-page checklist for taking any argument apart (conclusion, premises, hidden assumptions, validity, evidence quality)
- a "fallacy and bias spotter" cheat sheet with the canonical examples from the course
- a worked steelman of a position the learner disagrees with
- a correlation-vs-causation analysis of a real headline ("compared with what?")
- a source-evaluation walkthrough using lateral reading
- a short written reflection: "What is one belief I updated during this course, and what evidence moved me?"

The goal is not to win arguments but to think clearly under pressure — to ask the right question first, weigh the evidence honestly, and change your mind for the right reasons.
