# Professional Ethics: No Tiny Crimes
**ID**: `professionalEthics` - **Discipline**: Professional Ethics - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Train professionals to recognize ethical risk before it becomes scandal, litigation, burnout, or institutional folklore. The course treats ethics as a practical operating skill: noticing conflicts, naming duties, documenting uncertainty, escalating well, and designing environments where good people are less likely to make bad decisions.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus had the right tone and core modules, but it needed more professional specificity: stakeholder analysis, legal-versus-ethical judgment, codes of conduct, speak-up systems, investigation hygiene, and culture design. This rewrite keeps the "tiny rationalization" lens while turning the course into a workplace-ready toolkit.

## Chapter 1: Integrity, Role Duties, and the Law/Ethics Gap
**Key concepts**: professional integrity, fiduciary duty, duty of care, duty of loyalty, legal compliance, ethical discretion, minimum standard, professional code.

**Practical questions**
- What does this role require that ordinary personal morality might not?
- When is "legal" still not good enough?
- Which promises has the organization made to clients, users, patients, investors, employees, or the public?

**Workplace skill**
- Write a role-duty map that separates legal obligations, professional standards, organizational values, and personal judgment.

## Chapter 2: Stakeholders, Harms, and Incentives
**Key concepts**: stakeholder analysis, foreseeable harm, externalities, moral distance, incentives, pressure, conflicts of interest, independence, disclosure.

**Practical questions**
- Who benefits, who bears risk, and who is missing from the meeting?
- What incentive could make a reasonable person shade the truth?
- Is disclosure enough, or does the conflict need removal, review, or refusal?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a conflict register for gifts, side relationships, sales pressure, referral arrangements, data access, and personal financial interests.

## Chapter 3: Ethical Frameworks That Actually Help
**Key concepts**: consequences, rights, justice, common good, virtue, care, reversibility, publicity test, proportionality, moral residue.

**Practical questions**
- Which option creates the least avoidable harm?
- Which duties or rights should not be traded away?
- Would this decision still look acceptable if a regulator, client, colleague, or journalist read the file?

**Workplace skill**
- Use a one-page decision memo: facts, stakeholders, options, rules, ethical lenses, recommendation, uncertainty, and review path.

## Chapter 4: Speaking Up, Escalation, and Documentation
**Key concepts**: psychological safety, retaliation risk, escalation threshold, protected reporting, whistleblowing, chain of command, documentation hygiene, contemporaneous notes.

**Practical questions**
- Is this a concern, a breach, a near miss, or an emergency?
- Who needs to know, by when, and through what channel?
- What should be documented without exaggeration, speculation, or silence?

**Workplace skill**
- Draft an escalation note that states facts, uncertainty, policy basis, risk, requested action, and timeline.

## Chapter 5: Fair Process, Investigations, and Bias
**Key concepts**: procedural justice, due process, consistency, confidentiality, conflicts in investigations, evidence handling, bias, adverse impact, proportional response.

**Practical questions**
- Is the process fair enough for someone you dislike?
- Are similar cases being treated similarly?
- What evidence would change the decision?

**Workplace skill**
- Design an investigation checklist that protects complainants, respondents, witnesses, records, and decision integrity.

## Chapter 6: Ethical Culture and Control Design
**Key concepts**: tone at the top, tone in the middle, normalization of deviance, near misses, speak-up culture, incentives, control ownership, postmortems.

**Practical questions**
- What behavior is rewarded, ignored, punished, or quietly admired?
- Where does the process rely on heroic honesty instead of good design?
- Which near miss should become training before it becomes a scandal?

**Workplace skill**
- Convert a recurring ethical failure into a control improvement, training prompt, or policy clarification.

## Chapter 7: Sector Dilemmas
**Key concepts**: client confidentiality, patient safety, investor protection, public service, data privacy, procurement integrity, research ethics, professional independence.

**Case bank**
- Finance: suitability, conflicts, disclosure, research independence.
- Healthcare: consent, safety, billing, vulnerable patients.
- Law and public service: confidentiality, candor, public trust.
- Technology: data use, AI harms, security disclosure, manipulative design.
- Management: hiring fairness, harassment response, pressure to misstate metrics.

## Chapter 8: Capstone: The Tiny Crime Audit
Students audit a fictional or real workflow for small rationalizations that could accumulate into ethical failure. The final deliverable includes risk signals, stakeholder harms, relevant duties, escalation points, documentation standards, and one culture or control change.

## Capstone
Students complete a Tiny Crime Audit of a fictional or real workflow, identifying small rationalizations, stakeholder harms, duties, escalation points, documentation standards, and one culture or control improvement.

## Assessment Ideas
- Role-duty map
- Conflict-of-interest register
- Ethical decision memo
- Escalation note
- Investigation checklist
- Tiny Crime Audit capstone
