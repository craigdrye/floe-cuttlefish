# AIGP: AI Governance Professional
**ID**: `aigp` | **Discipline**: Technology | **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course prepares an adult learner to do the actual work of AI governance and to pass the IAPP AIGP exam as a side effect of doing that work well. An AI governance professional sits between the people who want to ship a model and the people who will have to answer for it — regulators, auditors, a board, a harmed customer. The job is to turn enthusiasm and ambiguity into decisions that survive scrutiny: this system is in scope, that use is prohibited, this one is high-risk and needs a conformity assessment, that vendor claim needs evidence, and here is the human who can stop the system when it goes wrong. The goal is fluency in that translation — turning a vague "we're adding AI" into a documented, defensible position.

The course is deliberately framework-literate but not framework-worshipping. Vocabulary and obligations differ across the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, the EU AI Act, ISO/IEC 42001, and an organization's own policies, but the reasoning underneath is stable: name the system and its purpose, identify who could be harmed, classify the risk, assign accountable owners, choose proportionate controls, prove the controls work, and keep watching after launch. You will internalize the AIGP exam's four domains — the foundations of AI governance, how laws and standards apply to AI, governing AI development, and governing AI deployment and use — and the specific facts that distinguish a real answer from a plausible-sounding one: who is a "provider" versus a "deployer," when an impact assessment is legally required versus merely wise, and why "we added a human" is not the same as meaningful human oversight.

By the end you should be able to take a real proposed AI system and do five things well: scope it, classify its risk against the relevant regime, assemble the documentation an auditor would demand, evaluate it for the failure modes that matter (including the generative ones — hallucination, prompt injection, data leakage), and write an approval, conditional-approval, or rejection that a skeptical reviewer would sign. That combination — scope, classification, evidence, evaluation, defensible judgment — is what separates a governance professional from someone who merely forwards a policy PDF.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test AI governance roles and accountability, AI system inventory and risk triage, the NIST AI RMF (Govern/Map/Measure/Manage), the EU AI Act (risk tiers, provider/deployer obligations, conformity assessment, GPAI rules), AI impact and fundamental-rights assessments, ISO/IEC 42001 management systems, fairness/bias and data provenance, generative-AI evaluation and security, transparency and human oversight, third-party/vendor AI risk, post-deployment monitoring and incident response, or AIGP exam strategy.

The voice is a senior governance lead mentoring a capable analyst preparing for the AIGP: concrete artifacts over abstractions, no acronym left undefined on first use, and a relentless focus on the *next defensible decision*. Every chapter surfaces Common traps because the highest-value learning here is recognizing the confident-but-wrong move — confusing a tactic with a control, treating a vendor's marketing as evidence, classifying a system by how it was built rather than what it does, or mistaking disclosure for oversight. Distractors in any derived questions should be plausible-but-flawed, not silly.

Keep facts current. As of 2026: the **AIGP Body of Knowledge v2.1** (effective 2 February 2026) frames four domains; the exam is **100 questions (85 scored + 15 unscored pilot)** with scaled scoring **100–500, passing at 300**. The **EU AI Act** is in force on a staged timeline — prohibited practices and AI-literacy duties since 2 February 2025; **GPAI** (general-purpose AI) model obligations and governance since 2 August 2025; the bulk of **high-risk (Annex III)** and transparency obligations from **2 August 2026**; product-embedded high-risk systems extending to 2027–2028 under the AI omnibus simplification. Top penalties for prohibited practices reach **€35M or 7% of global turnover**. The **NIST AI RMF 1.0** (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) and its **Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600-1, finalized July 2024)** are the U.S. anchor; **ISO/IEC 42001** is the certifiable AI management-system standard. Prefer these over the older draft framings.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
AIGP domain weights are approximate ranges, not promises. Allocate study time by weight, your error log, and how unfamiliar the legal material feels.

| Domain | Approx. Exam Weight | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| I. Foundations of AI Governance (AI/ML concepts, risks, harms, the case for governance) | ~18–22% | Build vocabulary early; you cannot govern what you cannot name |
| II. How Laws, Standards & Frameworks Apply to AI (EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, sectoral and privacy law) | ~28–34% | Highest-yield and most detail-heavy; memorize role definitions and dates |
| III. Governing AI Development (data, training, testing, documentation, impact assessment) | ~22–26% | Learn by producing artifacts, not by reading about them |
| IV. Governing AI Deployment & Use (oversight, transparency, monitoring, incident response, third parties) | ~22–26% | Tie everything back to the post-launch reality where governance actually lives |

Suggested cadence: 6–10 weeks, 60–90 total hours, 4–5 study blocks per week, one weekly mixed quiz. Every block should produce something retrievable — a solved set of scenario questions, a one-page role/obligation comparison, a filled-in template (inventory entry, risk triage, impact assessment), or an error-log entry. Reading the EU AI Act recitals without practicing classification is governance-flavored wallpaper. The exam rewards judgment on fact patterns, so train on *scenarios* far more than on definitions.

## Chapter 1: AI, Risk, and the Case for Governance
- **Core questions**: What is this system actually doing — prediction, classification, generation, recommendation? What kinds of harm does AI create that ordinary software does not? Why does anyone need a governance function at all?
- **Key concepts**: supervised vs unsupervised vs reinforcement learning; training vs inference; model vs system vs use case; foundation/general-purpose models; the harm taxonomy (individual, group, societal, environmental); allocative vs representational harm; automation bias and over-reliance; the difference between an AI *risk* (uncertain effect on objectives) and an AI *incident* (a realized harm).
- **Applied skills**: describe an AI system in one sentence that names its purpose, inputs, decision, and affected people; list the plausible harms before reaching for any control; explain to a skeptical executive why "the model is accurate" does not answer "is it safe to deploy."
- **Common traps**: equating accuracy with safety; treating AI risk as purely technical and ignoring rights, fairness, and trust; assuming a low-stakes pilot stays low-stakes after it scales; describing a system by its technology ("it's an LLM") instead of its function and its impact on people.

## Chapter 2: Governance Roles, Accountability, and Decision Rights
- **Core questions**: Who owns this AI system, and who is accountable when it fails? Which decisions require documented approval, and from whom? How do we prove accountability is real rather than a name on an org chart?
- **Key concepts**: model owner, system/business owner, accountable executive, AI risk committee, three-lines-of-defense; policy vs standard vs procedure; decision rights and escalation paths; RACI for an AI lifecycle; the "accountable deployer" concept; documented sign-off as the evidence of a decision.
- **Applied skills**: build an AI governance role map with named owners, approvers, escalation paths, and review cadence; identify the single accountable person for a given system; distinguish a policy (what must be true) from a procedure (how it gets done).
- **Common traps**: spreading accountability so widely that no one is actually responsible; confusing the person who built the model with the person accountable for its use; treating a steering committee as a substitute for a decision; assuming a written policy is being followed because it exists.

## Chapter 3: How Laws and Frameworks Apply — EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001
- **Core questions**: Which regime applies, and in which role does my organization sit? Is this use prohibited, high-risk, limited-risk, or minimal-risk? Which framework gives me the controls, and which gives me the obligations?
- **Key concepts**: EU AI Act risk tiers (prohibited, high-risk/Annex III, limited-risk transparency, minimal-risk) and the **provider vs deployer vs importer/distributor** distinction; conformity assessment, CE marking, the EU database registration, and technical documentation; GPAI model obligations (model documentation, copyright policy, training-data summary; systemic-risk tier); key dates (2 Feb 2025 prohibitions; 2 Aug 2025 GPAI; 2 Aug 2026 most high-risk); the NIST AI RMF functions **Govern, Map, Measure, Manage** and trustworthiness characteristics (valid/reliable, safe, secure/resilient, accountable/transparent, explainable, privacy-enhanced, fair); ISO/IEC 42001 as a certifiable AI management system (plan-do-check-act); how privacy law (GDPR) interacts with AI governance.
- **Applied skills**: classify a use case under the EU AI Act and name the role the organization plays; write a one-page regulatory-readiness memo (regime, role, risk tier, obligations, gaps); map a NIST AI RMF function to the control that closes a specific gap.
- **Common traps**: assuming the EU AI Act doesn't apply because the company is non-EU (it can apply when outputs are used in the EU); confusing "provider" obligations with "deployer" obligations; treating the NIST AI RMF (voluntary, control-oriented) as if it imposed legal obligations; classifying by intent ("we don't mean it to be high-risk") instead of by the Act's criteria.

## Chapter 4: AI System Inventory and Risk Triage
- **Core questions**: Is this system even in scope for governance? What is its intended purpose, and who is the affected population? Which facts would push it into a higher risk tier?
- **Key concepts**: AI use-case inventory and the AI register; intended purpose and reasonably foreseeable misuse; user/affected populations and vulnerable groups; risk-tiering criteria (autonomy, stakes, reversibility, scale, sensitivity of data); material change and re-triage triggers; shadow AI (ungoverned tools employees adopt on their own).
- **Applied skills**: complete an inventory entry and a risk-triage worksheet with explicit rationale; decide whether a change (new data source, new population, model update) is "material" enough to re-trigger review; surface shadow AI through procurement and access signals.
- **Common traps**: inventorying only the systems you already knew about; tiering by gut instead of stated criteria; treating a model update as cosmetic when it changes behavior; letting "it's just a productivity tool" keep a system off the register.

## Chapter 5: Data, Fairness, and the AI Impact Assessment
- **Core questions**: Where did the training and input data come from, and may we use it? Who could be treated unfairly, and how would we know? What does a proportionate mitigation look like for the residual risk we can't eliminate?
- **Key concepts**: data provenance, lineage, and consent/lawful basis; data quality, representativeness, and labeling bias; protected attributes and proxies; fairness definitions in tension (demographic parity vs equalized odds vs calibration — you usually cannot satisfy all at once); bias testing and disparate-impact analysis; the AI/algorithmic impact assessment and the EU fundamental-rights impact assessment (FRIA) for certain deployers; residual risk and proportionality.
- **Applied skills**: write an AI impact assessment naming affected stakeholders, foreseeable harms, mitigations, residual risk, and an approval recommendation; choose and defend a fairness metric appropriate to the context; trace a dataset to its source and flag a provenance gap.
- **Common traps**: assuming "we removed the protected attribute" eliminates bias (proxies remain); chasing a single fairness number as if it were universal; treating an impact assessment as a launch formality instead of a decision document; ignoring data provenance until a copyright or consent question lands.

## Chapter 6: Documentation, Testing, and Lifecycle Controls in Development
- **Core questions**: What evidence would an auditor demand for this model, and do we have it? How was the system tested before release, and against what threshold? What gets logged so we can reconstruct a decision later?
- **Key concepts**: technical documentation and model cards; datasheets for datasets; evaluation sets, holdout/validation, and acceptance thresholds; reproducibility and versioning; logging and traceability requirements (and the EU AI Act's record-keeping duties); change management and re-validation; pre-deployment review gates.
- **Applied skills**: assemble the documentation package for a system entering review (purpose, data, performance, limitations, intended/excluded uses); define a release gate with a pass/fail threshold tied to a real metric; specify what must be logged to support later contestability and audit.
- **Common traps**: documenting after launch to "catch up" instead of generating evidence as you build; reporting only aggregate accuracy and hiding subgroup performance; setting an evaluation threshold no one can fail; treating logging as an IT detail rather than a governance requirement.

## Chapter 7: Generative AI Evaluation and Security
- **Core questions**: What failure mode are we actually testing for? Can this system be manipulated or made to leak data it shouldn't? Which evaluation result is allowed to block a release?
- **Key concepts**: foundation/general-purpose models and the build-vs-buy distinction; hallucination and grounding/retrieval (RAG); prompt injection (direct and indirect) and jailbreaks; data leakage and training-data extraction; toxic/unsafe content and content-safety filters; evaluation sets, benchmarks, and red-teaming; the NIST Generative AI Profile (AI 600-1) risk categories; escalation thresholds and kill switches.
- **Applied skills**: write a generative-AI evaluation plan covering quality, safety, prompt injection, and data leakage, with explicit escalation thresholds; design a red-team exercise for a customer-facing assistant; decide which test failures are release-blocking versus monitor-and-mitigate.
- **Common traps**: treating a public benchmark score as proof of safety in your context; testing only the happy path and skipping adversarial inputs; assuming a vendor's content filter handles *indirect* prompt injection; shipping a RAG system without checking whether retrieval can surface data the user shouldn't see.

## Chapter 8: Deployment — Transparency, Human Oversight, Monitoring, and Vendors
- **Core questions**: What must affected people be told, and can they contest a decision? Can a human meaningfully intervene, or is the "human in the loop" just decoration? After launch, what tells us the system is drifting — and what must the vendor disclose?
- **Key concepts**: transparency and disclosure duties (AI-interaction notices, synthetic-content/deepfake labeling under EU AI Act limited-risk rules); explainability and contestability; human-in-the-loop vs human-on-the-loop vs human-in-command, and what makes oversight *meaningful* (authority, competence, time, and a real ability to override); post-deployment monitoring, drift, and performance decay; AI incident response and serious-incident reporting; third-party/vendor due diligence, contractual controls, audit rights, and re-review triggers on model updates.
- **Applied skills**: write a transparency-and-oversight checklist for an AI-assisted decision workflow; design a monitoring plan with drift signals, thresholds, and named owners; conduct a vendor AI risk review (required evidence, contract controls, monitoring, accept/reject) and decide what model change re-triggers review.
- **Common traps**: calling a disclosure "oversight" when no human can actually stop the system; rubber-stamp human review under time pressure (oversight theater); assuming a launched model stays accurate forever; accepting a vendor's marketing claims as evidence and signing away audit rights; failing to define who reports a serious incident, and on what clock.

## Capstone
A product team wants to deploy an AI system that screens job applicants and drafts personalized rejection emails, built on a third-party general-purpose model with a retrieval layer over the company's HR data — and they want to launch in the EU and the US. Turn this into a complete governance review packet. Scope and inventory the system; triage its risk and classify it under the EU AI Act (name the role, the tier, and the obligations, including any high-risk and GPAI implications); identify the affected populations and write an AI impact assessment with fairness analysis and residual risk; assemble the documentation and release gates you would require from the development team; build a generative-AI evaluation plan covering hallucination, prompt injection, and data leakage from the HR retrieval layer; specify transparency notices, a *meaningful* human-oversight design, and a post-deployment monitoring plan with drift signals and owners; and conduct a vendor risk review of the model provider with contract controls and re-review triggers. Deliverable: a decision memo that issues an approval, a conditional approval (with the specific conditions and evidence required), or a rejection — written so a skeptical auditor, a regulator, and a rejected applicant's lawyer would each find it defensible.

## Assessment Ideas
- Classification drills: given a fact pattern, place the use case in the correct EU AI Act tier and role, graded on rationale and on catching the high-risk/GPAI nuances.
- Risk-triage and impact-assessment exercises graded on scope, stakeholder and harm analysis, mitigation quality, and residual-risk judgment.
- Generative-AI evaluation plans graded on coverage of hallucination, prompt injection, and leakage, and on whether the escalation thresholds are actually enforceable.
- Capstone graded on governance completeness, evidence quality, regulatory accuracy, and defensible approval/rejection logic.
