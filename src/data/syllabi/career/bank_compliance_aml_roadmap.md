# Bank Compliance and AML Roadmap
**ID**: `bankComplianceAmlRoadmap` - **Discipline**: Finance - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This is a career roadmap, not an exam cram. It is for someone deciding whether bank compliance and anti-money-laundering work is for them, breaking into a first analyst seat, or trying to understand how the pieces of a financial-crimes program fit together so they can move up. The work itself is deceptively human: a bank takes in money, people, and transactions it cannot fully see, and the compliance function's job is to make defensible decisions about which of them pose a real risk — and to leave a paper trail that a regulator, an auditor, and a future colleague can all reconstruct. Most of the value an analyst adds is not catching the villain. It is separating the unusual-but-explainable from the genuinely suspicious, and writing it down clearly enough that the bank's decision holds up.

The roadmap follows the actual shape of the job. You will learn where the function sits inside a bank (the three lines of defense, who owns what, where compliance ends and the financial-intelligence unit begins), and the foundational legal scaffolding — the Bank Secrecy Act and its descendants — without trying to memorize regulation text. Then you walk the lifecycle a customer and their money pass through: onboarding and know-your-customer due diligence, sanctions and watchlist screening, ongoing transaction monitoring, alert triage, investigation, and the suspicious activity report. Each stage produces a specific work product, and each has a characteristic way of going wrong. The course is built around those work products and those failure modes because that is what the job rewards.

By the end you should be able to read a customer file or an alert and do four things a hiring manager actually wants: form a risk hypothesis from the facts, gather the evidence that would confirm or kill it, reach a proportionate disposition (clear, escalate, file, or de-risk), and document it so the next reviewer reaches the same conclusion from the same record. You should also understand the career ladder above the analyst seat — quality control, investigations, sanctions, model tuning, BSA officer — and what each one demands. Tools and screens change between banks; this reasoning is portable.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when learners want a practical career path *into and through* bank financial-crimes compliance: how a BSA/AML program is structured, KYC and customer due diligence, beneficial-ownership rules, sanctions and watchlist screening, transaction monitoring and alert triage, investigations, the SAR/CTR reporting machinery, quality control, and how analyst decisions get tested by audit and examiners. This is the role-navigation and analyst-craft course. Keep it distinct from `amlCompliance` (ACAMS/CAMS exam framing and typologies) and `crcm` (the broader consumer-regulatory compliance-manager track); when a question is purely exam-prep or purely consumer-compliance, it belongs in those.

The voice is a seasoned BSA officer mentoring a new analyst: concrete files, plain-language risk reasoning, no jargon left undefined, and a relentless focus on the *defensible decision and the record behind it*. Every chapter surfaces Common traps, because in this field the costly error is rarely exotic — it is the analyst who clears an alert with a convenient story, overstates suspicion in a narrative, treats a screening hit as guilt, or files reflexively to avoid the harder judgment call. Keep facts current and frame the moving parts honestly: the CTR threshold (cash over $10,000 in a day), the bank SAR threshold ($5,000 with a suspect, $25,000 regardless of suspect) and 30-day filing clock, FinCEN's 2018 CDD Rule and its beneficial-ownership 25%/control-prong test, OFAC's strict-liability standard and 50% ownership rule, and the fact that Corporate Transparency Act beneficial-ownership-information (BOI) reporting is a genuinely unsettled, shifting area in 2025-2026 — teach the principle, flag that the scope has changed and may change again, and never present a contested specific as settled. Distractors should be plausible-but-flawed, not silly.

## Chapter 1: The Compliance Function Inside a Bank
- **Core questions**: Where does this decision live, and who owns it? What is compliance actually controlling for? How does the function relate to the business, to audit, and to the regulator?
- **Key concepts**: the three lines of defense (business owns the risk; compliance and risk set and monitor the rules; internal audit independently tests); the BSA/AML program's required pillars (a designated BSA officer, internal controls, independent testing, training, and — since the CDD Rule — ongoing customer due diligence); the difference between policy, procedure, and control; where compliance hands off to the financial-intelligence unit / investigations; risk appetite as a business decision, not an analyst one.
- **Applied skills**: draw a one-page map of a risk that links the obligation, the control meant to catch it, the owner, the evidence the control produces, and the escalation path; place a given decision in the correct line of defense.
- **Common traps**: assuming compliance "owns" a risk the business actually owns; confusing a written policy with a working control; treating independent testing as adversarial rather than as the thing that protects the program; thinking the BSA officer signs off on every analyst decision.

## Chapter 2: The Legal Scaffolding (Without Memorizing the Code)
- **Core questions**: What is the law actually trying to make the bank do? Which obligation is triggered by these facts? What is mandatory reporting versus discretionary judgment?
- **Key concepts**: the Bank Secrecy Act (1970) as the foundation — recordkeeping and reporting to help detect financial crime; the USA PATRIOT Act's expansion (customer identification programs, enhanced due diligence for correspondent and private banking); the Currency Transaction Report for cash movement over $10,000 in a day (mandatory, mechanical, not a suspicion judgment); the suspicious-activity regime as the discretionary heart of the job; the principle that the bank, not the customer, bears the obligation.
- **Applied skills**: given a fact pattern, name whether a CTR, a SAR, both, or neither is in play, and why; distinguish a rule-driven mandatory filing from a judgment-driven one.
- **Common traps**: treating a CTR as evidence of wrongdoing (it is routine, threshold-based reporting); thinking structuring "below $10,000" is legal because no single CTR triggers (structuring to evade reporting is itself a crime); reading the law as a checklist and missing the risk-based intent behind it.

## Chapter 3: Onboarding, KYC, and Customer Due Diligence
- **Core questions**: Do we actually know who this customer is and what they will plausibly do? Which missing fact would change the risk rating? When is "enough" documentation actually enough?
- **Key concepts**: the Customer Identification Program (verify identity at account opening); customer due diligence and the risk-rating model; expected activity and the customer profile; source of funds versus source of wealth; beneficial ownership under FinCEN's 2018 CDD Rule (the 25% equity prong and the single control prong for legal-entity customers); enhanced due diligence triggers (high-risk geography, cash-intensive business, complex ownership); the separate, *shifting* Corporate Transparency Act BOI reporting regime to FinCEN — a related but distinct obligation whose scope has changed in 2025 and remains unsettled.
- **Applied skills**: write a customer risk memo that states the profile, the expected activity, the gaps, the risk drivers, and the recommended next step; identify beneficial owners from an ownership chart and apply the 25%/control test.
- **Common traps**: collecting documents without asking whether the activity makes sense for this customer; rating risk on industry label alone and ignoring the actual transaction pattern; conflating the bank's CDD beneficial-ownership collection with the customer's separate CTA/BOI filing duty; accepting a plausible story in place of a verifying record.

## Chapter 4: Sanctions and Watchlist Screening
- **Core questions**: Is this a credible match or a name collision? What facts separate this customer from the listed party? When must activity stop *now* rather than after review?
- **Key concepts**: OFAC sanctions and strict liability (intent is not a defense — a violation is a violation); the SDN and consolidated lists; fuzzy matching and why screening generates mostly false positives; politically exposed persons (PEPs) as a risk factor, not a prohibition; the OFAC 50% Rule (an entity owned 50%+ by blocked parties is itself blocked, even if not separately listed); blocking versus rejecting; the difference between a screening alert and a confirmed hit.
- **Applied skills**: write a screening disposition that states the match logic, the distinguishing evidence, and the escalation recommendation; reason through an ownership structure to apply the 50% Rule.
- **Common traps**: treating a fuzzy name match as guilt; clearing a true sanctions hit at the analyst level instead of escalating immediately; assuming a low-name-similarity score means safe without checking the 50% ownership path; letting a transaction proceed during an unresolved credible match because stopping it felt disproportionate.

## Chapter 5: Transaction Monitoring and Alert Triage
- **Core questions**: What is genuinely unusual *for this customer*, not just unusual in the abstract? What benign explanation fits the facts? What evidence is still missing before I can disposition?
- **Key concepts**: rules-based monitoring and scenario thresholds; the baseline / peer-group comparison that makes "unusual" meaningful; classic patterns (structuring, rapid movement / velocity, round-dollar activity, funnel accounts, mismatch with stated business); the false-positive reality of automated alerts; tuning a scenario versus suppressing it; the alert as a *hypothesis to test*, not a verdict.
- **Applied skills**: work an alert into a worksheet that separates observed facts, red flags, mitigating evidence, and a conclusion; decide whether an alert clears, needs more information, or escalates to investigation; recommend a tuning change with its rationale.
- **Common traps**: clearing an alert with an unverified convenient story ("seasonal business, makes sense"); judging "unusual" against the population instead of the customer's own baseline; suppressing a noisy scenario that is firing on a real pattern instead of tuning it; treating alert volume as productivity rather than measuring quality.

## Chapter 6: Investigation and Case Building
- **Core questions**: What is the narrative the evidence supports? Which facts raise suspicion, and which reduce it? Could another reviewer reconstruct my conclusion from the file alone?
- **Key concepts**: building a case chronology; link analysis across accounts, parties, and counterparties; internal records, transaction data, and adverse media as evidence with different weights; investigative scope (knowing when to stop pulling threads); evidence sufficiency for suspicion versus proof; the audit trail and why every conclusion needs its supporting record.
- **Applied skills**: assemble an investigation summary with timeline, parties, activity, red flags, mitigating facts, and a disposition recommendation; weigh contradictory evidence and state the residual uncertainty.
- **Common traps**: anchoring on the first hypothesis and reading every fact as confirmation; treating adverse media as proven fact rather than a lead to verify; expanding scope endlessly instead of reaching a defensible stopping point; recording the conclusion without the evidence that produced it.

## Chapter 7: SAR Decisions and Reporting Mechanics
- **Core questions**: Is suspicion genuinely supported, or am I filing to be safe? What does the narrative need to say — and what must it *not* overstate? Have I met the obligation correctly and on time?
- **Key concepts**: the suspicious-activity decision and its thresholds (banks generally file when a suspect is identified and the amount is $5,000 or more, or $25,000 or more regardless of suspect); the 30-day filing clock from detection (60 days where no suspect is identified); the SAR narrative structure — who, what, when, where, why, and how; SAR confidentiality and the legal prohibition on tipping off; continuing-activity SARs; the distinction between "must report" and "defensible to clear."
- **Applied skills**: draft a SAR-style narrative that is specific, sequenced, and free of overstatement; decide file-versus-clear on a borderline case and articulate the rationale either way; identify a tipping-off risk before it happens.
- **Common traps**: defensive over-filing that buries genuine suspicion in noise; narratives that assert conclusions ("the customer is laundering money") instead of laying out facts; missing the filing deadline because detection was logged late; any disclosure to the customer that a SAR exists or is contemplated.

## Chapter 8: Quality Control, Examiner Readiness, and Career Path
- **Core questions**: Would this decision survive a QA review and an exam? What does the program's health look like in aggregate? Where does this job go next?
- **Key concepts**: quality control and quality assurance as distinct checks; what a regulatory examiner actually tests (program adequacy, decision consistency, timeliness, documentation, remediation of prior findings); issue management and corrective action; common program metrics (alert clear rates, SAR conversion, backlog, timeliness) and how they can mislead; the career ladder — analyst, QC reviewer, investigator, sanctions specialist, monitoring/model tuning, BSA officer — and what each role demands.
- **Applied skills**: run a QA review of a peer's case and write reviewer feedback that improves the record; pair a volume metric with a quality metric to tell a true story about program health; map your own next role and the skills it requires.
- **Common traps**: treating QA as box-ticking instead of decision improvement; celebrating a falling alert backlog that was cleared by lowering rigor; presenting activity metrics as risk metrics to leadership; assuming examiner readiness is a project rather than a steady-state property of good documentation.

## Capstone
Take a single fictional bank customer from onboarding through a filed report, producing the work product at each stage. Build: (1) a one-page function map placing the customer's risk in the three lines of defense; (2) a KYC / customer due-diligence risk memo that identifies beneficial owners and rates the risk with stated drivers and gaps; (3) a sanctions screening disposition that resolves a fuzzy match and applies the 50% Rule; (4) a transaction-monitoring alert worksheet separating facts, red flags, and mitigating evidence; (5) an investigation summary with chronology, parties, and a file-versus-clear recommendation; (6) a SAR-style narrative (who/what/when/where/why/how) if you chose to file, with a short note on tipping-off risk and the filing deadline; and (7) a QA reviewer checklist a colleague could use to confirm the whole file is defensible. Close with one paragraph naming the single piece of evidence that, if it appeared, would change your final decision — the habit that separates a defensible analyst from a confident one.
