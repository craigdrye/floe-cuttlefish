# Medical Reasoning
**ID**: `medical` · **Discipline**: Healthcare · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Most diagnostic errors are not knowledge failures — they are thinking failures. The clinician usually knew the disease; they just stopped looking too early, anchored on the first plausible story, trusted a reassuring test that did not exclude the dangerous thing, or handed off a case with the uncertainty quietly sanded off. This course trains the discipline that catches those errors: how to gather the facts that actually move a decision, build a differential that ranks by danger as well as likelihood, decide whether a test will change anything before you order it, and say out loud what you are still unsure about. It is a reasoning and patient-safety course, not a treatment manual — you will leave better at *thinking through* a case, not at prescribing.

The spine of the course is the loop a careful clinician runs on every patient: compress the story into a one-sentence problem representation, ask what is most likely and what is most dangerous, gather the few facts that separate those, test only when the result would change the plan, and document so the next person inherits the reasoning rather than just the conclusion. Running underneath all of it is a single question that the National Academies' *Improving Diagnosis in Health Care* put at the center of the field: **could this reasoning be wrong, and what would I do to find out?** We treat cognitive bias not as a lecture topic but as a live threat to be managed at the bedside, and we treat communication — handoffs, uncertainty, safety-net instructions — as part of the diagnosis, not an afterthought to it.

This is written for clinicians in training, healthcare-adjacent professionals working from cases, medical writers and health-product teams who need to reason like a clinician, and advanced learners doing case simulation. It is deliberately framework-first and educational: scenarios are simulated, the goal is defensible reasoning under uncertainty, and the bar is not "name the disease" but "show the work that would have caught the miss."

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests clinical *reasoning* rather than treatment recall: building a problem representation, prioritizing a differential by likelihood and danger, deciding whether and which data to gather, interpreting a test against pretest probability, naming and defusing cognitive bias, communicating uncertainty, writing a defensible handoff, or reviewing a diagnostic error for its root cause. Keep cases generic and educational; this is not a place for dosing, procedure technique, or board-style minutiae about a single condition.

The voice is a senior clinician-educator running a reasoning rounds: calm, concrete, intolerant of hand-waving, and far more interested in *why* an answer is right than in the answer. The best questions force a prioritization — what is killing the patient, what test is falsely reassuring, what assumption is carrying the whole case — rather than a lookup. Distractors should be the real cognitive traps: the most available diagnosis, the satisfying-but-incomplete story, the negative test misread as exclusion, the confident handoff that drops the danger. Where a fact appears (a likelihood ratio, a red-flag pattern, a seriousness criterion) it must be correct and current, but judgment outranks recall: when a case is ambiguous, the right answer is the one that keeps the patient safe and keeps the reasoning reconstructable. Favor base-rate discipline over pattern-matching, and reward the answer that names its own uncertainty.

### Where to start
There is no single exam behind this course; it is the reasoning floor under clinical training, the USMLE/COMLEX clinical-vignette style, and competence frameworks like the AAMC Core EPAs and the Society to Improve Diagnosis in Medicine's diagnostic-excellence work. Work the chapters in order — the loop is sequential, and you cannot reason about which test to order before you can state the differential it is meant to resolve. After each chapter, do its applied skill on a real or invented case rather than re-reading; reasoning is a production skill, not a recognition one. Drill the Common traps as their own set, because nearly every error in a morbidity-and-mortality conference maps to one of them.

## Chapter 1: The Clinical Reasoning Loop and Problem Representation
- **Core questions**: Can I state this case in one disciplined sentence? What is the working diagnosis, and what is the safety net if it is wrong? Am I reasoning forward from the data, or backward from a guess?
- **Key concepts**: chief concern vs presenting complaint; the one-sentence problem representation (demographics + tempo + key qualified findings); illness scripts and how experts pattern-match; system 1 (fast) vs system 2 (slow) reasoning and when to deliberately switch; working diagnosis vs differential; the safety net as an explicit part of every plan.
- **Applied skills**: compress a messy narrative into a single problem-representation sentence using semantic qualifiers (acute vs chronic, focal vs diffuse, etc.); write a working diagnosis paired with the safety-net condition you are protecting against.
- **Common traps**: a problem representation that just relists symptoms instead of abstracting them; committing to a working diagnosis with no stated safety net; pattern-matching with system 1 on a case that demanded deliberate system-2 analysis; treating the chief complaint as the whole problem.

## Chapter 2: Triage, Stability, and Time-Critical Diagnoses
- **Core questions**: Is this patient stable right now? What cannot wait for the workup to finish? Which reassuring sign might be the dangerous false negative?
- **Key concepts**: the ABCDE / stability assessment before the diagnostic luxury of a full differential; abnormal vital signs as the cheapest red flag; the "cannot-miss" / time-critical diagnoses behind common presentations (e.g., the killers behind chest pain, headache, abdominal pain, dyspnea); escalation thresholds; the difference between *sick* and *not sick* as a first-pass judgment.
- **Applied skills**: sort a presentation into immediate action / urgent evaluation / routine follow-up and justify the line; for a common complaint, list the time-critical diagnoses you are obligated to consider before anything benign.
- **Common traps**: doing a tidy differential while the patient is physiologically unstable; reading normal vitals as reassurance in a patient who is compensating; anchoring on the common benign cause and never naming the rare lethal one; letting a calm-looking patient downgrade your urgency against the data.

## Chapter 3: Focused History, Exam, and Hypothesis-Driven Data Gathering
- **Core questions**: What single question would most change my differential right now? Which finding would move a dangerous diagnosis up or down? Am I gathering data to confirm a hunch or to discriminate between hypotheses?
- **Key concepts**: hypothesis-driven history (ask to separate competing diagnoses, not to fill a template); pertinent positives and negatives; symptom chronology and tempo as diagnostic data; risk factors as base-rate modifiers; the real sensitivity and specificity limits of the physical exam; the difference between a complete history and a discriminating one.
- **Applied skills**: for a given presentation, write the three history questions and one exam maneuver most likely to change the differential, and say what each result would mean; turn an undifferentiated complaint into a focused, discriminating workup plan.
- **Common traps**: collecting exhaustive data that confirms what you already believed (confirmation gathering); omitting the pertinent negative that would have excluded the dangerous diagnosis; trusting an exam finding beyond its real test characteristics; gathering breadth instead of the few facts that discriminate.

## Chapter 4: Building and Prioritizing the Differential
- **Core questions**: What mechanism ties these findings together? Which diagnosis explains the *whole* pattern, not just the loudest symptom? Which item is the tempting mismatch I should rank low but keep visible?
- **Key concepts**: anatomical and pathophysiologic frameworks; VINDICATE / systematic category checklists to force breadth; ranking by *both* likelihood and danger (the most-likely vs most-dangerous axis); base rates and how prevalence shapes the list; competing hypotheses and the discriminating feature between them; "explains the whole pattern" as a quality test.
- **Applied skills**: build a differential table with each candidate's supporting features, opposing features, and the next datum that would confirm or refute it; explicitly separate the most-likely diagnosis from the most-dangerous one and state how you are covering both.
- **Common traps**: a differential that is one diagnosis plus decoration; ranking purely by likelihood and dropping the low-probability lethal item; ignoring base rates (chasing the zebra in a horse population, or vice versa); forcing one diagnosis to explain findings it cannot, instead of allowing a second process.

## Chapter 5: Tests, Evidence, and Bayesian Updating
- **Core questions**: Will this test actually change management? What does a negative result fail to exclude? Given my pretest probability, what does this result really mean?
- **Key concepts**: pretest probability and how to estimate it; sensitivity, specificity, and positive/negative likelihood ratios; how a likelihood ratio shifts pretest into posttest probability (and why a "normal" result barely moves a high pretest probability); test and treatment thresholds; the trap of false reassurance from a sensitive-but-not-perfect test; pre-test framing before ordering, not after.
- **Applied skills**: write a test-selection memo for a simulated case stating the pretest probability, what result would cross the action threshold, and what a negative result would still leave on the table; explain why a given test should *not* be ordered because no result would change the plan.
- **Common traps**: ordering a test whose result cannot change management; reading a negative result as exclusion when sensitivity (or pretest probability) does not support it; ignoring pretest probability and treating the test result as the answer; chasing incidental findings the test was never meant to address.

## Chapter 6: Cognitive Bias and Diagnostic Error
- **Core questions**: What assumption is silently carrying this case? What alternative has not actually been tested? What single finding would prove my current diagnosis wrong — and have I looked for it?
- **Key concepts**: anchoring and premature closure; availability and recency bias; confirmation bias; diagnostic momentum (the label that travels and hardens across handoffs); search satisficing (stopping at the first finding); affective and framing bias; the diagnostic time-out / metacognitive pause as a debiasing tool; "what else could this be?" as a routine, not a rescue.
- **Applied skills**: apply a structured diagnostic-pause checklist to an ambiguous case (what's the worst it could be, what doesn't fit, what would change my mind); identify in a worked case which named bias drove the error and at what step it entered.
- **Common traps**: confusing a confident diagnosis with a verified one; accepting an inherited label without re-deriving it (momentum); stopping the search at the first abnormality that fits; treating a debiasing checklist as paperwork rather than re-running the reasoning.

## Chapter 7: Communicating Uncertainty, Documentation, and Handoffs
- **Core questions**: What does the next clinician absolutely need to know to keep this patient safe? Which uncertainty must be explicit rather than implied? What follow-up failure could turn a near-miss into harm?
- **Key concepts**: the assessment-and-plan as reasoning made visible (not a problem list); calibrated uncertainty language (likely / cannot exclude / pending) instead of false confidence or vague hedging; structured handoff (e.g., IPASS-style) carrying problem representation, differential, pending data, contingencies, and explicit if-then escalation triggers; closed-loop communication; safety-net / return-precaution instructions as a documented deliverable.
- **Applied skills**: write a handoff note containing the problem representation, prioritized differential, pending data, contingency plan, and a named escalation trigger; convert an overconfident assessment into calibrated uncertainty language without becoming uselessly vague.
- **Common traps**: a handoff that transmits the conclusion but drops the danger and the pending items; documentation that hides uncertainty to look decisive; "follow up as needed" instead of a specific if-then safety net; assuming a verbal handoff was received without closing the loop.

## Chapter 8: Case Conferences and Safety-Net Reasoning
- **Core questions**: Where exactly did the reasoning go wrong, and at which step? Was this a knowledge, process, communication, or system failure? What changes so the next clinician does not repeat it?
- **Key concepts**: morbidity-and-mortality and diagnostic-error review without hindsight bias; the taxonomy of diagnostic-error causes (knowledge gap vs faulty data gathering vs flawed synthesis vs system/handoff/follow-up failure); near-miss review as free learning; follow-up reliability and the diagnosis that depends on a result no one chased; system factors (workload, interruptions, fragmented records) as causes, not excuses; reflective practice and calibration over time.
- **Applied skills**: run a structured diagnostic-error mini-review on a simulated bad outcome — locate the step where reasoning failed, classify the contributing factors, and separate the individual fix from the system fix; write a safer next-time process rather than a blame note.
- **Common traps**: hindsight bias rewriting an uncertain case as obviously mismanaged; pinning a system failure on the nearest individual; calling every error a knowledge gap when it was process or communication; logging a lesson with no change to the process that produced it.

## Capstone
Work three simulated cases end to end and produce the reasoning record for each. **Case one (urgent):** a presentation with a time-critical diagnosis hiding behind a common benign one — assess stability, state the problem representation, build a differential ranked by likelihood *and* danger, and choose the test by what would actually change management. **Case two (ambiguous outpatient):** a vague, slow-tempo complaint with no single fitting diagnosis — show base-rate-aware reasoning, name the cognitive bias most likely to bite you here, run a diagnostic pause, and write calibrated uncertainty plus a specific safety net. **Case three (handoff failure):** inherit a case carrying a confident label and incomplete data — re-derive the differential, identify the diagnostic momentum and the dropped pending result, and write a corrected handoff with explicit escalation triggers. Then, across all three, produce a one-page diagnostic-error mini-review of where reasoning could fail and the system change that would prevent it. The deliverable is an ordered reasoning log per case: problem representation → prioritized differential → test strategy with thresholds → communication and safety net. The grade is simple and unforgiving: if the worst plausible diagnosis came true, would your documented reasoning show you looked for it — and would the next clinician have everything they need to catch what you missed?

## Assessment Ideas
- Problem-representation and differential exercises graded on prioritization by danger, mechanism coherence, and whether the dangerous miss was named.
- Test-selection questions graded on decision relevance, pretest-probability reasoning, and false-reassurance handling.
- Bias and error-review tasks graded on correctly locating the failed step and separating individual fixes from system fixes.
- Capstone graded on patient safety, calibrated uncertainty, handoff completeness, and overall diagnostic discipline.
