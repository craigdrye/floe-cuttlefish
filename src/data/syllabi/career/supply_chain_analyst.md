# Supply Chain Analyst
**ID**: `supplyChain` · **Discipline**: Operations · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Train learners to analyze supply chains as connected systems of demand, inventory, suppliers, production, logistics, service levels, cost, and risk. The goal is practical decision support, not spreadsheet decoration.

## Course Design Notes
Comparable supply chain programs from MITx, ASCM, Coursera, and university specializations emphasize forecasting, inventory, logistics, sourcing, analytics, S&OP, and resilience. This syllabus keeps those fundamentals while adding scenario communication and exception management, the daily work that makes analysts useful.

## Chapter 1: Supply Chain Map and Metrics
- **Key concepts**: plan-source-make-deliver-return, nodes, flows, lead time, throughput, service level, cost-to-serve.
- **Practice questions**: Where does value flow? Where does information lag? Which metric would show the system is failing?
- **Analyst output**: a supply chain map with nodes, flows, owners, metrics, and risk points.

## Chapter 2: Demand and Forecasting
- **Key concepts**: baseline demand, seasonality, trend, forecast error, bias, promotions, judgment overrides.
- **Practice questions**: What pattern is historical? What event is exceptional? Which forecast is good enough for the decision?
- **Analyst output**: a forecast review with error measures, assumptions, and recommended adjustment.

## Chapter 3: Inventory and Service Levels
- **Key concepts**: safety stock, reorder point, EOQ, lead-time variability, stockout risk, working capital, obsolescence.
- **Practice questions**: What service level is worth the cost? Which SKU carries too much inventory? Where is stockout risk highest?
- **Analyst output**: an inventory policy table with reorder points, safety stock, and exception notes.

## Chapter 4: Suppliers and Sourcing Risk
- **Key concepts**: supplier performance, on-time delivery, quality, dual sourcing, contracts, geopolitical risk, scorecards.
- **Practice questions**: Which supplier is reliable but expensive? Which risk is hidden by averages? What backup is realistic?
- **Analyst output**: a supplier scorecard with cost, quality, delivery, risk, and recommended action.

## Chapter 5: Operations and Capacity
- **Key concepts**: bottlenecks, utilization, throughput, takt time, queueing intuition, labor constraints, process variability.
- **Practice questions**: Where is the constraint? What happens when demand spikes? Which intervention increases flow?
- **Analyst output**: a capacity analysis with bottleneck, utilization, scenario, and decision recommendation.

## Chapter 6: Transportation, Warehousing, and Network Tradeoffs
- **Key concepts**: freight modes, warehouse design, last mile, network location, cost versus speed, carbon, resilience.
- **Practice questions**: When is faster shipping worth it? Which location reduces total cost? Where does consolidation help?
- **Analyst output**: a logistics scenario comparing cost, service, emissions, and risk.

## Chapter 7: S&OP and Cross-Functional Planning
- **Key concepts**: sales and operations planning, demand review, supply review, constrained plan, executive tradeoffs.
- **Practice questions**: Which demand should be prioritized? What is the constraint? What decision needs leadership?
- **Analyst output**: an S&OP briefing with demand, supply, gaps, options, and recommendation.

## Chapter 8: Resilience, Analytics, and Decision Communication
- **Key concepts**: disruption planning, scenario modeling, dashboard design, root cause, exception management, narrative.
- **Practice questions**: What could break the plan? Which dashboard signal needs action? How do you explain uncertainty?
- **Analyst output**: an executive memo translating analysis into tradeoff, recommendation, and next action.

## Capstone
Build a supply chain analyst pack for a fictional product line: demand forecast, inventory policy, supplier scorecard, capacity check, logistics scenario, S&OP briefing, and resilience plan.

## Assessment Ideas
- Forecast and inventory work graded on assumptions, error logic, and decision relevance.
- Supplier and logistics analyses graded on tradeoff clarity.
- Capstone graded on system thinking, prioritization, and executive communication.
