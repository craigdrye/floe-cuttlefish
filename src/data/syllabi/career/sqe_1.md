# SQE 1: Functioning Legal Knowledge for England and Wales
**ID**: `sqe1` · **Discipline**: Law

## Course Aim
The Solicitors Qualifying Examination Part 1 is the SRA's foundation-knowledge-and-application stage and, viewed honestly, it is an English-law-of-things-and-procedure machine. It does not reward recitation of case names or statutes for their own sake. It rewards the reader who, in 100 seconds, can identify which body of doctrine a fact pattern lives in, locate the rule by name and section, apply it to the only set of facts that matter, and then choose the single best answer when three of the options sound almost right. The exam wants newly qualified solicitor reflexes: spot the issue, separate procedure from substance, keep ethics one click away, and never confuse a clean rule with the rule the question is actually asking.

This syllabus treats the SRA Functioning Legal Knowledge specification as the contract between candidate and regulator. FLK1 covers Business Law and Practice (including partnerships, LLPs, taxation of business, and insolvency), Dispute Resolution, Contract, Tort, the Legal System of England and Wales, Constitutional and Administrative Law including retained EU Law, and Legal Services regulation. FLK2 covers Property Practice (residential and commercial conveyancing), Wills and Administration of Estates, Solicitors Accounts under the SRA Accounts Rules 2019, Land Law, Trusts, and Criminal Law and Practice. Ethics and Professional Conduct under the SRA Standards and Regulations weave through both papers and are never timetabled separately. Taxation (income tax, capital gains tax, inheritance tax, VAT) and the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 carve-outs likewise appear wherever they bite.

The goal is straightforward: build defensible working knowledge across the entire specification and rehearse the SBAQ technique until it costs you no thought. Floe condenses the SRA's five-option single-best-answer (SBAQ) format into four options because four-option MCQ is the format Floe carries; the discipline is identical — one best, three near-misses, every distractor a thing a tired trainee might genuinely choose. You will leave with confidence across business, dispute, contract, tort, property, wills, accounts, land, trusts, crime, and the constitutional plumbing that holds them together, and you will recognise instantly when professional conduct quietly takes over a question that looked like it was about land law.

The voice of this course is a supervising solicitor who is firm about citations, calm about deadlines, intolerant of guesswork, and unimpressed by elegant prose that ducks the rule. Cite by short name and section: section 2 of the Law of Property (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1989, section 9 of the Wills Act 1837, CPR 7.5, SRA Code paragraph 1.4. If you cannot name it, you cannot rely on it.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test SRA-specified knowledge for FLK1 or FLK2, English and Welsh procedural rules, statutory citations in their proper form, ethical conduct under the SRA Standards and Regulations, or the discipline of choosing the single best answer under exam time pressure. Use English-law terminology throughout: claimant not plaintiff, particulars of claim not complaint, defence not answer, without prejudice and without prejudice save as to costs in their proper places, High Court / County Court / Crown Court / Magistrates' Court / Court of Appeal / Supreme Court rather than vague references to "the court". Statutes go by short name with section, not by acronym only.

Floe presents four options per question — one correct, three plausible distractors — rather than the SRA's five. Each distractor must remain a serious trap, not filler: a correct rule applied to wrong facts, a wrong rule applied to correct facts, a confusion of two adjacent doctrines, or an ethical breach hiding inside a tactical recommendation. The course voice should sound like a supervising solicitor: firm about ethics, calm about deadlines, impatient with templated answers. SQE 1 sits in the UK English-law domain on Floe; never import US procedural language into an English question.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The SQE 1 in its post-2024 format consists of two papers, FLK1 and FLK2, sat on separate days. Each paper is 180 SBAQs delivered as 2 sessions of 90 questions, each session 2 hours 33 minutes long, for roughly 5 hours of testing per paper. The SRA sets the pass mark per sitting (typically in the 56-60% range) by reference to an Angoff-style standard-setting exercise — there is no fixed cut score across sittings. Ethics is integrated into both papers and is not awarded a separate weighting. Taxation and FSMA appear wherever they are realistic. The SRA does not publish topic weights; the specification is the contract.

| Topic | Paper | Study Posture |
|---|---|---|
| Business Law and Practice | FLK1 | High volume; partnership, LLP, company, insolvency, and business tax all sit here |
| Dispute Resolution | FLK1 | CPR muscle memory; track timings and Part 36 mechanics carefully |
| Contract | FLK1 | Foundational; revisit weekly with remedies and misrepresentation |
| Tort | FLK1 | Negligence elements plus occupiers, nuisance, Rylands, vicarious, product |
| Legal System of England and Wales | FLK1 | Short but cheap marks; courts, precedent, sources, statutory interpretation |
| Constitutional, Administrative and EU Law | FLK1 | JR grounds, HRA 1998, retained EU law under EUWA 2018 and REUL Act 2023 |
| Legal Services | FLK1 | Regulation, complaints, money laundering, FSMA section 327 carve-out |
| Property Practice | FLK2 | Residential and commercial conveyancing; SDLT on the transaction |
| Wills and Administration of Estates | FLK2 | Validity, intestacy, IHT including NRB and RNRB, grant procedure |
| Solicitors Accounts | FLK2 | Client account discipline under SRA Accounts Rules 2019 |
| Land Law | FLK2 | Easements, covenants, mortgages, co-ownership, LRA 2002 registration |
| Trusts | FLK2 | Express, resulting, constructive; certainties; Trustee Act 2000 duties |
| Criminal Law and Practice | FLK2 | Offences, PACE 1984, sentencing, evidence basics |
| Ethics and Professional Conduct | Both | Woven through every topic; never optional |

Suggested cadence: 24-36 weeks of study, 400-600 hours total, daily retrieval practice, and a weekly 90-question mixed paper that mirrors a real session length. Anchor materials to a recognised provider: QLTS, BPP, The University of Law, BarBri, or Kaplan SQE — and use the official SRA practice questions release for calibration. Read once, retrieve three times; reading a textbook without timed retrieval is wallpaper with a statute citation on it.

## Chapter 1: Ethics and Professional Conduct in Every Answer
**Core questions:** What does the SRA Standards and Regulations require when client interest, duty to the court, and commercial pressure pull in three directions? When does professional conduct override the substantive answer a question seems to want?

**Key concepts**
- SRA Principles 1-7 and the Code of Conduct for Solicitors, RELs and RFLs
- Conflict of interest under Code paragraphs 6.1-6.2 and the limited consent exception
- Confidentiality (paragraph 6.3) and disclosure (paragraph 6.4) and where they collide
- Money laundering, proceeds of crime, source-of-funds checks, tipping off under POCA 2002
- Undertakings, duties to third parties, and the overriding duty to the court (paragraph 1.4)
- Equality, diversity, and inclusion obligations

**Applied legal skills**
- Translate a messy client scenario into the specific Principle or paragraph engaged
- Separate "decline to act" cases from "act with informed consent in writing" cases
- Recognise when an undertaking has been given by conduct and what discharge requires

**Common traps**
- Treating client confidentiality as absolute — Code 6.3 yields to the duty not to mislead the court (1.4) and to disclosure required by law (6.4(a))
- Acting for both parties on the same matter when their interests conflict — Code 6.2 requires a substantially common interest or competing for the same objective, with informed written consent and no rule against acting
- Reporting only "serious" breaches under paragraph 7.7 — the test is "any facts or matters that the SRA should investigate", which is broader than the trainee instinct
- Treating Principle order as decorative — Principles 1 (rule of law) and 2 (public trust) outrank Principle 7 (client's best interests) where they conflict

**Authoritative sources**
- SRA Standards and Regulations (current version) including the Principles and the Code of Conduct for Solicitors, RELs and RFLs
- SRA Functioning Legal Knowledge Assessment Specification

**Practice deliverables**
- Conduct trap deck: 40 short scenarios labelled by Principle, required action, and distractor logic
- One-page hierarchy chart: court duty, public interest, client interest, firm interest
- Weekly 30-question conduct set with written postmortem on every miss

## Chapter 2: Business Law and Practice
**Core questions:** How do partnerships, LLPs, and companies form, govern themselves, raise finance, and unwind? Which tax rules attach to which transactions and which directors' duties bite when?

**Key concepts**
- Companies Act 2006 incorporation, articles, shareholder agreement; share allotment under sections 549-551; directors' duties sections 171-177 (powers, success, independent judgment, reasonable care/skill/diligence, conflicts, third-party benefits, declare interest)
- Members' resolutions: ordinary above 50%, special at or above 75%; written resolution procedure for private companies
- Partnership Act 1890 default rules; LLP Act 2000 hybrid liability; sole trader exposure
- Share issues, debentures, fixed and floating charges, Companies House registration within 21 days
- Insolvency Act 1986: administration, CVA, liquidation; preferences (section 239), transactions at undervalue (section 238), floating charge avoidance (section 245), wrongful trading (section 214)
- Derivative claim under Companies Act 2006 section 260
- Business taxation: corporation tax, income tax on sole traders and partners, VAT registration threshold £90,000 (2024-25), CGT business asset disposal relief at 10% up to £1m lifetime, stamp duty on shares

**Applied legal skills**
- Choose the right vehicle for a client's commercial goal and tax position
- Draft a board minute that records a section 175 conflict authorisation properly
- Sequence the steps to put a floating charge in place and register it within 21 days

**Common traps**
- Calling a section 175 conflict authorisation when the conflict is in fact a section 177 personal interest declaration — different procedure, different threshold
- Treating a written resolution as available for removing a director under section 168 — section 288(2) excludes it; physical meeting required
- Confusing administration (Insolvency Act Schedule B1) with CVA (Part I) — different purposes, different moratoria, different stakeholder votes
- Treating CGT business asset disposal relief as automatic — qualifying conditions (5% holding, officer or employee, 2-year holding) must be met before the 10% rate applies

**Authoritative sources**
- Companies Act 2006; Insolvency Act 1986; Partnership Act 1890; LLP Act 2000
- BPP, ULaw, BarBri, and Kaplan business-law manuals; SRA FLK specification

**Practice deliverables**
- Decision tree comparing sole trader, partnership, LLP, and limited company outcomes
- Flowchart of insolvency routes with creditor priorities and director duties on the brink

## Chapter 3: Dispute Resolution and Civil Procedure
**Core questions:** How does a civil claim move through the courts in England and Wales? When does the CPR timetable forgive a slip, and when does it bite?

**Key concepts**
- Pre-action protocols, letter of claim, proportionality under CPR 1.1
- CPR Part 7 vs Part 8; County Court vs High Court allocation (£100,000 threshold for personal injury, £100,000 generally for non-personal injury claims, complexity overlay)
- Particulars of claim under CPR 16, defence and counterclaim, Part 20 claims
- Track allocation, disclosure under CPR 31 or PD 51U / Disclosure Pilot, witness statements
- Interim applications: summary judgment under CPR 24, strike out under CPR 3.4, default judgment under CPR 12
- Interim injunctions and American Cyanamid balance of convenience; freezing (Mareva) and search (Anton Piller) orders
- Expert evidence under CPR 35 and the Civil Justice Council guidance
- Part 36 offers: cost consequences for claimant and defendant; acceptance windows
- ADR mediation and arbitration under the Arbitration Act 1996

**Applied legal skills**
- Calculate a service deadline under CPR 7.5 and react when the deadline is missed
- Decide whether to issue in the County Court or the High Court based on value and complexity
- Read a Part 36 offer and explain costs consequences if it is beaten or not beaten

**Common traps**
- Treating Part 36 cost consequences as automatic on any reasonable offer — only Part 36-compliant offers trigger CPR 36.17; near-misses fall back on Calderbank
- Confusing summary judgment (CPR 24, no real prospect plus no other compelling reason) with strike out (CPR 3.4, statement of case discloses no reasonable grounds or is abuse)
- Calendaring service from issue rather than from the four-month CPR 7.5(1) window for England and Wales (six months for service out of the jurisdiction)
- Treating mediation as compulsory — courts can robustly encourage and penalise refusal in costs but cannot compel

**Authoritative sources**
- Civil Procedure Rules and Practice Directions; Civil Procedure White Book
- BPP and ULaw dispute resolution manuals

**Practice deliverables**
- CPR timeline poster from pre-action to enforcement with all default periods
- Worked Part 36 scenario set covering claimant and defendant offers

## Chapter 4: Contract, Tort, and the Law of Obligations
**Core questions:** When does a binding contract exist, what does it require, and what happens when it breaks? When does one person owe another a duty in tort, and what remedies follow?

**Key concepts**
- Offer, acceptance, consideration, intention to create legal relations, certainty, capacity
- Terms: express, implied by statute (Sale of Goods Act 1979 sections 12-15; Consumer Rights Act 2015), implied by fact and by law
- Exclusion clauses under the Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 (B2B) and Consumer Rights Act 2015 (B2C unfair terms)
- Misrepresentation Act 1967; rescission and damages routes
- Breach and remedies: damages on expectation measure under Hadley v Baxendale (remoteness), mitigation, specific performance, injunctions; frustration
- Negligence: duty (Caparo foreseeability + proximity + fair, just, reasonable; Robinson confines Caparo to novel cases), breach (Bolam reasonable practitioner, Bolitho logical scrutiny), causation (but-for plus Wagon Mound remoteness)
- Occupiers' Liability Acts 1957 and 1984; private and public nuisance; Rylands v Fletcher strict liability
- Vicarious liability employer/employee under Lister v Hesley Hall and close-connection test
- Contributory negligence under the Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act 1945

**Applied legal skills**
- Spot a battle of the forms and resolve it under the last shot rule
- Distinguish a representation from a term and pick the right remedy
- Apply the Caparo or Robinson framework to a novel duty of care question

**Common traps**
- Awarding rescission for innocent misrepresentation after affirmation, lapse of time, or third-party rights — bars apply
- Treating mitigation as a duty rather than a limit on damages — claimant is not bound to mitigate but cannot recover avoidable loss
- Applying Wagon Mound remoteness to contract damages — contract uses Hadley v Baxendale (reasonable contemplation), tort uses Wagon Mound (reasonably foreseeable type of damage)
- Confusing Rylands v Fletcher non-natural user with ordinary nuisance — strict liability requires the four limbs (accumulation, non-natural use, escape, damage of a foreseeable type)

**Authoritative sources**
- Sale of Goods Act 1979; Consumer Rights Act 2015; UCTA 1977; Misrepresentation Act 1967
- Occupiers' Liability Acts 1957 and 1984; Defamation Act 2013; HRA 1998 Article 8 case law

**Practice deliverables**
- One-page remedies map for contract and tort side by side
- Negligence flowchart with every defence parked at the right step

## Chapter 5: Property Practice, Land Law, and Solicitors Accounts
**Core questions:** How does a residential or commercial conveyance flow from instructions to completion? How are interests in land created, protected, and registered? How do client account rules prevent disaster?

**Key concepts**
- Residential conveyancing: instructions, drafting, pre-contract enquiries, official copies, searches (local, water, planning, environmental, chancel), mortgage offer, exchange with 10% deposit, completion using Standard Conditions of Sale 5th edition, transfer (Form TR1), HM Land Registry registration, SDLT (0/£250k/various bands; 5% surcharge for additional dwellings)
- Commercial conveyancing: FRI leases (full repairing and insuring), alienation, alterations, rent review, break clauses, service charge, dilapidations; Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 Part II security of tenure and contracting-out procedure
- Land Registration Act 2002 Schedule 3 overriding interests; notices and restrictions on the register
- Section 2 Law of Property (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1989 — contracts for the disposition of an interest in land must be in writing, signed by both parties, and incorporate all expressly agreed terms (or be by exchange of identical signed parts)
- Co-ownership: joint tenancy vs tenancy in common; severance under LPA 1925 section 36(2); TLATA 1996 powers and disputes
- Easements: express, implied (Wheeldon v Burrows, section 62 LPA 1925), prescription (lost modern grant; Prescription Act 1832)
- Covenants positive vs restrictive; running with the land at common law and in equity (Tulk v Moxhay)
- Mortgages, redemption, undue influence (Etridge), priority under LRA 2002
- SRA Accounts Rules 2019: Rule 2 (client money held in client account), Rule 3 (mixed payments), Rule 4 (disbursements), Rule 8 (five-weekly reconciliations), Rule 12 (records kept six years)

**Applied legal skills**
- Draft a Standard Conditions of Sale completion statement
- Spot the difference between a joint tenancy severance and a tenancy in common from the outset
- Reconcile a client ledger after a misposted receipt and identify the breach

**Common traps**
- LP(MP)A 1989 section 2 — contracts for the sale or disposition of land MUST be in writing, signed by both parties, and contain all expressly agreed terms. Lock-out, option, and land contracts in this form; bare oral promises fail. Do not confuse with the section 1 formality of a deed for the transfer itself.
- Treating risk as passing on completion — under Standard Condition 5.1.1, risk passes to the buyer on exchange, hence the requirement to insure from exchange
- Confusing a notice (LRA 2002 section 32) protecting a third-party interest with a restriction (section 40) restricting registered disposition — different mechanisms, different effects
- Reporting an Accounts Rules misposting as serious to the SRA when prompt internal correction and the breach register suffice — only material or systemic breaches engage paragraph 7.7

**Authoritative sources**
- LPA 1925; LP(MP)A 1989; LRA 2002; Wills Act 1837 (next chapter)
- SRA Accounts Rules 2019; Standard Conditions of Sale 5th edition; SCPC 3rd edition

**Practice deliverables**
- Conveyancing checklist from instructions to post-completion SDLT and registration
- Accounts rules drill: 30 short ledger entries to label as breach or compliant

## Chapter 6: Wills, Administration of Estates, and Trusts
**Core questions:** What makes a will valid, and how does the estate get administered through the IHT and probate steps? How do trusts arise, who holds the duties, and what can beneficiaries actually do?

**Key concepts**
- Wills Act 1837 section 9: writing, signed by the testator (or someone in their presence at their direction) with intention to give effect, attested by two witnesses present at the same time, each of whom signs in the testator's presence
- Revocation: by intention, destruction, marriage (Wills Act section 18), divorce (section 18A)
- Intestacy under the Administration of Estates Act 1925 as amended by the Inheritance and Trustees' Powers Act 2014; statutory legacy and life-interest abolition for spouses
- Family provision under the Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975
- Gifts: specific, general, demonstrative, residuary; ademption; abatement; failure
- Grants: probate (for executors) and letters of administration (intestacy or no executor proving)
- Inheritance tax: nil-rate band £325,000; residence nil-rate band up to £175,000 with tapering above £2m; exemptions spouse and charity; business property relief and agricultural property relief
- Transferable NRB under IHTA 1984 section 8A
- Trusts: express, resulting, constructive; the three certainties (intention, subject matter, objects); formalities including LPA 1925 section 53(1)(b) for declarations over land
- Trustees' duties: loyalty, the statutory duty of care under Trustee Act 2000 section 1, investment powers under the Trustee Act 2000
- Beneficiaries' rights including Saunders v Vautier where all beneficiaries are sui juris and absolutely entitled; variation under the Variation of Trusts Act 1958

**Applied legal skills**
- Draft a basic will compliant with section 9 and consider a witness's beneficiary status under section 15
- Calculate IHT exposure with NRB, RNRB taper, and transferable allowances
- Identify when a trust fails for uncertainty of objects (Re Baden discretionary trust test)

**Common traps**
- Treating beneficiary status as fatal to a witness's witness role — the will remains valid, but the beneficiary loses the gift under Wills Act 1837 section 15
- Forgetting that marriage revokes a prior will under section 18 unless made in contemplation of that marriage
- Confusing the Re Baden test for a discretionary trust (any given postulant) with the IRC v Broadway Cottages test for a fixed trust (complete list)
- Assuming RNRB applies to every estate — it requires a qualifying residential interest closely inherited by lineal descendants

**Authoritative sources**
- Wills Act 1837; AEA 1925; I(PFD)A 1975; IHTA 1984; Trustee Act 2000; VTA 1958

**Practice deliverables**
- Probate timeline with IHT400, grant, collection, and distribution
- Trust certainties drill: 20 short clauses to label valid or uncertain

## Chapter 7: Criminal Law and Practice
**Core questions:** What must the prosecution prove for the common offences, and how does a case travel from arrest to sentence? What rights protect a suspect at the police station?

**Key concepts**
- PACE 1984 and Codes A-H; detention clocks (24/36/96 hours); interview rules; identification under Code D (VIPER procedure)
- Right to silence and adverse inferences under CJPOA 1994 sections 34-37 (failure to mention facts later relied on, refusal to account for objects/marks/presence)
- Summary vs either-way vs indictable-only offences; plea before venue and allocation; sending under CDA 1998 section 51
- Sentencing under Criminal Justice Act 2003 section 142 purposes; Sentencing Council guidelines
- Theft Act 1968 section 1 (dishonesty per Ivey, appropriation, property, belonging to another, intent permanently to deprive); robbery section 8 (force or threat of force immediately before or at the time of stealing); burglary section 9
- Fraud Act 2006 (false representation, failing to disclose, abuse of position)
- Offences against the person: assault and battery (common law / CJA 1988 section 39); ABH (OAPA 1861 section 47); GBH/wounding sections 20 and 18
- Homicide: common-law murder; Homicide Act 1957 partial defences (diminished responsibility, loss of control under Coroners and Justice Act 2009)
- Defences: self-defence (Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008 section 76 "reasonable in the circumstances"); duress (per minas; circumstances); insanity (M'Naghten rules)
- Evidence: confessions under PACE section 76 (oppression / unreliability) and unfairness exclusion under section 78; hearsay under Criminal Justice Act 2003 section 114 (four gateways)
- Trial procedure in magistrates' and Crown Court; appeals; bail under the Bail Act 1976

**Applied legal skills**
- Advise a suspect on whether to comment in interview given adverse-inference risk
- Compare summary, either-way, and indictable-only routes and the consequences of each
- Apply the Sentencing Council guidelines step by step to a worked scenario

**Common traps**
- Treating section 78 PACE as available only after a section 76 finding — section 78 unfairness is a separate, broader discretion
- Confusing diminished responsibility (Homicide Act 1957 section 2, amended by CJA 2009) with loss of control (CJA 2009 section 54) — different elements, different burdens
- Treating an adverse inference as guilt — the inference is permissive and cannot be the sole basis of conviction (CJPOA 1994 section 38(3))
- Treating section 8 robbery as theft plus force — the force must be at the time of or immediately before stealing and for the purpose of stealing

**Authoritative sources**
- PACE 1984 and Codes A-H; Theft Act 1968; Fraud Act 2006; OAPA 1861; CJA 2003; CJA 1988; Homicide Act 1957; CJA 2009; Bail Act 1976

**Practice deliverables**
- Police station advice script with the key statutory time limits highlighted
- One-page table of homicide offences with their distinguishing elements

## Chapter 8: Public Law, Retained EU Law, and Legal Services Regulation
**Core questions:** Who can challenge a public body decision, on what grounds, and what remedies are available? How does retained EU law sit alongside domestic law after the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 and the Retained EU Law (Revocation and Reform) Act 2023?

**Key concepts**
- Parliamentary sovereignty; rule of law; separation of powers; conventions
- Judicial review grounds: illegality, irrationality (Wednesbury), procedural impropriety, legitimate expectation, proportionality (where engaged via HRA or retained EU principles)
- HRA 1998: section 3 interpretive duty, section 4 declaration of incompatibility, section 6 public-authority duty, section 7 standing, section 19 ministerial statements; Convention rights
- Retained EU law under EUWA 2018 and the REUL Act 2023 sunset and renaming to assimilated law
- Statutory interpretation: literal, golden, mischief, purposive; Pepper v Hart use of Hansard; ECHR-compatible interpretation
- Regulation of legal services: SRA, LSB, Legal Ombudsman; FSMA 2000 section 327 incidental-activities exclusion for solicitors

**Applied legal skills**
- Identify whether a claim belongs in judicial review or private law
- Apply a proportionality analysis to a qualified Convention right
- Spot when financial services activity needs FCA authorisation versus a section 327 exclusion

**Common traps**
- Treating a section 4 declaration of incompatibility as striking down — courts cannot strike down primary legislation; only Parliament can amend or repeal
- Confusing legitimate expectation (substantive vs procedural) — substantive requires conspicuous unfairness amounting to abuse of power
- Treating proportionality as the universal standard — only engaged via HRA, retained EU principles, or specific statutory contexts
- Assuming solicitors are fully exempt from FSMA — only the section 327 exclusion applies, and only when incidental and supervised by a designated professional body

**Authoritative sources**
- HRA 1998; EUWA 2018; REUL Act 2023; FSMA 2000; Senior Courts Act 1981 section 31

**Practice deliverables**
- Judicial review pre-action checklist aligned to the Pre-Action Protocol
- Regulatory map for solicitors, the SRA, the LSB, the LeO, and the FCA

## Chapter 9: Capstone — Exam-Day Discipline for SBAQ Papers
**Core questions:** How do you read 180 SBAQs across two sessions per paper without your judgment fraying? When do you trust your first instinct, and when do you flag and return?

**Key concepts**
- SBAQ pacing target of roughly 1.7 minutes per question (90 questions × 2hr 33min); session-level reset
- Single best answer technique: eliminate the impossible, compare the remaining two, commit, move
- Distractor patterns: technically true but not the best, correct rule wrong facts, right facts wrong rule
- Time per question discipline and recovery plans for slow patches
- Ethics override: when conduct quietly changes the right answer
- Citation accuracy: section number, statute name, year, in that order

**Applied legal skills**
- Mark up a long stem fact pattern in under 40 seconds
- Build a personal flag-and-return rule that fits your accuracy curve
- Convert each miss into a one-line rule on an error log card

**Common traps**
- Spending more than 2 minutes on a single SBAQ — you cannot recover the time without sacrificing later accuracy
- Changing first-instinct answers without specific contrary reasoning — net negative on every published study
- Treating the most authoritative-sounding option as correct — examiners reward the option that fits the facts, not the option that sounds the most expert

**Authoritative sources**
- SRA published SQE 1 sample questions; provider mock-paper bank; Floe error-log workflow

**Practice deliverables**
- Two timed half-papers per fortnight with full postmortems
- Error log spreadsheet sorted by topic, sub-topic, and distractor family
