# CFA Level I: Tiny Finance Mountain
**ID**: `cfaLevelOne` · **Discipline**: Finance

## Course Aim
CFA Level I is not one giant formula sheet. It is a professional judgment exam disguised as 180 tidy multiple-choice questions. This syllabus treats the official curriculum as the map, but trains the habits candidates actually need: reading a fact pattern without panicking, linking accounting to valuation, knowing when a calculator answer is nonsense, and keeping Ethics close enough that it can gently tap you on the shoulder before you do something expensive.

The goal is simple and serious: build a working junior analyst toolkit while preparing for the exam. You will leave with formula fluency, statement-reading stamina, security analysis basics, portfolio intuition, and a calm little exam-day machine in your head.

## Course Design Notes
Keep this course rigorous, kind, and retrieval-heavy. Route questions here when they test official CFA Level I topics, analyst calculator habits, ethical judgment, accounting interpretation, valuation basics, portfolio reasoning, timed multiple-choice discipline, or formula use in a realistic investment context. The course voice should make a difficult credential feel survivable without making the finance sloppy.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
Official Level I topic weights are ranges, not promises. Allocate time by weight, difficulty, and your personal error log.

| Topic | Exam Weight | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| Ethical and Professional Standards | 15-20% | Revisit weekly; judgment improves with repetition |
| Quantitative Methods | 6-9% | Learn early; it powers return, risk, and valuation work |
| Economics | 6-9% | Build intuition before memorizing graphs |
| Financial Statement Analysis | 11-14% | High-yield, detail-heavy, and best learned with statements open |
| Corporate Issuers | 6-9% | Connect governance, capital structure, and corporate finance |
| Equity Investments | 11-14% | Turn market structure and valuation into analyst muscle |
| Fixed Income | 11-14% | Practice cash flows, yields, duration, and credit until they behave |
| Derivatives | 5-8% | Keep the payoff diagrams close; no heroic fog allowed |
| Alternative Investments | 7-10% | Compare structures, risks, fees, liquidity, and diversification |
| Portfolio Management | 8-12% | Tie risk, return, CAPM, IPS basics, and risk management together |

Suggested cadence: 16-20 weeks, 300+ total hours, 5-6 study blocks per week, and one weekly mixed quiz. Every study block should produce either solved questions, a one-page concept sheet, a calculator drill, or an error-log entry. Reading without retrieval practice is finance-flavored wallpaper.

## Chapter 1: Ethics, Standards, and Professional Reflexes
**Core questions:** What does a fiduciary-ish professional owe clients, employers, markets, and the profession? When law, firm policy, and CFA Institute Standards disagree, what action best preserves integrity?

**Key concepts**
- Code of Ethics and Standards I-VII: professionalism, capital market integrity, duties to clients and employers, investment analysis, conflicts, and responsibilities as a CFA Institute member or candidate
- Material nonpublic information, misrepresentation, suitability, loyalty, priority of transactions, referral fees, and performance presentation
- Ethical decision-making frameworks: identify stakeholders, duties, conflicts, and required disclosures

**Applied finance skills**
- Translate a messy workplace scenario into the exact violated standard
- Separate "disclose and proceed" cases from "stop, refuse, or escalate" cases
- Write clean client-facing disclosures that are specific enough to matter

**Common traps**
- Picking the "most correct intention" instead of the "most correct action"; the exam rewards what you do, not how sincerely you feel about it
- Forgetting the stricter-rule principle when local law and the Standards conflict; the higher standard wins, and you do not get to plead jurisdiction
- Treating disclosure as a cure-all; some conflicts must be eliminated or refused, not merely disclosed
- Reading mosaic-theory work as insider trading, or labeling genuine material nonpublic information as "just analysis"

**Practice deliverables**
- Ethics trap deck: 40 short scenarios labeled by standard, required action, and distractor logic
- One-page "stricter rule wins" checklist for cross-border law, firm policy, and CFA Standards
- Weekly 30-question Ethics set with written postmortem on every miss

## Chapter 2: Quantitative Methods and the Analyst Calculator
**Core questions:** How do money, probability, and data move across time? What can a statistic tell you, and what is it politely refusing to tell you?

**Key concepts**
- Interest rates, discount rates, required returns, compounding, annuities, NPV, IRR, money-weighted and time-weighted returns
- Descriptive statistics, probability, normal distributions, sampling, confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, correlation, and regression intuition
- Data quality, visualization, fintech, big data, AI, machine learning, and the practical limits of model output

**Applied finance skills**
- Use a BA II Plus or HP 12C for time value, cash flow, and return calculations without menu wandering
- Interpret a statistical claim in plain investment language
- Diagnose whether a dataset is useful, biased, stale, or just wearing a very convincing spreadsheet costume

**Common traps**
- Confusing money-weighted return (IRR, sensitive to cash-flow timing) with time-weighted return (the manager-skill measure); the exam loves making you pick the wrong one
- Sign errors and stray calculator memory: forgetting to clear TVM registers or entering an outflow as positive
- Mixing up the standard deviation of a population, a sample, and a sampling distribution of the mean
- Reading a statistically significant result as economically meaningful, or treating correlation as causation

**Practice deliverables**
- Calculator speed sheet: 50 TVM/cash flow problems timed at 90 seconds each
- Mini research memo interpreting one regression table and naming its investment use and limits
- Formula map connecting return measures, variance, covariance, correlation, and portfolio risk

## Chapter 3: Economics for Market Context
**Core questions:** What determines output, inflation, rates, currencies, and market structure? How do macro forces show up in security analysis?

**Key concepts**
- Supply and demand, elasticity, market structures, firm behavior, aggregate output, business cycles, inflation, monetary and fiscal policy
- International trade, capital flows, exchange rates, currency regimes, cross-rates, forward rates, and interest rate parity
- Economic indicators and the difference between cyclical noise and investable signal

**Applied finance skills**
- Explain how a rate hike can affect equity valuation, bond prices, currency levels, and corporate financing costs
- Calculate and interpret currency cross-rates and forward premiums or discounts
- Build a short macro dashboard for an analyst morning meeting

**Common traps**
- Currency-quote confusion: misreading the base versus the price currency, and inverting cross-rates under pressure
- Forgetting that under interest rate parity the higher-yielding currency trades at a forward discount, not a premium
- Memorizing market-structure graphs without being able to say what happens to price and quantity when a curve shifts
- Treating a single backward-looking indicator (last quarter's GDP) as a tradeable forward signal

**Practice deliverables**
- Two-page macro briefing with one chart each for growth, inflation, policy rates, and currency movement
- Exchange-rate drill set covering cross-rates, bid-ask spreads, and forward points
- Market-structure comparison table: perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, monopoly

## Chapter 4: Financial Statement Analysis
**Core questions:** What do the statements say, what do the notes quietly modify, and how do accounting choices affect valuation?

**Key concepts**
- Financial reporting framework, regulatory filings, notes, auditor reports, income statement, balance sheet, cash flow statement, and statement links
- Revenue recognition, inventories, long-lived assets, taxes, noncurrent liabilities, leases, financial instruments, and quality of earnings
- Ratio analysis: profitability, liquidity, solvency, efficiency, coverage, and DuPont decomposition

**Applied finance skills**
- Reconstruct business performance from the three statements
- Compare firms after adjusting for inventory method, depreciation, capitalization, leverage, and unusual items
- Identify accounting choices that make earnings look smoother, shinier, or suspiciously well behaved

**Common traps**
- Cash-flow classification under IFRS versus US GAAP: where interest paid, interest received, and dividends are allowed to sit differs, and the exam knows it
- FIFO versus weighted-average effects in a rising-price environment: which one inflates ending inventory, which one inflates COGS, and what that does to ratios
- Confusing the deferred-tax story: temporary versus permanent differences, and which produces a deferred tax asset versus liability
- Treating net income as cash, or ignoring that an operating lease and a finance lease land in different statement lines

**Practice deliverables**
- Three-statement walkthrough for one public company with ten ratios and a brief interpretation
- Accounting adjustment lab: capitalize vs expense, FIFO vs weighted average, depreciation method effects
- Cash-flow classification quiz with notes on why CFO, CFI, and CFF errors are so sneaky

## Chapter 5: Corporate Issuers and Capital Decisions
**Core questions:** How do companies govern themselves, fund themselves, invest, and manage liquidity?

**Key concepts**
- Organizational forms, stakeholder interests, corporate governance, ESG considerations, business models, working capital, and liquidity
- Capital budgeting, NPV, IRR, payback, profitability index, cost of capital, capital structure, leverage, and payout policy
- Agency conflicts and the real-world messiness of "maximize value"

**Applied finance skills**
- Evaluate whether a project creates value using cash-flow forecasts and hurdle rates
- Calculate WACC and explain what can move it
- Diagnose a company's working-capital cycle and liquidity pressure points

**Common traps**
- Trusting IRR over NPV for mutually exclusive projects; when they disagree, NPV is the tiebreaker and IRR can flatter the wrong choice
- Putting sunk costs into a project's cash flows, or leaving out opportunity costs and externalities
- Using the coupon rate instead of the current market rate for the cost of debt, and forgetting the after-tax adjustment in WACC
- Confusing the marginal cost of capital with the historical average cost, or holding capital weights fixed when the structure is clearly shifting

**Practice deliverables**
- Capital budgeting model for two mutually exclusive projects with recommendation memo
- WACC worksheet with sensitivity table for debt cost, tax rate, beta, and capital mix
- Governance red-flag memo for a real issuer's proxy or annual report

## Chapter 6: Equity Investments
**Core questions:** How do equity markets function, and how do analysts estimate value?

**Key concepts**
- Market organization, order types, market efficiency, indexes, industry and company analysis, competitive positioning, and equity valuation
- Dividend discount models, free cash flow ideas, price multiples, enterprise value multiples, and asset-based valuation
- Differences among common shares, preferred shares, private equity features, and public market structures

**Applied finance skills**
- Read a quote, understand the trading mechanism, and explain the cost of immediacy
- Build a comparable-company table and defend which multiples are meaningful
- Connect industry structure to revenue growth, margins, reinvestment, and valuation

**Common traps**
- Misapplying the Gordon growth model when g is near or above r, or assuming a perpetual growth rate that quietly exceeds the economy forever
- Comparing P/E across firms with different capital structures or accounting choices without normalizing
- Confusing trailing versus leading multiples, or pairing an equity multiple (P/E) with an enterprise-value numerator
- Reading the three forms of market efficiency backward, or assuming "efficient" means "always correctly priced"

**Practice deliverables**
- One-page industry map with drivers, risks, and likely valuation multiples
- Equity valuation snapshot using at least two multiples and one intrinsic model
- Market microstructure quiz on orders, bid-ask spreads, indexes, and efficiency

## Chapter 7: Fixed Income
**Core questions:** How do promised cash flows become prices, yields, duration, convexity, and credit risk?

**Key concepts**
- Bond features, issuance, trading, yield measures, spot rates, forward rates, term structure, duration, convexity, and immunization basics
- Credit analysis, ratings, default risk, recovery, spreads, securitization, mortgage-backed securities, and structured products
- Interest rate risk, reinvestment risk, liquidity risk, call risk, and embedded options

**Applied finance skills**
- Price a plain bond, interpret yield measures, and explain why price and yield move opposite ways
- Estimate duration-based price changes and know when convexity matters
- Compare credit spreads and identify what compensation investors are demanding

**Common traps**
- Blurring Macaulay, modified, and effective duration; effective duration is the one to use when cash flows can change, as with embedded options
- Forgetting that convexity is always a friend to a long straight bond, and that a callable bond's price compression caps the upside
- Confusing yield measures: current yield, YTM, and yield to call are not interchangeable, and YTM assumes reinvestment at the YTM
- Misreading how a call option helps the issuer and hurts the investor, while a put does the reverse

**Practice deliverables**
- Bond math packet: price, accrued interest, current yield, YTM, spot/forward intuition, duration, convexity
- Credit tear sheet comparing two issuers in the same sector
- Securitization diagram showing cash flows, tranching, prepayment, and credit risk

## Chapter 8: Derivatives Without the Fog Machine
**Core questions:** What does the contract promise, what drives its value, and what risk is being transferred?

**Key concepts**
- Forwards, futures, swaps, options, underlying assets, settlement, margin, clearing, and counterparty risk
- Payoff diagrams, no-arbitrage pricing logic, cost of carry, put-call parity, forward parity, and one-period binomial valuation
- Hedging, speculation, leverage, and why "small initial investment" is not the same as "small risk"

**Applied finance skills**
- Draw and interpret derivative payoffs before calculating
- Explain the difference between price and value for a forward or swap
- Identify whether a derivative position hedges or amplifies the exposure in the stem

**Common traps**
- Conflating the price of a forward (set at initiation) with the value of a forward (which drifts to zero only at the start, then moves)
- Scrambling put-call parity; memorize the fiduciary call equals protective put relationship and rebuild the rest from it
- Forgetting that a long position in an option is limited-loss while a short option is limited-gain, unlimited-loss
- Reading "small initial outlay" as "small risk"; leverage cuts both ways, and futures can demand margin you did not budget for

**Practice deliverables**
- Payoff sketchbook for long/short forwards, calls, puts, covered calls, and protective puts
- Put-call parity drill with mispricing identification
- Hedging memo: choose a derivative instrument for a currency, rate, or equity exposure

## Chapter 9: Alternative Investments
**Core questions:** What changes when assets are less liquid, less transparent, more specialized, or fee-heavy?

**Key concepts**
- Real estate, infrastructure, natural resources, commodities, hedge funds, private equity, private credit, and digital assets
- Direct investment, co-investment, fund structures, compensation, lockups, liquidity, appraisal smoothing, leverage, and diversification
- Return measurement, risk sources, due diligence, and benchmark challenges

**Applied finance skills**
- Compare alternatives by liquidity, valuation uncertainty, fee structure, leverage, and role in a portfolio
- Explain why reported volatility can understate economic risk
- Evaluate whether an alternative asset is diversifying or merely complicated

**Common traps**
- Believing reported volatility and correlation; appraisal-based and stale pricing smooth returns and understate true risk and correlation
- Botching the fee math: management fees and carried interest stack, and a hurdle rate plus catch-up changes the split more than it looks
- Treating illiquidity as free diversification rather than a risk and a cost that needs compensation
- Assuming a hedge fund "hedges"; many strategies add leverage and tail risk rather than reducing exposure

**Practice deliverables**
- Alternatives comparison grid across seven asset categories
- Fee waterfall exercise for a fund with management fees, carried interest, and hurdle rate
- Due-diligence checklist for a hypothetical private fund allocation

## Chapter 10: Portfolio Management and Risk
**Core questions:** How do individual securities become a portfolio, and how does a portfolio serve an investor?

**Key concepts**
- Risk aversion, expected return, variance, covariance, correlation, diversification, efficient frontier, capital allocation line, CAPM, beta, security market line
- Performance measures: Sharpe ratio, Treynor ratio, M-squared, Jensen's alpha
- Portfolio planning, investment policy statements, fintech in investment management, risk governance, risk budgeting, and risk modification

**Applied finance skills**
- Calculate portfolio risk and explain the diversification benefit in words
- Use CAPM carefully, including its assumptions and common interpretation traps
- Draft a basic IPS-style summary from investor objectives and constraints

**Common traps**
- Forgetting the covariance term when combining assets; portfolio variance is not the weighted average of variances, and the cross term is the whole point of diversification
- Confusing the capital market line (total risk, standard deviation) with the security market line (systematic risk, beta)
- Mixing up the performance ratios: Sharpe uses total risk, Treynor and Jensen's alpha use beta, and they can rank managers differently
- Writing an IPS that states return objectives without reconciling them against the stated risk tolerance and constraints

**Practice deliverables**
- Two-asset and three-asset portfolio risk worksheet
- CAPM and performance-measure drill set
- Basic investor profile with return objective, risk tolerance, constraints, and recommended allocation logic

## Chapter 11: Integrated Practice and Exam Strategy
Level I rewards breadth, repetition, and composure. The exam is split into two 135-minute sessions of 90 questions each, so the default pace is about 90 seconds per question. All questions are equally weighted and there is no penalty for guessing, which means blank answers are tiny acts of self-sabotage.

**Practice sequence**
- Phase 1: Coverage. Read, annotate lightly, and answer end-of-reading or topic questions immediately.
- Phase 2: Retrieval. Mix topics, rebuild formulas from memory, and review every miss by concept, not just by correct answer.
- Phase 3: Mocks. Sit at least two full mock exams under timed conditions, then spend more time reviewing than testing.
- Phase 4: Final polish. Ethics, FSA, Equity, Fixed Income, Portfolio Management, and personal weak spots get the last concentrated passes.

**Exam-day habits**
- Read the final sentence first when a stem is long.
- Circle the qualifier mentally: most likely, least likely, most appropriate, least accurate.
- Estimate before calculating; if the calculator answer violates intuition, investigate.
- Guess strategically, mark, move, and return. The exam is not impressed by noble suffering.
- Keep Ethics warm through the final week; it is both high-weight and easy to lose by becoming overconfident.

## Capstone: Junior Analyst Simulation
Build a 12-page analyst packet for one public company:
- Business model and industry overview
- Three-statement summary and ratio analysis
- Capital structure and WACC estimate
- Equity valuation using comparable multiples and one intrinsic approach
- Fixed-income or credit perspective if the company has debt
- Macro and currency exposures
- Portfolio role: why own it, avoid it, hedge it, or size it modestly
- Ethics appendix naming conflicts, source quality, and disclosure needs

The capstone is not meant to predict a stock price with cosmic precision. It is meant to prove you can connect the CFA Level I toolkit into a coherent investment conversation without hiding behind jargon.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly mixed-topic quizzes with written error-log entries, not just score tracking.
- Calculator drills for time value of money, cash flow, yield, portfolio, and valuation calculations.
- Ethics scenario sets revisited throughout the course so judgment stays fresh.
- Two or more timed mock exams, followed by review sessions that classify misses by concept, reading error, calculation error, or pacing error.
- A final analyst packet defense that checks whether formulas, accounting, valuation, risk, and ethics cohere.
