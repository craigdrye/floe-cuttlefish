# NCLEX-PN: The Practical Nurse Licensure Climb
**ID**: `nclexPn` · **Discipline**: Nursing

## Course Aim
The NCLEX-PN is the National Council Licensure Examination for Practical Nurses, written by the National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN) and required for LPN/LVN licensure in the United States and parts of Canada. It is a computer-adaptive test that decides whether a candidate is safe to practice at the practical-nurse level. The exam does not reward the candidate who knows the most facts. It rewards the bedside practical nurse who consistently chooses the safest action within PN scope, with the right priority and timing, even when several answer choices look reasonable. The 2023 PN Test Plan reframes that judgment around the NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (CJMM): recognize cues, analyze cues, prioritize hypotheses, generate solutions, take action, evaluate outcomes — adapted to the PN's narrower scope.

The PN role is fundamentally about data collection and care-plan execution, not data interpretation and care-plan authoring. PNs collect vital signs, intake and output, signs and symptoms, and patient cues — but the RN performs the comprehensive initial assessment and formulates nursing diagnoses. PNs assist with and execute the plan of care; the RN authors it. PNs administer the bulk of routine medications by oral, intramuscular, subcutaneous, rectal, topical, ophthalmic, otic, vaginal, and basic enteral routes — but in most states do not give IV push medications, blood products, IV vesicants or chemotherapy, or first-time-dose medications without RN witness. PNs reinforce teaching the RN has already initiated and evaluated; PNs do not perform the first round of teaching on a new diagnosis or evaluate teaching outcomes.

The course voice should make a high-stakes licensure exam feel survivable without softening clinical safety. Floe's NCLEX-PN track treats the 2023 NCSBN PN Test Plan as the map and builds four durable habits: (1) a scope-of-practice reflex that instantly identifies which answer stays inside PN authority; (2) prioritization by ABCs → Maslow → unstable-over-stable → acute-over-chronic; (3) safe-medication discipline keyed to the high-risk drugs PNs administer (insulin, warfarin, heparin, digoxin, lithium, opioids, potassium); and (4) therapeutic communication that explores rather than deflects.

You will leave with a calm scope reflex, prioritization habits, safe-medication discipline, infection-control fluency, and a therapeutic-communication voice that does not false-reassure, advise, give "why" questions, or redirect away from feelings.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test NCSBN PN client-needs categories, scope-of-practice judgment, PN-level prioritization, delegation TO the PN from the RN (and FROM the PN to UAP/CNA), safe medication administration at PN scope, infection control, basic care and comfort, therapeutic communication, or the disciplined CAT habits the exam rewards.

The NCLEX-PN differs from the NCLEX-RN in three load-bearing ways, and Floe routes accordingly:

- **Scope.** The PN scope is narrower than the RN's. Items that ask the candidate to choose the action a PN should take must respect that PN cannot perform the initial admission assessment, author the care plan, push IV medications, hang blood products, do first-time teaching, evaluate teaching outcomes, or triage. PN can collect data, reinforce teaching, contribute to the care plan, administer most non-IV-push medications to stable patients, and supervise UAPs.
- **Delegation patterns.** Delegation TO the PN comes from the RN. Delegation FROM the PN goes to UAP/CNA — and only stable, predictable tasks within UAP training (bathing, feeding stable patients, ADLs, vital signs on stable patients, ambulating stable patients, oral suctioning, passive range of motion). The PN does not delegate professional judgment.
- **CJMM emphasis.** Both exams use the Clinical Judgment Measurement Model, but PN items more often anchor at "recognize cues" and "take action within scope" than at "generate hypotheses" and "evaluate outcomes."

The course differs from a generic AP Biology, Anatomy & Physiology, or pre-nursing program because it is licensure-grade: items reward the safest, scope-correct option even when other options look clinically reasonable.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The NCLEX-PN uses a Computerized Adaptive Test (CAT) format. Candidates answer between 85 and 205 scored items in up to 5 hours of testing time. The 2023 PN Test Plan uses the following client-needs categories and percentages.

| Client Needs Category | Weight | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| Safe & Effective Care Environment — Coordinated Care | 18-24% | Scope, supervision, delegation TO PN and FROM PN, advocacy, informed-consent assistance, hand-off |
| Safe & Effective Care Environment — Safety & Infection Control | 10-16% | Standard precautions, transmission-based precautions, error prevention, restraints, fall prevention |
| Health Promotion & Maintenance | 6-12% | Growth and development milestones, lifespan needs, screening, immunizations, lifestyle teaching |
| Psychosocial Integrity | 9-15% | Coping, grief, abuse, therapeutic communication, mental-health basics, end-of-life, cultural care |
| Physiological Integrity — Basic Care & Comfort | 7-13% | ADLs, mobility, nutrition, elimination, sleep, non-pharm pain management |
| Physiological Integrity — Pharmacological Therapies | 10-16% | Safe administration, expected effects, adverse reactions, PN-scope routes, hold parameters |
| Physiological Integrity — Reduction of Risk Potential | 9-15% | Vital signs, lab values, procedure prep and follow-up, complication recognition |
| Physiological Integrity — Physiological Adaptation | 7-13% | Acute and chronic conditions, fluid and electrolyte balance, emergencies |

**CAT mechanics.** The test starts with an item of medium difficulty. Each correct answer drives the next item harder; each miss drives it easier. The exam ends when the candidate's ability is estimated above or below the passing standard with 95% confidence, when 5 hours elapses, or when 205 items are answered. Minimum length is 85 items. "The test got hard" is usually good news — it means the engine is testing at the candidate's edge of ability.

**Same CJMM as the RN exam, PN-scope restricted.** Items use the six cognitive skills of the Clinical Judgment Measurement Model, but the action steps must stay within PN scope. If the "best" action belongs to the RN, the right PN answer is to collect data, notify the RN, and stay in scope.

**Study cadence.** Plan 6-12 weeks and 4-6 hours per day of mixed item practice plus targeted content review. The strongest predictor of passing is sustained question practice with deep rationale review, not rereading textbooks. Use Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN as the core content reference; ATI PN review for question bank volume; NCSBN Learning Extension Review Course (the test maker's own product) for the closest alignment to actual item style.

## Chapter 1: PN Scope of Practice, Supervision, and Delegation
**Core questions:** What can a practical nurse legally do, what must the registered nurse do, and what stays with the provider? Who can the PN delegate tasks to, and how do you document the hand-off?

**Key concepts**
- PN scope: data collection, signs/symptoms, vitals, I&O; contributing to the plan of care; reinforcing teaching; administering most non-IV-push medications to stable patients; performing routine dressing changes after the first sterile change; basic wound care; tube feedings; ostomy care; urinary catheterization; nasogastric tube management; oxygen administration via low-flow devices
- RN-only activities: initial nursing assessment with formulation of nursing diagnoses; authoring and revising the care plan; IV push medications; blood product administration; central-line meds in most states; titration of high-risk IV drips (vasopressors, insulin drips, heparin drips); first-time-dose teaching; evaluation of teaching outcomes; complex triage
- Provider-only activities: medical diagnosis; prescribing; writing orders; informed consent obtained from patient (the nurse may witness the signature only, not obtain consent)
- Delegation FROM the PN to UAP/CNA — only stable, predictable, routine tasks within UAP training: bathing stable patients, feeding stable patients, ADLs, vital signs on stable patients, transferring ambulatory patients, oral suctioning, passive range of motion
- The five rights of delegation: right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction/communication, right supervision/evaluation
- State Nurse Practice Act variation and the candidate's duty to know their state's scope rules

**Applied PN skills**
- Identify the one answer that stays in PN scope when other choices belong to the RN
- Document a delegated task with appropriate supervision
- Recognize the cue that demands immediate RN/provider notification rather than PN action

**Common traps**
- "PN can reinforce teaching, NOT do initial teaching." If the option includes the words "newly diagnosed," "newly prescribed," or "first time," the initial teaching belongs to the RN.
- "PN can change a routine dressing, NOT a first sterile post-op dressing." The first post-op sterile dressing change belongs to the RN or surgeon.
- "PN can give oral/IM/SQ meds, NOT IV push, NOT blood products, NOT IV chemotherapy." Any IV push option is almost always the wrong PN answer.
- Delegating a vital signs check on an unstable post-op patient to a UAP. UAPs take vitals on stable patients only; unstable patients require nurse-level assessment.
- Picking "the PN obtains informed consent" — only the provider obtains; the nurse witnesses the signature.

**Authoritative sources.** NCSBN 2023 PN Test Plan; Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN (Silvestri); ATI PN Comprehensive Review; the candidate's State Nurse Practice Act.

## Chapter 2: Safety and Infection Control
**Core questions:** How do you prevent harm to patients, staff, and yourself? When does a standard precaution upgrade to contact, droplet, or airborne isolation, and what PPE follows? How do you safely use restraints and prevent falls?

**Key concepts**
- Standard precautions for every patient every time: hand hygiene, gloves for body fluids, mask/eye protection for splash risk
- Contact precautions (MRSA, VRE, C. difficile, RSV, scabies, lice, draining wounds): gown and gloves on entry; dedicated equipment; private or cohort room
- Droplet precautions (influenza, pertussis, meningococcal meningitis, mumps, rubella): surgical mask within 6 feet; private room or cohort
- Airborne precautions (tuberculosis, measles, varicella, disseminated zoster, COVID-19 in aerosol-generating procedures): N95 respirator (fit-tested); negative-pressure airborne isolation room; door closed
- Hand hygiene: alcohol-based hand rub for most pathogens; soap and water for C. difficile spores, visibly soiled hands, and after restroom use
- Sharps safety and needle-stick protocols
- Fire safety: RACE (Rescue, Alarm, Contain, Extinguish/Evacuate) and PASS (Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep)
- Fall prevention: Morse Fall Scale; interventions for high-risk — bed in low position, locked; call light and personal items in reach; non-skid footwear; adequate lighting; bed/chair alarms; toileting rounds; ambulating assistance for confused or weak patients
- Restraints: least restrictive first; non-physical alternatives before physical; physical restraints require a provider order renewed every 24 hours; chemical restraints require a provider order; Q15-minute observation; Q2-hour release for circulation, ROM, ADLs, toileting; tie with quick-release knot to bed frame (not side rail)
- Medication error reporting and incident report culture
- Sentinel events and root-cause analysis

**Applied PN skills**
- Choose PPE that matches the pathogen, not the patient's room number
- Run a RACE/PASS response from memory under stress
- Pick the safest action when an answer tempts you to take a shortcut

**Common traps**
- Picking alcohol-based hand rub after caring for a C. difficile patient. Alcohol does not kill spores; soap and water is the standard.
- Picking a surgical mask for a TB patient. TB is airborne; an N95 in a negative-pressure room is required.
- Documenting "patient educated" when the PN only reinforced the RN's teaching. Document "teaching reinforced," not "patient educated."
- Tying a restraint to the side rail instead of the bed frame. Side-rail attachment can injure the patient when the rail is lowered.
- Skipping the Q2-hour release for restraints because the patient "is finally calm." The schedule is non-negotiable.

**Authoritative sources.** CDC Isolation Precautions (2007, updated); NCSBN PN Test Plan; Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN.

## Chapter 3: Pharmacology at the Practical-Nurse Level
**Core questions:** What does a PN safely administer, and what would you stop and ask the RN about? What classes show up most on the NCLEX-PN, and what are their absolute-must-know expected effects, adverse reactions, and hold parameters?

**Key concepts**
- The six rights of medication administration: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time, right documentation; plus right reason, right response, right to refuse
- Routes the PN performs: PO, sublingual, buccal, IM, SC, rectal, topical, ophthalmic, otic, vaginal, basic enteral (G-tube, J-tube), maintenance IV without push, intermittent IV piggyback in many states
- Routes that require the RN (or provider) in most states: IV push, blood products, IV vesicants/chemotherapy, central-line meds, titration of high-risk drips, first-time IV antibiotic doses (varies by state)
- High-alert drugs with narrow therapeutic index — double-check protocols: insulin, heparin, warfarin, digoxin, lithium, potassium chloride
- Insulin onset/peak/duration: rapid-acting (lispro, aspart) onset 15 min, peak 30-90 min; regular onset 30 min, peak 2-3 hr; NPH onset 1-2 hr, peak 4-12 hr; long-acting (glargine, detemir) onset 1-2 hr, no significant peak, duration ~24 hr
- Anticoagulants: heparin monitored by aPTT (goal 1.5-2.5× control); warfarin monitored by PT/INR (goal 2-3 for most indications); heparin antidote protamine sulfate; warfarin antidote vitamin K
- Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT): platelet drop > 50% or < 100,000 typically days 5-10 — paradoxical thrombosis risk
- Digoxin hold: apical pulse < 60 in adults, < 70 in children, < 90-110 in infants; toxicity signs include visual halos, nausea, bradycardia
- Beta blockers: hold for HR < 60 or SBP < 100 typically
- ACE inhibitors: dry cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema
- Lithium: narrow therapeutic range 0.6-1.2 mEq/L; toxicity at > 1.5; maintain sodium and hydration
- Antibiotics: peak/trough for aminoglycosides and vancomycin (RN typically draws); allergy histories; renal/hepatic dose adjustments
- Corticosteroids: hyperglycemia, hypertension, immunosuppression, peptic ulcer; never stop abruptly after long-term use (adrenal suppression — must taper)
- Opioids: respiratory depression as the lethal toxicity; reversal with naloxone; constipation prophylaxis
- Pediatric and geriatric dosing considerations; renal/hepatic adjustments

**Applied PN skills**
- Recognize the vital sign or lab that should hold a medication (digoxin at apical < 60; antihypertensives at SBP < 100; heparin at platelets dropping)
- Identify early adverse reactions (HIT, lithium toxicity, digoxin toxicity, serotonin syndrome)
- Reinforce teaching using simple, accurate language; document reinforcement

**Common traps**
- Administering digoxin at apical pulse 54 because the patient "looks fine." Hold and notify; do not administer.
- Giving the first dose of a new IV antibiotic via IV push. IV push is not PN scope in most states.
- Telling a warfarin patient to "stop eating green vegetables." Goal is consistent vitamin K intake, not elimination.
- Holding regular insulin when blood glucose is 180 because "the patient just ate." Regular insulin is given before meals; the dose covers the meal.
- Splitting a dose to "be safe" when the patient's pulse is borderline. Splitting is outside PN scope without an order.

**Authoritative sources.** Davis's Drug Guide for Nurses; Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN (pharmacology chapters); Lippincott Pharmacology for Nurses; ATI PN Pharmacology Made Easy.

## Chapter 4: Basic Care, Comfort, and Health Promotion Across the Lifespan
**Core questions:** What do patients need to feel safe, oriented, fed, mobile, and clean? How does age change the assessment and intervention?

**Key concepts**
- ADLs, mobility, transfer techniques, body mechanics (use legs, keep load close, no twisting)
- Position by condition: post-tonsillectomy — side-lying or prone for drainage; total hip replacement — abductor pillow, no flexion past 90°, no crossing legs; post-stroke — affected side supported, head of bed elevated; increased ICP — head of bed 30°, neck neutral, no flat or Trendelenburg; post-thoracentesis — affected side up; post-liver biopsy — right side down to tamponade
- Therapeutic diets: renal diet limits Na⁺, K⁺, phosphorus, and protein; cardiac diet (DASH) limits Na⁺ and saturated fat; diabetic diet uses carbohydrate counting; dysphagia diet uses thickened liquids (nectar, honey, pudding consistency); low-residue for IBD flare; high-fiber for constipation; gluten-free for celiac
- Elimination: bowel and bladder retraining; ostomy basics (stoma should be pink/red and moist; black or dusky stoma is emergent); catheter care (bag below bladder level, secure tubing, no kinks); enema technique
- Sleep, rest, and non-pharmacologic pain management — positioning, heat, cold, distraction, music, guided imagery, massage, TENS
- Pain management principles: scheduled around-the-clock dosing for chronic pain; PRN for breakthrough; reassess pain 30 minutes after IV/IM, 60 minutes after PO
- Lifespan milestones: 2 months — social smile, head holds briefly; 4 months — head control, rolls one direction; 6-8 months — sits unsupported, transfers objects; 9-12 months — pulls to stand, pincer grasp, first words; 12-15 months — walks; 2 years — two-word phrases, half-speech intelligible; 3 years — three-word sentences, fully intelligible to strangers
- Adolescent risk factors: identity vs. role confusion; risk-taking; eating disorders; substance use; safer-sex teaching
- Adult screening recommendations: blood pressure annual; cholesterol every 4-6 years; mammography (varies by guideline body); colonoscopy starting at 45 (American Cancer Society) or 50 (USPSTF); cervical cancer screening per age cohort
- Older-adult risks: polypharmacy, falls, delirium superimposed on dementia, malnutrition, social isolation
- U.S. pediatric immunization schedule highlights: at 2 months — DTaP, Hib, IPV, PCV13, RV, Hep B (second dose); at 6 months — first influenza eligibility; at 12-15 months — MMR, varicella, Hep A series

**Applied PN skills**
- Choose a position that protects the at-risk patient
- Match a therapeutic diet to a condition
- Reinforce age-appropriate teaching using motivational-interviewing language

**Common traps**
- Putting a post-tonsillectomy child supine and flat — risks aspirating blood and secretions.
- Crossing the legs of a total-hip patient. Adduction past midline can dislocate the prosthesis.
- Putting a head-injury patient in Trendelenburg. Lowering the head raises ICP; head of bed 30° is standard.
- Giving thin liquids to a dysphagia patient because "the patient says they can swallow." Speech therapy and chart orders dictate consistency.
- Encouraging an ostomy patient that a dusky stoma is "normal as it heals." Dusky or black indicates ischemia and is emergent.

**Authoritative sources.** Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN; ATI PN Maternal Newborn / Nursing Care of Children; Mosby's Manual of Diagnostic and Laboratory Tests; CDC Pediatric Immunization Schedule.

## Chapter 5: Psychosocial Integrity, Therapeutic Communication, and End-of-Life Care
**Core questions:** How do you respond to a patient who is anxious, grieving, angry, suicidal, or in a mental-health crisis? How does the PN reinforce care without crossing into therapy or psychiatric diagnosis?

**Key concepts**
- Therapeutic communication: open-ended exploration ("Tell me more"); reflection; silence; clarification; summarization
- Nontherapeutic communication: false reassurance ("You'll be fine"); advice-giving; "why" questions; changing the subject; minimizing; closed yes/no for emotional content
- Defense mechanisms: denial, projection, displacement, rationalization, regression, intellectualization, reaction formation, sublimation
- Grief (Kübler-Ross): denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance — non-linear; patients move between stages
- Crisis intervention basics: safety first, then de-escalation
- Suicide risk: assess directly ("Are you thinking about hurting or killing yourself?"); plan, means, intent; one-to-one observation for high risk; environmental safety (remove sharps, cords, belts)
- Abuse and neglect: signs (bruising patterns, fear of caregiver, malnutrition, inconsistent history); mandatory reporting in all states
- Mental health basics: anxiety, depression, bipolar disorder (mania safety — set limits, redirect, high-protein finger foods), schizophrenia (positive vs. negative symptoms), substance use, dementia vs. delirium vs. depression
- Dementia vs. delirium vs. depression: dementia — chronic, gradual, irreversible, global cognitive loss; delirium — acute, fluctuating, often reversible, attention deficits dominant; depression — persistent low mood, vegetative signs, normal sensorium
- Cultural and spiritual care: ask about beliefs; integrate dietary, modesty, and prayer practices
- End-of-life: hospice eligibility typically requires prognosis of 6 months or less; comfort over cure; pain managed by-the-clock; family support
- HIPAA and confidentiality: minimum necessary disclosure; patient consent for non-essential sharing

**Applied PN skills**
- Pick the therapeutic response that explores rather than redirects
- Identify safety risk and respond with the right level of intervention
- Document a mental-health observation objectively, using the patient's own words in quotes

**Common traps**
- Avoiding direct questions about suicide because "asking might plant the idea." Direct questioning does not increase risk and is the standard of care.
- Reassuring a newly diagnosed cancer patient that "many people beat this." False reassurance forecloses conversation and is nontherapeutic.
- Asking a depressed patient "Why are you feeling so negative?" "Why" questions sound accusatory; use open exploration instead.
- Picking restraints as the first response to an agitated dementia patient. Least restrictive first: redirect, reorient, address underlying need (pain, toilet, hunger).
- Confusing dementia and delirium. Delirium is acute and warrants immediate medical workup for cause (infection, hypoxia, electrolytes, medication).

**Authoritative sources.** Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN (psychosocial and mental health chapters); ATI PN Mental Health; Varcarolis Foundations of Psychiatric-Mental Health Nursing; National Hospice and Palliative Care Organization guidance.

## Chapter 6: Reduction of Risk and Physiological Adaptation
**Core questions:** How do you spot deterioration early using vital signs, labs, and patient cues? How do you prepare a patient for and recover them from a procedure within PN scope?

**Key concepts**
- Vital signs and lab norms to memorize: HR 60-100; RR 12-20; SpO₂ ≥ 95%; temperature 36.5-37.5°C; BP < 120/80; glucose fasting 70-99, postprandial < 140
- Electrolytes: Na 135-145; K 3.5-5.0; Ca 8.5-10.5; Mg 1.5-2.5; phosphorus 2.5-4.5
- Renal: BUN 6-20; creatinine 0.6-1.2
- Coagulation: INR 0.8-1.2 (therapeutic on warfarin 2-3 for most indications); aPTT 25-35 (therapeutic on heparin 60-80)
- Hematology: H&H 12-16 women / 14-18 men, hematocrit roughly 3× hemoglobin; WBC 4.5-11; platelets 150-400 thousand
- Fluid and electrolyte imbalances: hyperkalemia (peaked T waves, widened QRS, ventricular dysrhythmias) is the most emergent electrolyte derangement; hypokalemia (flattened T, U waves, weakness, digoxin toxicity risk); hypercalcemia (stones, bones, groans, moans); hyponatremia (confusion, seizures); hypernatremia (thirst, agitation)
- Pre-op care: NPO status (typically NPO after midnight or per protocol); consent witnessing within scope; pre-op checklist; remove jewelry, dentures; void before transport
- Post-op care: airway as first priority; pain assessment; turn-cough-deep-breathe q2h; incentive spirometry 10×/hour while awake; early ambulation; DVT prophylaxis (SCDs, prophylactic anticoagulation); wound assessment for hemorrhage, dehiscence, evisceration
- Diabetic emergencies: hypoglycemia — confusion, sweating, tachycardia (treat with 15 g rapid carbs if alert; D50 IV if RN-administered and unconscious; recheck 15 min); DKA in type 1 — hyperglycemia, ketones, acidosis, Kussmaul respirations; HHS in type 2 — extreme hyperglycemia, dehydration, minimal ketones
- Cardiac warning signs demanding immediate notification: chest pain with radiation; new dysrhythmia; hypotension; bradycardia; tachycardia; signs of fluid overload (crackles, JVD, peripheral edema)
- Respiratory warning signs: stridor, accessory muscle use, SpO₂ < 90%, sudden dyspnea, frothy sputum
- Neurologic warning signs: new focal deficit (FAST — face, arm, speech, time); decreasing LOC; pupil asymmetry; seizure
- Wound care, drains (Jackson-Pratt, Hemovac, Penrose), NG tube management, oxygen delivery devices (nasal cannula 1-6 L; simple face mask 5-10 L; Venturi precise FiO₂; non-rebreather 10-15 L; CPAP/BiPAP)

**Applied PN skills**
- Recognize the abnormal value that needs reporting now versus next round
- Prioritize among four post-op patients using ABCs and acute-over-chronic logic
- Calmly notify the RN with SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation)

**Common traps**
- Treating hyperkalemia muscle weakness as the priority cue when peaked T waves are also present. Cardiac findings always outrank musculoskeletal findings.
- Treating low urine output as the priority over peaked T waves with K⁺ 6.8. Treat the immediate cardiac threat first; address cause second.
- Ambulating a fresh post-op patient before pain control and orthostatic check. Skipping the pre-ambulation check produces falls.
- Forgetting that stridor post-thyroidectomy may signal expanding hematoma — airway emergency that beats every other concern in report.
- Picking aspirin for a patient with sudden facial droop. Pre-imaging aspirin can worsen hemorrhagic stroke; the action is notification, not initiation.

**Authoritative sources.** Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN (medical-surgical chapters); ATI PN Medical-Surgical; Mosby's Manual of Diagnostic and Laboratory Tests; AHA BLS provider guidelines.

## Chapter 7: Capstone — Exam-Day Discipline and the Adaptive Test
**Core questions:** How does CAT scoring actually work, and how do you behave during 85-205 items so that the exam ends with a pass? What habits keep accuracy stable when items get harder?

**Key concepts**
- CAT mechanics: items adjust to your demonstrated ability; minimum 85, maximum 205, can end at any point after 85 once the 95% confidence rule is satisfied
- "The test got hard" usually means you are answering at the boundary of your ability — a good sign, not a bad one
- Time budget: roughly 1-2 minutes per item on average; no item is worth 5 minutes
- Item types: multiple choice; multiple response (select all that apply — partial credit on the 2023 plan); fill-in-the-blank (often calculations); ordered response (drag-and-drop sequencing); hot-spot (click an image); chart/exhibit; audio
- Strategy: rephrase the stem in your own words; identify what is being asked (priority, scope, safest action, expected outcome, teaching evaluation); eliminate by safety and scope; choose the answer that protects the patient and stays in PN role
- Two-acceptable-options rule: when two answers both look reasonable, choose the one that most directly addresses airway/breathing/circulation, then safety, then PN scope
- Test anxiety management: box-breathing 4-4-4-4; refocus on the next item only; do not count items completed
- Scheduled breaks: optional 10-minute breaks after about 2 hours; food and water in the locker
- What to do after the screen goes blank: walk out, do not discuss content, do not check unauthorized sources, await result via Quick Results (most states) or the licensing board

**Applied PN skills**
- Default to the safest answer when two choices look equally reasonable
- Use ABCs → Maslow → unstable-over-stable → acute-over-chronic
- Trust the process — first-instinct answers are right more often than panic rewrites

**Common traps**
- Picking the longest or most professional-sounding answer. Phrasing is not a clue; safety and scope are.
- Selecting an option that "the nurse" could do without checking whether the option is in PN scope.
- Changing an answer when you cannot articulate why. Defend the change with a specific rationale or leave the first instinct.
- Trying to game the CAT by missing on purpose to make items easier. The algorithm cannot be gamed and missing lowers your ability estimate.
- Stopping to count how many items have appeared. The exam ends when it ends; counting wastes mental energy.

**Practice deliverables**
- Three timed 85-question simulations under exam conditions with rationale review for every item
- Personal "trap log" naming which distractor styles fool you most
- Two-page exam-day script for arrival, identification, breaks, and post-exam behavior

**Authoritative sources.** NCSBN 2023 PN Test Plan; NCSBN Learning Extension NCLEX-PN Review Course; Saunders Comprehensive Review for the NCLEX-PN; ATI PN Comprehensive Predictor.
