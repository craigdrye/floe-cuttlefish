# LSAT: Reasoning Without the Drama
**ID**: `lsat` · **Discipline**: Law School Admissions (United States)

## Course Aim
The LSAT is administered by LSAC and, since August 2024, consists of two scored sections of Logical Reasoning, one scored section of Reading Comprehension, and one unscored experimental section (which the test-taker cannot identify), each 35 minutes, plus a separately delivered LSAT Writing sample. Logical Games / Analytical Reasoning was retired in August 2024 and is no longer tested. Scores are scaled 120-180, with approximately 75-76 scored questions across the three scored sections (roughly 52-56 in Logical Reasoning and 26-28 in Reading Comprehension). LSAT-Flex, the pandemic-era short form, has been retired.

This course teaches the post-2024 LSAT as it is: a measurement of careful reading, structural thinking, and stamina under timed conditions. The test does not require outside knowledge. It does not reward memorized facts about law, science, or literature. It rewards the candidate who can identify the structure of an argument, infer only what the text licenses, and read a 450-word passage closely enough to map its argument and answer detail questions within seven minutes.

The aim of this course is operational fluency in two skills: argument analysis (Logical Reasoning) and structural reading (Reading Comprehension). By the end you should be able to identify any LR question stem within five seconds, anticipate the answer shape before reading the choices, and read an RC passage with enough attention to answer six to eight questions on it accurately in five to seven minutes. The discipline is not cleverness — it is repetition. A bored LSAT-taker who has seen the patterns eight hundred times is faster, calmer, and more accurate than a clever one who improvises.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions to this track only when they test the post-August-2024 LSAT format. Logical Reasoning question types covered: Must Be True, Most Strongly Supported, Main Point, Method/Argument Structure, Role of a Statement, Flaw, Strengthen, Weaken, Necessary Assumption, Sufficient Assumption, Principle (justify or apply), Parallel Reasoning, Parallel Flaw, Paradox/Resolve. Reading Comprehension question types covered: main point, primary purpose, author attitude, inference, function of paragraph, application to a new scenario, organization, comparative passage relationship. This track is distinct from MBE: the LSAT tests no law, only reasoning and reading. It is distinct from MPRE: there are no rules to memorize, only argument structure and inference. Every question should reward textual citation over instinct.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
Current LSAT format (each scored section 35 minutes; total scored time ~105 minutes plus a ~70-minute experimental section and breaks; LSAT Writing delivered separately).

The error log should categorize misses by question type, time spent on the question, and confidence — not just right or wrong.

## Chapter 1: Logical Reasoning Foundations
**Core questions:** What is an argument, which parts of it does the question stem care about, and what is the logical move at the center?

**Key concepts**
- Argument anatomy: premise, sub-conclusion, main conclusion; non-argumentative stimuli (sets of facts; explanations)
- Conditional logic: "if A then B" (A sufficient, B necessary); contrapositive "if not B then not A" (logically equivalent); the converse "if B then A" and the inverse "if not A then not B" are NOT logically equivalent
- Conditional indicators: sufficient indicators (if, when, all, every, any, in order to, people who); necessary indicators (then, only, only if, must, requires, depends on, unless = "if not")
- Compound conditionals: chains (A → B → C); the chain is only as strong as its weakest link
- Quantifier logic: "all," "most," "some" (existential — at least one); valid inferences (all + most = some; some + all = some; most + most overlap = some)
- Causal reasoning: correlation vs. causation; alternative cause; reverse causation; chance/coincidence; selection effect
- Common LSAT flaws by name: confusing necessary with sufficient; confusing correlation with causation; equivocation; straw man; ad hominem (or "source"); circular reasoning; appeal to authority; appeal to ignorance (absence of evidence as evidence of absence); hasty generalization (unrepresentative sample); composition/division; false dichotomy; scope shift; mistake of degree; post hoc ergo propter hoc

**Applied skills**
- Diagram a conditional and its contrapositive in 10 seconds
- Identify a causal argument's structure (cause/effect/relationship) and the three alternative explanations
- Name a flaw precisely without paraphrasing

**Common traps**
- Necessary vs. sufficient: "if A then B" means A is sufficient for B and B is necessary for A. The contrapositive "if not B then not A" is logically equivalent. The CONVERSE "if B then A" is NOT necessarily true. Test-takers confuse these constantly.
- Treating "most" as "almost all" (most means more than half, including 51%)
- Treating "some" as "many" (some means at least one)
- Inferring causation from correlation without ruling out alternatives

**Authoritative sources:** LSAC LSAT Prep Guide; PowerScore LSAT Logical Reasoning Bible; 7Sage curriculum.

## Chapter 2: Helpful Family — Strengthen, Weaken, Necessary Assumption, Sufficient Assumption, Principle
**Core questions:** What unstated belief is the argument standing on, and how would you push it over or prop it up without overshooting?

**Key concepts**
- Necessary assumption (Negation Test): assume the negation of the candidate; if the argument collapses, it is necessary. Necessary assumptions are usually modest — "no other cause," "the survey was not biased," "the sample is representative"
- Sufficient assumption: when added to the premises, makes the conclusion follow with certainty. Sufficient assumptions are usually stronger and often patch a conditional gap (mapping a premise term to a conclusion term)
- Strengthen: any choice that makes the conclusion more likely; common shapes — ruling out an alternative cause, supplying a missing link, providing a similar precedent
- Weaken: any choice that makes the conclusion less likely; common shapes — alternative cause, reverse causation, unrepresentative sample, missing counterexample, scope mismatch
- Principle: justify (which abstract principle, if assumed, justifies the specific conclusion?) and apply (given an abstract principle in the stimulus, which specific case conforms?)

**Applied skills**
- Run the Negation Test mechanically on every Necessary Assumption answer choice
- Recognize the "term-shift" gap that Sufficient Assumption questions always exploit (a term in the conclusion that does not match a term in the premise)
- For Strengthen/Weaken causal arguments, anticipate alternative causes before reading the choices

**Common traps**
- Picking a too-strong Necessary Assumption (the necessary one is the weakest one that still kills the argument when negated)
- Treating Strengthen as "agrees with the conclusion" (it must affect the conclusion's probability via the argument's structure)
- For Principle-Apply, picking the choice that matches the topic rather than the abstract rule
- For Sufficient Assumption, missing the term-shift and picking a choice that does not bridge premise to conclusion

**Authoritative sources:** PowerScore LR Bible (Chapters on Assumption and Strengthen/Weaken families); 7Sage core curriculum.

## Chapter 3: Descriptive Family — Must Be True, Most Strongly Supported, Main Point, Role, Method
**Core questions:** What does the stimulus actually say, what does it most strongly support, what is its conclusion, and how is the argument built?

**Key concepts**
- Must Be True: airtight inference from the stimulus alone; no outside knowledge; correct answers usually combine two stated facts via conditional or quantifier logic
- Most Strongly Supported: best-supported by the stimulus, not strictly proven; correct answers track the strongest pattern in the text without overreach
- Main Point: the conclusion the argument is making (not the topic, not a premise, not a sub-conclusion); the "what is the author trying to convince me of?" question
- Role of a Statement: how a specific claim functions in the argument (main conclusion, sub-conclusion, premise, evidence for a sub-conclusion, evidence the author opposes, background)
- Method / Argument Structure: how the argument is built (e.g., "the author counters a hypothesis by providing a counterexample"; "the author cites an authority and applies its principle to a specific case")

**Applied skills**
- Detect conclusion indicators (therefore, thus, hence, so, accordingly, it follows that, consequently); detect premise indicators (because, since, given that, for, in light of)
- Distinguish Main Point from a sub-conclusion by asking "what supports what?"
- Match a candidate Method answer to the structural moves actually present (no extra moves; no missing moves)

**Common traps**
- Picking the topic instead of the conclusion for Main Point
- Picking an MBT answer that is "probably true" but not certain (must means must)
- Treating Most Strongly Supported as a Must Be True (MSS is weaker; MBT is airtight)
- For Method, picking the choice that adds a step the author did not actually take

**Authoritative sources:** PowerScore LR Bible (Must Be True chapter; Role and Method chapters).

## Chapter 4: Disagreement Family — Flaw, Parallel Reasoning, Parallel Flaw, Paradox
**Core questions:** What went wrong in the reasoning, what else went wrong in the same way, and what fact would dissolve the apparent contradiction?

**Key concepts**
- Flaw: name the specific reasoning error using the standard taxonomy (necessary/sufficient confusion, correlation/causation, ad hominem, straw man, equivocation, hasty generalization, circular, appeal to authority/ignorance, scope shift, mistake of degree, composition/division, false dichotomy)
- Parallel Reasoning: matching structure (premise types, conclusion type, conditional vs. causal, scope of conclusion) regardless of topic; ignore subject matter entirely
- Parallel Flaw: matching the specific error in the same logical move
- Paradox/Resolve: identify the apparent contradiction (two facts that seem mutually inconsistent); find the fact that explains both without contradicting either; correct answers are usually third variables that reconcile

**Applied skills**
- Pre-name the flaw before reading the choices
- For Parallel questions, abstract the stimulus argument into a skeleton before evaluating answers
- For Paradox, articulate the apparent contradiction in one sentence ("how can both X and Y be true?") before searching for resolutions

**Common traps**
- Picking a Flaw choice that describes a possible flaw but not the one the argument made
- For Parallel Reasoning, picking the answer whose topic resembles the stimulus
- For Paradox, picking an answer that resolves only one half of the contradiction
- For Parallel Flaw, mistaking surface similarity for structural sameness

**Authoritative sources:** PowerScore LR Bible (Flaw and Parallel chapters); 7Sage flaw taxonomy.

## Chapter 5: Reading Comprehension Passage Strategy
**Core questions:** What is the author claiming, where in the passage is each claim located, and what is the author's attitude?

**Key concepts**
- Passage mapping: scope (what the passage is and is not about), purpose (why the author wrote it — to argue, explain, describe, critique), structure (how paragraphs relate), author attitude (positive, negative, neutral, mixed; with intensity)
- Four genres: law (cases, statutes, doctrines); natural science; social science; humanities (literature, philosophy, arts criticism)
- Active reading without over-annotation: one mental phrase per paragraph plus the location of the main conclusion; circle pivot words (however, but, yet, although, despite, nevertheless, in fact)
- Comparative passages: identify where Passage A and Passage B agree, disagree, address different scopes, or talk past each other
- Tracking reported views vs. author's view: "some scholars argue..." is not the author

**Applied skills**
- Read a 450-word passage in 2-3 minutes, then answer 5-7 questions on it in 5-6 minutes
- For comparative passages, build a two-column mental chart of A vs. B before reading questions
- Identify the author's attitude with a specific textual citation (a sentence, not a paraphrase)

**Common traps**
- Underlining everything; this slows reading without aiding retrieval
- Confusing a view reported by the author with the author's own view
- Reading the passage like a study text (slow, thorough); RC rewards strategic reading, not encyclopedic reading
- Forgetting that comparative passages are designed for cross-passage questions, not parallel single-passage questions

**Authoritative sources:** LSAC LSAT Prep Guide; PowerScore LSAT Reading Comprehension Bible; 7Sage curriculum.

## Chapter 6: Reading Comprehension Question Types
**Core questions:** Is the question about the whole passage or a specific line, and is the answer literal or inferential?

**Key concepts**
- Global questions: main point, primary purpose, organization
- Local questions: detail (often line-referenced), function/role of a sentence or paragraph, reference (what does "this" refer to?)
- Inference questions: what the author would agree with, application to a new case
- Tone and attitude: pinning author's stance with textual evidence; "most strongly suggests" vs. "explicitly states"
- Comparative-passage question shapes: which idea both authors would endorse, which one would dispute, how each would respond to a third case

**Applied skills**
- For a Main Point question, return to the passage to identify the conclusion sentence; do not rely on memory
- For a detail question, locate the line reference and read the surrounding context (5 lines above and below)
- For an inference question, never use outside knowledge; the answer is licensed by the text

**Common traps**
- Picking an answer that goes "one step too far" beyond what the text says
- For tone questions, picking an extreme attitude when the author is qualified
- For organization questions, picking an answer that adds a step the passage did not take
- For comparative-passage questions, picking an answer that is true of one passage but not the other

**Authoritative sources:** PowerScore RC Bible; 7Sage RC curriculum.

## Chapter 7: Comparative Reading and Cross-Passage Synthesis
**Core questions:** How do two authors relate, and what would each one say about the other's argument or about a hypothetical case?

**Key concepts**
- Mapping A vs. B: shared subject (the topic the two passages address); points of agreement; points of disagreement; areas where one is silent and the other is not
- Scope differences vs. substantive disagreement: A may address a narrower question than B without disagreeing
- Hypothetical application: which author would more likely agree with a new statement; which would dispute it; which would view it as outside their scope

**Applied skills**
- For each comparative passage, write a one-sentence relationship statement before turning to questions ("A argues X; B argues X with caveat Y" or "A and B agree on X but disagree on Y")
- Identify silent vs. opposed positions (silence is not disagreement)

**Common traps**
- Treating silence as disagreement (only express claims by an author license inference)
- Picking an answer that one author would endorse and the other would be neutral on, when the question asks for shared agreement

**Authoritative sources:** PowerScore RC Bible (Comparative Passages); 7Sage comparative reading sets.

## Chapter 8: Timing, Stamina, and Test-Day Process
**Core questions:** What is your minute-by-minute plan for each 35-minute section, and how do you maintain accuracy across four sections?

**Key concepts**
- LR pacing: target ~1:25 per question on average; the first 10 questions are usually easier (target ~1:00); the last 5-7 are the hardest (target ~1:45)
- RC pacing: per passage, 2-3 minutes reading + 5-7 minutes on 5-7 questions ≈ 8-9 minutes; four passages in 35 minutes leaves a small buffer
- Skip-and-return: leave a question after ~1:30 of confusion; circle it; come back with remaining time; never blank-bubble
- Fatigue management: brief mental reset between sections; do not over-eat at the break; bring familiar snacks and water
- Digital interface: practice on the LawHub digital format; highlight and underline efficiently; bubble checks are unnecessary on digital but pacing checks (10/20/30 minute marks) remain

**Applied skills**
- Run section-pacing drills with a stopwatch; record per-question time
- Identify which question types take you longest; develop a triage rule (e.g., skip Parallel Flaw on first pass)

**Common traps**
- Burning eight minutes on a single hard LR question and starving the last five
- Reading an RC passage like a textbook (slow); aim for structural reading, not memorization
- Treating the experimental section as identifiable (you can't); apply full effort to all sections

**Authoritative sources:** LSAC LSAT Prep Guide; PowerScore Bibles; 7Sage timing analytics.

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
**Pacing.** Each section is 35 minutes. LR: ~1:25 per question, with the first 10 easier and last 5-7 hardest. RC: ~8:45 per passage (2-3 minutes reading + 5-6 minutes questions). Never burn more than 2 minutes on a single LR question on the first pass — skip, circle, and return with the remaining time. Never blank-bubble; eliminate two and guess.

**Reading the call.** Read the question stem before reading the stimulus when efficiency requires it (especially for Parallel and Method questions). Recognize the question type within five seconds: stem-spotting saves minutes.

**Eliminating answer choices.** On LR, the wrong answers usually fail by (a) overshooting (too strong); (b) shifting scope; (c) introducing a new topic; (d) reversing necessary/sufficient; (e) being true but irrelevant. On RC, the wrong answers usually fail by (a) extending beyond the text; (b) misattributing a view (author vs. reported); (c) using extreme language; (d) being true of one comparative passage but not both.

**Heuristics that work.** "Most" means more than half. "Some" means at least one. The contrapositive is logically equivalent; the converse is not. The author is not every voice in the passage. Pre-name the flaw before reading the choices. Anticipate the answer shape on Strengthen/Weaken before reading choices.

**Heuristics that fail.** "Whichever choice sounds smartest." "Whichever choice mentions the same topic as the stimulus." "Whichever choice has the most LSAT-y vocabulary." None of these win points.
