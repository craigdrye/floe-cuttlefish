# Personal Finance: Money, Sorted
*The money skills nobody taught you in school. Budget without misery, escape high-interest debt, and stop overpaying for fees and scams.*

**ID**: `personalFinance` · **Discipline**: Practical Life Skills · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Most people pick up money habits by accident: a little from family, a little from whatever bank or card landed in their mailbox, and a lot from trial and expensive error. The result is a quiet, low-grade money anxiety that has nothing to do with how much you earn. This course is for the curious adult who wants to feel genuinely in control of their finances without a finance degree, a spreadsheet obsession, or a guilt trip about every coffee.

We start where money actually lives: a budget that gives every dollar a job, an emergency fund that turns disasters into inconveniences, and a clear-eyed plan for escaping high-interest debt. From there we demystify the jargon that keeps people overpaying, APR versus APY, compound interest, credit scores, expense ratios, so the financial world stops feeling like a rigged game you were never given the rules to. Then we cover the grown-up building blocks: insurance that protects you from the things that could actually wreck you, retirement accounts like the 401(k), IRA, and HSA, the basics of how taxes really work, and how to spot the fees and scams quietly draining accounts every day.

The throughline is judgment and order of operations, not complicated math. Pay yourself first. Grab free employer-match money. Kill the 24% credit card before chasing a 7% investment. Insure the big risks, self-fund the small ones. Never trust an urgent caller demanding gift cards. None of this requires being rich or a numbers person; it requires understanding a handful of plain-language ideas and doing the simple things in the right order, consistently.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want practical, real-world money literacy: a young adult getting their first real paycheck, someone climbing out of credit card debt, a person who keeps getting blindsided by fees, or anyone who feels vaguely behind and does not know where to start. This is a "smart, confident everyday adult" course, not a CFP or CFA exam-prep track, it favors mechanism and decision-making over formulas, tax-code citations, or investment-product deep dives. Questions are scenario-driven ("you have $300 spare, a thin emergency fund, and a 22% card, now what?") because that is how money decisions actually present themselves. The voice is warm and plain ("For Dummies" energy) but never dumbed-down-wrong: rules of thumb are flagged as rules of thumb, and where a near-universal truth exists (the employer match is free money, minimum payments are a trap, the IRS will not call demanding gift cards), the course is firm about it. Examples use round, illustrative numbers so the reasoning, not the arithmetic, is the lesson. Where U.S.-specific terms appear (401k, IRA, HSA, AnnualCreditReport.com, W-4), they are labeled as such.

## Chapter 1: Budgeting & the 50/30/20 Idea
- **Core questions**: What is a budget, really? What do 50/30/20 mean? What separates a need from a want? What does "pay yourself first" do? Why track before you cut? What is lifestyle creep?
- **Key concepts**: a budget as a forward plan giving every dollar a job, the 50/30/20 framework on after-tax income, needs vs wants, paying yourself first via automation, expense tracking, lifestyle inflation.
- **Applied skills**: building a simple budget; sorting expenses honestly into needs and wants; automating savings; tracking spending to find leaks; protecting savings when income rises.
- **Common traps**: thinking budgeting means deprivation; confusing categories with the 50/30/20 buckets; calling wants "needs"; cutting from memory instead of data; letting every raise get absorbed by spending.

## Chapter 2: Emergency Funds & Saving
- **Core questions**: What is an emergency fund actually for? How big should it be? Where should it live?
- **Key concepts**: cash reserved for true surprises, three-to-six months of essential expenses, the starter fund, liquidity and safety, high-yield savings, FDIC insurance.
- **Applied skills**: setting a realistic fund target; choosing the right account; keeping emergency money separate, safe, and accessible.
- **Common traps**: investing emergency cash in stocks; locking it in a long CD; targeting too little (a week) or hoarding too much; treating planned costs as emergencies.

## Chapter 3: Debt Payoff: Snowball vs Avalanche
- **Core questions**: What does the avalanche method target? Why does the snowball motivate people? What must you do regardless of method? Where should extra cash go?
- **Key concepts**: avalanche (highest interest rate first), snowball (smallest balance first), paying all minimums, prioritizing by interest rate, the math-vs-motivation tradeoff.
- **Applied skills**: choosing a payoff method that fits your psychology; always covering minimums; directing extra dollars to the most expensive debt; recognizing when 0% debt is low priority.
- **Common traps**: confusing snowball and avalanche; skipping a minimum and triggering penalty rates; spreading extra payments evenly; overpaying cheap debt before expensive debt; closing cards reflexively.

## Chapter 4: Credit Scores & Reports
- **Core questions**: What does a score predict? What is the biggest factor? What is utilization? Where are free reports? Report vs score? Hard vs soft inquiry?
- **Key concepts**: FICO/VantageScore as repayment-likelihood, payment history as the top factor, credit utilization, AnnualCreditReport.com, report (the file) vs score (the number), hard and soft inquiries.
- **Applied skills**: paying on time above all; keeping utilization low; pulling free reports and checking for errors; understanding why self-checks are harmless and applications are not.
- **Common traps**: thinking the score includes income or savings; believing card count matters most; paying for "free" reports; confusing report with score; fearing that checking your own score hurts it.

## Chapter 5: Interest: APR, APY & Compounding
- **Core questions**: How do APR and APY differ? What is compound interest, plainly? What is the Rule of 72? Why does starting early win? How does compounding hurt borrowers?
- **Key concepts**: APR (simple yearly rate) vs APY (includes compounding), interest-on-interest, the Rule of 72 for doubling time, time in the market, compounding against you on card balances.
- **Applied skills**: comparing loans by APR and savings by APY; estimating doubling time; appreciating why early dollars matter most; paying statement balances in full to dodge interest.
- **Common traps**: assuming APR and APY are identical; confusing simple and compound interest; thinking compounding is a one-time bonus; waiting for the "perfect" time to invest; ignoring daily compounding on cards.

## Chapter 6: Good vs Bad Debt
- **Core questions**: What makes debt "good"? What is a clear bad-debt example? Why is the minimum-payment a trap? What is debt-to-income?
- **Key concepts**: good debt funds appreciating/income-producing assets at low rates, bad debt funds depreciating/consumed things at high rates, the minimum-payment trap, debt-to-income ratio.
- **Applied skills**: judging debt by use, rate, and terms rather than labels; always paying above the minimum on high-rate debt; estimating DTI and understanding why lenders watch it.
- **Common traps**: judging debt by the lender or by any nonzero rate; mistaking minimum payments for fast payoff; thinking minimums earn a rate cut; misdefining DTI as lifetime totals or score-based.

## Chapter 7: Insurance Basics
- **Core questions**: What is insurance for? Premium vs deductible? When do you need life insurance? Term vs whole life?
- **Key concepts**: insurance as risk transfer (small known cost for protection from catastrophe), premium vs deductible vs coverage limit, life insurance as income replacement for dependents, term vs whole life.
- **Applied skills**: insuring big risks and self-funding small ones; balancing premium against deductible with an emergency fund; deciding whether you need life insurance; choosing term over whole life for most families.
- **Common traps**: treating insurance as an investment; confusing premium with deductible; buying life insurance with no dependents; assuming term builds cash value or lasts forever.

## Chapter 8: Retirement: 401(k), IRA & HSA
- **Core questions**: Why never skip the 401(k) match? Traditional vs Roth? What is an IRA? What is the HSA triple advantage? What is a sensible savings order?
- **Key concepts**: the employer match as a guaranteed 100% return, Traditional vs Roth (tax break now vs later), the self-opened IRA, the HSA triple tax advantage with a high-deductible plan, the priority order of saving.
- **Applied skills**: contributing enough to capture the full match; choosing Traditional or Roth by expected tax rate; opening an IRA; using an HSA as a stealth retirement account; sequencing contributions sensibly.
- **Common traps**: leaving free match money on the table; misunderstanding the timing of the tax break; confusing an IRA with a 401(k) or Social Security; thinking HSAs are unlimited or universally available; maxing an IRA before grabbing the match.

## Chapter 9: Taxes, Fees & Scams
- **Core questions**: How do marginal brackets work? Deduction vs credit? Why is a huge refund not a win? What is an expense ratio? How do you dodge overdraft fees? How do you spot urgency scams, phishing, and Ponzi schemes?
- **Key concepts**: marginal tax brackets, deductions (lower taxable income) vs credits (cut tax owed directly), refunds as interest-free loans to the government, fund expense ratios, avoidable overdraft fees, scam red flags (urgency, odd payment, recruiting).
- **Applied skills**: understanding that a raise never lowers take-home; valuing credits over equivalent deductions; adjusting withholding; favoring low-cost funds; declining overdraft coverage; refusing gift-card demands and phishing links.
- **Common traps**: thinking a higher bracket taxes all income; confusing deductions and credits; celebrating a giant refund; ignoring expense ratios; opting into overdraft coverage; trusting urgent callers, surprise links, and guaranteed-return pitches.

## Chapter 10: Putting It All Together
- **Core questions**: Where does spare cash go first? Why is idle cash a quiet risk? Why diversify? What hides in the fine print? What is the overall order of operations?
- **Key concepts**: high-interest payoff as a guaranteed return, inflation eroding idle cash, diversification, reading fine print on teaser offers, the personal-finance order of operations.
- **Applied skills**: prioritizing high-rate debt over investing; investing long-term money to beat inflation; diversifying with low-cost index funds; spotting temporary 0% APR traps; sequencing the whole plan correctly.
- **Common traps**: chasing investments while paying 22% interest; assuming cash is risk-free; expecting diversification to guarantee no losses; missing the post-promo rate jump; doing the right things in the wrong order.

## Capstone
Take your real money situation, or a realistic invented one, and run it through the whole course like a competent, calm adult. Start with the budget: where does the money go, and which expenses are genuine needs versus wants? Check the safety net: is there an emergency fund, and is it the right size in the right kind of account? List every debt with its interest rate and decide, snowball or avalanche, where extra dollars should land, paying minimums on the rest. Confirm you are capturing any employer match, the single best deal available, before investing elsewhere. Then look outward for leaks: fees you are overpaying, a refund that signals over-withholding, insurance gaps where dependents would be exposed, and any "too good to be true" offers or suspicious messages. Finally, write your own order of operations in one short list and name the next single action you will take this week. The goal is not a perfect spreadsheet; it is demonstrating the judgment this course builds, understand the idea, sort the priorities, respect the traps, and never feel helpless about money again.
