# How the US Government Works
A plain-language tour of the machine you live inside — branches, balances, and how power actually flows.

**ID**: `usGovernmentWorks` · **Discipline**: Civics & Government

## Course Aim
Most adults carry a hazy, half-remembered version of high-school civics: three branches, a few amendments, something about checks and balances. That fog is exactly what makes the news confusing and political arguments slippery. This course is the clear, unhurried explanation you wish someone had given you the first time — built for a curious adult who wants to *actually understand* how the United States governs itself, not memorize trivia for a test.

We treat the government as a system of people, powers, and pressure points. You will learn what each branch can and cannot do, why the framers deliberately made government slow, how the same power (taxing, spending, regulating) gets divided between Washington, your statehouse, and your city council, and what the Constitution really says versus what people assume it says. Every idea is anchored to a concrete, real-world scenario: a vetoed bill, a confirmation fight, a government shutdown, a pothole on your street.

The tone is friendly and strictly non-partisan. The goal is not to tell you what to think about any policy, but to give you the mental map that lets you follow any policy fight with confidence — to read a headline and immediately know which branch, which level, and which check is in play. By the end, "separation of powers" and "the power of the purse" stop being phrases and become tools you can use.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want grounded, non-partisan civic literacy: understanding institutions and mechanisms rather than current events, party platforms, or campaign strategy. It pairs naturally with practical-politics and current-affairs tracks (which cover *who* is doing what right now) by supplying the durable structural knowledge underneath them. The course assumes no prior coursework and is designed for adults returning to civics after years away. Questions favor recognition of the right mechanism over date-and-name memorization, mix easy confidence-builders with a few genuinely tricky distinctions (deficit vs. debt, mandatory vs. discretionary spending, separation of powers vs. federalism), and always reward identifying *which* lever of government is being pulled.

## Chapter 1: Three Branches and Separation of Powers
- **Core questions**: What does each branch do? Which article of the Constitution creates which branch? Why split power at all?
- **Key concepts**: legislative/executive/judicial branches, Articles I–III, separation of powers, Montesquieu’s influence, "ambition counteracts ambition."
- **Applied skills**: Given any government action, name which branch is responsible and why.
- **Common traps**: Confusing "making," "carrying out," and "interpreting" the law; assuming the President can create law; thinking the design is meant to be efficient (it is meant to be restrained).

## Chapter 2: Checks and Balances
- **Core questions**: How does each branch limit the others? What happens when they disagree?
- **Key concepts**: veto and override (two-thirds), Senate advice and consent, judicial review, impeachment (House charges, Senate tries), confirmation power.
- **Applied skills**: Spot a check in action in a real news story and identify which branch is checking which.
- **Common traps**: Mixing up who impeaches versus who tries; assuming a veto is final; believing one branch can command another.

## Chapter 3: Federalism — Federal, State, and Local
- **Core questions**: Who has power over what? Where does a city get its authority? What happens when federal and state law conflict?
- **Key concepts**: federalism, Tenth Amendment reserved powers, Supremacy Clause, concurrent powers, local government as a creature of the state (Dillon’s Rule).
- **Applied skills**: For any issue (schools, immigration, potholes, currency), name the level of government responsible.
- **Common traps**: Confusing federalism (levels) with separation of powers (branches); thinking cities derive power from the Constitution; assuming states can nullify valid federal law.

## Chapter 4: The Constitution and the Bill of Rights
- **Core questions**: What is in the Constitution, and what is just assumed to be? What does the Bill of Rights actually protect?
- **Key concepts**: the Preamble (purpose, not power), the Bill of Rights (first ten amendments), First Amendment freedoms, due process, equal protection, incorporation via the Fourteenth Amendment.
- **Applied skills**: Match a real grievance (a search, a protest, an unfair trial) to the right constitutional protection.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the Declaration of Independence with the Constitution; thinking the Preamble grants powers; assuming the Bill of Rights always limited the states.

## Chapter 5: Congress — House and Senate
- **Core questions**: Why two chambers? How is each composed? Where does a bill actually live and die?
- **Key concepts**: bicameralism, apportionment by census, equal Senate representation, the Great Compromise, the Origination Clause, filibuster and cloture, committee gatekeeping.
- **Applied skills**: Explain why a bill with majority support can still fail, and why small states have outsized Senate influence.
- **Common traps**: Confusing House and Senate rules; thinking 51 Senate votes is always enough; assuming the President kills bills in committee.

## Chapter 6: The Presidency and Executive Agencies
- **Core questions**: What can a President actually do alone? What is an executive order? Who runs the agencies?
- **Key concepts**: commander in chief, executive orders (implementation, not legislation), the Cabinet, line of succession, Twenty-Fifth and Twenty-Second Amendments.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguish a legitimate executive action from one that would require Congress or survive a court challenge.
- **Common traps**: Believing executive orders can make new law or override courts; overstating presidential war and spending power.

## Chapter 7: The Federal Courts and Judicial Review
- **Core questions**: How does a case reach the Supreme Court? Why do judges serve for life? How do courts check the other branches?
- **Key concepts**: district courts, courts of appeals, the Supreme Court, Marbury v. Madison, judicial review, life tenure, certiorari and the rule of four, stare decisis.
- **Applied skills**: Trace a case up the federal ladder and explain why the Court hears so few of the cases brought to it.
- **Common traps**: Flipping the order of trial and appeal; thinking the Court must hear every appeal; confusing precedent with unrelated criminal protections.

## Chapter 8: The Federal Budget and the Power of the Purse
- **Core questions**: Who controls the money? What is the President’s budget for? Why do shutdowns happen?
- **Key concepts**: power of the purse, the President’s budget request, appropriations, shutdowns, mandatory vs. discretionary spending, deficit vs. debt.
- **Applied skills**: Read a budget headline and identify whether it concerns proposing, appropriating, or spending — and at whose hand.
- **Common traps**: Confusing deficit (a yearly gap) with debt (the accumulated total); assuming budgets auto-renew; thinking the President can spend without appropriations.

## Chapter 9: The Bureaucracy — How Government Runs
- **Core questions**: Who actually does the work of government? Where do agencies get their power? What is a regulation?
- **Key concepts**: the federal bureaucracy, enabling statutes and delegation, regulations and notice-and-comment, civil service vs. political appointees (Pendleton Act, the end of the spoils system), independent regulatory agencies.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguish a statute from a regulation, and a career civil servant from a political appointee.
- **Common traps**: Thinking agencies invent their own authority; confusing the Cabinet (leadership) with the bureaucracy (workforce); assuming all agencies answer directly to the President.

## Chapter 10: Amendments and How Power Flows
- **Core questions**: How is the Constitution changed? How is the President really chosen? How has federal power grown over time?
- **Key concepts**: Article V amendment process, the 27 amendments, expanding voting rights (15th, 19th, 24th, 26th), the Electoral College, the Necessary and Proper (Elastic) Clause, the Commerce Clause, treaty and war powers.
- **Applied skills**: Explain why amendments are rare, why a President can lose the popular vote and still win, and how "implied powers" expanded federal reach.
- **Common traps**: Thinking the President signs amendments; confusing the elastic clause with the supremacy clause; assuming the President is chosen by national popular vote.

## Capstone
Take a single real-world episode — a contested bill, a court case, a confirmation fight, or a shutdown — and narrate it end to end using the course vocabulary. Identify which branch and which level of government acted, which checks were triggered, where the money came from, and where the final authority rested. The goal: read any government story in the news and instantly map it onto branches, levels, and checks — turning vague civic anxiety into clear, confident understanding of the machine you live inside.
