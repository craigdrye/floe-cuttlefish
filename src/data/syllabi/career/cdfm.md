# CDFM
**ID**: `cdfm` - **Discipline**: Government

## Course Aim
CDFM is for people who need to make defense money behave under real mission pressure. This course turns budget language, fiscal-law limits, funds control, audit evidence, and program reporting into practical judgment: what can be obligated, who has authority, what the numbers mean, and when a friendly spreadsheet is quietly causing trouble.

The aim is not to memorize acronyms in isolation. Learners should be able to read a budget or execution scenario, identify the control point, explain the risk, and choose the next defensible action.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test defense financial management, appropriations, PPBE, fiscal law, obligations, internal controls, audit readiness, EVM, accounting, Prompt Payment, FIAR remediation, or resource-management decision support. Keep the tone clear, operational, and exam-serious with enough warmth to make the material less punishing.

## Chapter 1: Resource Environment and Budget Roles
**Core questions:** Who controls which part of the federal resource process? How do strategy, authorization, appropriations, apportionment, and execution fit together?

**Key concepts**
- Congressional, OMB, Treasury, DoD, component, and program-office roles
- Authorization versus appropriation, budget authority, apportionment, allotment, commitment, obligation, outlay
- Continuing resolutions, new-start restrictions, rates of operation, and execution limits

**Practice deliverables**
- Funds-flow map from appropriation to outlay
- Role-matching drill for Congress, OMB, CBO, Treasury, DoD, and program offices
- Scenario set on continuing-resolution constraints

## Chapter 2: Fiscal Law and Funds Availability
**Core questions:** Is the money legally available for this purpose, time, and amount? What makes an obligation proper?

**Key concepts**
- Purpose, time, and amount availability
- Bona fide need rule, Anti-Deficiency Act risk, reprogramming and transfer controls
- Commitments, obligations, deobligations, expired funds, cancelled funds, and documentation

**Practice deliverables**
- Purpose-time-amount issue-spotting packet
- Obligation review checklist
- Three short memos explaining whether a proposed spend is allowed

## Chapter 3: PPBE and Program Execution
**Core questions:** How do planning, programming, budgeting, and execution connect? What does financial data say about program health?

**Key concepts**
- PPBE phases and feedback loops
- Budget formulation, justification, execution reviews, unfunded requirements, and variance analysis
- Burn rate, phasing, estimate at completion, earned value, schedule pressure, and lifecycle cost

**Practice deliverables**
- Monthly execution dashboard with risk notes
- EVM mini-drill: PV, EV, AC, CPI, SPI, and plain-English interpretation
- Budget narrative explaining a variance without hiding the problem

## Chapter 4: Internal Controls and Stewardship
**Core questions:** What control failed, what evidence proves it worked, and what would prevent recurrence?

**Key concepts**
- Segregation of duties, certifying-officer responsibility, payment support, receipt evidence
- Risk assessment, control design, testing, corrective action, and validation
- Prompt Payment, improper payments, travel or vendor payment review, and documentation discipline

**Practice deliverables**
- Control test design for a payment process
- Corrective-action plan with owner, due date, evidence, and validation step
- Case review of an unsupported payment package

## Chapter 5: Accounting, Audit Readiness, and FIAR
**Core questions:** How do budgetary and proprietary accounting differ? What makes a finding truly remediated?

**Key concepts**
- Budgetary versus proprietary accounting
- Financial statements, audit evidence, audit opinions, material weakness, and repeat findings
- FIAR-style remediation, root cause, document retention, system controls, and reconciliation

**Practice deliverables**
- Audit-opinion matching quiz
- Finding-to-root-cause worksheet
- Reconciliation exercise connecting obligation, receipt, invoice, and payment evidence

## Chapter 6: Exam Judgment and Workplace Transfer
**Core questions:** When two answers sound responsible, which one is more complete, better controlled, and more legally available?

**Key concepts**
- Reading for authority, timing, amount, documentation, and escalation
- Distinguishing mission urgency from legal authority
- Writing finance advice that is useful to commanders and program managers

**Practice deliverables**
- Error log sorted by fiscal law, execution math, controls, accounting, and governance
- Timed mixed-question set with short written rationales
- Final decision memo on a pressured funding scenario
