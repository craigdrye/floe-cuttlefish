# CAIA: The Alternatives Decoder Ring
**ID**: `caia` · **Discipline**: Finance — Alternative Investments

## Course Aim
The Chartered Alternative Investment Analyst designation, awarded by the CAIA Association, is the credential for people who refuse to believe the investable universe ends at public equities and investment-grade bonds. Level I builds the vocabulary across hedge funds, private equity, real assets, structured products, and the ethical frame the industry is asked to operate inside. Level II turns vocabulary into allocation work: sizing illiquidity premia, choosing managers, building a risk-aware alternatives sleeve for an institutional portfolio, and writing the recommendation memo the investment committee will actually approve.

This course is designed to make the alternatives universe feel less like a series of marketing decks and more like a set of cash-flow machines, each with its own fee, liquidity, leverage, and reporting profile. You will leave able to read a private equity J-curve and translate it into a pacing model, compute and interpret IRR/MOIC/DPI/RVPI/TVPI/PME without confusing them, distinguish a market-neutral strategy from a relative-value one from a multi-strategy pod, model the fee drag of 2-and-20 with a hard vs soft hurdle, and reason about why an endowment might tolerate ten years of illiquidity in return for an expected premium that may or may not exist.

The voice of this credential is the alternative-investments practitioner — the person who reads PPMs at midnight, models J-curves for the pacing committee, sits on the IC at a $30bn pension or $5bn endowment, evaluates hedge fund / PE / real estate / real assets managers across vintages and cycles, runs operational and investment DD, drafts the LP-side comment letter on a continuation vehicle, and quietly notices the side-letter MFN clause that nobody else on the call has read. You understand fees, illiquidity, and due diligence in their full ugliness — that 2-and-20 with a 1.5% organisational expense and a soft hurdle is a very different deal from 1.5-and-15 with a hard hurdle, and that the difference compounds over a fund life.

Compared with the CFA's alternative investments section, CAIA goes much deeper on each alt class (full chapters on real estate types, commodity term-structure mechanics, HF strategy taxonomy, PE waterfall and clawback math, structured product tranching), on manager selection (ODD vs IDD, factor analysis, fraud detection, smoothing diagnostics), and on the practical machinery of accessing alternatives (master/feeder, BDC, interval fund, '40 Act, UCITS, listed PE, '34 Act registered alts, separately managed accounts, co-investments, secondaries). CFA touches these; CAIA lives in them.

## Course Design Notes
Keep this course curious, careful, and concrete. Route questions here when they test CAIA Level I or Level II topics: professional standards and ethics, introduction to alternative investments, real assets, hedge funds, private equity, private debt and structured products, asset allocation and institutional investors, risk and manager selection, methods for alternative investing, accessing alternatives, and current/integrated topics. Favour realistic prompts with named structures (a Cayman master/feeder with a US onshore feeder; a Delaware-domiciled BDC; a closed-end PE fund of vintage 2019; a continuation vehicle for a single trophy asset), specific numbers (preferred return 8%, carry 20%, catch-up 100%, hurdle hard vs soft), and the kind of distractor a candidate would actually pick at minute 80 of a four-hour sitting.

Where CAIA overlaps the CFA alternatives chapter, prefer the deeper, more practical treatment — not "PE has J-curves" but the specific contributions/distributions pattern and how to translate a vintage-year cash flow stream into IRR, MOIC, DPI, RVPI, TVPI and KS-PME. Where it touches FRM on hedge fund risk, lean into the allocator's lens (manager selection, ODD, smoothing, fees) rather than the bank's lens (capital, VaR, FRTB).

**Rhythm.** Three weekly retrieval blocks plus one Saturday timed mixed quiz; two full mock exams in the final fortnight. Level I rewards pace — at 200 questions in 240 minutes, you have ~1.2 minutes per question, so you move faster than CFA L1. ## Chapter 1: Ethics and the CAIA Professional Standards
**Core questions.** How do the CAIA Member Code, the CFA Institute Asset Manager Code (AMC), and local regulation interact when a manager markets a private fund across jurisdictions? When does a side letter become a fair-dealing problem?

**Key concepts.** CAIA Member Code (integrity, professionalism, duties to clients, market integrity, communications); CFA AMC for asset managers; material nonpublic information, fair dealing, suitability, performance presentation; marketing rules — GIPS for alternative strategies, SEC Marketing Rule, hypothetical/projected performance, side letters and MFN clauses; conflicts of interest in funds-of-funds, capital introduction, placement agents, GP-led continuation vehicles; gift, entertainment, and political contribution policies.

**Applied skills.** Spot the standard violated in a marketing deck that quotes a hypothetical IRR without disclosure; resolve a side-letter clause that contradicts the master LPA; write a fair-allocation policy for an oversubscribed deal.

**Common traps.**
- "Confidential" side letters do not cure unequal treatment; MFN procedures or disclosure do.
- Hypothetical/backtested performance has its own disclosure standard; presenting it as actual is a violation regardless of footnotes.
- GP-led continuation vehicles are not banned — but disclosure, valuation, and the LPAC process matter.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Association *Standards of Practice Handbook*; CFA Institute *Asset Manager Code*; SEC Marketing Rule (206(4)-1).

## Chapter 2: Introduction to Alternatives and the Taxonomy
**Core questions.** What actually makes an alternative alternative? Where does the premium come from — illiquidity, complexity, manager skill, or expensive beta repackaging?

**Key concepts.** The alt universe — hedge funds, private equity, private debt, real assets, structured products, digital assets; sources of return — beta, alternative beta (carry, value, momentum, illiquidity), residual alpha; fund structures — limited partnerships, master/feeder (US onshore + Cayman master), UCITS, '40 Act, BDC, REIT, interval funds; fees and incentives — management fee, performance fee, hurdle rate (hard vs soft), high-water mark, GP catch-up (typically 100% catch-up to 8%/20% split), clawback, European vs American waterfall; side pockets, gates, lock-ups, key-person clauses.

**Applied skills.** Decompose a hedge fund's return into beta, alternative beta, and residual alpha; compare net IRR after a 2-and-20 with European vs American waterfall; price a 1.5-and-15 hard-hurdle structure against a 2-and-20 soft-hurdle structure across a realistic return path.

**Common traps.**
- "Hurdle" without "hard" or "soft" qualifier is ambiguous — a soft hurdle, once breached, applies carry to the *entire* return.
- 2-and-20 net of a 0.5% organisational expense is not 2-and-20; total cost of ownership matters.
- Master/feeder is not a tax avoidance vehicle — it is a structure for combining taxable and tax-exempt investors.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level I *Alternative Investments*; Anson *Handbook of Alternative Assets*.

## Chapter 3: Real Assets — Real Estate, Infrastructure, Commodities, Natural Resources
**Core questions.** How do you earn a return from things you can touch? When is the roll yield on a commodity index more important than the spot price? Why does a cap rate of 5% not equal an IRR of 5%?

**Key concepts.** Real estate — core, value-add, opportunistic; private vs public (REITs); NPI vs NCREIF Property Index; cap rate = NOI / Price, vs IRR (forward, total return) vs cash-on-cash; REIT metrics — FFO, AFFO, modified FFO; appraisal-based vs transaction-based indices and the smoothing problem. Infrastructure — greenfield vs brownfield, regulated utilities (price-cap, RAB-based) vs concession (PPP, availability payments); GP-led continuation funds in infra. Commodities — futures-based vs physical; theory of storage; convenience yield; contango/backwardation; roll yield in long-only indices; CTAs (trend-following, time-series momentum). Natural resources — timberland, farmland, water, mining; ESG and stranded-asset risk. Art and collectibles as alternative real assets.

**Applied skills.** Estimate roll yield on a long-only oil index in steep contango; compare core REIT vs value-add closed-end fund on risk-adjusted basis; underwrite cash flows of a regulated water utility under RAB; translate appraisal NAV-based real-estate volatility to a transaction-based estimate.

**Common traps.**
- Cap rate is a yield on price *today*; IRR depends on the full holding-period cash flow plus exit.
- Roll yield is futures-curve based, not equal to physical storage cost.
- Real estate NAV-based indices are smoothed; de-smoothing (Geltner/Fisher) typically triples observed volatility.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level I *Real Assets* chapters; Geltner et al. *Commercial Real Estate Analysis*; CFA Institute on real estate metrics.

## Chapter 4: Hedge Funds — Strategies, Fees, Leverage, Replication
**Core questions.** What is each hedge fund strategy actually doing with its risk budget, and when does it stop working? What does 2-and-20 with a high-water mark actually cost across cycles?

**Key concepts.** Equity strategies — long/short equity, equity market-neutral (beta-neutral pair trading), dedicated short bias. Event-driven — merger arb (annualised spread = (offer − target px)/target px × 12/months, adjusted for break risk), distressed and special situations, activist. Relative value — fixed-income arbitrage (LTCM-style swap-spread, on-the-run/off-the-run), convertible arb, volatility arb. Macro and managed futures (CTAs) — discretionary vs systematic, trend, carry, value, term-structure. Multi-strategy and pod-shop architecture (Citadel/Millennium style); fund of funds; hedge fund replication (factor-based, payoff distribution). Fee mechanics — 2-and-20, high-water marks, hurdle (hard/soft), management fee offset, founder share classes, side pockets for illiquid trades, gates and suspensions, lock-ups (hard/soft).

**Applied skills.** Diagnose unexpected market exposure in a "market-neutral" book; estimate annualised return on a friendly all-cash merger spread; identify the regime in which a trend-following CTA blows up (sharp reversal); translate a high-water mark recovery into a fee-free period for the LP.

**Common traps.**
- "Beta-neutral" at portfolio level can hide large sector or factor tilts that show up as beta in stress.
- A soft hurdle with full catch-up effectively becomes no hurdle once breached.
- Side pockets are not refunds; they segregate illiquid positions and run them off separately.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level I *Hedge Funds*; Lo *Hedge Funds: An Analytic Perspective*; Stefanini *Investment Strategies of Hedge Funds*.

## Chapter 5: Private Equity — Buyouts, Growth, VC, Secondaries
**Core questions.** How does a closed-end private equity fund actually generate and report returns over a ten-year life? When is a GP-led continuation vehicle a fair exit and when is it the GP selling to itself?

**Key concepts.** Buyout — LBO mechanics, sources & uses, debt structures (senior, mezz, PIK), value creation levers (multiple expansion, EBITDA growth, deleveraging), GP exits (strategic, secondary buyout, IPO, dividend recap). Growth equity (minority, often no leverage) and venture capital — stage risk, power-law return distribution, SAFE notes and convertible notes, anti-dilution and liquidation preferences (1x non-participating standard). Secondaries — LP-led (LP selling its stake) and GP-led continuation vehicles (single-asset or multi-asset), NAV-based lending. Fund mechanics — capital calls, distributions, J-curve, vintage diversification, commitment pacing models. Performance metrics — IRR, MOIC, DPI (distributions/paid-in), RVPI (residual/paid-in), TVPI = DPI + RVPI, KS-PME, direct alpha, since-inception IRR. Waterfall mechanics — European (whole fund) vs American (deal-by-deal), preferred return (8% standard), GP catch-up (100% to 20%/80% split), clawback at fund end.

**Applied skills.** Compute IRR, MOIC, TVPI, DPI, RVPI on a sample cash-flow stream; translate gross-to-net returns through a European waterfall with catch-up; evaluate a single-asset continuation vehicle for conflicts; build a commitment pacing model to reach a 10% steady-state allocation.

**Common traps.**
- IRR rewards short hold periods; the same MOIC over 4 vs 10 years yields very different IRR.
- DPI is the only one that has actually paid you in cash; RVPI is unrealised marks.
- Clawback only matters in American waterfalls where the GP receives early carry.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level I *Private Equity*; Kaplan & Schoar on PME; Phalippou *Private Equity Laid Bare*.

## Chapter 6: Private Debt and Structured Products
**Core questions.** How does securitisation turn a pool of cash flows into tranches with different risk profiles, and where do investors lose money first? What is the structural difference between unitranche, mezzanine, and PIK?

**Key concepts.** Private debt — direct lending (senior secured to upper-middle-market borrowers), unitranche (single tranche, blended pricing), second lien, mezzanine (typically with warrants/PIK), PIK toggle, distressed-for-control. Structured products — securitisation mechanics (true sale, bankruptcy-remote SPV), ABS, RMBS (agency vs non-agency), CMBS, CLOs (broadly syndicated vs middle-market); tranching with attachment/detachment points, equity tranche economics, credit enhancement (subordination, overcollateralisation, excess spread); credit derivatives — single-name CDS, CDX/iTraxx, synthetic CDOs, tranche correlation. Business Development Companies (BDCs) as public access to direct lending.

**Applied skills.** Walk losses up a CLO capital stack given a default and recovery scenario; compare a senior secured direct lending fund to a public BDC on fees and liquidity; identify the embedded leverage in a CLO equity tranche; price an attachment point.

**Common traps.**
- CLO equity is not "equity in the loan companies"; it is the residual cash flow after debt tranches are paid.
- Unitranche pricing is a blended cost; comparing it to senior pricing alone is misleading.
- Mezz with PIK does not pay cash until exit — IRR depends on the exit assumption.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level I *Private Debt and Structured Products*; Fabozzi *Handbook of Mortgage-Backed Securities*; CFA Institute on CLO mechanics.

## Chapter 7: Asset Allocation and Institutional Investors (Level II)
**Core questions.** How does an endowment, pension, sovereign wealth fund, or insurance general account actually build an alternatives sleeve, and what governance keeps it intact across cycles?

**Key concepts.** Mean-variance optimisation and its sensitivities (estimation error in expected returns); Black-Litterman; risk parity (equal risk contribution, leverage on bonds); factor-based allocation; liability-driven investment (LDI) for pensions — hedge ratio, surplus risk, glide path. The endowment model (Yale, Harvard) — illiquidity premium, manager selection, spending rules (3-5% with smoothing). Pension plans — DB vs DC, funded status, PBO/ABO, glide path; sovereign wealth funds (Norges, GIC, Abu Dhabi) — political constraints, transparency; family offices and OCIOs; insurance general accounts and life vs P&C ALM constraints; foundations and private wealth.

**Applied skills.** Build a strategic allocation for an endowment with a 5% spending rule and 10-year horizon; translate a pension's 95% funded status into an LDI glide-path tilt; critique a risk-parity portfolio in a rising-rate, rising-correlation regime; design an alternatives sleeve subject to a 25% illiquidity budget.

**Common traps.**
- A higher allocation to PE does not raise expected return linearly; capacity constraints and fees matter.
- LDI is not "buy long bonds"; it is "match duration and inflation sensitivity to liability."
- Endowment model assumes access to top-quartile managers — without access, the premium is a myth.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level II *Asset Allocation and Institutional Investors*; Swensen *Pioneering Portfolio Management*; Ang *Asset Management*.

## Chapter 8: Risk and Manager Selection (Level II)
**Core questions.** How do you select a manager whose alpha is real, and detect one whose returns are smoothed, factor-loaded, or fabricated?

**Key concepts.** Operational due diligence (ODD) vs investment due diligence (IDD); service provider verification (administrator, auditor, custodian, prime broker independence — the Madoff post-mortem); style analysis, factor exposures (Fama-French + alt factors); return smoothing detection (Getmansky-Lo-Makarov AR(1) model); fraud red flags (smooth returns, single auditor, family relationships across service providers, unusual asset valuations); risk reporting and transparency — position-level vs aggregate; risk overlays at the allocator (multi-strategy risk aggregation, look-through reporting).

**Applied skills.** Design an ODD checklist that would have flagged a real-world blowup; detect serial correlation in monthly hedge fund returns and adjust Sharpe accordingly; build a style analysis of a long/short equity fund to identify factor tilts; structure a side letter to give the LP look-through risk reporting.

**Common traps.**
- A high Sharpe with strong serial correlation is a red flag, not a virtue.
- "Independent auditor" must mean independent of administrator and custodian, not just unrelated to the GP.
- Look-through risk reporting in multi-strat is essential; aggregate-only reporting hides pod-level risk.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level II *Risk and Manager Selection*; Getmansky/Lo/Makarov on smoothing; AIMA ODD guidance.

## Chapter 9: Methods for Alternative Investments (Level II)
**Core questions.** How do you measure performance of an illiquid, irregular cash-flow vehicle in a way that is both fair to the manager and useful to the LP?

**Key concepts.** Time-weighted return (TWR, manager-centric) vs money-weighted return / IRR (LP-centric); since-inception IRR; PME variants — Kaplan-Schoar (KS-PME ratio), PME+ (adjusts for survivorship), direct alpha (annualised excess return vs public index); benchmark construction for alts (peer group vs absolute return vs public-plus); statistical methods — bootstrap and Monte Carlo for performance dispersion; return-smoothing diagnostics; rolling-window factor regressions.

**Applied skills.** Run a KS-PME on a 2015-vintage PE fund vs the S&P 500; convert TWR to IRR and explain the divergence; unsmooth a hedge fund return series and recompute Sharpe and downside metrics.

**Common traps.**
- TWR and IRR can differ materially even on the same fund — TWR ignores cash-flow timing.
- KS-PME = 1.0 means PE matched the public market on the same cash flow timing; not "PE earned 0%."
- Direct alpha can be negative even with positive IRR if the public benchmark did better.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level II *Methods*; Kaplan-Schoar 2005 paper; Long & Nickels PME+.

## Chapter 10: Accessing Alternatives (Level II)
**Core questions.** How does an allocator gain exposure to alternative strategies through the *right* wrapper for the investor's tax, liquidity, and regulatory profile?

**Key concepts.** Liquid alts — '40 Act mutual funds and ETFs with alternative strategies (long/short equity, managed futures, market-neutral); listed PE (publicly traded PE managers and listed PE funds); BDCs (regulated 1940 Act investment companies offering middle-market credit); interval funds (periodic liquidity, often quarterly with 5-25% repurchase offers); UCITS for European-domiciled alt strategies; co-investments alongside PE funds (often fee-free); separately managed accounts (SMAs) at scale; fund-of-funds; secondaries (LP-led and GP-led); evergreen / open-ended PE structures.

**Applied skills.** Choose the right wrapper for a HNW client with quarterly liquidity needs and PE exposure desire; structure a co-investment programme to lower blended fees; size a secondaries allocation for vintage diversification.

**Common traps.**
- Daily-liquid hedge fund replicas cannot capture strategies whose alpha depends on illiquidity.
- Interval funds are not daily liquid; the repurchase offer can be pro-rated if oversubscribed.
- Listed PE trades at NAV premia/discounts that can dwarf underlying NAV moves.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level II *Accessing Alternatives*; ICI on '40 Act structures; CAIA Association *Liquid Alts* white papers.

## Chapter 11: Current and Integrated Topics (Level II)
**Core questions.** What integrated themes will appear in this cycle's exam, and how do they cut across the asset-class chapters?

**Key concepts.** ESG integration in alts — UN PRI, SFDR Article 8/9, materiality vs values, greenwashing risk; climate transition risk in real assets and PE portfolio companies; impact investing and blended finance; crypto as an alt allocation — custody (qualified custodian, MPC, cold storage), staking yields, ETF wrappers (spot BTC/ETH ETFs); AI in alts — manager selection ML models, LLMs in deal sourcing, model risk governance; fee innovation — fee-on-deployed-capital, fee waivers, founder share classes, GP commitment expectations.

**Applied skills.** Build an ESG overlay on a PE portfolio company review; size a 1-3% crypto allocation with operational due diligence; critique an AI-based manager selection model for survivorship and look-ahead bias.

**Common traps.**
- "ESG integrated" is not always "impact"; the two require different measurement.
- Crypto custody risk is a *real* operational risk, not just market risk.
- Backtested AI signals on a sparse alt return database almost always look amazing in-sample.

**Authoritative sources.** CAIA Level II *Current and Integrated Topics* readings (refresh each cycle); UN PRI guidance; SFDR primary documents.

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
CAIA exams reward candidates who recognise structures quickly and resist the lure of the most technical-sounding answer. Level I gives you 200 questions in 240 minutes — roughly 1.2 minutes per question — so pace is critical; do not deep-think Level I prompts past 90 seconds without flagging and moving. Level II combines 100 MCQs with 3 essay sets across the same 240 minutes; budget approximately 120 minutes for MCQs (~1.2 min each) and 120 minutes for the constructed-response sections, planning your essay outline in the first 5 minutes of each set. Permitted calculators are the TI BA II Plus (including Professional) and the HP 12C; bring a fresh battery.

Read every prompt for the fund type, the role you are playing (LP, GP, allocator, consultant, ODD officer), and the metric being requested. Practice translating between IRR and MOIC, gross and net, TWR and MWR, until conversions are automatic. Build a one-page performance metrics card (IRR, MOIC, DPI, RVPI, TVPI, KS-PME, direct alpha) and a one-page structures card (LP/GP, master/feeder, '40 Act, BDC, REIT, interval fund) and rewrite each weekly. On exam day, the disciplined candidate spends the first thirty seconds naming the structure and the role, the next two minutes choosing the answer, and the last thirty seconds checking the answer matches the question that was actually asked.
