# Personality Profiles: Know the Frameworks, Spot the Hype
**ID**: `personalityTests` · **Discipline**: Self & Society

## Course Aim
Personality tests sit at an awkward intersection of useful psychology, useful pop culture, and elaborate horoscope. This course teaches the actual frameworks that drive hiring, team coaching, and self-knowledge: Big Five / OCEAN, HEXACO, Myers-Briggs (MBTI), DISC, Enneagram, CliftonStrengths, the Dark Triad, and the workplace assessments (Hogan, Caliper, Predictive Index, SHL) you will encounter in industry. It also teaches the psychometric lens that separates them — reliability, validity, the Barnum effect, and what "scientifically supported" actually means.

You will leave with the ability to read your own results without flattering yourself, interpret a colleague's profile without overcommitting to it, and tell which instruments belong in serious selection processes versus office team-building. The course is intentionally even-handed: MBTI gets a fair hearing on what it explains AND on why personality psychologists prefer Big Five. The Forer effect is named so you can spot it.

This is a Fundamentals course, written for adult professionals 20+. It pairs especially well with our Career Skills, Negotiation, and People Management tracks because hiring, feedback, and team design constantly invoke these models — usually unrigorously.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test (a) the structure of a named instrument (Big Five facets, MBTI dichotomies, Enneagram types, DISC quadrants), (b) workplace use (hiring legality, validity for job performance, group versus individual feedback), or (c) the psychometric properties (reliability vs validity, test-retest, construct validity, criterion validity). Keep the voice curious, clear-eyed, and unimpressed by mystique.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
Not a credentialing exam — this is a literacy course. Suggested cadence: 6-10 hours total, 1-2 hours per chapter, paired with self-testing on a single instrument (e.g. take a public Big Five) to anchor the abstract concepts.

## Chapter 1: Psychometric Foundations
**Core questions:** What does it mean for a personality test to be "valid"? Why is test-retest reliability necessary but not sufficient? How do we tell a real instrument from a magazine quiz?

**Key concepts**
- Reliability vs validity (a test can be reliably wrong)
- Test-retest reliability, internal consistency (Cronbach's α), inter-rater reliability
- Construct validity, convergent and discriminant validity, criterion-related validity (concurrent + predictive)
- Standardization, norming, percentile scores, T-scores
- Adverse impact in employment selection (4/5ths rule under Uniform Guidelines)

**Applied skills**
- Read a test's technical manual and identify its psychometric claims
- Spot the Forer effect (general statements that "feel personal")
- Tell when a result is statistically meaningful versus noise

**Common traps**
- Confusing reliability with validity — a horoscope can be reliable
- Trusting face validity ("the questions look right") instead of empirical validity
- Forgetting that single-administration scores can swing 5-10 points on retest
- Using a tool validated for self-insight as a hiring filter without job analysis

## Chapter 2: Big Five / OCEAN
**Core questions:** What does each factor measure? How are factors related to facets? Which factor predicts job performance most reliably?

**Key concepts**
- Openness to experience, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism
- Facet structure (NEO-PI-R: 6 facets per factor, 30 total)
- Conscientiousness is the strongest single predictor of job performance across domains
- Universality claims across cultures (Schmitt 2007, McCrae & Costa lexical work)
- Difference scores vs absolute scores

**Applied skills**
- Match a behavior pattern to the correct factor
- Read NEO scores at the facet level, not just factor level
- Avoid the common mistake of treating Neuroticism as "bad"

**Common traps**
- Calling Openness "intelligence" — they correlate but are not the same construct
- Treating Agreeableness as universally desirable (low-A predicts certain leadership outcomes)
- Conflating Extraversion with sociability — it covers reward sensitivity and assertiveness too

## Chapter 3: HEXACO
**Core questions:** What does Honesty-Humility add over Big Five? Where does it predict better?

**Key concepts**
- Six factors: Honesty-Humility, Emotionality (renamed from Neuroticism), eXtraversion, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, Openness
- Honesty-Humility predicts ethical workplace behavior, counterproductive behavior, Dark Triad
- HEXACO realigns Agreeableness and Emotionality differently from Big Five

**Applied skills**
- Choose HEXACO over Big Five when ethical behavior is the criterion
- Translate Big Five results to HEXACO and notice the gaps

**Common traps**
- Assuming HEXACO and Big Five are interchangeable — Honesty-Humility is the differentiator
- Forgetting that Emotionality is recalibrated, not identical to Neuroticism

## Chapter 4: Myers-Briggs (MBTI)
**Core questions:** What are the four dichotomies? Why is MBTI controversial in personality science? Where is it still useful?

**Key concepts**
- Dichotomies: E/I, S/N, T/F, J/P
- 16 types (INTJ, ESFP, etc.)
- Cognitive functions (Ni, Ne, Ti, Te, Fi, Fe, Si, Se) — the dynamic underbelly
- Test-retest reliability concerns (~50% of takers get a different type within weeks)
- Pichot's critique, NPC critiques, MBTI's resilience in corporate use
- Distinction: MBTI/Myers-Briggs (paid, official) vs 16Personalities (free, popular, but uses Big Five-style scales)

**Applied skills**
- Map an MBTI letter to the Big Five trait it most resembles (E↔Extraversion, N↔Openness, F/T↔Agreeableness, J/P↔Conscientiousness)
- Use MBTI as a conversation starter, not a hiring filter

**Common traps**
- Reporting MBTI like blood type — it is dimensional, not categorical
- Confusing 16Personalities (Big-Five-based) with the official MBTI instrument
- Citing "X% of executives are TJ" as evidence of selection rather than self-selection

## Chapter 5: DISC
**Core questions:** What do the four styles measure? What is DISC actually used for?

**Key concepts**
- Dominance, Influence, Steadiness, Conscientiousness (sometimes called Compliance)
- Marston's original framework, descendants (Wiley DiSC, TTI, Wonderscape)
- Behavioural style snapshot, not deep personality
- Common in sales training, team coaching, communication style guides

**Applied skills**
- Match a behaviour pattern to the right DISC quadrant
- Recognize DISC's narrow scope versus Big Five's broad

**Common traps**
- Treating DISC as comprehensive personality — it covers behavioural style only
- Using high-D as shorthand for "good leader" — context matters

## Chapter 6: Enneagram
**Core questions:** What are the nine types? How do wings and integration/disintegration arrows work? What's the empirical status?

**Key concepts**
- Types 1-9 with names (Reformer, Helper, Achiever, Individualist, Investigator, Loyalist, Enthusiast, Challenger, Peacemaker)
- Wings (the adjacent type that flavours your core)
- Integration arrow (growth in stress-free conditions) vs disintegration arrow (under stress)
- Triads (head 5-6-7, heart 2-3-4, gut 8-9-1)
- Limited empirical validation — popular in coaching, spiritual direction, less so in academic psychology

**Applied skills**
- Identify a type by its core motivation, not surface behaviour
- Use wings and arrows for nuance rather than treating types as boxes

**Common traps**
- Picking your "favourite" type rather than the one that fits — typing yourself is harder than typing others
- Treating Enneagram as scientifically equivalent to Big Five — it isn't

## Chapter 7: CliftonStrengths
**Core questions:** What are the 34 themes? Why is the asset-framing strategically chosen? What is "strengths-based development"?

**Key concepts**
- 34 themes grouped into 4 domains: Executing, Influencing, Relationship Building, Strategic Thinking
- Top 5 themes report (default) vs full 34
- Strengths-based development premise: build on what you do well rather than fix weaknesses
- Gallup empirical claims about engagement and performance

**Applied skills**
- Map top themes to roles, not just project tasks
- Use the 34 ranking to anticipate where you'll under-deliver

**Common traps**
- Avoiding tasks that need a "low theme" — most jobs need a portfolio
- Treating themes as personality factors — they are behavioral talents

## Chapter 8: Dark Triad
**Core questions:** What are the three traits? How are they measured? What predicts what?

**Key concepts**
- Narcissism (grandiose vs vulnerable), Machiavellianism, Psychopathy (primary/secondary)
- Measurement: Short Dark Triad (SD3), Dirty Dozen, NPI for narcissism
- Predicts counterproductive workplace behavior, exploitation, short-term mating strategies
- Light Triad as a counterpoint (Kantianism, Humanism, Faith in Humanity)
- Overlap between Dark Triad and low Honesty-Humility (HEXACO)

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish grandiose from vulnerable narcissism
- Recognize Dark Triad red flags in interview signals (within ethics + legality)

**Common traps**
- Diagnosing colleagues — clinical disorders are not the same as elevated traits
- Forgetting that mild Machiavellianism is common in successful leaders

## Chapter 9: Workplace Instruments (Hogan / Caliper / PI / SHL)
**Core questions:** Which tools are commonly used in selection? What do they measure? What about legal risk?

**Key concepts**
- Hogan Personality Inventory (HPI), Hogan Development Survey (HDS — derailers), Hogan MVPI (motives)
- Caliper Profile
- Predictive Index Behavioral Assessment (PI-BA), PI Cognitive Assessment
- SHL OPQ32, Talent Q, Saville Wave
- US: Title VII + ADA + Uniform Guidelines; 4/5ths rule; job analysis prerequisite
- UK/EU: GDPR + employment law; automated decision-making constraints (Art 22 GDPR)

**Applied skills**
- Identify which instrument fits a hiring objective
- Document the validation evidence (job analysis, criterion study) needed to defend use

**Common traps**
- Using a personality test as the SOLE filter — leads to adverse impact and challenge
- Skipping job analysis — without it, validity claims collapse
- Ignoring the difference between development tools (Hogan HDS) and selection tools (HPI)

## Chapter 10: Forer Effect, Barnum Statements, and Hype
**Core questions:** Why do generic readings feel personal? How do you spot a fake personality test?

**Key concepts**
- Forer (1949) original demonstration — class accepted identical profile as accurate
- Barnum statements: "You have a tendency to be self-critical" — true of most adults
- P.T. Barnum / Carnival-style design choices in commercial tests
- Confirmation bias in self-test interpretation
- Cold reading techniques applied to test feedback

**Applied skills**
- Audit a personality result for Barnum statements
- Test claims of "scientific" by looking for the actual technical manual

**Common traps**
- "It feels right" is not evidence — it's the Forer effect
- "Used by Fortune 500 companies" is a marketing claim, not validity
- "Custom personalized report" can be 90% Barnum text + 10% interpolation

## Capstone: Using Personality Tests Well
Take one Big Five test (IPIP-NEO or Big Five Aspects Scale). Take one of the popular ones (MBTI, Enneagram, or DISC). Compare. Write down: which result surprised you, which one is more useful for self-direction, which one is more defensible if you had to justify it to HR. The point of this course is not to dismiss any tool, but to use each for what it can actually do — and to spot the moment you're being sold something dressed up in psychology jargon.
