# Series 87: Research Analyst Communications
**ID**: `series87` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course prepares you to pass FINRA's Series 87 — the Research Analyst Qualification Examination's regulatory half — and, in doing so, to think like a published equity analyst who never accidentally hands a regulator, a plaintiff, or a compliance officer something to use against them. The Series 86 exam asks whether you can build a defensible thesis: read the filings, model the company, value the security, and reach a recommendation. The Series 87 exam asks a different and, for many strong analysts, harder question — whether you can *communicate* that thesis inside a thicket of rules built on a single hard-won lesson from the dot-com era: that a research analyst whose pay, access, and reputation depend on keeping bankers and issuers happy will, absent real controls, tell investors what is convenient rather than what is true. Series 87 is, at bottom, the conflict-of-interest exam.

The course follows the lifecycle of a research communication rather than the table of contents of a rule. You start with the two-exam map and the regulatory architecture — FINRA Rule 2241, SEC Regulation AC, and the structural separation principles that came out of the Global Research Analyst Settlement — so every later rule has a reason to exist. From there you walk the report itself: what makes a research report fair and balanced, how a price target must disclose its basis, and where fact, estimate, and opinion must stay visibly separated. Then come the conflicts that the whole regime exists to manage — investment-banking influence, analyst compensation, personal trading, and the quiet-period and solicitation restrictions — and the disclosures that make those conflicts visible to the reader. You cover the spoken word as carefully as the written: public appearances, media, and sales-force communications, where an off-script sentence can become a violation. You finish with dissemination and fair-access rules, supervision and principal approval, recordkeeping, and the material-nonpublic-information (MNPI) boundary that sits underneath all research work.

By the end you should be able to take a draft report, a proposed TV appearance, a banker's "small favor," or a channel-check result and do four things an examiner and a hiring desk both reward: identify which obligation is actually triggered, name the disclosure or control that satisfies it, decide whether to proceed, fix, escalate, or refuse — and document the call so it survives later scrutiny. The specific disclosure templates and surveillance tools differ between firms; the reasoning, and the instinct to protect the reader and the record, is what transfers.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when learners want the *regulated-communication* half of the Research Analyst track: the research report standards, fair-and-balanced presentation, conflict-of-interest management and disclosures, public appearances and other research communications, dissemination and fair access, supervisory review and principal approval, recordkeeping, and the analyst-side MNPI boundary — all framed around the FINRA Series 87 exam and its governing rules (FINRA Rule 2241, SEC Regulation AC, and the legacy of the Global Settlement). Keep valuation, modeling, accounting, and forecasting *light* here; that analytical competence is the Series 86 exam and lives in the `series86` course. When a question is about how to build the number — a DCF assumption, a comparable multiple, a three-statement linkage — send it to `series86`; when it is about how that number may be communicated, disclosed, supervised, and stored, it belongs here. Both exams require the SIE as a co-requisite, and a candidate generally cannot register as a research analyst until both the 86 and 87 are passed, so treat them as two halves of one credential rather than a sequence.

The voice is a supervisory analyst or research-compliance officer mentoring a sharp junior who can already model a company but has never been the name at the top of a published note. It is concrete (a real report, a real appearance, a real disclosure), plain about what each rule is actually *for*, and merciless about the trap that sinks good analysts: confusing being *right* with being *compliant*. A true thesis, communicated with an undisclosed conflict or a missing disclosure or to one favored client first, is still a violation. Every chapter carries Common traps because in research conduct the costly error is rarely exotic — it is the analyst who treats a delivered disclosure as a cure for the underlying conflict, lets optimism slide into promissory language, previews an unpublished rating in a hallway, or assumes a channel check can't possibly be MNPI. Keep facts current and frame the regime honestly: this is a conflict-management regime, not a ban on conflicts — most conflicts are permitted *if disclosed and controlled*, and the exam tests whether you know which is which. Teach the durable principle; where a firm-specific practice or a disclosure format varies, say so rather than presenting one firm's template as the law. Distractors should be the plausible-but-wrong calls a real analyst might make under deadline pressure, not obvious howlers.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The Series 87 is a focused, multiple-choice exam (roughly 50 scored items, around 1.5 hours) covering the *communication* functions of the FINRA Research Analyst content outline. It pairs with the Series 86 (the analytical exam) and the SIE; passing both 86 and 87, on top of the SIE, is what qualifies a research analyst. Think of the 87 as testing four overlapping skills: spotting a conflict, knowing the required disclosure, knowing who must review and approve, and knowing what must be preserved.

Three implications for how you study. First, the center of gravity is conflicts and disclosures — Rule 2241's structural, compensation, personal-trading, and disclosure provisions plus Regulation AC's certification requirement. Spend most of your reasoning practice there, because nearly every scenario question reduces to "what conflict is in play and what does it require." Second, learn the regime as *triggers and consequences*, not definitions: the exam rewards "this fact pattern triggers this disclosure / this quiet period / this approval," not "define a research report." Third, do not let the supervision, dissemination, and recordkeeping topics feel like afterthoughts — they are quiet point-earners that experienced analysts under-study because they feel administrative. A workable rhythm: master the architecture and the report standards first (Chapters 1–2) so you have a frame; drill conflicts, disclosures, and public appearances hardest (Chapters 3–5) because that is where the questions cluster; then make a focused pass through dissemination, supervision, recordkeeping, and the MNPI boundary (Chapters 6–8). Practice with fact-pattern questions and a final mixed timed set, not flashcards — the 87 is a judgment test wearing a multiple-choice costume.

## Chapter 1: The Two-Exam Map and the Research Conduct Architecture
- **Core questions**: Where does the analytical exam end and the communication exam begin, and why is *this* problem a Series 87 problem? Why does a whole rulebook exist around equity research in the first place? Which authority — FINRA Rule 2241, SEC Regulation AC, or the legacy of the Global Settlement — is actually governing the situation in front of you?
- **Key concepts**: the split between Series 86 (information and data gathering, analysis, valuation, forecasting, recommendation logic) and Series 87 (preparing and disseminating research communications, conflicts, disclosures, public appearances, supervision, recordkeeping), with the SIE as co-requisite to both; the definition of a *research report* (a communication that includes analysis of equity securities and provides information reasonably sufficient on which to base an investment decision) and who counts as a *research analyst*; the origin story — the 2003 Global Research Analyst Settlement and its core insight that banking-driven incentives corrupt research — and how it hardened into FINRA Rule 2241; SEC Regulation AC's two requirements (the analyst certifies the views are their own, and discloses whether compensation was tied to the specific recommendation).
- **Applied skills**: classify a given problem as analytical (Series 86) versus communication/conduct (Series 87) and route it accordingly; for a sample communication, state whether it meets the research-report definition and which governing authority applies; build a two-exam study map listing the Series 87 conduct domains, your weak spots, and the rule triggers each domain tests.
- **Common traps**: assuming research conduct rules ban conflicts (the regime *manages and discloses* most conflicts rather than forbidding them); treating Regulation AC as a formality rather than a substantive certification and disclosure obligation; calling a communication a "research report" without checking the definition (some marketing and brief market commentary are carved out); thinking a brilliant Series 86 thesis can excuse a Series 87 conduct failure.

## Chapter 2: Research Report Standards and Fair, Balanced Presentation
- **Core questions**: Could a reasonable investor separate what you *know* from what you *estimate* from what you *believe* in this report? Does the price target visibly disclose its basis and the assumptions behind it? Is the risk discussion specific enough to mean anything, or is it boilerplate?
- **Key concepts**: the fair-and-balanced standard for research communications (no exaggerated, unwarranted, promissory, or misleading claims); the requirement that a price target have a *reasonable basis* and disclose the valuation methods and assumptions used to derive it; rating-system clarity (defining each rating, its meaning and time horizon, and the distribution of ratings the firm assigns); the obligation to discuss risks specific to the thesis, not generic market risk; keeping fact, estimate, and opinion distinguishable; sourcing and the duty not to misstate or omit material facts.
- **Applied skills**: audit a draft report for fair-and-balanced defects, missing price-target basis, undefined ratings, and boilerplate risk language; rewrite a promissory or exaggerated sentence into a defensible one; build a report-quality checklist covering thesis support, valuation basis, rating definitions, risk specificity, assumptions, and sourcing.
- **Common traps**: stating a price target without disclosing the valuation method and key assumptions that produced it; letting optimism cross into promissory or guarantee-style language ("this stock will reach…"); using a rating without disclosing what it means or the firm's rating distribution; treating a generic "markets are risky" paragraph as a real, thesis-specific risk discussion; blurring opinion into fact so the reader can't tell the difference.

## Chapter 3: Conflicts of Interest — The Core of the Regime
- **Core questions**: What is the analyst, the firm, or the issuer getting out of *this* report, and does that interest threaten the analyst's objectivity? Which conflicts must be structurally prevented, which must be controlled, and which only need to be disclosed? Is the analyst about to be improperly influenced — and by whom?
- **Key concepts**: the categories of conflict the rule targets — investment-banking influence over research, analyst *compensation* tied to specific banking transactions or to banking revenue, the analyst's and firm's *ownership* or financial interest in the subject company, personal trading by the analyst, and issuer or banker *pressure* on coverage and ratings; structural separations required by Rule 2241 (research budget and analyst compensation cannot be tied to specific investment-banking transactions; banking personnel cannot supervise analysts or control coverage decisions; no promising favorable research to win or retain banking business); restrictions on analyst participation in solicitation (e.g., pitches and roadshows) and on analyst personal trading against their own recommendations and around report publication.
- **Applied skills**: given a fact pattern, name the conflict category and whether the regime *prohibits*, *controls*, or merely requires *disclosure* of it; spot prohibited banking influence (a banker editing a rating, compensation tied to a deal) versus a permitted-but-disclosable interest (firm ownership in the issuer); write a conflict memo for a proposed report listing each conflict, its required treatment, and the independence risks.
- **Common traps**: assuming disclosure cures everything (some banking-influence and compensation arrangements are flatly prohibited, not merely disclosed); confusing a *firm-level* conflict (the firm owns 1%+ of the issuer) with an *analyst-level* conflict (the analyst personally owns shares) and applying the wrong treatment; missing that an analyst trading ahead of or against a published recommendation is restricted regardless of disclosure; treating banker pressure as a personality problem to manage quietly instead of a control breach to escalate and document.

## Chapter 4: Required Disclosures and Analyst Certification
- **Core questions**: For this specific report and this analyst, which disclosures are mandatory — and are they placed where a reader will actually see them? Has the analyst made the Regulation AC certification, and is it true? What turns an optional courtesy into a required disclosure?
- **Key concepts**: the standard Rule 2241 disclosures — the firm's investment-banking relationship with and compensation from the subject company, the analyst's and firm's ownership/financial interest, whether the analyst received compensation based on banking revenue, the meaning and distribution of the firm's ratings, the price-target valuation basis and risks, and any other material conflict; placement and prominence requirements (clear, comprehensive, and not buried); Regulation AC certification (the analyst attests the views are genuinely theirs and discloses whether any compensation was, is, or will be tied directly or indirectly to the specific recommendations or views); the parallel disclosure obligations that attach when the same content appears in a public appearance.
- **Applied skills**: assemble the complete disclosure set for a sample report given the firm-and-analyst relationships described; check a Regulation AC certification for completeness and truthfulness; flag a disclosure that is technically present but so buried or vague that it fails the prominence and clarity standard.
- **Common traps**: omitting an analyst-level disclosure (personal ownership, a household member's holdings, a board seat) because the firm-level disclosure was made; treating Regulation AC as boilerplate and not actually confirming the compensation statement is accurate; burying material disclosures in fine print and assuming "it was disclosed" ends the analysis; forgetting that the disclosure obligation follows the *content* — the same conflicts must surface when the thesis is delivered verbally.

## Chapter 5: Public Appearances and Other Research Communications
- **Core questions**: Is this spoken or informal communication a regulated *public appearance*, and what does that pull in? Can the analyst support every claim live, on the spot, and with the required disclosures? Is anything being previewed that has not yet been published or fairly disseminated?
- **Key concepts**: the definition of a *public appearance* (an analyst's participation in a seminar, forum, radio/TV/print interview, or other public-facing setting recommending or offering an opinion on securities) and the disclosure obligations that attach to it; the rule that talking points must be fair, balanced, and supportable, with the same conflict disclosures the written report requires; the prohibition on promissory, exaggerated, or misleading statements in any medium; the special danger of *previewing* an unpublished rating, target, or change, and of selectively informing one audience before fair dissemination; communications with the firm's own sales force and with clients as research communications subject to the standards; informal and electronic channels treated by their substance, not their format.
- **Applied skills**: decide whether a given activity is a public appearance and list the disclosures and constraints it triggers; build a public-appearance prep sheet with approved, supportable points, the required oral/visual disclosures, prohibited statements, and escalation triggers; catch a sales-desk or client communication that previews unpublished research.
- **Common traps**: assuming disclosure rules apply only to written reports and not to interviews and appearances; letting a confident verbal answer drift into a promise or an unsupported claim under interviewer pressure; previewing a forthcoming upgrade/downgrade or target change to a favored audience before publication; treating a casual hallway, email, or chat comment as outside the rules because it wasn't a "real" report.

## Chapter 6: Dissemination, Fair Access, and Recordkeeping
- **Core questions**: Can this report go to one client, desk, or channel before the others — and if not, what makes distribution fair? When the report contains a material error, what must happen and how fast? What record must exist to prove every step later?
- **Key concepts**: fair and broad dissemination (research generally goes to all entitled clients on a fair basis; selective early distribution to favored clients or to the firm's own traders is the abuse the rule guards against); the relationship between when research is disseminated and the analyst's and firm's permitted trading; material corrections, updates, and the *withdrawal* or termination of coverage (and the notice obligations that come with dropping coverage); books-and-records and retention requirements that capture drafts, approvals, the published report, communications, and corrections; version control and the audit trail that lets a supervisor or examiner reconstruct who wrote, reviewed, approved, and changed what, and when.
- **Applied skills**: decide whether a proposed distribution sequence is fair or improperly selective; build a correction-and-dissemination playbook covering draft, approved, published, corrected, and withdrawn states and the notices each requires; design a records/audit-trail checklist that would let an examiner reconstruct a report's full history.
- **Common traps**: tipping the firm's traders or a favored client to a report or a rating change before fair dissemination; handling a material error as a quiet edit instead of a documented correction with proper notice; dropping coverage silently when a discontinuation notice is required; keeping the published PDF but not the drafts, review evidence, and communications the rules require — leaving no audit trail when one is needed most.

## Chapter 7: Supervision, Principal Approval, and Surveillance
- **Core questions**: Who must review and approve this communication *before* it goes out, and can that approval ever be after the fact? What is supervision supposed to catch that the analyst can't see in their own work? When a draft is unsupported or conflicted, what does a good supervisor do with it?
- **Key concepts**: principal/supervisory review and approval of research reports and public-appearance content before release; the firm's written supervisory procedures, surveillance, and the documentation that review actually happened; exception handling and escalation when a draft fails fair-and-balanced, disclosure, or conflict standards; the structural rule that investment-banking personnel cannot supervise analysts or sit in the approval chain for coverage; training and attestation as ongoing supervisory controls; how supervision, monitoring, and recordkeeping together form the evidence that the firm's controls work.
- **Applied skills**: build a supervisory-review checklist for a research report and a public appearance, including who reviews, what they check, and how approval is evidenced; route an unsupported or conflicted draft to the correct escalation path rather than waving it through; identify a supervisory breach (banking in the approval chain, retroactive "approval," missing evidence of review).
- **Common traps**: believing approval can be granted retroactively after a report has already gone out; letting investment-banking personnel influence or sit in the research approval chain; approving a draft without evidence that the price-target basis, disclosures, and risk discussion were actually checked; treating supervision as a rubber stamp rather than the control that protects both the reader and the firm.

## Chapter 8: The MNPI Boundary and Analyst Ethics
- **Core questions**: Is the analyst about to receive — or has the analyst already received — material nonpublic information, and from which channel? Can this management call, expert-network call, or channel-check result be used and published? What must be escalated *before* the information is acted on?
- **Key concepts**: material nonpublic information and the insider-trading and selective-disclosure risks that surround equity research; the channels that generate MNPI exposure — issuer management meetings, banker contacts inside the firm, expert-network calls, supplier/customer channel checks, and rumor flow; information barriers ("walls") between research and banking and between research and trading; Regulation FD's prohibition on issuers selectively disclosing material information (and what the analyst should do if it happens to them); the line between legitimate *mosaic-theory* analysis (assembling nonmaterial public and nonpublic pieces into a material conclusion) and trading or publishing on a single piece of MNPI; quiet periods and restrictions tied to the firm's banking involvement with an issuer.
- **Applied skills**: map the MNPI risk across a management meeting, an internal banker conversation, an expert-network call, and a channel check, marking where to stop, wall off, or escalate; decide whether a specific datapoint is usable/publishable or must be escalated to compliance first; distinguish a defensible mosaic conclusion from improper use of a single piece of MNPI.
- **Common traps**: assuming a channel check or expert call can't produce MNPI because it didn't come from the company directly; publishing or trading on a tip received in a selective-disclosure situation instead of stopping and escalating; mistaking the mosaic theory for permission to act on one clearly material nonpublic fact; ignoring a banking-driven quiet period or restricted-list status because the analyst "already had the view."

## Capstone
Work a single fictional research-report packet and a related public appearance all the way through the Series 87 conduct lifecycle, producing the work product at each stage. Take an analyst at a firm that has an investment-banking relationship with the subject company, where the analyst personally owns a small position and a banker has been "encouraging" a more favorable rating, and build: (1) a fair-and-balanced audit of the draft report that flags promissory or exaggerated language, a price target with no disclosed basis, undefined ratings, and boilerplate risk language, with specific rewrites; (2) a conflict memo that names every conflict (banking relationship, analyst ownership, banker pressure, compensation linkage) and states for each whether the regime prohibits, controls, or requires disclosure of it; (3) the complete required-disclosure set plus a checked Regulation AC certification; (4) a public-appearance prep sheet for a TV interview about the same stock, with approved supportable points, required oral disclosures, prohibited statements, and a hard line on previewing an unpublished change; (5) a dissemination-and-correction playbook covering fair distribution and the handling of a discovered material error; (6) a supervisory-review and approval note showing who must approve before release, the evidence of review, and the escalation taken on the banker pressure; and (7) an MNPI boundary map for the management meeting, the internal banker contact, and a channel check, marking where to wall off or escalate. Close with one paragraph naming the single fact in the packet that, if it surfaced in an examination or a lawsuit, would most damage the firm — and the one disclosure, control, or record that, done right, would protect it. That instinct — to find the thing that would hurt you and put the protection in place *before* publication — is what separates an analyst who is merely right from one who is also defensible.

## Assessment Ideas
- Report-draft audits graded on fair-and-balanced judgment, price-target-basis discipline, and the separation of fact, estimate, and opinion.
- Conflict scenarios graded on correctly sorting conflicts into prohibited, controlled, and disclosable, and on escalation judgment.
- Disclosure and Regulation AC exercises graded on completeness, accuracy, and prominence.
- Public-appearance scenarios graded on approved-language discipline, oral-disclosure completeness, and the unpublished-research line.
- Capstone graded on issue spotting across the conduct lifecycle, defensible next actions, and the quality of the "what would hurt us / what protects us" reasoning.
