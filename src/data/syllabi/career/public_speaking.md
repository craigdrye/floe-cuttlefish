# Public Speaking
**ID**: `publicSpeaking` · **Discipline**: Communication · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course builds the skill of speaking to a real audience with a real purpose: standing in front of people (or a camera) and helping them understand, decide, or act. It treats public speaking not as performance art but as a service to the listener. The speaker's job is to reduce the audience's effort: to make a complex idea easy to follow, an important decision easy to make, and a difficult moment easy to sit through. Everything else, including delivery polish and slide design, is in service of that.

Learners finish able to do the full arc of a workplace talk: analyze who is listening and what they need, design a message with one durable takeaway and a structure that carries it, support claims with evidence and story without manipulating, deliver with control over pace and pause, build slides that help rather than compete, field hostile and tangled questions without losing the room, and run the same playbook over a camera when half the audience is invisible. The emphasis throughout is on judgment under pressure, because that is where most speakers actually fail.

The voice of the course is practical and slightly irreverent. Nerves, filler words, and slides with 82 words on them are treated as normal and fixable, not as moral failings. The standard for "good" is not eloquence; it is whether the audience left able to do the thing the talk was for.

## Course Design Notes
The previous syllabus had a sound spine (message design, delivery, audience adaptation, Q&A) but three weaknesses: the Course Aim leaned on a cute metaphor instead of stating an outcome; no chapter listed its common traps, so learners could not see the failure modes they were being trained out of; and the content named no concrete, checkable standards (timing math, the 10-20-30 rule, accessibility contrast, the much-abused Mehrabian ratio), which left questions floating at the level of generic advice. This rewrite keeps the eight-chapter sequence, restructures every chapter to Core questions / Key concepts / Applied skills / Common traps, and anchors the material in named, verifiable conventions so that questions can be exam-grade rather than vibes-grade.

Chapters move from inside the speaker outward: mindset and audience first, then the message, then evidence, then the body and the slides, then the genre (informative vs. persuasive vs. executive), then live interaction, then the virtual layer that now sits on top of almost everything. The Capstone is a portfolio rather than a single talk, because the discipline is a set of reusable skills, not one good performance.

## Chapter 1: Speaker Mindset and Audience Analysis
**Core questions**
- Who is in the room, what do they already know, and what do they need to do afterward?
- What is this occasion actually asking for: a decision, an update, reassurance, or a sale?
- How do I convert nervous arousal into useful focus instead of runaway pace?

**Key concepts**: purpose, audience knowledge level, occasion and constraints, stakes, speaker credibility (ethos), the audience's "so what," ethical speaking, physiological arousal vs. anxiety, the first thirty seconds.

**Applied skills**: write an audience brief (knowledge level, concerns, desired outcome, risks, adaptation plan); reframe a mixed expert/novice audience by leading with the shared decision and rationing jargon; build a pre-talk reset routine (breathing, anchor on the first sentence, controlled start).

**Common traps**: apologizing in the opening ("I didn't have much time"), which trains the audience to discount everything that follows; designing for the smartest person in the room and losing everyone else; treating nerves as a verdict on competence rather than as energy to redirect; opening with definitions or agenda housekeeping before the audience knows why they should care.

## Chapter 2: Message Design and Structure
**Core questions**
- What is the one sentence the audience should be able to repeat tomorrow?
- Which structure makes this idea easiest to follow (problem-solution, chronological, topical, compare/contrast)?
- Where will listeners get lost, and what transition prevents it?

**Key concepts**: central message / through-line, thesis, preview and signposting, transitions as connective tissue, the open-body-close arc, the rule of three, the call to action, the clean landing.

**Applied skills**: distill a sprawling update into a single takeaway, then test whether each section supports it; build an outline with a purposeful open, a three-part body, signposted transitions, and a close that restates the takeaway and names the next step; rewrite a weak transition that simply juxtaposes unrelated points.

**Common traps**: leading with nineteen facts instead of one message; a "messy middle" where good data has no through-line, so the audience keeps asking how points connect; trailing off ("so, yeah") instead of landing; introducing a brand-new complex idea in the final minute where it cannot be supported.

## Chapter 3: Evidence, Story, and Clarity
**Core questions**
- Which kind of evidence (example, statistic, expert testimony, analogy, story) will this specific audience actually trust?
- What story makes the stakes concrete without manipulating the listener?
- Which jargon should be translated, which defined, and which simply deleted?

**Key concepts**: evidence types and quality, citation and sourcing, the difference between a statistic and a meaningful comparison, analogy as a translation device, story spine, plain language vs. dumbing down, concrete vs. abstract, the curse of knowledge.

**Applied skills**: translate a dense technical cause into plain impact while keeping the detail available for Q&A; convert a briefing into a five-minute explanation with one example and one number that matters; pressure-test a statistic by asking "compared with what?"

**Common traps**: mistaking confusion for proof of expertise; deleting all substance in the name of "simplifying"; stacking acronyms so every sub-audience is equally lost; using a story that is emotionally effective but misrepresents the data; precision theater (a suspiciously specific number invented to sound authoritative).

## Chapter 4: Delivery Mechanics
**Core questions**
- Which delivery habits help comprehension, and which merely fill silence?
- Where should a deliberate pause do the work that words cannot?
- What does my body language signal about authority and ease?

**Key concepts**: pace and the comprehension cost of rushing, the strategic pause, projection vs. shouting, articulation, vocal variety and emphasis, eye contact, posture and stable stance, purposeful gesture, filler words, timing discipline and the rehearsal buffer. The Mehrabian "7-38-55" ratio is taught here as a cautionary tale: it came from narrow studies on communicating feelings when words and tone conflict, and does NOT mean content is only 7% of communication.

**Applied skills**: do the timing math (rehearse to slot length minus a buffer for live variability); slow down and pause before a key number so it lands; raise projection and articulation for the back row without shouting; record, review against a delivery checklist, and revise.

**Common traps**: sprinting through the key sentence when nerves spike; turning to read the slide and dropping both projection and eye contact; apologizing for pauses, which converts the strongest tool into a liability; pacing and gesturing that distract instead of support; quoting the Mehrabian ratio to claim words barely matter.

## Chapter 5: Visuals and Presentation Aids
**Core questions**
- Does this slide help the audience think, or does it just prove the speaker filled a template?
- What belongs on the slide, what belongs in speech, and what should be cut entirely?
- Can someone in the back row read and understand each visual within a few seconds?

**Key concepts**: slide as support not script, signal-to-noise and visual hierarchy, one idea per slide, chart clarity and honest axes, image and data-ink discipline, speaker notes, handouts vs. live slides, the demo. Named conventions: Guy Kawasaki's 10-20-30 rule (about 10 slides, 20 minutes, no font below 30 point, originally for pitch decks); WCAG AA color-contrast minimums (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text) so slides remain readable for low-vision audiences.

**Applied skills**: cut an 82-word slide down to a single supported point; redesign a cluttered deck for an executive vs. a teaching context; check a slide for contrast and font size against the back-row and accessibility standard; decide what to say aloud rather than display.

**Common traps**: reading slides aloud (telling the audience the speaker adds nothing beyond the deck); shrinking fonts to fit more rather than cutting content; decoration (explosions, clip art) used to rescue an overloaded slide; charts with truncated or mislabeled axes; treating 10-20-30 as a universal law rather than a starting discipline.

## Chapter 6: Informative, Persuasive, and Executive Speaking
**Core questions**
- Am I informing, persuading, recommending, or facilitating a decision, and does my structure match?
- Which objection should I answer before the audience raises it?
- What single decision does this meeting need to produce?

**Key concepts**: informative vs. persuasive speech, the executive briefing, decision-first structure, the recommendation-evidence-risk-next-step pattern, stakeholder framing, pre-empting objections, the difference between giving information and asking for a decision.

**Applied skills**: deliver a three-minute executive briefing that leads with the decision and the two or three facts that shape it; structure a persuasive ask as recommendation, evidence, risk control, and next step; trim a 35-slide project history into the few facts an executive needs to approve a budget.

**Common traps**: burying the recommendation so leaders have to hunt for it; opening with origin-story "lore" that burns the time budget before the ask arrives; presenting all risks with no proposed action; withholding a recommendation to "stay flexible," which just makes the audience do the speaker's job.

## Chapter 7: Q&A, Panels, and Impromptu Speaking
**Core questions**
- What is the real question behind the question?
- When should I answer directly, clarify, redirect, or promise a follow-up?
- How do I stay concise and composed without sounding evasive?

**Key concepts**: the answer-bridge-return structure, restating a tangled question, admitting uncertainty with authority, handling hostile or loaded questions, panel etiquette and concise handoffs, impromptu structure (point-reason-example-point), the difference between confidence and fabrication.

**Applied skills**: restate a multi-part question into one answerable version before answering; respond to an aggressive challenge by acknowledging the concern, answering the substance calmly, and offering follow-up; say "I don't have that figure; I'll confirm and send the source" instead of inventing a number; build on a co-panelist's point rather than repeating it.

**Common traps**: answering the last noun you heard instead of the actual question; matching a hostile questioner's tone and losing the rest of the room; inventing a precise figure to sound confident; turning one question into a 20-minute lecture that eats the rest of Q&A; reflexively blaming another team.

## Chapter 8: Virtual and Hybrid Presentation Skills
**Core questions**
- What changes when the audience is remote, multitasking, and partly invisible?
- How do I invite participation without creating chaos?
- What backup plan keeps the meeting alive when the tech fails?
- In a hybrid room, how do I keep remote attendees from becoming second-class participants?

**Key concepts**: camera presence and looking at the lens (not the on-screen faces) to create apparent eye contact, camera height at or slightly above eye level, screen-share hygiene, using chat as a structured channel, deliberate pacing for lag and divided attention, hybrid equity, the tech check, the recording, accessibility (captions, alt text, contrast).

**Applied skills**: deliver a virtual briefing with timed chat prompts, clear visuals, and a stated backup plan; set up a frame that looks like eye contact and a readable, accessible screen share; design a hybrid agenda that gives remote attendees an equal voice; run a two-minute tech check before a high-stakes call.

**Common traps**: looking at the gallery of faces instead of the lens, so you never appear to make eye contact; pacing as if the room can keep up despite lag; opening chat with no structure and drowning in noise; having no plan for when the screen share dies; letting the in-room conversation steamroll the remote attendees in a hybrid meeting.

## Capstone: Speaker Portfolio
Learners assemble a portfolio that exercises the whole arc:
- an audience brief for a real upcoming talk
- an informative speech: outline plus a recording
- a persuasive or recommendation talk using the recommendation-evidence-risk-next-step structure
- a redesigned slide deck (before/after) that passes a back-row and contrast check
- a Q&A response log including at least one hostile and one "I don't know" answer
- a peer-feedback and revision memo
- a virtual presentation plan with chat prompts, accessibility notes, and a backup plan
- a final live or recorded presentation that ties it together

## Assessment Ideas
- Audience analysis brief
- Outline and through-line check
- Informative speech (recorded)
- Persuasive / recommendation talk
- Slide redesign with accessibility and back-row review
- Q&A and impromptu drill (including hostile-question handling)
- Virtual briefing with backup plan
- Speaker Portfolio capstone
