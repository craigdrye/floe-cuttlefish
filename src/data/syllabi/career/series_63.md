# Series 63: The State-Law Detective License
**ID**: `series63` · **Discipline**: Finance

## Course Aim
The Series 63 is the Uniform Securities Agent State Law Examination — administered by FINRA but written by NASAA, the North American Securities Administrators Association. It is the "blue-sky" license: a state-law co-qualification that pairs with the Series 6, 7, 22, or 79 to let an agent solicit business in essentially every US state. Most candidates take it within 30 days of passing the Series 7 because almost every employer requires it before allowing client contact. Without it, you can have your Series 7 and still be unable to call a customer.

The exam is short — 60 scored questions in 75 minutes — but it has earned a reputation for being trap-laden out of proportion to its length. The reason is that the Uniform Securities Act (USA), passed in its current form in 1956 and modified by NASAA over the decades, contains adjacent terms that *sound* interchangeable but are not: "exempt security" vs "exempt transaction," "offer" vs "sale," "broker-dealer" vs "agent," "investment adviser" vs "investment adviser representative." Reading the wrong noun out of a fact pattern costs the question. The Series 63 is a definitions exam at heart — the most lawyerly of the FINRA-administered tests, even though FINRA only proctors it.

What you leave with is the instinct to ask the right four questions of any state-securities fact pattern: (1) Is this thing a *security* under the Act? (2) Is the *person* a BD, agent, IA, or IA rep — and is there an exclusion? (3) Is the *security or transaction* exempt from registration? (4) What can the Administrator actually *do* in response? Master those four, and you have basically mapped the exam. The Floe Series 63 voice is precise, lawyerly, and intentionally repetitive — we will drill the definition of "agent" forty different ways because that is what the exam does to you.

The Series 63 is not technically a hard exam in topic count. It *is* a hard exam in misreading rate. Speed matters too: 75 seconds per question is the tightest per-question budget of any FINRA-administered exam.

## Course Design Notes
Route any question involving NASAA Model Rules, the Uniform Securities Act sections 101-509, state-level registration, "federal covered securities" under NSMIA, agent/IA-rep distinctions, Administrator enforcement powers, NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest and Unethical Practices, or definitions like "offer," "sale," "fraud," and "exempt transaction" here. The voice should be precise, dry, and lawyerly. Always anchor each chapter on the federal-vs-state line: NSMIA preempted state substantive regulation for many securities (so notice-filing only), and Dodd-Frank moved the IA registration line to $100M AUM.

Where SIE is "what is a security under federal law," Series 63 is "what is a security under *this state's* Act, and what exclusions or exemptions remove the registration burden." Where Series 7 is "is this product right for this client," Series 63 is "did the agent commit a dishonest or unethical practice under NASAA's Model Rule." Where Series 79 is "is this offering 506(b), 506(c), or Reg A+," Series 63 is "what state filings, fees, and notice obligations apply to that federal offering."

Lean retrieval-heavy and definition-first. Use traps deliberately — for any "exempt security" question, include "exempt transaction" as a wrong answer, and vice versa.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The Series 63 has **60 scored questions plus 5 unscored pretest items in 75 minutes**. Passing is 72% (43 of 60 correct). The NASAA current outline:

| Topic | Exam Weight | Scored Qs | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Regulation of Investment Advisers and IARs (incl. federal covered) | 5% | 3 | Light, but know the $100M/$110M Dodd-Frank line and LATE exclusions |
| Regulation of Broker-Dealers and Agents | 33% | 20 | Biggest category — BD/agent definitions, registrations, exclusions, multi-state |
| Regulation of Securities and Issuers | 22% | 13 | Federal covered, exempt securities, exempt transactions, registration methods |
| Remedies and Administrative Provisions | 11% | 7 | Administrator powers, civil/criminal penalties, recordkeeping, rescission |
| Ethical Practices and Fiduciary Obligations | 29% | 17 | NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest and Unethical Practices |

**Study cadence: 2-3 weeks at 6-8 hours per week** is realistic for a candidate who just passed the Series 7. The exam is short enough that a single intensive weekend can pass it, but the trap-density rewards an extra week of scenario practice.

- **2-week sprint (post-Series 7)**: Week 1 — definitions + BD/agent registration + securities/exempt transactions; Week 2 — NASAA ethics rules + Administrator powers + 2 full-length practice exams. Drill 30 questions/day minimum.
- **3-week standard**: Week 1 — definitions + IA/IAR; Week 2 — BD/agent registration + securities registration + exempts; Week 3 — ethics + administrator + 2 full-length exams. Use the third week to stress-test edge cases.
- **4-week deep prep (no Series 7 yet)**: Same as 3-week but add a full week dedicated to NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest and Unethical Practices — that single document drives nearly 30% of the exam.

Open the question bank in day 1. Do not read first and drill later — Series 63 has too many false-friend definitions to learn passively.

## Chapter 1: Definitions That Run the Whole Exam
**Core questions:** Is this person a broker-dealer, agent, investment adviser, IA representative, issuer, or none of the above under the Uniform Securities Act?

**Key concepts**
- "Security" under USA §401(m): Howey test, statutory inclusions (notes, stocks, bonds, investment contracts), exclusions (insurance, fixed annuities, commodity futures, currencies)
- "Person" includes individuals, corporations, partnerships, governments, trusts (very broad)
- "Offer," "offer to sell," "sale": gifts of assessable stock are sales; bona fide gifts of non-assessable stock are not
- "Broker-dealer" §401(c) definition; exclusions: agents, banks (subject to GLBA carve-outs), issuers, out-of-state BDs with no place of business serving only existing clients (snowbird), other excluded persons
- "Agent" §401(b): individuals representing a BD or issuer in effecting transactions; exclusions for clerical/ministerial employees and certain issuer reps for exempt securities or exempt transactions
- "Investment adviser" §401(f) ABC test: advice, business, compensation
- "Investment adviser representative" §401(g)

**Applied skills**
- Identify whether a clerical employee is an "agent" requiring registration (almost always no, unless they take orders)
- Determine when a BD has a "place of business" in a state (a desk and a phone for client meetings = yes)
- Recognize an investment contract hidden inside a non-traditional offering (Howey: investment of money in a common enterprise with expectation of profit from the efforts of others)

**Common traps**
- An *agent* under the USA is an *individual*, not an entity. Entities are BDs. Candidates miss this every time.
- A BD has *no place of business* in a state if it only solicits *existing* clients who are temporarily there (snowbird exclusion). Soliciting any *new* clients destroys the exclusion.
- Issuer reps are excluded from agent registration *only* when effecting transactions in exempt securities or exempt transactions — not for all issuer transactions.
- Insurance products (whole life, term, fixed annuities) are *not* securities. Variable life and variable annuities *are* securities under federal law and require federal registration; states often still require notice filing.

**Authoritative sources:** Uniform Securities Act §§401-414; NASAA Model Rule on Definitions; SEC v. W.J. Howey Co. (1946); Reves v. Ernst & Young (1990, family resemblance test for notes).

## Chapter 2: Registration of Broker-Dealers and Agents
**Core questions:** Who must register, in which state, and what filings, fees, bonds, and ongoing obligations apply?

**Key concepts**
- BD registration: Form BD, financial requirements (net capital), surety bond if Administrator requires
- Agent registration: Form U4, fingerprints, examination, fee
- Snowbird rule (existing-client temporary visitor); de minimis exemption (varies — most states recognize fewer than 5-6 clients in 12 months for IAs, not for BDs)
- Out-of-state BD: must register in any state where it has a place of business *or* solicits non-exempt customers
- Effectiveness: registration becomes effective at noon on the 30th day after a complete filing, absent a stop order
- Termination: Form U5 within 30 days; Administrator retains jurisdiction for 1 year after termination

**Applied skills**
- Identify when an out-of-state BD must register in a given state
- Determine whether a single client interaction triggers agent registration
- Process a termination and the associated reporting obligations

**Common traps**
- An *agent* registration belongs to the BD employing the agent. When the agent leaves the BD, *both* the agent and the BD must file U5s within 30 days, and the agent's registration ceases until re-registered through a new BD.
- BD de minimis exemptions are rare. The IA de minimis (5 or fewer non-institutional clients in 12 months with no place of business) does not apply to BDs.
- The Administrator retains *one year* of jurisdiction over a terminated agent for purposes of investigating misconduct that occurred during the registration period.
- Surety bond: Administrator may require a bond, but cannot require it if the BD meets minimum net capital under Rule 15c3-1.

**Authoritative sources:** USA §§201-203, 305; NASAA Model Rule on BD Registration; FINRA Form U4/U5 General Instructions.

## Chapter 3: Investment Advisers and IA Reps Under State Law
**Core questions:** Is this person an IA under state law, federal law, or both — and what does the Dodd-Frank $100M / $110M line do?

**Key concepts**
- IA definition (§401(f) ABC test): Advice on securities, in the Business of providing such advice, for Compensation
- IA exclusions: **LATE** (Lawyers, Accountants, Teachers, Engineers — only when advice is *incidental* to their profession and no special compensation), BDs (when advice is solely incidental to brokerage and no special compensation), publishers (general circulation, not personalized advice), federal-covered IAs (state preempted)
- IAR definition; exclusions for clerical and for individuals serving only institutional clients
- Federal vs state IA registration: under $100M AUM → state; $100M-$110M AUM → either; over $110M → must register with SEC
- Switching IARs: must withdraw from state if AUM crosses $110M; must withdraw from SEC if AUM drops below $90M
- Brochure (ADV Part 2) delivery: initial 48 hours before agreement, or at signing with 5-day rescission right; annual delivery within 120 days of fiscal year end if material changes
- Performance fees: limited to "qualified clients" — $1.1M AUM with adviser or $2.2M net worth (excluding primary residence), per SEC Rule 205-3 (most recent inflation adjustment 2021)

**Applied skills**
- Categorize an adviser as state, federal-covered, or excluded
- Apply the LATE exclusion to a fact pattern (CPA giving incidental advice — excluded; CPA charging separately for investment advice — IA)
- Determine brochure delivery timing and exceptions

**Common traps**
- A *publisher* exclusion requires general circulation *and* impersonal advice. A newsletter customized for one subscriber loses the exclusion.
- The $100M-$110M "buffer band" is intentional — to avoid constant switching when AUM hovers near the line. Adviser stays where it is until it definitively crosses the relevant threshold.
- Performance fees are barred for *non-qualified* clients under the Advisers Act (and most state laws). Hedge funds satisfy this by limiting investors to qualified clients/purchasers.
- A BD whose advice is *not* "solely incidental" to brokerage (e.g., charges a separate advisory fee, holds itself out as an advisor) loses the BD exclusion and must register as an IA.

**Authoritative sources:** Dodd-Frank Title IV; IAA §202(a)(11); SEC Rule 203A; Rule 205-3 (qualified clients); NASAA Statement on Federal Covered Advisers.

## Chapter 4: Securities Registration and Exemptions
**Core questions:** Does this security have to register at the state level, and if not, why is it exempt?

**Key concepts**
- Three state registration methods: **Notification** / **Filing** (simplest, for established issuers — USA §302), **Coordination** (with concurrent federal registration — §303), **Qualification** (full state review, smaller in-state offerings — §304)
- Federal covered securities under **NSMIA** (1996): exchange-listed (NMS Tier 1), 1940 Act registered investment companies, Reg D Rule 506 offerings, large issuer secondary offerings, qualified plan securities. States limited to notice filings, fees, and antifraud enforcement.
- **Exempt securities** §402(a): US/state government, Canadian government, foreign government recognized by US, bank/S&L issues, insurance company issues (except variable products), public utility issues, nonprofit issues, commercial paper (270 days or less, $50K minimum denomination, top 3 ratings), employee benefit plan interests
- **Exempt transactions** §402(b): isolated nonissuer, unsolicited brokerage, institutional investor, sales to existing security holders (rights offerings), preorganization (10 or fewer subscribers, 12 months, no consideration), private placement (10 or fewer offers in 12 months — Uniform Limited Offering Exemption / ULOE)
- Effectiveness by Coordination: simultaneous with federal effectiveness; by Filing or Qualification: 30 days after complete filing
- Form D: filed with SEC within 15 days of first sale; states may require concurrent notice filing

**Applied skills**
- Identify whether a transaction or the *security itself* creates the exemption (a single muni bond sold via an unsolicited inquiry is double-exempt — both the security and the transaction)
- Determine state filing requirements for a Reg D 506 offering (notice filing + fee only, per NSMIA)
- Spot when a "private placement" loses its exemption (general solicitation, too many offerees, lack of accredited investor verification under 506(c))

**Common traps**
- An **exempt security** stays exempt in any transaction. An **exempt transaction** does not. The same bond can be sold in an exempt transaction (institutional) one day and a non-exempt transaction (retail) the next.
- **Federal covered ≠ exempt.** Federal covered means states cannot require substantive registration — but states *can* require notice filings, fees, consent to service of process, and antifraud enforcement.
- Rule 506(b): up to 35 non-accredited (but sophisticated) investors + unlimited accredited; no general solicitation. Rule 506(c): unlimited accredited only, *verified* accredited, general solicitation permitted.
- A bona fide gift is *not* an offer or sale. But a "gift" of *assessable* stock (stock with future capital calls) is treated as a sale.

**Authoritative sources:** Uniform Securities Act §§301-306, 401-402; NSMIA (Pub. L. 104-290); Regulation D (17 CFR §§230.500-506); NASAA model rule on ULOE.

## Chapter 5: Ethical Practices, Conflicts, and Compensation
**Core questions:** What conduct counts as fraud, dishonest practice, or unethical behavior under NASAA's Statement of Policy?

**Key concepts**
- Fraud under USA §501: misrepresentation or omission of material fact, employment of a scheme to defraud
- NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest and Unethical Practices — the single most exam-driving document. Examples: churning, unauthorized trading, guaranteeing against loss, sharing in customer accounts without written consent and proportionate sharing, selling away, borrowing from / lending to customers (limited exceptions for affiliates and family), front running, market manipulation
- Suitability under NASAA Model Rule (BD agents); fiduciary duty for IAs (loyalty + care + best execution + disclosure of conflicts)
- Borrowing from clients: generally prohibited unless client is in the business of lending (bank), is an affiliate, or is an immediate family member — and firm has consented in writing
- Soft dollars under §28(e) of Exchange Act safe harbor: brokerage and research products only; mixed-use disclosure required
- Agency cross transactions: written disclosure, written consent, annual statement of cross transactions
- Custody rules for IAs (NASAA Model Custody Rule): qualified custodian, surprise audit, $35K-$50K bond depending on jurisdiction; deemed-custody triggers include directly deducting fees from client accounts (some states deem this custody, some don't)

**Applied skills**
- Identify a dishonest practice from a one-paragraph scenario (sharing without written consent; guaranteeing against loss)
- Distinguish between permitted and prohibited gifts/entertainment ($100 gift limit per FINRA; NASAA generally aligns)
- Apply the IA custody rule to a fee-deduction scenario

**Common traps**
- "Sharing in customer accounts" is allowed under FINRA Rule 3240 if: (1) the customer consents in writing, (2) the firm consents in writing, (3) sharing is *proportionate* to the agent's capital contribution. Immediate family exception waives proportionality but still requires firm consent.
- Guaranteeing a customer against loss is *always* prohibited, even with written consent. This is a per se violation.
- Soft dollars must fall within the §28(e) safe harbor — research and brokerage. Office furniture, salaries, computer hardware (general use): outside safe harbor and must be disclosed.
- "Selling away" is selling a security not on the BD's approved product list without the BD's knowledge and written approval. It is a per se violation.

**Authoritative sources:** USA §501; NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest and Unethical Practices of BDs and Agents (1983, amended); NASAA Model Rule on Unethical Business Practices of IAs/IARs; SEC §28(e) Soft Dollars safe harbor.

## Chapter 6: Administrator Powers, Remedies, and Recordkeeping
**Core questions:** What can the state Administrator do, what are the procedural and statutory limits, and what penalties attach to violations?

**Key concepts**
- Administrator powers §401(2): rulemaking, registration, enforcement, investigation (subpoena power that extends out of state for in-state investigations)
- Civil and criminal penalties: criminal up to **3 years prison and $5,000** fine under USA (states often adopt higher); civil up to triple damages plus costs
- Statute of limitations §509: civil = 2 years from discovery or 3 years from sale, whichever is **earlier**; criminal = 5 years
- Cease-and-desist orders (Administrator can issue without prior hearing for "serious risk" situations, subject to subsequent hearing)
- Denial, suspension, revocation of registration: requires notice + hearing, except summary suspension in serious cases
- Rescission offers: principal plus interest at legal rate (often 6-8%), less income received by investor (dividends/coupons)
- Recordkeeping: BD records — **3 years** total (first 2 years readily accessible). IA records — **5 years** total (first 2 years readily accessible)
- Consent to service of process: must be filed with the Administrator; legal process can be served on the Administrator who forwards to the registrant

**Applied skills**
- Calculate a rescission offer amount (principal + statutory interest − income received)
- Determine when the Administrator's subpoena reaches across state lines (when conducting an investigation of in-state conduct, yes)
- Identify which violation triggers criminal vs civil treatment (willful = criminal possible; non-willful = civil only)

**Common traps**
- Criminal violations under the USA require **willfulness** (knowledge of wrongdoing) — though "no knowledge of the rule itself" is not a defense if conduct was knowing.
- Statute of limitations: 2 years from discovery **OR** 3 years from sale, whichever is **first** to expire. Candidates often invert this.
- Recordkeeping difference: **BDs 3 years, IAs 5 years**. The "first 2 readily accessible" rule applies to both.
- A *cease and desist* can be issued without a prior hearing if the Administrator finds an emergency; the registrant must then be given an opportunity for hearing.

**Authoritative sources:** USA §§401-414, 508-509; NASAA Model Rule on Recordkeeping (BDs 3 yrs; IAs 5 yrs); SEC Rule 17a-4 (BDs); SEC Rule 204-2 (IAs).

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
75 minutes for 60 scored items is **75 seconds each** — Series 63 is the most time-pressured FINRA exam per question. There is no calculator (none needed — almost no math on this exam) and most questions are pure recall.

- **Read the question stem twice before reading the answers.** Misreading "exempt security" as "exempt transaction" is the single most common loss on this exam. Underline the key noun on scratch paper if it helps.
- **When in doubt, the answer is usually the most restrictive interpretation of the Act.** State law is protective of investors; defaults run that direction.
- **The four-question filter**: For any fact pattern, ask (1) is this a security? (2) what is this person? (3) is there an exemption or exclusion? (4) what can the Administrator do? Run that filter on every item.
- **Speed**: Aim for 20 questions every 25 minutes. If you are at question 20 at the 30-minute mark, you are slow — flag and move.
- **Day before**: Re-read NASAA's Statement of Policy on Dishonest and Unethical Practices. Re-skim USA §§401-402 definitions. Light review only. Sleep.

If your last two full-length practice exams averaged 80%+, you are ready. The Series 63 is famously variable on exam day because of trap-density — give yourself a 5-point buffer.
