# SQE 2: Practical Legal Skills for Day-One Solicitors
**ID**: `sqe2` · **Discipline**: Law

## Course Aim
SQE 2 is where the SRA stops asking "do you know the rule?" and starts asking "would a competent newly qualified solicitor actually act on it?" The exam is structured around the SRA's Statement of Solicitor Competence and tests the six practical legal skills — client interview and attendance note, advocacy, case and matter analysis, legal research, legal writing, legal drafting — across five practice areas: criminal litigation, dispute resolution, property practice, wills and intestacy with probate administration, and business organisations including rules and procedures. Ethics under the SRA Standards and Regulations are interlaced through every station; there is no separate ethics paper.

The assessment runs over three-and-a-half days: 16 written tasks taken across approximately three days at a Pearson VUE-style centre (legal research, legal writing, legal drafting, case and matter analysis), plus four oral tasks (two client interviews each followed by an attendance note, and two advocacy submissions) on the remaining day. Each task is graded against both legal skill (technique, structure, professional conduct) and application of law (correct rules, appropriate citations, accurate procedure) on a comparative judgment basis. Marks are aggregated across all 20 stations; you cannot specialise into a corner.

Floe presents SQE 2 material as single-best-answer multiple-choice scenarios that compress the oral and written stations into one decision per question. Every prompt should read like a real piece of practice in disguise: a trainee in a high street firm interviewing a worried client; a duty solicitor at the police station deciding whether to advise comment in interview; a property solicitor in a commercial conveyance choosing between exchanging and synchronising. The wrong options must feel like things a tired trainee might genuinely do. The course voice is a supervising solicitor who is firm about ethics, calm about deadlines, and unimpressed by templated answers.

The goal is the day-one solicitor reflex: spot the procedural step, place the ethical guardrail, name the rule, and act. Citations matter: CrimPR 2020 Part 8 for case management, CPR Part 36 for offers, CPIA 1996 section 3 for disclosure, SRA Code paragraph 1.4 for the duty not to mislead the court. If you cannot name it under exam pressure, you cannot rely on it.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test applied legal skills under SRA SQE 2 conditions across the six skills and five practice areas. Voice should sound like a supervising solicitor: firm about ethics, calm about deadlines, impatient with templated answers. Every distractor should map to a real practice mistake — failing a conflict check before substantive discussion, drafting particulars of claim that omit a required element under CPR 16.4, advising on the law in a forum without authority, missing a procedural deadline by one day.

Floe presents four options per question — one correct, three plausible distractors — and the right answer is the one a competent newly qualified solicitor would actually take, not the cleverest legal argument available. Use English procedural and substantive terminology throughout: claimant, particulars of claim, without prejudice, Crown Court, magistrates' court, Form TR1, IHT400. Where a statutory citation is needed, cite by short name and section.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The SQE 2 assessment is 16 written tasks plus 4 oral tasks delivered over approximately 3.5 days, all booked at SRA-approved venues. There is no published topic weighting between practice areas; the SRA randomises tasks across the five areas so that the candidate cannot specialise into a corner. Each task tests at least one legal skill plus at least one practice area; ethics weaves through both axes.

| Skill | Number of Stations | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| Client Interview and Attendance Note | 2 oral + note | Listen first, structure second, never lead the client |
| Advocacy | 2 oral | Submissions that land in under 5 minutes, with authority |
| Case and Matter Analysis | 4 written | Read fast, plan visibly, recommend clearly |
| Legal Research | 4 written | From statute to commentary to case in a defensible chain |
| Legal Writing | 4 written | Letters and emails that explain without lecturing |
| Legal Drafting | 4 written | Particulars, witness statements, clauses, attendance notes |

| Practice Area | Stations Across All Skills | Focus |
|---|---:|---|
| Criminal Litigation | ~4 | CrimPR 2020, sentencing, disclosure under CPIA 1996, custody time limits |
| Dispute Resolution | ~4 | CPR Parts 1-72, Part 36 offers, costs management, disclosure |
| Property Practice | ~4 | Residential and commercial conveyancing; leases |
| Wills and Intestacy / Probate Administration | ~4 | Grants, IHT400, distribution priorities |
| Business Organisations | ~4 | Resolutions, written resolutions, share allotment, dividends, CS01 filings |

Suggested cadence: roughly 300 hours of focused skills practice typically following SQE 1 by at least two weeks (often run in parallel with the SRA's Qualifying Work Experience). Weekly mock interviews recorded on phone audio, weekly drafting timed to 60 minutes per task, standing case analysis rotations through the five practice areas, and at least two full mock oral submissions per fortnight with peer review against the SRA's published criteria.

## Chapter 1: Client Interview and Attendance Note Discipline
**Core questions:** How do you open an interview with a client you have never met, gather facts without leading, identify ethical hazards in real time, and produce an attendance note that a partner could rely on a year later?

**Key concepts**
- WASP and similar interview structures: welcome, acquiring information, supplying advice, parting
- Open and closed question sequencing, active listening, summarising
- Conflict checks, identity and source-of-funds verification, capacity
- Client care letter requirements under the SRA Transparency Rules and Code paragraph 8.6
- Attendance note structure: parties, time, advice given, next steps, who said what

**Applied legal skills**
- Spot a money laundering red flag mid-interview without breaching tipping-off rules under POCA 2002 section 333A
- Convert a rambling client narrative into a chronological fact summary
- Decide when to advise on the spot and when to take instructions back to the file

**Common traps**
- When taking instructions in a criminal case at the police station, never give legal advice if you suspect a conflict between two clients — withdraw and offer to refer to another solicitor. Do not try to act for both unless the conflict can be managed within SRA Code paragraph 6.2
- Asking leading questions at the fact-gathering stage — the SRA marks this down on interview stations because it imports your assumptions into the client narrative
- Failing to record advice given in the attendance note — if it is not noted, you cannot defend it later
- Promising an outcome ("I'm sure we'll win this") rather than discussing prospects — breaches paragraph 8.6 transparency obligations

**Authoritative sources**
- SRA Standards and Regulations, in particular the Code paragraphs 6.1-6.3, 7.1, 8.6
- SRA SQE 2 Assessment Specification (interview and attendance note criteria)
- Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017

**Practice deliverables**
- Recorded mock interviews scored against an SRA-aligned competency grid
- Attendance note bank: 20 worked examples in property, dispute, and family

## Chapter 2: Advocacy in the Magistrates' and County Courts
**Core questions:** How do you make a bail application, a plea in mitigation, or an interim application submission that is brief, courteous, and grounded in authority? When do you concede and when do you press?

**Key concepts**
- Bail Act 1976 schedule 1 considerations and the exceptions to bail
- Sentencing Council guidelines structure for a plea in mitigation
- Interim applications under CPR 23: structure, evidence, order sought
- Court etiquette, modes of address (your Worships, your Honour, my Lord/Lady), duty to the court under SRA Principle 1 and Code paragraph 1.4
- Skeleton arguments and authority bundles
- Oral advocacy timing: roughly 45 minutes per advocacy oral including 15-20 minutes' reading time plus a 4-5 minute submission

**Applied legal skills**
- Open and close a bail application within 4-5 minutes
- Use a Sentencing Council guideline step by step in a real mitigation
- Recover gracefully from a judge's intervention that changes your case theory

**Common traps**
- Treating the right to comment on a defendant's character as automatic — a plea in mitigation cannot contradict the basis of plea
- Citing authority you have not read — the SRA marks down advocacy stations where the candidate cannot answer a clarifying question
- Failing to address the specific Bail Act 1976 schedule 1 grounds being relied on by the prosecution
- Reading rather than presenting — advocacy oral stations award structure and authority, not eloquence

**Authoritative sources**
- Bail Act 1976; Sentencing Code (Sentencing Act 2020); Criminal Procedure Rules 2020
- SRA SQE 2 Assessment Specification (advocacy criteria)

**Practice deliverables**
- Weekly five-minute submission, recorded and self-marked against the criteria
- Authority bundle template with index, tabs, and pin-cites

## Chapter 3: Legal Research and the Defensible Chain
**Core questions:** Where do you start when the partner says "find out whether we can rectify this"? How do you record the research so a colleague can repeat it?

**Key concepts**
- Statute first, then statutory instruments, then case law, then commentary
- Westlaw, LexisLibrary, Practical Law, and free sources used in practice
- Reading a case for ratio, obiter, and subsequent treatment
- Updating: KeyCite, CaseAnalysis, and "is it still good law?"
- Research notes that record query, sources, reasoning, and a defensible conclusion
- Time budget: a legal research task on SQE 2 runs about 45 minutes including reading the instructions

**Applied legal skills**
- Build a research trail in 45 minutes that withstands a partner review
- Recognise when a question is about jurisdiction, not substance
- Decline to give advice outside a solicitor's competence

**Common traps**
- Going to commentary first because it is faster — the SRA marks down research that does not anchor to primary authority
- Citing a case without checking subsequent treatment — overruled or distinguished cases are a research-station failure
- Confusing retained EU law (EUWA 2018 / REUL Act 2023 assimilated law) with current EU law — the latter no longer applies in England and Wales
- Stopping at the first answer that fits the facts — research stations reward consideration of counterarguments

**Authoritative sources**
- Westlaw, LexisLibrary, Practical Law; The Law Society research guides
- SRA SQE 2 Assessment Specification (legal research criteria)

**Practice deliverables**
- Five worked research notes, each with hyperlinks, citations, and a one-paragraph conclusion
- Personal research checklist taped above the desk

## Chapter 4: Legal Writing for Real Clients
**Core questions:** How do you write a letter or email that the client actually reads, that withstands later disclosure, and that complies with the SRA Code?

**Key concepts**
- Plain English without losing precision; defined terms used sparingly
- Without prejudice and without prejudice save as to costs labelling
- Privilege: legal advice privilege and litigation privilege boundaries; common-interest privilege
- Tone for a worried client versus a sophisticated commercial party
- File-note shadowing: every external letter has an internal record

**Applied legal skills**
- Translate a draft particulars of claim into a client-friendly summary letter
- Spot when a draft email risks waiver of privilege
- Choose between letter, email, and call for the same message

**Common traps**
- Labelling client-care letters "without prejudice" — without prejudice attaches to genuine settlement communications, not retainer terms
- Marking a letter to opposing solicitors with the wrong privilege label and inviting an inadvertent disclosure dispute
- Using boilerplate that contradicts the substantive advice in the body (a partner-marked station failure)
- Burying a deadline mid-paragraph — material dates belong at the top of the letter and in a bullet at the bottom

**Authoritative sources**
- SRA Code paragraph 8.6 transparency; Solicitors Practice Notes on writing to clients
- Three Rivers (No 5 and No 6) on legal advice privilege scope

**Practice deliverables**
- Five client letters across criminal, civil, property, probate, and corporate
- One redrafted partner letter showing tracked changes and rationale

## Chapter 5: Legal Drafting Across the Five Practice Areas
**Core questions:** What does a competent first draft look like for particulars of claim, a witness statement, a simple share purchase clause, a will codicil, and a contract for sale?

**Key concepts**
- CPR Part 16 requirements for particulars of claim (16.4) and defence (16.5)
- Practice Direction 32 statement of truth and witness statement formalities; PD 57AC for Business and Property Courts trial witness statements
- Share purchase agreement (SPA) boilerplate: warranties, indemnities, limitation clauses
- Wills Act 1837 section 9 plus codicil revocation and republication
- Standard Conditions of Sale 5th edition (residential) and Standard Commercial Property Conditions 3rd edition (commercial)
- Drafting time budget: roughly 45 minutes per drafting task on SQE 2

**Applied legal skills**
- Plead a misrepresentation claim with each element on its own paragraph
- Draft a witness statement that respects PD 57AC trial witness statement rules in the Business and Property Courts
- Write a clear definitions clause that does not collapse later in the document

**Common traps**
- Drafting particulars of claim alleging breach of an oral contract for the sale of goods over £50,000 without naming each contractual term, the breach, the loss, and the relief sought — CPR 16.4 requires a concise statement of facts including all material allegations
- Including evidence in particulars of claim — CPR 16 forbids it; evidence goes in witness statements
- Drafting a witness statement in the lawyer's voice rather than the witness's first-person voice — PD 32 paragraph 18.1 expressly requires the witness's own words
- Forgetting to date and pin every defined term back to its first introduction — drafting marks penalise the orphaned defined term

**Authoritative sources**
- CPR Parts 16, 22 (statement of truth), 32; PD 57AC
- Wills Act 1837 section 9; SCS 5th edition; SCPC 3rd edition

**Practice deliverables**
- Drafting portfolio with one document per practice area, partner-marked
- Style guide page for paragraph numbering, defined terms, and date conventions

## Chapter 6: Case and Matter Analysis Under Time Pressure
**Core questions:** Given a 12-page file extract and 60 minutes, can you identify the issues, weigh the evidence, advise on next steps, and flag every ethical and procedural risk?

**Key concepts**
- Issue spotting across substantive law, procedure, evidence, and conduct
- Limitation periods: Limitation Act 1980 baseline (6 years for contract section 5; 6 years for tort other than personal injury section 2; 3 years for personal injury section 11; 1 year for defamation; 15-year longstop under the Latent Damage Act 1986)
- Costs analysis: budgets under CPR 3, hourly rates, fixed costs, conditional fee arrangements, damages-based agreements
- Risk and merits assessment frameworks for litigation funding decisions
- Strategy memos that separate facts, law, options, and recommendation

**Applied legal skills**
- Read a file under exam conditions and write a structured note inside an hour
- Quantify a claim with general and special damages categories
- Identify the moment when ADR becomes the better commercial answer

**Common traps**
- Treating limitation as a single 6-year period — different causes of action have different periods; personal injury is 3 years from accrual or knowledge whichever is later; fraud or concealment runs from discoverability under section 32
- Missing a CPR 3 costs budget deadline by submitting Precedent H late — the costs sanction under CPR 3.14 limits recoverable costs to court fees
- Forgetting that a Part 36 offer's 21-day acceptance window starts on service of the offer, not on the date typed at the top of it
- Recommending litigation when the client's commercial interest points to mediation — case analysis stations reward commercial judgment

**Authoritative sources**
- Limitation Act 1980; Latent Damage Act 1986; CPR Parts 3 (costs management), 36, 44
- SRA SQE 2 Assessment Specification (case and matter analysis criteria)

**Practice deliverables**
- Eight timed case analyses, two per practice area excluding criminal
- Personal issue-spotting checklist refined after each mock

## Chapter 7: Criminal Litigation in Practice
**Core questions:** From police station to plea to sentence, what would a competent solicitor do next? Where do duty solicitor obligations sit alongside private-client representation?

**Key concepts**
- PACE 1984 detention rights, interview decisions, identification procedures under Code D
- CrimPR 2020 Parts on case management, disclosure, evidence
- Plea before venue under MCA 1980 section 17A; allocation; sending under CDA 1998 section 51
- Custody time limits (POA 1985 section 22; CPR / CrimPR) and extension applications
- Disclosure under the Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996: initial duty (section 3), defence statements (section 6A), continuing duty (section 7A)
- Sentencing Council guidelines; mitigating and aggravating factors
- Appeals against conviction and sentence; slip rules under the Sentencing Act 2020

**Applied legal skills**
- Advise on whether to comment in interview given inferences under sections 34 to 37 CJPOA 1994
- Run a bail application when previous bail has been breached
- Prepare a plea in mitigation that does not undermine basis of plea

**Common traps**
- Treating defence statement service as optional — CPIA 1996 section 6A requires it in Crown Court cases, and adverse inferences may follow under section 11
- Confusing the duty solicitor scheme (free for everyone at the police station; means-tested at court) with the magistrates'-court representation order
- Forgetting to apply for an extension of custody time limits in time — once the CTL expires, the defendant must be released on bail
- Advising silence in interview without considering adverse inferences under CJPOA 1994 section 34 — silence is sometimes the right call, but the advice must be recorded with reasoning

**Authoritative sources**
- PACE 1984 and Codes; CPIA 1996; CrimPR 2020; CJPOA 1994; Bail Act 1976; Sentencing Act 2020

**Practice deliverables**
- Police station decision tree from arrival to release or charge
- Five basis-of-plea documents drafted against worked facts

## Chapter 8: Dispute Resolution and Property Practice in Practice
**Core questions:** What does competent civil litigation conduct look like under the CPR? What does a clean residential or commercial conveyancing file look like from instruction to post-completion?

**Key concepts**
- Pre-action protocols, letter of claim, ADR options
- Issue, service, statements of case, default judgments, summary judgment
- Disclosure under CPR 31 and PD 51U Disclosure Pilot; witness evidence under PD 57AC; expert evidence under CPR 35
- Costs management under CPR 3, Precedent H, costs budgets and budget discussion reports
- Part 36 offers: mechanics, cost consequences under CPR 36.17
- Residential conveyancing: searches, enquiries, exchange, completion, SDLT, registration with HM Land Registry
- Commercial property: heads of terms, due diligence, certificates of title; FRI leases under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 Part II

**Applied legal skills**
- Calculate exact service deadlines and react to non-service
- Draft a Part 36 offer with the costs trap clearly built in
- Spot a chain break risk and propose a contractual fix

**Common traps**
- Treating exchange as the same as completion — risk passes on exchange under SCS 5.1.1, but legal title passes on completion and registration
- Submitting Form TR1 to HM Land Registry without ensuring all parties have signed in the right capacity — single common drafting error
- Forgetting the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954 Part II contracting-out procedure: warning notice plus tenant's declaration before the lease is granted
- Drafting a Part 36 offer without a 21-day relevant period — the offer is then merely a Calderbank and loses Part 36 cost consequences

**Authoritative sources**
- CPR Parts 1-72 (especially 3, 31, 35, 36, 44); PD 57AC; LRA 2002; LTA 1954

**Practice deliverables**
- Litigation file from letter of claim to Part 36 acceptance
- Conveyancing file with completion statement and SDLT5

## Chapter 9: Wills, Probate, and Business Organisations in Practice
**Core questions:** When the client is a grieving family member or a founder selling a company, what does competent matter management look like?

**Key concepts**
- Validity of wills, intestacy rules, family provision claims under the I(PFD)A 1975
- Grant of probate, letters of administration, IHT400 and IHT205 successors; PA1P and PA1A applications
- Estate administration: collecting assets, paying debts, distributing per Administration of Estates Act 1925
- Company incorporation, shareholders agreements, share transfers via stock transfer form
- Board and shareholder decision-making, written resolutions under CA 2006 sections 288-300; conflicts under section 175
- Filings at Companies House: confirmation statement CS01, allotments SH01, dividends procedural records

**Applied legal skills**
- Advise on whether a deed of variation is appropriate within two years (s 142 IHTA 1984)
- Sequence the steps of a share sale from heads of terms to completion
- Spot a section 175 conflict and structure the authorisation properly

**Common traps**
- Treating IHT400 as required on every estate — the excepted estates regime (IHT205 successor procedures) covers low-value or fully spouse-exempt estates
- Confusing section 175 authorisation (positive vote excluding the conflicted director) with section 177 declaration (interest in a proposed transaction)
- Forgetting that a stock transfer form for shares over £1,000 attracts 0.5% stamp duty before HMRC will stamp for Companies House registration
- Distributing an estate before the six-month I(PFD)A 1975 claim window has expired — personal representatives risk personal liability

**Authoritative sources**
- Wills Act 1837; AEA 1925; IHTA 1984; I(PFD)A 1975; Companies Act 2006

**Practice deliverables**
- Estate administration timeline from death to final distribution
- Share sale completion agenda with board minutes and stock transfer forms

## Chapter 10: Ethics Under Pressure and Capstone Exam-Day Discipline
**Core questions:** When the file is messy, the client is anxious, and the deadline is tomorrow, how do you keep the SRA Principles intact and still deliver? How do you pace the 3.5-day assessment without your judgment fraying?

**Key concepts**
- Hierarchy of obligations: court, public interest, client, firm
- Confidentiality versus disclosure to the court under SRA Principle 2 and Code 1.4
- Money laundering reports under POCA 2002 section 330 and tipping-off offences under section 333A
- Acting for both sides: substantially common interest exception and informed consent in writing (Code 6.2)
- Reporting concerns under the SRA Code paragraph 7.7
- SQE 2 pacing: legal research task ~45 minutes; legal writing ~45 minutes; drafting ~45 minutes; case analysis ~60 minutes; advocacy oral ~45 minutes including reading time and a 4-5 minute submission (e.g. plea in mitigation or bail application); interview ~25 minutes followed by a 25-minute attendance note

**Applied legal skills**
- Identify the exact Principle or paragraph engaged within 20 seconds of reading the stem
- Decline to act in a way that protects the client and your practising certificate
- Write a defensible attendance note when a partner overrules your conduct concern

**Common traps**
- Spending more than the budgeted time on a single written task — the SRA does not reward heroics on one station at the expense of another
- Letting an oral advocacy submission drift past 5 minutes — the assessor scores brevity, not endurance
- Treating an attendance note as a transcript — it is a record of advice given, decisions taken, and next steps, not every word said
- Reporting only on completed wrongdoing — Code 7.7 covers "any facts or matters" the SRA should investigate, including ongoing risk

**Authoritative sources**
- SRA Standards and Regulations; POCA 2002 sections 330, 333A; SRA SQE 2 Assessment Specification
- Provider mock-oral and mock-written banks; recorded peer-review sessions

**Practice deliverables**
- 40-card conduct trap deck mapped to SQE 2 stations
- Weekly hot-seat drills with a study partner reading scenarios aloud
