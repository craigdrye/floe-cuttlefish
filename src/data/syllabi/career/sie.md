# SIE: The Securities Industry on Training Wheels
**ID**: `sie` · **Discipline**: Finance

## Course Aim
The Securities Industry Essentials exam is the front door to every meaningful FINRA license. It gates the Series 6, 7, 22, 79, 82, 86, 87, and 99 — without it, none of them can be sat. The SIE itself does not let you sell anything. What it does is certify that you understand the universe a registered person operates inside: the instruments, the venues, the regulators, the prohibited practices, and the rough mechanics of how trades become positions and how positions become tax events. Pass it and you are *eligible*; fail it and the rest of your study plan is theoretical.

The exam itself is a 75-question, 105-minute walk through capital markets vocabulary. It is closed-book, calculator-on-screen-only, and 70% to pass. Roughly half of the exam is product taxonomy — equities, debt, packaged products, options, alternatives, derivatives — and the other half divides among trading mechanics, customer accounts, prohibited activities, and the regulatory framework. The SIE is *not* trying to make you a desk-ready professional. It is trying to confirm you will not embarrass a firm in compliance training, and that the next exam in your sequence can build on something real.

What you leave with is a working ground-floor literacy: the ability to describe what a security *is*, which market segment it trades in, who has rulemaking authority over it, what the registered-person rulebook forbids you from doing with it, and where the customer-account guardrails are. After SIE, a Series 7 product question stops being "what is a covered call" and starts being "is a covered call right for this client." That promotion from vocabulary to judgment is the entire point.

The Floe SIE voice is calm, scaffolding, and slightly dry. We assume you are not yet on a desk. We over-explain regulator pairings, under-explain emotional content, and quietly drill the same product taxonomies over and over until they become reflexive.

## Course Design Notes
Route any pre-Series 7, pre-Series 79, or pre-Series 6 question here, plus anything in the "what is a security" / "who regulates whom" / "what's the difference between an SAR and a CTR" cluster. The SIE voice is intentionally introductory and retrieval-heavy. Where Series 7 chases applied judgment ("which strategy for Maria"), SIE chases recall ("what is a straddle"). Where Series 63 chases definitional traps under the Uniform Securities Act, SIE chases the federal framework as a whole. Where Series 79 chases deal mechanics, SIE chases the underlying instruments.

Lean on short prompts, repeating product taxonomies, regulator-to-role pairings, and prohibited-practice spotting drills. Use first-name customers ("Aiden, 24, opening his first Roth") but keep the fact patterns light — the SIE is not a scenario exam. Mix in numeric items but keep the math at one or two operations max (taxable equivalent yield, ADR FX adjustment, SIPC coverage math). The exam allows an on-screen four-function calculator only.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The SIE has 75 scored questions plus 10 unscored pretest items in 1 hour 45 minutes. The passing score is 70%. FINRA publishes four content areas:

| Topic | Exam Weight | Scored Qs | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Knowledge of Capital Markets | 16% | 12 | Memorize regulators, market segments, and offering types early |
| Understanding Products and Their Risks | 44% | 33 | The longest unit; build a product map and revisit weekly |
| Understanding Trading, Customer Accounts, and Prohibited Activities | 31% | 23 | Practice with realistic account scenarios and red flags |
| Overview of the Regulatory Framework | 9% | 7 | Light but easy points; do not skip registration and reporting rules |

**Study cadence: 4-6 weeks at 6-8 hours per week** is the realistic baseline for a candidate with no finance background. Candidates with a finance degree or related coursework can compress to 3-4 weeks at 8-10 hours.

- **4-week sprint (finance background)**: Week 1 — Capital Markets + Products: Equities/Debt; Week 2 — Products: Pooled + Options + Alternatives; Week 3 — Customer Accounts + Trading + Prohibited Activities + Reg Framework; Week 4 — full-length practice exams (2 minimum), error log review, weak-area drill.
- **8-week standard**: Two weeks per content area, weekly mixed quiz of 25 questions, full-length exam at week 6 and week 8.
- **12-week deep prep (career-changer, no finance background)**: Three weeks per content area, daily 15-minute spaced review, full-length exam at weeks 8, 10, and 12.

Open the question bank in week 1 — do not "read first, drill later." Retrieval practice from day one outperforms reading-heavy prep on every published study of exam outcomes. End every block with a 20-question mixed quiz and an error log; the SIE rewards spaced repetition more than marathon reading.

## Chapter 1: Capital Markets and Market Participants
**Core questions:** Who issues, who underwrites, who regulates, who trades, and who custodies? What does each market segment actually do?

**Key concepts**
- Primary vs secondary markets; IPO, follow-on, and private placement mechanics
- Issuers, broker-dealers, investment advisers, transfer agents, custodians, clearing firms, DTCC, OCC, NSCC
- Exchanges (NYSE, Nasdaq), OTC markets (OTCQX/QB/Pink), ATSs, dark pools, third and fourth markets
- Economic factors: monetary vs fiscal policy, FOMC tools (open market ops, discount rate, reserve requirements, IORB), business cycle phases
- T+1 settlement (effective May 28, 2024) for equities, corporate bonds, municipal bonds, and most ETFs

**Applied skills**
- Map a trade lifecycle from order entry through DTCC settlement on T+1
- Identify the correct regulator (SEC, FINRA, MSRB, FRB, OCC, state administrator) for a given firm, product, or activity
- Distinguish a primary offering from secondary trading from a one-paragraph fact pattern

**Common traps**
- Settlement is now **T+1** for equities, corporates, and munis (since May 28, 2024) — most prep books published before 2024 still say T+2. Cash trades are same-day; T-bills are next-business-day at auction.
- The MSRB *writes* municipal rules; it does not *enforce* them. FINRA enforces against broker-dealers, bank regulators enforce against bank dealers.
- Dark pools and ATSs are still secondary market venues — they are SEC-registered alternative trading systems, not unregulated.
- The Fed's "tools" question almost always tests open market operations as the *primary* day-to-day tool, not the discount rate.

**Authoritative sources:** FINRA SIE Content Outline (current); Kaplan SIE License Exam Manual; STC SIE Solomon ExamCram.

## Chapter 2: Equity Securities and Their Risks
**Core questions:** What rights do shareholders actually have? How do common, preferred, ADRs, and rights/warrants differ in cash flow and risk?

**Key concepts**
- Common vs preferred stock; cumulative, convertible, callable, participating preferred
- Voting rights (statutory vs cumulative), dividends (ex-date, record date, payable date), preemptive rights, par value, treasury stock
- ADRs (sponsored vs unsponsored), foreign currency translation risk, ADR ratios
- Rights (short-dated, issued to existing shareholders) vs warrants (long-dated, often sweeteners on debt)
- Corporate actions: stock splits (forward, reverse), spinoffs, stock dividends

**Applied skills**
- Compute the cost basis and share count after a 3:2 forward split or 1:5 reverse split
- Explain why cumulative preferred behaves more like a bond than common stock
- Identify the ADR-specific risks (currency, dividend conversion, custody)

**Common traps**
- Ex-dividend date is now generally **same day as record date** under T+1; the old "two business days before record" rule is obsolete.
- A *right* is short-dated (30-45 days) and issued pro rata to existing shareholders. A *warrant* is long-dated (often years) and usually attached to a bond offering as a sweetener.
- Cumulative preferred dividends in arrears must be paid before *any* common dividend, but they do not accrue interest — they just stack.
- Unsponsored ADRs are issued by depositary banks without the foreign issuer's involvement, which means no required disclosure to US investors.

**Authoritative sources:** FINRA SIE Content Outline §2; Kaplan SIE Manual ch. 4; Investopedia on ADR mechanics.

## Chapter 3: Debt Securities and the Yield Curve
**Core questions:** How do bonds price, why do yields move opposite to prices, and what makes one credit different from another?

**Key concepts**
- Treasuries (bills, notes, bonds, TIPS, STRIPS), agencies (Ginnie Mae explicit guarantee), GSEs (Fannie, Freddie — implicit only)
- Corporate bonds: secured, debenture, subordinated, income, convertible, zero-coupon
- Municipal bonds: GO (taxing power) vs revenue (project cash flow), tax-equivalent yield, bank-qualified, AMT bonds, BABs
- Money market: commercial paper (≤270 days), bankers acceptances, negotiable CDs, repos, Treasury bills
- Yield measures: nominal (coupon), current yield, YTM, YTC, basis points; premium vs discount

**Applied skills**
- Convert a muni yield into its taxable equivalent for an investor in any federal bracket (TEY = muni yield / (1 − tax rate))
- Order a defaulting company's capital structure by claim priority
- Spot the credit, interest-rate, reinvestment, and call risks in a fact pattern

**Common traps**
- Premium callable bonds quote **yield to worst** (typically YTC), not YTM. Discount callables quote YTM because the call would be the *best* case for the issuer (and best case for the issuer = worst case for the holder).
- Ginnie Mae is the only mortgage-backed agency with full faith and credit. Fannie and Freddie are GSE — implicit only, even post-conservatorship.
- TIPS principal adjusts with CPI semiannually; coupon rate is fixed but applied to the adjusted principal, so coupon dollars rise with inflation.
- Commercial paper exceeding 270 days requires Securities Act registration — the 270-day cap is what makes it exempt under §3(a)(3).

**Authoritative sources:** FINRA SIE Content Outline §2; MSRB EMMA; Wiley SIE Exam Prep ch. 5.

## Chapter 4: Pooled Products — Funds, ETFs, REITs, DPPs, Annuities
**Core questions:** What is the investor actually buying when they buy a pool? Who bears the fee, the liquidity risk, and the tax timing?

**Key concepts**
- Mutual fund share classes: A (front-load, breakpoints, LOI, ROA), B (back-load, converts to A), C (level load, no breakpoints), I (institutional)
- Open-end (priced at NAV, forward pricing) vs closed-end (priced at market, may trade at premium/discount); UITs (fixed portfolio)
- ETFs (in-kind creation/redemption via APs → tax efficient) vs ETNs (unsecured debt — issuer credit risk)
- REITs: equity (own property), mortgage (hold loans), hybrid; 90% taxable income distribution requirement; non-traded REIT illiquidity risks
- DPPs / limited partnerships: passive losses, illiquidity, depletion allowances, no flow-through of operating losses against ordinary income
- Annuities: variable (separate account, market risk), fixed (general account, insurance risk), indexed (cap/participation rate); accumulation vs annuitization; 1035 exchange

**Applied skills**
- Identify the right mutual fund share class for a stated holding period and dollar amount
- Distinguish a 1940 Act fund from a non-1940 product (hedge funds, structured notes)
- Explain qualified vs nonqualified annuity tax treatment

**Common traps**
- ETFs are tax-efficient because of the in-kind creation/redemption mechanism with authorized participants — not because they trade on an exchange.
- A closed-end fund's market price can diverge meaningfully from NAV; investors buy and sell on the exchange, not from the fund.
- A 1035 exchange is tax-free between *like* contracts (annuity to annuity, life to life, life to annuity — but NOT annuity to life).
- Class B shares converting to Class A is what closes the back-load tail; that conversion timing matters when comparing total cost of ownership.

**Authoritative sources:** FINRA SIE Content Outline §2; SEC Investor.gov on 1940 Act; NAIC on annuity classifications.

## Chapter 5: Options, Packaged Derivatives, and Alternatives
**Core questions:** What does a derivative actually obligate, and what does the holder of each side stand to gain or lose?

**Key concepts**
- Calls and puts; buyer (right) vs writer (obligation); intrinsic and time value
- Basic strategies: long call, long put, covered call, protective put, married put
- Option contract specs: 100-share multiplier, standard expiration (3rd Friday), OCC clearance
- Structured products: auto-callable notes, market-linked CDs, principal-protected notes; issuer credit risk is the hidden risk
- Hedge funds and private equity (concept only at SIE level): accredited investor + qualified purchaser thresholds, lockups, 2-and-20

**Applied skills**
- Read an OCC option symbol and identify expiration, strike, and call/put
- Determine max gain, max loss, and breakeven for long call, long put, covered call, and protective put
- Identify when an option is in, at, or out of the money

**Common traps**
- A *long* option's max loss is the premium paid. A *short* (written) option's max loss can be unlimited (naked call) or substantial (naked put — strike minus premium).
- Structured products marketed as "principal-protected" are only as protected as the *issuer* — if the issuer defaults, the protection is worthless. (Lehman 2008 is the canonical case.)
- Auto-callable notes typically pay an above-market coupon and call the principal if the underlying hits a strike, leaving the investor reinvested at lower rates.
- DPPs are limited partnerships, not REITs. Passive losses from DPPs can only offset passive income.

**Authoritative sources:** OCC Options Disclosure Document (ODD); FINRA Reg Notice 12-03 on structured products; SEC Investor Bulletin on accredited investor (2020 amendments).

## Chapter 6: Customer Accounts, Suitability, and Reg BI
**Core questions:** Which account is appropriate for which client, and what conduct will cost a registered person their license?

**Key concepts**
- Account types: individual, joint (JTWROS, TIC, TBE), custodial (UGMA/UTMA), trust, corporate, partnership
- Retirement vehicles: Traditional IRA, Roth IRA (income phase-outs, contribution limits — 2024 $7K under 50, $8K 50+), SEP, SIMPLE, 401(k), Roth 401(k), 403(b), 457
- RMD age 73 (rising to 75 in 2033) per SECURE 2.0; 10% early-withdrawal penalty and exceptions
- Education accounts: 529 (state plan, owner controls, no income limits) vs Coverdell ESA ($2K/year cap, age 30 spend-down)
- ABLE accounts (529A) for individuals with disabilities; SSI/Medicaid asset shielding
- **Reg BI four obligations**: Disclosure, Care, Conflict of Interest, Compliance — applies to broker-dealers
- Suitability under FINRA Rule 2111 (still applicable to non-recommendation contexts)
- Fiduciary standard under the Investment Advisers Act for IAs (best interest + duty of loyalty + duty of care)
- KYC under FINRA Rule 2090

**Applied skills**
- Match a client profile to the correct account and product
- Apply Reg BI's four obligations and contrast with the fiduciary standard
- Identify a custodial account beneficiary and reporting party (custodian files taxes; minor is owner)

**Common traps**
- **Reg BI applies to broker-dealers; the Investment Advisers Act fiduciary standard applies to IAs.** A dual-registered firm may switch hats by transaction. Reg BI is not a fiduciary standard.
- A Roth IRA contribution can be withdrawn anytime tax- and penalty-free (basis). Earnings withdrawn before 59½ and before 5-year clock are taxable plus 10% penalty.
- 529 owner is typically the parent, not the beneficiary; assets are reported on FAFSA as parent assets (small impact). UGMA/UTMA are reported as student assets (heavier hit).
- ABLE account contributions are gift-tax-free up to the annual exclusion ($18K in 2024); balances up to $100K do not count against SSI's $2K asset limit.

**Authoritative sources:** SEC Reg BI Adopting Release (2019); FINRA Rule 2090 (KYC) and Rule 2111 (Suitability); IRS Pub 590-A/B; SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022.

## Chapter 7: Trading, Prohibited Practices, and AML
**Core questions:** What conduct is forbidden by federal law and FINRA rules, and what reporting is mandatory when red flags appear?

**Key concepts**
- Order types: market, limit, stop, stop-limit, GTC, day, IOC, FOK, AON
- Prohibited practices: churning, front running, painting the tape, marking the close/open, spoofing, layering, selling away, insider trading
- Insider trading: classical theory, misappropriation theory, tippee liability (Dirks personal benefit test)
- AML / Bank Secrecy Act: Customer Identification Program (CIP), Customer Due Diligence (CDD) including beneficial ownership, suspicious activity reports (SARs), currency transaction reports (CTRs, $10,001+ in cash)
- OFAC SDN screening (block, do not just monitor)
- Regulation SHO (locate requirement for short sales; threshold securities)

**Applied skills**
- Spot a prohibited practice from a one-paragraph scenario (churn vs marking the close vs selling away)
- Identify when a CTR vs SAR must be filed (CTR = mechanical $10K cash threshold; SAR = suspicion-based, $5K threshold)
- Apply OFAC blocking requirements to an account-opening fact pattern

**Common traps**
- **Structuring** is a separate violation from the underlying activity — breaking a $15K deposit into two $8K deposits to dodge the CTR is itself a crime under 31 USC 5324.
- A SAR is confidential; the firm cannot tell the customer that one was filed. CTRs are non-confidential and mechanical.
- Reg BI's Care Obligation requires "reasonable basis," "customer-specific," and "quantitative" prongs — quantitative covers churn-equivalents (excessive trading) regardless of intent.
- Insider trading liability for a tippee requires the tipper to have breached a duty *for personal benefit* — friendship-only tips can still trigger Dirks liability under post-Salman law.

**Authoritative sources:** Bank Secrecy Act and FinCEN regulations (31 CFR Part 1023); FINRA Rule 3310 (AML compliance program); SEC Reg SHO (Rules 200-204); Dirks v. SEC and Salman v. United States.

## Chapter 8: Regulatory Framework and Forms
**Core questions:** Who regulates whom, what gets reported, and what triggers a Form U4 disclosure?

**Key concepts**
- SEC (federal securities laws), FINRA (broker-dealer SRO), MSRB (muni rulemaking), CBOE (options listing), FRB/OCC/FDIC (bank dealers), state administrators (Series 63 territory), SIPC (account custody insurance, $500K total / $250K cash)
- The five foundational Acts: Securities Act of 1933 (registration of new issues), Securities Exchange Act of 1934 (secondary market, SEC, Exchange registration), Investment Company Act of 1940, Investment Advisers Act of 1940, Trust Indenture Act of 1939
- Forms: U4 (registration), U5 (termination, within 30 days), BD (BD registration), ADV (IA registration), Form CRS (relationship summary, retail), 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, Schedule 13D/G, Form 144 (Rule 144 resale)
- SIPC vs FDIC: SIPC covers brokerage custody (not market losses); FDIC covers bank deposits up to $250K per depositor per insured bank per ownership category

**Applied skills**
- Determine which Act governs a given activity
- Identify when a CTR vs SAR vs 8-K vs Form U4 amendment must be filed
- Map a customer or registered-person event to the right disclosure

**Common traps**
- SIPC does **not** insure against market losses; it covers securities and cash held in custody at a failed brokerage, up to $500K (of which $250K may be cash). FDIC is for *bank* deposits.
- The 2020 accredited investor amendments expanded the definition to include holders of certain professional licenses (Series 7, 65, 82), knowledgeable employees of private funds, and family offices ≥ $5M AUM — not just $200K income / $1M net worth.
- Form U4 amendments must be filed within **30 days** of a reportable event (criminal disclosure, customer complaint, etc.) — not "promptly," not "annually."
- Form CRS is a Reg BI deliverable for retail customers — distinct from Form ADV Part 2 (the IA brochure).

**Authoritative sources:** SEC.gov on the major Acts; FINRA By-Laws and Forms U4/U5/BD; SIPC at sipc.org; FDIC at fdic.gov; SEC Rule 230.501 (accredited investor, 2020 amended).

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
You have 105 minutes for 75 scored items — 84 seconds per question with a small buffer. Mark and skip any product-trap question you do not recognize within 30 seconds; momentum matters more than heroics, and the SIE is generous with its review window.

- **Calculator**: on-screen four-function only. Scratch paper and pencil provided. No bringing your own calculator.
- **Timing rhythm**: 25 questions in 30 minutes is on pace. If you are at question 25 at the 40-minute mark, you are slow — flag and move.
- **Wrong-answer triage**: eliminate two answers fast, then choose between the remaining two with the strictest rule reading. SIE rewards the *most restrictive* reading of regulatory rules.
- **Panic protocol**: if you hit a question that drops your confidence, mark it, breathe twice, and move. Coming back with momentum after solving three known items is the single highest-leverage recovery move.
- **The day before**: light review only — your error log, two short product taxonomies, and 25 mixed questions. No marathon cramming. Sleep is the highest-yield prep on day-minus-one.

Trust the process: the SIE rewards consistent retrieval practice, not bravado. If your last three full-length exams averaged 78%+, you are ready.
