# Pharma Drug Safety Roadmap
**ID**: `pharmaDrugSafetyRoadmap` - **Discipline**: Healthcare - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This is the on-ramp for someone walking into a pharmacovigilance (PV) function for the first time — a new drug-safety associate, a clinical-research coordinator picking up safety duties, a pharmacist or nurse moving into industry, or a regulatory professional who needs to understand what the safety department actually does all day. Drug safety is the discipline of watching a medicine after it leaves the lab and noticing, fast and defensibly, when it might be hurting someone. The work is built from a single repeating loop: a report of a possible harm arrives, you decide what it is and how urgent it is, you process it into a clean record, you report it to the right authorities inside a fixed clock, and you watch the accumulating pattern for a signal that the benefit-risk balance has shifted. This course teaches the shape of that loop so a beginner can step into a case-processing or signal-review queue and understand the machine they have joined rather than just following an SOP they do not yet trust.

The spine is the individual case safety report (ICSR) and the regulatory clocks that govern it. You will learn the safety vocabulary that decides everything downstream — adverse event vs. adverse reaction, serious vs. non-serious, expected vs. unexpected, the causality question — because misjudging seriousness or expectedness on intake mis-routes the entire case. We build on the frameworks that actually govern the work: the ICH E2 series (E2A and E2D for what is reportable, E2B(R3) for the electronic format, E2C for aggregate reports), the US machinery of 21 CFR 314.80 / 600.80, FDA Form 3500A, and FAERS, and the EU system of Good Pharmacovigilance Practices (GVP) modules, the Qualified Person for Pharmacovigilance (QPPV), and the Pharmacovigilance System Master File (PSMF). Two questions sit under every decision in this course: **could this drug be harming a patient, and is the record defensible to an inspector?**

By the end you should be able to recognize and triage a safety case, capture minimum criteria and the follow-up that matters, judge seriousness and expectedness, make and document an expedited-reporting decision against the right clock, read a disproportionality output without over-trusting it, contribute to a signal evaluation, and keep records an inspector could open cold. This is the practical foundation of an entry-level PV role. For the deeper layer — exact GVP module obligations, aggregate-report authoring, RMP/REMS detail, and the shorthand an experienced safety scientist is expected to know cold — pair this roadmap with the broader clinical-research and healthcare-compliance tracks.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests entry-level pharmacovigilance and drug-safety operations: adverse-event language and seriousness/expectedness judgments; case intake, minimum criteria, follow-up, and duplicate detection; data quality and MedDRA coding at a working level; expedited and periodic reporting decisions and their regulatory clocks; medical review and causality; signal detection, disproportionality, and signal evaluation; aggregate safety reports (PBRER/PSUR, DSUR); risk minimization (RMP/REMS); and what makes a PV system inspection-ready (SOPs, reconciliation, audit trail, CAPA, the PSMF). The bar is *understanding the safety loop and doing the next defensible thing* — not memorizing every module clause.

The voice is a calm senior safety reviewer orienting a sharp new associate: concrete, unhurried, every acronym tied to a real queue task the first time it appears. Where a fact appears it must be current and correct (ICH E2A/E2B(R3)/E2D/E2C, 21 CFR 314.80/600.80, EU GVP, MedDRA), because a beginner who learns a wrong reporting clock carries it for years. Distractors should be the genuine beginner mistakes — downgrading a serious case to dodge the clock, starting the clock from the wrong date, treating a disproportionality score as proof of causation, fixing a value to clear a query — each encoding one specific failure mode. When a scenario is ambiguous, the right answer is the one that protects the patient and keeps the record reconstructable.

### Where to start
There is no single exam behind this roadmap; it is the practical floor under day-one competence in a PV function and under credentials such as the regulatory and safety certifications (e.g., RAC, DIA/SOCRA training paths). Work the chapters in order, because the safety loop is sequential — you cannot reason about a reporting clock before you can judge seriousness, or about signals before you understand what a clean ICSR contains. After each chapter, do its applied skill on a real or invented case rather than re-reading; PV work is production (writing the narrative, making the reportability call, drafting the signal note), not recognition. Drill the Common traps as their own set, because nearly every inspection finding and every late report maps to one of them.

## Chapter 1: The Drug-Safety Function and Safety Language
- **Core questions**: What is pharmacovigilance actually for, and where does my function sit between the patient, the company, and the regulator? Is this report even a safety case? Which single word in the case (serious? unexpected?) changes everything that happens next?
- **Key concepts**: pharmacovigilance and the benefit-risk mandate; the lifecycle distinction between clinical-trial safety and post-marketing safety; adverse event (AE) vs. adverse drug reaction (ADR, which implies a suspected causal link); product quality complaint vs. AE; special situations (pregnancy/lactation exposure, overdose, off-label use, medication error, lack of efficacy) and why they are tracked even without harm; the four pillars that classify every case — **seriousness, expectedness, causality, and reportability**.
- **Applied skills**: sort a mixed inbox of reports into safety case vs. product complaint vs. neither, and flag the special situations; for a given report, name which of the four pillars is still undetermined and what fact would resolve it.
- **Common traps**: confusing an adverse *event* (temporal) with an adverse *reaction* (causal) and prematurely asserting causality; dismissing a special situation because "no harm occurred yet"; treating a product quality complaint as outside scope when it co-occurs with an AE; assuming the company's job is to defend the drug rather than to surface its risks.

## Chapter 2: Case Intake, Minimum Criteria, and Follow-Up
- **Core questions**: Is this a valid case at all — do I have the four minimum criteria? What is the single follow-up question that would most change the assessment? Could another reviewer reconstruct exactly what I knew and when?
- **Key concepts**: the four minimum criteria for a valid ICSR (an identifiable **reporter**, an identifiable **patient**, a suspect **product**, and an **event**); sources of cases (spontaneous reports, literature, solicited/program reports, regulatory authorities); the case narrative as the human-readable spine; targeted follow-up vs. fishing; duplicate detection and case merging; the receipt date (Day 0) as the fact that starts every clock.
- **Applied skills**: check a raw report against the four minimum criteria and decide if it is a valid case or a pending one; write a tight follow-up request that targets the missing fact that matters (seriousness, dechallenge/rechallenge, dates) rather than everything; run a duplicate search before creating a new case.
- **Common traps**: creating a case that is missing a minimum criterion and treating it as complete; asking for everything instead of the one decisive follow-up; failing a duplicate check and inflating the case count (and the signal); recording the wrong receipt date and silently mis-starting the clock.

## Chapter 3: Coding, Seriousness, and Expectedness
- **Core questions**: What MedDRA term actually captures what the reporter described? Does this case meet a seriousness criterion — and have I resisted the urge to downgrade it? Is the reaction listed in the reference safety information, or is it unexpected?
- **Key concepts**: MedDRA hierarchy at a working level (verbatim term to Preferred Term (PT); System Organ Class; Standardised MedDRA Queries (SMQs)); coding the reaction faithfully without over- or under-coding; the six seriousness criteria — **death, life-threatening, hospitalization or its prolongation, persistent/significant disability or incapacity, congenital anomaly/birth defect, and Important Medical Event (IME)**; expectedness judged against the reference safety information (the Investigator's Brochure pre-approval, the approved label/SmPC post-approval); how seriousness + expectedness + (for trials) causality jointly create a SUSAR.
- **Applied skills**: code a verbatim complaint to a defensible PT and decide whether an SMQ applies; classify a case against the six seriousness criteria, including spotting an IME that is not on the obvious list; determine expectedness by comparing the event to the correct reference document.
- **Common traps**: down-coding "I was admitted overnight for it" to non-serious to avoid expedited handling; missing the Important Medical Event criterion because the case isn't death/hospitalization; checking expectedness against the wrong or outdated reference document; coding the reporter's diagnosis instead of the reported reaction (or vice versa) and distorting the data.

## Chapter 4: Causality and Medical Review
- **Core questions**: Is the drug a plausible cause of this event, or is something else more likely? What does the timing, the dechallenge/rechallenge, and the alternative-explanation picture tell me? What do I write so my reasoning survives scrutiny?
- **Key concepts**: causality assessment and common frameworks (WHO-UMC categories; the default that a spontaneous report carries at least a "reasonable possibility" unless ruled out); temporal relationship, dechallenge and rechallenge, dose-response; confounders and alternative etiologies (underlying disease, concomitant medications, comorbidity); the medical reviewer's role in upgrading/downgrading and in writing a defensible assessment; the distinction between the *reporter's* causality and the *company's* causality.
- **Applied skills**: walk a case through a structured causality assessment (timing, dechallenge, rechallenge, alternatives) and land on a defensible category; write a medical-review comment that states the evidence, the leading alternative, and the residual uncertainty rather than a bare verdict.
- **Common traps**: declaring "not related" to suppress a report without a documented alternative cause; treating a strong temporal association as proof of causation; ignoring a positive rechallenge because it is inconvenient; conflating the reporter's opinion with the company's required assessment.

## Chapter 5: Expedited Reporting and the Regulatory Clock
- **Core questions**: Is this case individually reportable on an expedited basis, and to whom? When did the clock start, and how many days do I have? Which markets, partners, and authorities also need it?
- **Key concepts**: the expedited-reporting trigger (broadly, serious + unexpected + at-least-possible causality); the **clinical-trial clocks** under ICH E2A — fatal/life-threatening SUSARs in **7 calendar days** (with a complete report to follow), other SUSARs in **15 calendar days**; the **post-marketing clock** — serious/unexpected reactions as **15-calendar-day** reports (US "15-day Alert reports" under 21 CFR 314.80 / 600.80; EU 15-day to EudraVigilance); the electronic backbone — ICH E2B(R3) ICSRs, FDA Form 3500A and FAERS in the US, EudraVigilance in the EU; non-serious post-marketing reactions on the slower periodic track; partner/affiliate notification under safety data exchange agreements (SDEAs).
- **Applied skills**: make a reportability decision for a given case and name the recipient, the clock, and the Day 0; for a fatal trial case, distinguish the 7-day initial from the follow-up obligation; write a reporting-decision log entry capturing rationale, timeline, recipient, and evidence.
- **Common traps**: starting the clock from the event date or the data-entry date instead of the date the company first received the four criteria; treating a 15-day case as a 7-day case (or the reverse) and missing the right deadline; forgetting partner/affiliate notification under the SDEA; assuming a single FAERS or EudraVigilance submission covers every market obligation.

## Chapter 6: Signal Detection and Disproportionality
- **Core questions**: Is this a genuinely new or changed safety pattern, or noise? What does the disproportionality statistic actually claim — and what does it not? What additional evidence would justify escalating to a formal signal?
- **Key concepts**: a safety signal as new information suggesting a possible new hazard or a change in known risk; signal *detection* (manual ICSR review + statistical screening) vs. signal *evaluation* vs. signal *management*; disproportionality measures — Proportional Reporting Ratio (PRR), Reporting Odds Ratio (ROR), Empirical Bayes (EBGM) — as screening tools, not proof; reporting biases (notoriety/Weber effect, underreporting, stimulated reporting); the drug-event pair and the role of SMQs; EU GVP Module IX framing of signal management; benefit-risk as the ultimate question.
- **Applied skills**: read a disproportionality output and state what it does and does not support; assemble the case-level evidence (consistency, biological plausibility, dechallenge/rechallenge, dose-response, alternative explanations) for or against escalation; draft a signal-evaluation note with the pattern, the supporting and opposing evidence, the residual uncertainty, and a clear recommendation.
- **Common traps**: treating a high PRR/ROR as proof of causation rather than a flag to investigate; chasing a spike that is pure notoriety/Weber effect after media coverage; ignoring underreporting when interpreting a low count; recommending escalation without weighing the alternative explanations or the benefit-risk context.

## Chapter 7: Aggregate Reports and Risk Minimization
- **Core questions**: Do the periodic safety reports tell one coherent benefit-risk story across all the data? Are the case counts reconciled across systems? What is the company actually doing to minimize the known risks, and is it working?
- **Key concepts**: aggregate/periodic reports — **PBRER/PSUR** (marketed products, ICH E2C(R2)) and **DSUR** (investigational products, ICH E2F) — as the cumulative benefit-risk narrative; case reconciliation across safety, clinical, quality, and partner databases; the **Risk Management Plan (RMP)** in the EU and **REMS** in the US as the formal risk-minimization toolkit (routine vs. additional measures: labeling, communications, restricted distribution); how individual cases and signals feed the aggregate story; the line-listing and the data-lock point.
- **Applied skills**: check whether case counts reconcile across sources for a reporting period and explain any gap; trace how a confirmed signal flows into a label change or an added RMP/REMS measure; sanity-check that an aggregate report's narrative matches its line listings (no orphan SAEs, no contradictory benefit-risk conclusion).
- **Common traps**: writing an aggregate report whose narrative contradicts its own line listings; failing reconciliation and submitting mismatched counts across the PSUR, the clinical database, and the partner; confusing routine risk minimization (the label) with additional measures (REMS/RMP-specific tools); treating risk minimization as a document rather than something whose effectiveness must be measured.

## Chapter 8: Quality, Compliance, and Inspection Readiness
- **Core questions**: If an inspector arrived tomorrow, could the PV system reconstruct any case and prove every clock was met? What is the controlled process, and where is the audit trail? When something went wrong, is there a real CAPA — or just a closed ticket?
- **Key concepts**: the PV quality system — SOPs, training records, controlled changes; the **Pharmacovigilance System Master File (PSMF)** and the **Qualified Person for Pharmacovigilance (QPPV)** with 24/7 accountability (EU); GxP data integrity / ALCOA+ applied to safety records and audit trails; compliance metrics (late-case rate, on-time submission rate); deviations, root-cause analysis, and **CAPA** (corrective vs. preventive); audits vs. regulatory inspections and what each tests; the principle that "inspection-ready" means evidence, not vibe.
- **Applied skills**: turn a vague audit/inspection observation into owned, dated CAPA items that separate the fix from the prevention; verify a case file is complete and reconstructable (intake, follow-up, coding, assessment, submission proof); check on-time-submission metrics and explain a late case honestly rather than reclassifying it.
- **Common traps**: mistaking a thick binder or a closed ticket for a complete, defensible record; writing a CAPA that only corrects the single instance and never prevents recurrence; back-dating or quietly editing a record to "fix" a late submission instead of documenting the deviation; assuming the QPPV/PSMF is paperwork rather than the legally accountable spine of the system.

## Capstone
Process one simulated safety issue end to end and produce the records it generates. A report arrives from a healthcare professional: a patient on your marketed product was hospitalized overnight for a reaction. First, confirm it is a valid case against the four minimum criteria and record the correct Day 0; if a criterion is missing, write the single targeted follow-up that would resolve it. Code the reaction to a defensible MedDRA PT, classify seriousness against the six criteria (catching that "hospitalized overnight" is serious), and judge expectedness against the current approved label. Run a structured causality assessment — timing, dechallenge/rechallenge, alternative explanations — and write a medical-review comment that states evidence, leading alternative, and residual uncertainty. Make the expedited-reporting decision: is this a serious/unexpected reaction, what is the clock (15 calendar days post-marketing), who are the recipients (FAERS/EudraVigilance plus any SDEA partner), and what is the rationale — captured in a reporting-decision log. Then zoom out: three more cases of the same reaction have arrived this quarter and a disproportionality flag (elevated PRR) just fired — draft a signal-evaluation note that weighs the case evidence and the reporting biases, states whether it warrants escalation, and points to how it would feed the next PSUR and a possible label/RMP change. Finally, an audit observation lands noting one of your four cases was submitted on Day 17 — write the CAPA that separates the corrective action from the preventive one. The deliverable is a single ordered worklog: validity and clock first, then coding/seriousness/expectedness, then causality, then the reporting decision, then the signal note, then the CAPA. The grade is simple: would this case keep the patient's risk visible and survive a regulatory inspection?
