# Sales Fundamentals
**ID**: `salesFundamentals` · **Discipline**: Commercial · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course builds disciplined, ethical, repeatable sales ability for the kind of considered purchase where the buyer has options, a process, and other people to convince. The goal is not charisma or pressure; it is judgment. A capable seller can find buyers who genuinely fit, ask questions that surface real pain, read how a buying decision actually gets made, frame value in the buyer's own terms, hold a negotiation without giving away margin reflexively, and keep an honest forecast that a manager can plan around.

The through-line is that selling well is mostly diagnosis. The seller who understands the buyer's problem, the cost of leaving it unsolved, and the path the decision must travel will out-perform the seller with the slicker pitch almost every time. That is why this syllabus leans heavily on discovery, qualification, and stakeholder reality, and why it treats objections and price as information to be understood before they are answered. It is also why disqualification is taught as a skill, not a failure: a seller who can say "this isn't a fit" protects the buyer, the forecast, and their own time.

The course is grounded in the frameworks practitioners actually use: SPIN-style questioning (Rackham), BANT and MEDDIC/MEDDPICC qualification, the Challenger "teach-tailor-take control" stance, and core negotiation concepts (BATNA, ZOPA, reservation price, tradeable concessions). Learners should finish able to run a full opportunity from first contact to a clean win/loss review, and to explain *why* each move advances or kills a deal.

## Course Design Notes
Mainstream sales curricula (HubSpot Academy, Salesforce Trailhead, Coursera, MEDDICC) converge on the same skeleton: discovery, qualification, value, multi-threaded stakeholder mapping, objection handling, and forecast discipline. This syllabus follows that skeleton but corrects three habits those courses often let slide. First, it insists on naming the framework behind a move (so "ask about urgency" becomes "ask an Implication question to expose the cost of inaction"). Second, it gives prospecting and post-loss learning their own full chapters, because outbound craft and honest review are where junior sellers most often have gaps. Third, every chapter carries an explicit **Common traps** section, because most real-world selling failures are not knowledge gaps but predictable reflexes: pitching before diagnosing, single-threading, discounting on the first objection, and forecasting on optimism.

Questions are written for adult learners and reward operational judgment over slogans. Distractors are deliberately built from the seductive-but-wrong moves a motivated beginner would actually make. Throughout, the buyer is treated as a person with a job to do, not a spreadsheet row to be closed.

## Chapter 1: What Selling Is For
- **Core questions**: What problem does this product solve, and for whom is it a poor fit? What does a trustworthy sales motion feel like from the buyer's side? When is "no" the right outcome?
- **Key concepts**: buyer problem, ideal-customer fit, consultative vs transactional selling, commercial ethics, the trust ledger, honest disqualification, customer lifetime value over single-deal value.
- **Applied skills**: write a buyer-fit statement (ideal customer, poor-fit customer, pain, value, proof); decide when to walk away from a poor-fit deal.
- **Common traps**: treating every lead as winnable; overpromising on roadmap to ease a call; confusing buyer politeness with buying intent; optimizing for the close instead of the renewal.

## Chapter 2: Prospecting and First Contact
- **Core questions**: Why this buyer, why now, why this message? What observable trigger suggests they might care? What earns a reply versus what earns the spam folder?
- **Key concepts**: ideal customer profile, target-account selection, trigger events, personalization at relevance (not just first-name tokens), referral and warm paths, multi-touch sequences, permission and deliverability, the difference between activity and progress.
- **Applied skills**: draft three outreach messages tied to specific buyer triggers; build a short prospecting sequence with a clear ask in each touch.
- **Common traps**: spray-and-pray volume with no relevance; fake personalization; leading with product instead of problem; mistaking opens and clicks for pipeline; ignoring referral and existing-network paths.

## Chapter 3: Discovery
- **Core questions**: What is the buyer trying to change, and what happens if they do nothing? Which problem is worth real money to fix? What is still unclear after this call?
- **Key concepts**: open vs closed questions, SPIN question types (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff), pain vs symptom, cost of inaction, current process and workarounds, decision criteria, active listening and silence, talk-to-listen ratio.
- **Applied skills**: build a discovery call map (question bank, expected signals, follow-up prompts); convert a Problem question into an Implication question that quantifies impact.
- **Common traps**: pitching features before diagnosing; asking only Situation questions the buyer finds tedious; accepting the first stated problem as the real one; dominating the call instead of listening.

## Chapter 4: Qualification and Stakeholder Mapping
- **Core questions**: Is there a real buying process here? Who can say yes with money? Who can quietly say no? What evidence shows this is a genuine opportunity rather than curiosity?
- **Key concepts**: BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) for fast/transactional deals; MEDDIC/MEDDPICC (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Paper Process, Identify Pain, Champion, Competition) for complex deals; champion vs coach vs blocker; economic buyer vs user; multi-threading; procurement and legal as a process, not an afterthought.
- **Applied skills**: write a qualified-opportunity brief (stakeholders, decision path, risks, next step); map a deal against MEDDIC and name the weakest letter.
- **Common traps**: single-threading on one friendly contact; confusing a champion with the economic buyer; treating "interesting" as qualified; discovering procurement and security only at signature.

## Chapter 5: Value, Proof, and ROI
- **Core questions**: What outcome matters to this buyer in financial or operational terms? What evidence makes the claim credible to *this* stakeholder? Why is the status quo more expensive than it looks?
- **Key concepts**: value hypothesis, business case, ROI and payback, quantified outcome language, differentiation, risk reduction, relevant social proof, the cost of the problem vs the price of the solution.
- **Applied skills**: build a value narrative linking pain, impact, solution, proof, and expected result; do clean ROI math (gross savings minus cost equals net) without inflating assumptions.
- **Common traps**: feature-dumping instead of translating to outcomes; self-declared "innovative/synergy" language; irrelevant or anonymous references; ROI models that quietly double-count or ignore cost.

## Chapter 6: Objections and Negotiation
- **Core questions**: Is this objection a real blocker or a request for confidence? What question should come before the answer? What am I getting in return for every concession?
- **Key concepts**: objection types (price, timing, authority, competition, status quo); clarify-before-answering; reframing; the negotiation toolkit (BATNA, reservation price, ZOPA, anchoring); tradeable concessions ("every concession buys something"); the difference between price and value objections; the cost of reflexive discounting on margin.
- **Applied skills**: build an objection playbook (listen, clarify, respond, confirm, next step); plan a negotiation by writing your BATNA, reservation price, and a list of low-cost/high-value tradeables.
- **Common traps**: discounting on the first "too expensive"; arguing with the buyer; giving concessions for free; conceding without re-anchoring on value; mistaking a stall for a real objection.

## Chapter 7: Pipeline and Forecast Hygiene
- **Core questions**: What evidence supports calling this deal Stage 3 (or 90%)? What must happen before it can advance? Which deals are real and which are comforting clutter?
- **Key concepts**: stages with exit criteria, mutual action plans, concrete next steps with owner and date, close probability tied to buyer behavior (not seller mood), slippage, single-threaded risk, pipeline coverage, disciplined disqualification and recycling.
- **Applied skills**: build a pipeline-review template that separates optimism from evidence; convert "send me info" into a specific next meeting with owner, date, and purpose.
- **Common traps**: forecasting on enthusiasm or a champion's smile; hoarding stale deals because a big number feels good; vague "check in someday" next steps; renaming dead deals to avoid disqualifying them.

## Chapter 8: Learning From Wins and Losses
- **Core questions**: Why did the buyer actually choose (or not)? What did we learn about fit, message, and process? What should change in the next cycle?
- **Key concepts**: structured win/loss review, separating facts from assumptions, the real (often quiet) reasons buyers decide, churn and expansion signals, clean handoff to customer success, controllable vs uncontrollable lessons, continuous improvement.
- **Applied skills**: run a win/loss review that yields product, buyer, process, and message lessons; design an expansion discovery for an existing account.
- **Common traps**: reflex-blaming procurement or "silly" buyers; inventing a heroic story that prevents learning; skipping reviews on wins (which hides lucky deals); treating expansion as a re-run of the original pitch.

## Capstone
Run a simulated sales cycle end to end on a fictional but realistic account: select the target and a trigger, draft outreach, run discovery (with named SPIN question types), qualify against MEDDIC and name the weakest letter, build a quantified value narrative, handle a price objection and one negotiation round with explicit BATNA and tradeables, and produce a forecast entry with stage evidence and a mutual next step. Then write the win/loss review you would run after the deal closes either way. Submit: outreach messages, discovery call notes, opportunity brief, value story, objection/negotiation plan, forecast entry, and win/loss template.

## Assessment Ideas
- Discovery graded on question quality (especially Implication/Need-payoff), listening, and problem diagnosis.
- Opportunity brief graded on MEDDIC/BANT evidence, stakeholder clarity, and honest weakest-link identification.
- Negotiation plan graded on BATNA realism, value re-anchoring, and whether concessions are traded rather than given.
- Final simulation graded on trust, value framing, commercial judgment, forecast discipline, and willingness to disqualify.
