# Medical Billing Roadmap
**ID**: `medicalBillingRoadmap` - **Discipline**: Healthcare - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Medical billing is the discipline of turning a clinical encounter into correctly paid revenue without breaking a rule or a patient relationship along the way. This course trains a learner to move an account through the full revenue cycle, from the moment a patient is scheduled to the moment the balance reaches zero, and to do it with the judgment a payer audit, a denial deadline, and an anxious patient all reward. The throughline is the same at every stage: identify the blocker, gather the evidence, name the owner, and take the defensible next action.

The course is deliberately practical. A biller does not get paid for knowing that authorization exists; they get paid for spotting that the billed CPT did not match the authorized service, for reading a remittance and recognizing that a CO-45 write-off is correct while a CO-50 denial is appealable, and for knowing that a missing referral is usually a front-end failure that no back-end appeal can rescue. Learners build fluency in the artifacts of the trade, the CMS-1500 and UB-04 claim forms and their 837 electronic equivalents, the ICD-10-CM, CPT, and HCPCS code systems, the CARC and RARC codes that explain a remittance, and the rules that govern timely filing, coordination of benefits, Medicare secondary payer status, and the appeals ladder.

Above all, the course teaches that billing sits at the intersection of money and trust. The correct move is almost never the fastest one or the one that quietly bills the patient for a payer's contractual write-off. It is the one that survives review, holds up against the contract and the regulation, and leaves the patient with an accurate, explainable balance. Fraud-adjacent shortcuts, changing a code to chase a dollar, backdating a referral, manipulating a date of service, are treated throughout as automatic wrong answers, because in practice they are.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests revenue-cycle workflow, eligibility and benefit verification, prior authorization and referral logic, clean-claim construction (forms, code systems, modifiers, place of service, NPI), claim scrubbing and edits, denial and rejection triage, the appeals ladder and its deadlines, remittance interpretation (ERA/835, EOB, CARC/RARC, group codes), underpayment and contract-variance review, coordination of benefits and payer order, patient responsibility, credit balances and refunds, and the consumer-protection rules (No Surprises Act, ABN) that constrain what a patient can be billed.

Questions should reward the learner who names the blocker, the evidence, the owner, and the next action, and who can distinguish a write-off from an appealable denial, a rejection from a denial, and a referral from an authorization. Distractors should embody real-world failure modes: reactive eligibility, document-dumping appeals, premature resubmission, billing the patient for a contractual obligation, and any code, date, or identifier manipulation, which is fraud and never the right answer. Keep the register adult, concrete, and exam-grade: cite the actual code, box, modifier, or deadline where it sharpens the question.

## Chapter 1: The Revenue Cycle End to End
- **Core questions**: Where in the cycle is this account, what is blocking it from moving forward, and who owns the next step? Why does front-end work prevent back-end rework?
- **Key concepts**: scheduling and patient access, registration, charge capture, claim submission, adjudication, remittance, payment posting, accounts receivable (A/R), A/R days, denial rate, clean-claim rate, front-end versus back-end ownership.
- **Applied skills**: map an encounter across the cycle and locate the control points; segment an aged-A/R backlog by payer, age, dollar value, and root cause; build a feedback loop that turns recurring denials into front-end fixes.
- **Common traps**: treating billing as starting when cash arrives; working a backlog alphabetically instead of by recoverable value; fixing each claim silently so the upstream defect never gets corrected; confusing a high denial rate with a workload problem rather than a process problem.

## Chapter 2: Eligibility, Benefits, and Coverage Order
- **Core questions**: Is coverage active on the date of service, and what does the benefit actually pay? Which plan is primary? Does Medicare sit first or second here?
- **Key concepts**: eligibility versus benefits versus payable service, deductible/copay/coinsurance/out-of-pocket max, network status, coordination of benefits (COB), the birthday rule for dependent children, Medicare Secondary Payer (MSP) rules and the MSP questionnaire, employer size triggers.
- **Applied skills**: verify active coverage and translate a benefit into an expected patient responsibility; determine primary versus secondary payer order; apply the birthday rule; run an MSP scenario for a working-age patient with employer coverage.
- **Common traps**: assuming active coverage means the service is payable; defaulting Medicare to primary when MSP rules make it secondary; using deductible size, card design, or patient preference to decide payer order; ignoring out-of-network liability because the plan is "active."

## Chapter 3: Authorizations, Referrals, and Medical Necessity
- **Core questions**: Was prior authorization or a referral required, was it obtained, and does the billed service match what was approved? Is this service medically necessary by the payer's published policy?
- **Key concepts**: prior authorization versus referral (distinct triggers and owners), authorization number/date/units, medical necessity and Local/National Coverage Determinations, Advance Beneficiary Notice (ABN) and the GA modifier, retroactive-authorization limits.
- **Applied skills**: catch authorization and referral requirements during scheduling; reconcile a billed claim against the authorized service, dates, and units; decide when an ABN must be issued and how the GA modifier protects patient billing on a likely-non-covered Medicare service.
- **Common traps**: conflating referrals with authorizations; appealing a no-authorization denial without evidence that approval matched the billed service; backdating a referral (fraud); billing a Medicare patient for a denied service with no valid ABN on file.

## Chapter 4: Clean Claims, Code Systems, and Submission
- **Core questions**: Is the claim complete and internally consistent? Does the documentation support every charge? Which form and code systems apply, and what edit must be fixed before submission?
- **Key concepts**: CMS-1500/837P (professional) versus UB-04/837I (institutional); ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes (alpha first character, 3–7 characters), CPT Category I (00100–99499), HCPCS Level II (letter + 4 digits, e.g., J-codes for drugs); modifiers (25, 26/TC, 59); place-of-service codes (11 office, 21 inpatient, 22 outpatient hospital, 02/10 telehealth); NPI Type 1 versus Type 2 and box 24J versus 33a; NCCI procedure-to-procedure edits; claim scrubbers.
- **Applied skills**: complete a clean-claim review and name the missing or inconsistent field; choose the correct form and code system for a setting; apply a modifier correctly; resolve an NCCI edit with the right modifier rather than an override; match POS and modifiers to the actual encounter.
- **Common traps**: submitting a claim with missing required data and expecting the payer to "fix" it; filling missing fields with zeros; reflexively overriding scrubber edits; changing a diagnosis or procedure code to one that pays better without documentation (fraud); mismatching place of service to the real site of care.

## Chapter 5: Rejections, Denials, and the Appeals Ladder
- **Core questions**: Did the claim reject before adjudication or deny after it? Why did the payer stop payment, can it be corrected or appealed, and what deadline applies?
- **Key concepts**: rejection (front-end/clearinghouse, never adjudicated) versus denial (adjudicated and refused); CARC versus RARC and the CO/PR/OA group codes; corrected claim versus appeal; timely filing (Medicare: 12 months from date of service; CO-29); the Medicare appeals ladder, redetermination (120 days), QIC reconsideration (180 days), ALJ; duplicate denials.
- **Applied skills**: triage a denial into correctable, appealable, or non-recoverable; read a CARC/RARC pair and decide the action; build a focused appeal packet that ties documentation to the payer's policy and the denial reason; protect a claim against a timely-filing deadline with proof of submission.
- **Common traps**: treating a rejection like a denial (or vice versa); document-dumping the entire chart instead of answering the denial reason; resubmitting before checking claim status and generating duplicate denials; letting a high-dollar claim age past the appeal deadline; appealing a missing-referral denial that is not appealable.

## Chapter 6: Remittance, Payment Posting, and Underpayments
- **Core questions**: Did the payer pay the contracted amount? What was written off, what is patient responsibility, and what should be posted, appealed, or refunded?
- **Key concepts**: electronic remittance advice (ERA/835) versus the patient EOB; allowed amount, contractual adjustment (CO group), patient responsibility (PR group); fee schedule and contract load errors; underpayment variance; credit balances and overpayment refunds, including escheat exposure on unclaimed credits; secondary-claim sequencing.
- **Applied skills**: post a remittance accurately and reconcile expected versus allowed versus paid; flag an underpayment pattern that signals a contract or fee-schedule load error; resolve a credit balance to the correct party; sequence a secondary claim so the patient statement reflects true responsibility.
- **Common traps**: billing the patient for a CO contractual write-off; ignoring small per-claim underpayments that aggregate into material loss; keeping or misapplying a credit balance; sending a patient statement before secondary processing finishes.

## Chapter 7: Patient Responsibility and Consumer-Protection Rules
- **Core questions**: What does the patient actually owe, when can they be billed, and what notice or estimate is required first? What protects the patient from a surprise bill?
- **Key concepts**: the No Surprises Act (balance-billing protections for emergency and certain out-of-network in-network-facility care; good faith estimate for uninsured/self-pay; $400 dispute threshold; 120-day patient dispute window), good faith estimates, transparent statements, compliant payment plans, financial-assistance and collections policy.
- **Applied skills**: produce an accurate patient-responsibility figure and an explainable statement; issue a compliant good faith estimate; recognize when balance billing is prohibited; correct a policy-violating payment-plan promise without damaging trust.
- **Common traps**: balance-billing a patient in a No Surprises Act protected scenario; hiding a deductible until the final statement; honoring an undeliverable payment-plan promise; sending a patient multiple conflicting balances.

## Capstone
Work a simulated billing queue from patient access through zero balance. For each account: confirm eligibility and the correct payer order (including any MSP or COB question); verify that authorization or referral requirements were met and that the billed service matches the approval; build a clean claim on the right form with correct codes, modifiers, POS, and NPI fields, clearing any NCCI edit; triage each denial by reading its CARC/RARC and deciding whether to correct, appeal (with a focused, policy-matched packet inside the deadline), or escalate; interpret the remittance to separate contractual write-offs from patient responsibility and flag any underpayment or credit balance; and finally prepare an accurate, compliant patient-balance explanation that honors No Surprises Act and ABN obligations. Deliver a queue summary naming, for every account, the blocker, the evidence, the owner, and the next action.
