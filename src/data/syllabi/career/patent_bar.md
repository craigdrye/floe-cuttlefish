# Patent Bar: The Open-Book Endurance Test

**ID**: `patentBar` | **Discipline**: Law | **Level**: Career

## Course Aim

The USPTO registration examination is one of the strangest professional exams in American law. It is open-book and the book is sitting right there: a fully searchable PDF of the Manual of Patent Examining Procedure (MPEP), thousands of pages of it, on the screen beside the question. And yet the pass rate hovers around half. The exam is not hard because the answer is hidden. It is hard because you have roughly three and a half minutes per question, the MPEP is a labyrinth, and the questions are engineered so that the answer that "sounds right" from a science or engineering background is reliably wrong. Passing is a skill of fast, accurate rule navigation under a clock, not a feat of memorization.

This course trains that skill and the prosecution reasoning underneath it. You will learn to recognize which of a handful of recurring topics a question is testing, jump to the controlling MPEP section in seconds, and apply the rule to a fact pattern that was specifically built to tempt you off the path. The big yield areas are stable and well documented: 35 U.S.C. 102 and 103 (novelty and obviousness under the America Invents Act), 35 U.S.C. 101 and 112 (eligibility, written description, enablement, definiteness), claim drafting and amendment practice, appeal and continuation procedure, PCT and national-stage filings, and the practitioner ethics rules. Master those, learn to read the MPEP rather than recall it, and the exam becomes a navigation problem you can win.

A word on the era you are testing in. The exam is computer-delivered at Prometric as 100 multiple-choice questions split into two three-hour sessions with a break between, 70 percent to pass, with 10 unscored pretest questions hidden among the scored ones. As of the September 24, 2025 update, the tested source material is the **MPEP Ninth Edition, Revision 01.2024**, plus the post-AIA statutes and rules, the PCT materials, and the 2024 subject-matter-eligibility guidance (including the AI eligibility examples). The version you study has to match the version they test, because the MPEP changes and an outdated answer is still a wrong answer. This course teaches the current, post-AIA, first-inventor-to-file world as the default and flags the pre-AIA rules only where a transition question can still appear.

## Course Design Notes

Keep this course practical, fast, and ruthlessly current. Route questions here when they test patent-bar reasoning or entry-level prosecution work: patentability under 101/102/103/112, claim analysis and construction, prior-art and effective-filing-date mechanics, obviousness and rejection response, MPEP navigation and search strategy, USPTO prosecution procedure (filing, restriction, RCE, appeal, abandonment, revival), PCT and national-stage practice, post-grant and correction procedures, and the USPTO Rules of Professional Conduct. Because the exam is open-book against a specific MPEP revision, every doctrinal prompt should be answerable from the MPEP, and prompts should make clear when an answer turns on **post-AIA** versus **pre-AIA** law, because that distinction quietly decides many questions. Treat the MPEP citation as part of the answer: a learner who gets the right result but cannot say where it lives in the manual has not yet passed. When a question is about real-world prosecution judgment rather than exam mechanics, say so, because good exam strategy and good prosecution strategy occasionally diverge.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm

The exam is **100 multiple-choice questions**, delivered in **two three-hour sessions** (50 questions each) with a scheduled break, scored on 90 questions (10 are unscored pretest items you cannot identify), **70 percent required to pass**. It is open-book: a searchable MPEP PDF is available the entire time, but there is no internet and no outside notes. Eligibility to sit is established before you study: **Category A** (a bachelor's or higher in a listed technical field), **Category B** (other degree plus documented technical coursework), or **Category C** (FE exam passage), all administered through the USPTO Office of Enrollment and Discipline under the current General Requirements Bulletin. Logistics matter: once accepted you get a roughly 90-day window to schedule at Prometric, extendable for a fee, and current retake rules (a temporary waiver as of 2026) shorten the wait between attempts while still requiring new fees each time.

| Topic Cluster | Roughly | What It Tests | Practice Posture |
|---|---|---|---|
| 102 / 103 prior art and obviousness | High yield | Effective filing date, AIA prior-art categories, the 102(b)(1)/(2) exceptions, Graham factors, motivation to combine | Timed sets with date timelines; locate the controlling MPEP 2100 section fast |
| 101 / 112 patentability | High yield | Eligibility (Alice/Mayo framework and the 2024 guidance), written description, enablement, definiteness | Issue-spot the requirement first, then cite |
| Claims, amendments, new matter | High yield | Claim construction, antecedent basis, dependent-claim scope, amendments, new-matter bars | Limitation-by-limitation reading |
| Prosecution procedure | High yield | Filing, restriction/election, non-final vs final, after-final, RCE, abandonment, revival, deadlines | Procedural decision trees; know the clocks |
| Appeals (PTAB) | Moderate | Notice of appeal, brief requirements, examiner's answer, reply brief, oral hearing | Map the appeal sequence end to end |
| PCT and national stage | Moderate | International filing, 30-month national stage, priority claims, MPEP 1800 | Treat as its own mini-system |
| Post-grant and corrections | Moderate | Reissue, reexamination, supplemental examination, IPR/PGR basics, certificates of correction | Match the defect to the right vehicle |
| Practitioner ethics | Steady | Duty of candor (Rule 56), conflicts, fees, signatures, unauthorized practice, inequitable conduct | Triage facts to the governing rule |

Suggested cadence: **6-10 weeks** of focused study for most candidates, more if the MPEP is unfamiliar territory. Every week should include MPEP search drills against a timer, two to three mixed multiple-choice sets pulled from the topic clusters above, a wrong-answer log sorted by topic and trap type, and at least one full timed half-session as the exam nears. The single highest-return habit is the search drill: pick a question, find the controlling MPEP section, and beat your previous time. Speed of navigation is the variable most candidates underinvest in and most failures trace back to.

## Chapter 1: The Exam as a Navigation Problem

**Core questions:** What is this exam actually testing, why does open-book not make it easy, and how do you turn 8,000 pages of MPEP into a fast lookup system?

**Key concepts**
- Exam mechanics: 100 questions, two three-hour sessions, 70 percent to pass, 10 hidden pretest items, fully searchable MPEP, no internet
- The current tested baseline: post-AIA statutes and rules and MPEP Ninth Edition Rev. 01.2024, and why the studied version must match the tested version
- MPEP architecture: chapter 700 (examination), 1200 (appeal), 1800 (PCT), 2100 (patentability), 2200 (reexamination), 2700 (term and adjustment), and the appendices of rules and statutes
- Search strategy: keyword choice, jumping to the chapter most likely to govern, and recognizing a question stem's topic from its first sentence

**Applied skills**
- Identify the governing topic cluster before reading the answer choices
- Open the MPEP to the controlling section in under 60 seconds
- Budget time so the unscored questions cannot sink you and no single question burns five minutes
- Read "which of the following is NOT" and "is most likely" stems with discipline

**Common traps**
- Treating open-book as low-stakes and underpracticing speed, then running out of time in session one
- Studying an outdated MPEP edition or pre-AIA rules as if they were current
- Searching the full MPEP from scratch every time instead of knowing which chapter owns each topic
- Trusting science-and-engineering intuition over the rule actually on the screen

**Deliverables**
- A one-page MPEP navigation map: topic cluster to controlling chapter and section
- A timed search-drill log showing lookup times trending down
- A personal time budget for each three-hour session

## Chapter 2: Patentable Subject Matter and the 101/112 Family

**Core questions:** Is the claimed thing the kind of thing the patent system protects, and does the disclosure actually support the claims?

**Key concepts**
- 35 U.S.C. 101 eligibility: the Alice/Mayo two-step framework, judicial exceptions (abstract ideas, laws of nature, natural phenomena), and the 2024 subject-matter-eligibility guidance including the AI eligibility examples
- 35 U.S.C. 112(a): written description and enablement as distinct requirements, and the undue-experimentation (Wands) factors
- 35 U.S.C. 112(b): definiteness, and 112(f) means-plus-function claiming and its construction consequences
- Utility under 101 and where each requirement lives in MPEP 2100

**Applied skills**
- Separate the eligibility question from the disclosure question instead of blurring them
- Run the two-step eligibility analysis in order and stop when the claim integrates the exception into a practical application
- Distinguish written description (did they show possession?) from enablement (could a skilled artisan make and use it?)
- Spot a 112(f) trigger and trace it to the corresponding structure in the specification

**Common traps**
- Collapsing written description and enablement into one undifferentiated "112" objection
- Declaring a claim ineligible the moment an abstract idea appears, skipping the integration and inventive-concept steps
- Treating the AI eligibility examples as a separate regime rather than an application of the existing framework
- Forgetting that definiteness is judged from the perspective of a person of ordinary skill reading the claim in light of the specification

**Deliverables**
- A patentability issue-spotting worksheet that names the specific requirement (101 vs 112(a) vs 112(b)) and its MPEP citation
- A short memo working a claim through the eligibility two-step
- A definiteness/means-plus-function checklist

## Chapter 3: Prior Art and the Effective Filing Date Under the AIA

**Core questions:** Under first-inventor-to-file, what counts as prior art against this claim, and which date controls?

**Key concepts**
- 35 U.S.C. 102(a)(1) (patented, described in a printed publication, in public use, on sale, or otherwise available) and 102(a)(2) (earlier-filed applications and patents)
- The 102(b)(1) and 102(b)(2) exceptions: the one-year grace period for inventor-originated disclosures and the disqualification of intervening disclosures
- Effective filing date mechanics: priority and benefit claims, provisional applications, and how they move the date that governs prior art
- Where the AIA prior-art analysis lives in MPEP 2150-2159, and the limited situations where pre-AIA 102 still matters

**Applied skills**
- Build a timeline of disclosures, filings, and priority dates before deciding anything
- Determine whether a reference qualifies as prior art under the correct 102 subsection
- Apply the grace-period exceptions to remove an inventor's own disclosure from the prior-art universe
- Decide whether a transition application is governed by AIA or pre-AIA 102

**Common traps**
- Applying pre-AIA "swearing behind" or interference logic to a first-inventor-to-file application
- Mishandling the one-year grace period by treating it as a blanket bar reset rather than an inventor-originated exception
- Missing that a benefit or priority claim changes the effective filing date and therefore the prior-art set
- Reading "on sale" or "public use" as requiring a printed document

**Deliverables**
- A prior-art timeline template with disclosure, filing, and priority dates and a per-reference qualification call
- A decision flow for the 102(b)(1)/(2) exceptions
- A short note on identifying AIA vs pre-AIA applications

## Chapter 4: Obviousness and Responding to Rejections

**Core questions:** Why would a skilled artisan have combined or modified the references, and what makes a 103 rejection beatable?

**Key concepts**
- 35 U.S.C. 103 and the Graham factors: scope and content of the prior art, differences, level of ordinary skill, and objective indicia
- Rationales for combining references under KSR, articulated reasoning with rational underpinning, and the requirement of a reasonable expectation of success
- Secondary considerations (commercial success, long-felt need, failure of others, unexpected results) and teaching away
- Building a prima facie case and the prosecution moves that rebut it, in MPEP 2140-2145

**Applied skills**
- Test whether the examiner articulated a real reason to combine, not just hindsight
- Identify the claim limitation no reference teaches and make it the center of the argument
- Marshal objective indicia as evidence rather than assertion
- Choose between amending claims and arguing against the rejection

**Common traps**
- Accepting a bare "it would have been obvious" rejection that lacks articulated reasoning
- Treating secondary considerations as decorative rather than as evidence that must be weighed
- Confusing a 102 anticipation argument (one reference, every limitation) with a 103 obviousness argument (combination)
- Arguing references individually when the rejection is based on their combined teaching

**Deliverables**
- An obviousness response outline mapping limitations to reference teachings, arguments, and evidence
- A secondary-considerations evidence checklist
- A worked example distinguishing a 102 from a 103 response

## Chapter 5: Claims, Amendments, and New Matter

**Core questions:** What does this claim actually cover, is every limitation supported, and will this amendment survive?

**Key concepts**
- Independent and dependent claims, claim differentiation, transitional phrases (comprising, consisting of, consisting essentially of), and antecedent basis
- Claim construction principles and the broadest-reasonable-interpretation standard during examination
- Amendment practice: proper format, support in the original disclosure, and the new-matter prohibition under 35 U.S.C. 132 and 251
- Restriction and election of species, and how claim structure interacts with restriction practice

**Applied skills**
- Read a claim limitation by limitation and check each against the specification
- Diagnose an antecedent-basis or indefiniteness problem from the claim language
- Determine whether a proposed amendment adds new matter
- Predict a restriction requirement from independent-claim structure

**Common traps**
- Misreading the scope difference between "comprising" and "consisting of"
- Adding a limitation by amendment that has no support in the original disclosure and calling it a clarification
- Assuming a dependent claim is automatically narrower without checking it actually adds a limitation
- Confusing claim construction during examination (broadest reasonable interpretation) with litigation-style construction

**Deliverables**
- A claim chart linking each limitation to its specification support
- A new-matter screening checklist for amendments
- A restriction/election decision note for a multi-claim set

## Chapter 6: Prosecution Procedure, Deadlines, and Keeping the Application Alive

**Core questions:** Given where the application is, what is the applicant's next move, what is the deadline, and what keeps prosecution alive?

**Key concepts**
- The flow from filing through first action: filing requirements, missing-parts practice, and information disclosure statements (IDS) under Rule 56 and 97/98
- Non-final and final rejections, after-final practice, the advisory action, and the request for continued examination (RCE)
- Continuation, continuation-in-part, and divisional applications and what each preserves
- Abandonment, revival (unintentional-delay standard), extensions of time under Rule 136, and patent term adjustment basics in MPEP 700 and 2700

**Applied skills**
- Place the application on the prosecution timeline and identify the available responses
- Compute response deadlines and the cost and limits of extensions of time
- Choose among amendment after final, RCE, continuation, and appeal
- Recognize when an IDS must be filed and what it requires

**Common traps**
- Treating a final rejection as the end of prosecution when RCE, continuation, or appeal remain
- Miscounting deadlines or assuming extensions of time are unlimited or free
- Filing a continuation-in-part and assuming the new matter gets the earlier effective filing date
- Missing the IDS timing rules and the consequences of late submission

**Deliverables**
- A prosecution decision tree from first action through final disposition
- A deadline-and-extensions reference card
- An office-action response plan for a sample final rejection

## Chapter 7: Appeals, PCT, and Post-Grant Procedure

**Core questions:** When examination stalls, how do you appeal; how does an international application reach the U.S.; and which vehicle fixes a granted patent's defect?

**Key concepts**
- PTAB appeals: notice of appeal, the appeal brief requirements, examiner's answer, reply brief, and oral hearing in MPEP 1200
- PCT practice: international filing, the international search and preliminary examination, and the 30-month national stage in MPEP 1800
- Priority and benefit claims across PCT, provisional, and foreign applications
- Post-grant and correction procedures: reissue (and the recapture rule), ex parte reexamination, supplemental examination, certificates of correction, and the basics of inter partes review and post-grant review

**Applied skills**
- Sequence an appeal correctly and identify what each filing must contain
- Map a PCT application's path to U.S. national stage and the controlling dates
- Match a specific defect (error in the patent, new prior art, broadening within two years) to the correct correction vehicle
- Distinguish reissue from reexamination from supplemental examination by purpose

**Common traps**
- Confusing the appeal sequence or omitting a required brief section
- Missing the 30-month national-stage deadline or conflating it with the priority date
- Attempting to broaden claims by reissue more than two years after grant (the recapture and broadening bars)
- Treating reexamination as a way to fix drafting errors rather than to address patentability over prior art

**Deliverables**
- An appeal sequence checklist from notice through hearing
- A PCT-to-national-stage timeline with the key dates
- A post-grant decision matrix matching defects to vehicles

## Chapter 8: Practitioner Ethics and Professional Judgment

**Core questions:** What duties does a registered practitioner owe the Office and the client, and when do those duties constrain what you may do?

**Key concepts**
- The duty of candor and good faith and the duty to disclose material information under 37 CFR 1.56, and the inequitable-conduct consequences of breaching it
- The USPTO Rules of Professional Conduct: competence, conflicts of interest, confidentiality, candor toward the Office, and fees
- Signature and certification requirements under Rule 11.18, and the prohibition on unauthorized practice before the Office
- Client communication, scope of representation, and the boundaries of an agent's versus an attorney's authority

**Applied skills**
- Spot a duty-of-disclosure issue and identify what must go into an IDS
- Recognize a conflict of interest and determine whether it can be waived
- Apply the signature and certification rules to a filing
- Triage an ethics fact pattern to the governing rule and the required next step

**Common traps**
- Treating the duty of candor as satisfied by disclosing only favorable references
- Confusing the USPTO Rules of Professional Conduct with a state bar's rules where they diverge
- Assuming a patent agent may give legal advice or appear in matters reserved to attorneys
- Missing that a Rule 11.18 certification carries real sanctions for false statements

**Deliverables**
- An ethics triage worksheet: facts, governing rule, risk, recommended step
- A duty-of-disclosure/IDS checklist
- A conflict-of-interest screening note

## Capstone: The Mock Registration Exam and Prosecution File

Assemble two things that together prove exam readiness and prosecution judgment.

First, a **full timed mock exam**: two three-hour sessions, 100 questions drawn across the topic clusters in the exam map, taken open-book against the current MPEP edition, followed by a wrong-answer autopsy that sorts every miss by topic, by trap type, and by whether the failure was a rule gap or a navigation-speed problem. Record your MPEP lookup time for the questions you missed.

Second, a **prosecution packet for a fictional invention**: a patentability memo working the claims through 101/102/103/112 with MPEP citations, a prior-art timeline with effective-filing-date analysis, a claim chart tying limitations to specification support, an office-action response plan choosing among amendment, RCE, and appeal, a post-grant or correction decision note, and an ethics triage on a disclosure question. Close with a one-page strategy reflection: which topic clusters you can answer from memory, which you must look up, and exactly where in the MPEP you go for each. The reflection is the real deliverable. It is the difference between knowing the law and being able to find it before the clock runs out.

## Assessment Ideas

- Timed MPEP search drills graded on lookup speed and correct authority selection.
- Mixed multiple-choice sets by topic cluster, graded with a wrong-answer log sorted by topic and trap type.
- Patentability, prior-art, and obviousness exercises graded on limitation-by-limitation reasoning and correct MPEP citation.
- Procedure exercises graded on deadline accuracy and correct choice of next step.
- A full two-session mock exam plus a prosecution packet graded on prosecution judgment, procedural accuracy, and ethical awareness.
