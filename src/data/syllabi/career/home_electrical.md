# Home Electrical: Safe & Savvy
*Understand the wiring behind your walls, do the small jobs confidently, and know exactly when to put the screwdriver down.*

**ID**: `homeElectrical` · **Discipline**: Home & Trades Literacy · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Most adults live their whole lives surrounded by household electricity and never get a clear mental model of how it works. They flip breakers they do not understand, fear outlets they could safely replace, and sometimes attempt things they really should not. This course closes that gap. It gives a curious, capable adult an honest, working understanding of residential electricity: what volts, amps, and watts actually mean; how power travels from the meter through the panel to the outlet in your wall; and why the safety devices in your home behave the way they do.

The spine of the course is safety, not bravado. Every practical skill here, replacing a switch, swapping an outlet, hanging a light fixture, is taught inside a strict ritual: de-energize at the breaker, lock it out, verify dead with a tested tester, and only then touch a wire. We treat panel labels as guesses, treat every conductor as live until proven otherwise, and respect the one-hand habit. The goal is not to turn you into an electrician; it is to make you the kind of homeowner who is genuinely competent at the small jobs and genuinely wise about the large ones.

Equally important is knowing where DIY ends. Adding circuits, opening the main panel, dealing with aluminum wiring, or touching anything near the meter and main lugs is licensed-electrician territory, for reasons of safety, code, permits, and insurance. By the end, you will read your home’s electrical system the way a careful driver reads a road: anticipating hazards, recognizing warning signs (warm outlets, dimming lights, that fishy burning smell), and making calm, correct calls about what to handle and what to hand off.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want practical, real-world home competence rather than exam prep, when they have shown curiosity about how everyday systems work, or when they have flagged interest in home improvement, DIY, or trades literacy. It pairs well with plumbing, home-maintenance, and general "how things work" tracks. The tone is friendly and concrete, scenario-first, never condescending, and it leans hard on intuition (the water-pressure analogy, "the breaker protects the wire") rather than rote code citation. Questions favor decisions a real person faces in a real house. Safety framing is woven through every chapter, not quarantined into one. The course assumes US-style split-phase 120/240 V residential wiring and notes where a licensed electrician (and local code) must take over.

## Chapter 1: Volts, Amps, Watts & Ohm’s Law
**Core questions**: What is voltage really? How do watts relate to volts and amps? What does Ohm’s law tell you about current and resistance? Why is it the current, not the voltage alone, that harms a person?

**Key concepts**: voltage as pressure, current as flow, resistance, power (P = V × I), Ohm’s law (I = V ÷ R), short circuits, lethal current thresholds, series vs parallel.

**Applied skills**: Estimate the amps an appliance draws from its wattage; predict whether two devices will overload one circuit; explain why a short causes a current surge.

**Common traps**: Thinking voltage is "stored" in an outlet; adding instead of multiplying volts and amps; believing only high voltage is dangerous; confusing electron speed with current.

## Chapter 2: The Breaker Panel & How Power Reaches You
**Core questions**: What does a breaker actually protect? What does the main breaker do? What are hot, neutral, and ground for? How does a US panel make 240 V from two 120 V legs?

**Key concepts**: branch breakers, main breaker, busbars, split-phase service, hot/neutral/ground roles, double-pole breakers, neutral-ground bonding at the main.

**Applied skills**: Identify a 240 V double-pole circuit in a panel; explain why the main does not fully de-energize the service entrance; trace the path from utility to outlet.

**Common traps**: Thinking the breaker saves money or boosts voltage; assuming the main resets the meter; believing ground is just a spare hot.

## Chapter 3: Why Breakers Trip
**Core questions**: What is the difference between an overload and a short? Why does a breaker that re-trips instantly signal a fault? Why must you never upsize a breaker to stop nuisance trips?

**Key concepts**: overload vs short circuit vs ground fault, thermal vs magnetic trip behavior, breaker-to-wire matching, daisy-chained loads, GFCI vs panel-breaker trips.

**Applied skills**: Diagnose whether a recurring trip is an overload or a fault; decide when a tripped breaker means "investigate" vs "redistribute load."

**Common traps**: Upsizing a breaker beyond the wire rating; repeatedly resetting a faulting circuit; thinking power strips add capacity; assuming every trip is a nuisance.

## Chapter 4: GFCI & AFCI Protection
**Core questions**: What does a GFCI detect and where is it required? What does an AFCI catch that a regular breaker misses? Why test a GFCI monthly? How can a GFCI protect an ungrounded outlet?

**Key concepts**: ground-fault current imbalance, milliamp shock thresholds, arc-fault detection, wet-location requirements, dual-function breakers, TEST/RESET, GFCI in ungrounded homes.

**Applied skills**: Decide which outlets need GFCI protection; explain the distinct jobs of GFCI (shock) vs AFCI (fire); reset a downstream GFCI feeding a dead outlet.

**Common traps**: Confusing GFCI with overload protection; assuming AFCIs guard against shock; thinking a GFCI needs a ground wire to work.

## Chapter 5: Wire Gauge, Ampacity & Grounding
**Core questions**: Why does a lower gauge number mean a thicker wire? How do you match a breaker to a wire? Why does grounding keep you safe during a fault? Why do undersized wires overheat?

**Key concepts**: AWG (inverse numbering), ampacity, 14 AWG/15 A and 12 AWG/20 A pairings, the ground fault path, I²R heating, cheater adapters, extension-cord sizing and voltage drop.

**Applied skills**: Pick the correct breaker for a given wire; choose an extension cord by gauge and length; explain why a grounded metal case trips the breaker on a fault.

**Common traps**: Reading AWG backwards; oversizing a breaker for "headroom"; trusting cheater adapters; using thin long cords for heavy tools.

## Chapter 6: Outlets, Switches & 3-Way Switches
**Core questions**: Which wire should a switch interrupt? How do two 3-way switches control one light? What is reversed polarity and why does it matter? Where do the wires land on an outlet’s terminals?

**Key concepts**: switching the hot, traveler and common wires, 3-way circuits, reversed polarity, brass/silver/green terminals, tamper-resistant receptacles, parallel home wiring.

**Applied skills**: Wire an outlet to the correct terminals; recognize and fix reversed polarity; reason through a 3-way switch pair.

**Common traps**: Switching the neutral instead of the hot; swapping hot and neutral on terminals; assuming 3-ways are just two switches in series.

## Chapter 7: Lockout / Verify-Dead Safety
**Core questions**: What is the correct first step before any electrical work? How do you verify a circuit is truly dead? Why must you test the tester? Why lock out the breaker and keep one hand back?

**Key concepts**: de-energize at the source, lockout/tagout, verify-dead testing, "live-dead-live" tester validation, mislabeled panels, the one-hand rule.

**Applied skills**: Run the full safety ritual on a real circuit; validate a non-contact tester; lock out a breaker in a shared space.

**Common traps**: Trusting a wall switch or a panel label; testing with fingers; trusting a silent tester without proving it works; racing instead of locking out.

## Chapter 8: DIY Replacements Done Safely
**Core questions**: Why photograph wiring first? How is a fixture properly supported? What makes a wire-nut joint sound? How do you safely restore and verify power afterward?

**Key concepts**: documenting connections, fixture-rated boxes (and fan-rated boxes), wire-nut/back-wire connections, correct strip length, receptacle testers, restoring power last.

**Applied skills**: Swap a switch, outlet, or light fixture end to end; make a reliable wire connection; confirm correct wiring with a three-light tester before plugging in equipment.

**Common traps**: Hanging fixtures from their wires or drywall anchors; loose backstab/wire-nut joints; exposed bare copper; testing a finished outlet with your hands.

## Chapter 9: Reading the Meter & Energy Use
**Core questions**: What does the meter actually measure? How do you estimate what an appliance costs to run? What is the difference between watts and watt-hours? Where does "phantom" load come from?

**Key concepts**: kilowatt-hours, energy = power × time, cost = kW × hours × rate, watts vs watt-hours, standby/phantom loads.

**Applied skills**: Estimate the daily and monthly cost of a heater, dryer, or other appliance; spot which devices drive a bill; explain a slowly climbing meter at night.

**Common traps**: Confusing power (W) with energy (kWh); thinking the meter reads voltage or peak amps; ignoring phantom loads.

## Chapter 10: Common Hazards & When to Call a Pro
**Core questions**: Why is backstabbing risky? Why is aluminum wiring a fire concern? What do warm outlets and burning smells mean? Where does DIY clearly end and a licensed electrician begin? How do you help someone being shocked?

**Key concepts**: backstab vs screw connections, aluminum wiring and approved repairs, overheating warning signs, permits and inspections, insurance/resale implications, de-energize-first rescue, knowing your limits.

**Applied skills**: Recognize overload and overheating warning signs; identify licensed-electrician jobs (new circuits, panel work, service/meter); respond safely to an electrical-shock emergency.

**Common traps**: Treating warm outlets as normal; DIY-repairing aluminum connections; running extension cords as permanent under-rug wiring; skipping permits; grabbing a shock victim still in contact with live current.

## Capstone: The Confident, Cautious Homeowner
Learners pull the whole course together around a realistic home scenario. Given a house with a labeled (and partly mislabeled) panel, a mix of outlets and circuits, and a punch list of tasks, they: read the panel and identify circuit types (including the 240 V double-pole); run the full de-energize, lock-out, verify-dead ritual; correctly replace a switch and an outlet (right terminals, sound connections, no reversed polarity); choose appropriate GFCI/AFCI protection for the right locations; estimate the running cost of the home’s biggest loads from the meter; and, crucially, triage the punch list into "safe DIY," "needs more learning," and "stop and call a licensed electrician" (new circuit, aluminum wiring, scorched mystery splice). The capstone rewards not just correct wiring but correct judgment, the savvy to know which jobs are theirs and which are a pro’s.

## Research Notes
- Course content reflects standard US residential practice: NFPA 70 (National Electrical Code) conventions for GFCI/AFCI placement, breaker-to-wire ampacity matching, and grounding/bonding. Learners are reminded that local code and permitting governs real work.
- Safety framing draws on widely taught electrical-safety practice: de-energize at the source, lockout/tagout, verify-dead with a validated tester ("live-dead-live"), and the one-hand rule for working near possibly-live equipment.
- Hazard chapters reflect commonly documented home fire causes (arc faults, loose/backstab connections, aluminum branch wiring, overloaded circuits, misused extension cords) as emphasized by fire-safety and electrical-safety education resources.
