# PMP: Scope Creep Rodeo
**ID**: `pmpWrangler` · **Discipline**: Career

## Course Aim
PMP preparation is not about memorizing a giant stack of process names and hoping the exam respects your stationery. It is about thinking like a project leader under pressure: protect value, make work visible, engage humans early, tailor the delivery approach, and keep change from galloping through the budget with a tiny flag.

This syllabus treats the PMP as both a career toolkit and an exam. Learners practice predictive, agile, and hybrid project management; stakeholder and team leadership; governance and compliance; risk, quality, procurement, schedule, and finance control; and the PMI-style judgment needed for scenario questions. The tone is calm, practical, and just a little cute because scope creep is easier to face when your clipboard has a sense of humor.

## Course Design Notes
Keep this course scenario-first and workplace-useful. Route questions here when they test PMI-style judgment, project leadership, predictive/agile/hybrid tailoring, stakeholder engagement, change control, risk, governance, business value, earned value, procurement, quality, or exam pacing. Prompts should account for the 2026 exam-content transition when date or blueprint weight matters.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The PMP exam is organized around People, Process, and Business Environment domains. Through July 7, 2026, PMI's current ECO weights are People 42%, Process 50%, and Business Environment 8%, with about half the exam representing predictive approaches and half representing agile or hybrid approaches. Beginning July 8, 2026, PMI's new ECO shifts the weights to People 33%, Process 41%, and Business Environment 26%, with approximately 40% predictive and 60% adaptive/agile or hybrid coverage.

This course is built to survive both blueprints: it trains the 2021 exam shape while increasing attention to business environment, value delivery, visual/case-based questions, governance, sustainability, and strategic outcomes for the 2026 transition.

| Domain | Current Weight Through July 7, 2026 | July 2026 ECO Weight | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| People | 42% | 33% | Lead, coach, negotiate, communicate, resolve conflict, and keep humans from becoming untracked dependencies |
| Process | 50% | 41% | Plan and manage work across predictive, agile, and hybrid delivery while controlling risk, quality, scope, schedule, cost, and change |
| Business Environment | 8% | 26% | Connect projects to strategy, benefits, compliance, governance, external change, and measurable value |

Suggested cadence: 8-10 weeks for experienced project managers, 12-16 weeks for learners rebuilding fundamentals. Every week should include scenario questions, one artifact-building drill, one agile/predictive comparison drill, and a wrong-answer log. The log is not a shame drawer; it is your project risk register wearing study glasses.

## Chapter 1: PMP Operating System
**Core questions:** What does PMI mean by "best next action"? How does a project manager choose the right move before reaching for a template?

**Key concepts**
- PMP eligibility, exam structure, domain weights, breaks, timing, and question types
- PMI mindset: servant leadership, value delivery, ethics, collaboration, transparency, and tailoring
- Predictive, adaptive/agile, and hybrid life cycles; when each approach fits
- Process groups, performance domains, principles, artifacts, and the difference between knowing a term and choosing an action
- Error-log categories: missed concept, misread scenario, wrong life cycle, premature escalation, ignored stakeholder, math slip, and "I picked the spicy answer"

**Applied skills**
- Read a scenario and identify the project context before selecting a response
- Choose between analyze, collaborate, escalate, document, or change-control actions
- Translate PMP language into workplace behavior without becoming a process vending machine

**Common traps**
- Studying the old PMBOK process-group memory map as if the exam still tests inputs, tools, and outputs by name
- Assuming the exam rewards real-world expedience; PMI rewards its idealized servant-leader, value-first mindset
- Treating "predictive" as the default and agile as a bolt-on, when roughly half (and soon a majority) of items lean adaptive
- Booking the exam date before confirming the 35 contact-hour and experience eligibility, then scrambling

**Deliverables**
- Personal PMP study charter with target exam date, eligibility tasks, weekly hours, and score-risk areas
- Domain map that links People, Process, and Business Environment to personal project experience
- 60-question diagnostic with a written postmortem and first-pass study backlog

## Chapter 2: People - Teams, Conflict, and Communication
**Core questions:** How do project leaders build trust, handle conflict, and keep work moving when humans arrive with calendars, feelings, incentives, and opinions?

**Key concepts**
- Team formation, ground rules, working agreements, team charter, accountability, and empowerment
- Conflict sources, conflict-resolution strategies, negotiation, facilitation, and emotional intelligence
- Servant leadership, coaching, mentoring, motivation, training, recognition, and performance improvement
- Virtual and distributed teams, cultural awareness, accessibility, psychological safety, and inclusive communication
- Communication planning, information radiators, feedback loops, reporting needs, and stakeholder-specific messaging

**Applied skills**
- In predictive work, define roles, responsibilities, escalation paths, and formal communication cadence
- In agile work, support self-managing teams, product ownership, daily coordination, reviews, retrospectives, and impediment removal
- In hybrid work, keep formal governance aligned with iterative delivery so nobody is surprised by either the sprint board or the steering committee

**Common traps**
- Escalating a conflict to the sponsor when the scenario expects the PM to facilitate the parties directly first
- Choosing "avoid/withdraw" as conflict resolution when collaborate/problem-solve is the PMI-preferred answer absent a real reason to defer
- Confusing the servant leader's job: a PM removes impediments and coaches, but does not assign tasks to a self-managing agile team
- Picking the answer that "manages" stakeholders rather than the one that engages, listens to, or collaborates with them
- Reaching for a formal status report when the scenario points to a face-to-face conversation or an information radiator

**Deliverables**
- Team charter and RACI/RASCI comparison for a sample project
- Conflict scenario response memo using PMI-style first action, next action, and escalation logic
- Communication plan with stakeholder groups, channels, cadence, feedback loops, and reporting artifacts
- 75 People-domain scenario questions with wrong-answer analysis

## Chapter 3: Stakeholders, Vision, and Business Value
**Core questions:** Who cares, what do they value, and how do we keep the project connected to why it exists?

**Key concepts**
- Stakeholder identification, power/interest and influence/impact mapping, expectations, engagement strategies, and satisfaction monitoring
- Shared vision, product goals, success criteria, acceptance criteria, benefits, outcomes, and value measures
- Business case, benefits management plan, strategic alignment, organizational culture, and change readiness
- Governance structures, decision rights, approval thresholds, and sponsor engagement
- External business environment: regulatory, market, technology, economic, sustainability, and organizational changes

**Applied skills**
- Build a stakeholder engagement plan for predictive delivery and a stakeholder collaboration rhythm for agile delivery
- Link business value to scope baselines, product backlogs, release plans, and incremental delivery options
- Reassess value when assumptions change rather than defending an old plan because it has nice columns

**Common traps**
- Treating the business case as a launch document instead of a living test the project must keep passing
- Optimizing for on-time, on-budget delivery (outputs) while the question is really about benefits and outcomes (value)
- Forgetting to re-identify stakeholders mid-project; the register is not a one-time census
- Underweighting Business Environment because the current ECO is only 8%, then losing easy points as the 2026 ECO raises it to 26%
- Confusing compliance and regulatory constraints with negotiable stakeholder preferences

**Deliverables**
- Stakeholder register and engagement assessment matrix
- Benefits map connecting outputs, outcomes, KPIs, owners, and measurement timing
- Change-impact brief for a project affected by new regulation, technology, or market pressure
- 50 mixed People/Business Environment questions focused on value and stakeholder judgment

## Chapter 4: Starting and Planning the Work
**Core questions:** What are we making, how will we make it, and what must be true before execution should begin?

**Key concepts**
- Project charter, assumptions, constraints, success criteria, high-level risks, milestones, and approval authority
- Scope planning, requirements elicitation, WBS, work packages, decomposition, backlog creation, and prioritization
- Schedule planning: activities, dependencies, critical path, milestones, rolling wave planning, release planning, velocity, and lead/lag
- Cost and finance planning: estimates, budget, contingency, management reserve, funding limits, and financial reporting
- Resource planning, procurement strategy, make-or-buy analysis, contract types, and supplier selection

**Applied skills**
- Build a WBS for stable scope and a prioritized backlog for evolving scope
- Estimate with analogous, parametric, bottom-up, three-point, story-point, and relative-sizing methods
- Tailor governance and planning depth based on uncertainty, complexity, risk, compliance, and stakeholder tolerance

**Common traps**
- Confusing the project charter (authorizes the project, names the PM) with the scope statement or the project management plan
- Putting deliverables-as-verbs in the WBS; WBS elements are nouns/deliverables, decomposed to work packages, not activities
- Treating contingency reserve and management reserve as interchangeable; the PM controls contingency, management controls the reserve
- Reading "critical path" as "most important work" rather than the longest path with the least float
- Story-pointing a fixed-scope, fixed-price contract, or building a Gantt chart for genuinely uncertain discovery work

**Deliverables**
- Project charter for a sample initiative with measurable success criteria
- WBS plus backlog version of the same project, with notes on when each representation helps
- Schedule network diagram with critical path, float, crashing, and fast-tracking decisions
- Cost baseline and reserve rationale for a scenario with uncertainty

## Chapter 5: Executing, Monitoring, and Controlling
**Core questions:** How do project managers keep delivery honest once the plan meets reality?

**Key concepts**
- Directing work, managing knowledge, lessons learned, issue logs, decision logs, and performance reporting
- Integrated change control, change requests, configuration management, approval thresholds, and baseline updates
- Quality planning, quality assurance, quality control, acceptance, inspection, prevention, and continuous improvement
- Earned value management: PV, EV, AC, BAC, CV, SV, CPI, SPI, EAC, ETC, VAC, and TCPI
- Agile metrics: burn charts, cumulative flow, velocity, cycle time, lead time, throughput, escaped defects, and customer feedback

**Applied skills**
- Use change control for baselined predictive work and backlog refinement for adaptive work
- Explain when a variance needs analysis, corrective action, governance approval, or simple communication
- Read charts, dashboards, and scenario data without letting one metric boss the whole project around

**Common traps**
- Mixing up the EVM sign conventions: negative CV/SV is over budget/behind, CPI/SPI below 1.0 is unfavorable
- Confusing quality (meeting requirements) with grade (a category of features); low grade can be acceptable, low quality is not
- Implementing a change before it is approved through integrated change control, even when the sponsor "verbally agreed"
- Treating gold-plating (adding unrequested scope) as good customer service rather than uncontrolled scope creep
- Forecasting EAC with the wrong formula for the scenario; atypical variance, typical variance (BAC/CPI), and reset-estimate cases differ

**Deliverables**
- Change request packet with impact analysis across scope, schedule, cost, risk, quality, resources, and benefits
- Earned value worksheet with interpretation, not just formulas
- Agile metrics dashboard and recommendation memo for a team with flow problems
- 100 Process-domain questions, including formulas, charts, and "what should the PM do next?" scenarios

## Chapter 6: Risk, Issues, Compliance, and Procurement
**Core questions:** What might happen, what already happened, who owns the response, and which obligations are non-negotiable?

**Key concepts**
- Risk management planning, risk identification, probability/impact assessment, qualitative and quantitative analysis, expected monetary value, response strategies, triggers, owners, and reserves
- Issue management, impediment removal, escalation, root-cause analysis, and decision tracking
- Compliance requirements, auditability, regulatory obligations, health and safety, security, privacy, and contractual duties
- Procurement planning, bid documents, source selection, contract administration, claims, disputes, and closure
- Threats and opportunities: avoid, mitigate, transfer, accept, escalate, exploit, enhance, share

**Applied skills**
- Maintain a risk register for plan-driven work and lightweight risk visibility for agile teams
- Separate risks from issues, assumptions from constraints, and compliance needs from preferences
- Integrate supplier work into sprint/release planning or predictive baselines without losing accountability

**Common traps**
- Calling a risk an issue (or vice versa); a risk is a future uncertainty, an issue is already happening
- Mismatching the response to the risk type: mitigate/avoid/transfer/accept are for threats; exploit/enhance/share/accept are for opportunities
- Assuming "transfer" or insurance removes the risk; it shifts impact and ownership but does not eliminate the threat
- Letting the contract type hide where risk actually sits: fixed-price loads it on the seller, cost-reimbursable on the buyer
- Treating a compliance or safety obligation as a risk to be weighed against cost, when it is a non-negotiable constraint

**Deliverables**
- Risk register with ranked threats, opportunities, triggers, owners, and response plans
- Compliance checklist and escalation path for a regulated project
- Procurement strategy brief comparing fixed-price, cost-reimbursable, and time-and-materials contracts
- Scenario drill: convert five project surprises into risk response, issue response, or change-control action

## Chapter 7: Agile, Hybrid, and Tailoring Lab
**Core questions:** How do you choose a way of working that fits the work instead of forcing the work into a fashionable hat?

**Key concepts**
- Agile mindset, servant leadership, empiricism, transparency, inspection, adaptation, and customer collaboration
- Scrum roles/events/artifacts, Kanban flow, WIP limits, Lean waste reduction, XP quality practices, and scaling considerations
- Product vision, roadmap, release plan, backlog, user stories, definition of ready, definition of done, MVP, and incremental value
- Hybrid patterns: predictive governance with agile delivery, phased delivery with iterative development, and compliance-heavy agile
- Tailoring factors: uncertainty, complexity, team maturity, stakeholder availability, regulatory pressure, architecture, funding, and risk

**Applied skills**
- Pick the delivery approach from scenario clues rather than buzzwords
- Reconcile agile change tolerance with organizational reporting, procurement, compliance, and finance needs
- Coach teams and stakeholders through transition without declaring every meeting a ceremony and calling it transformation

**Common traps**
- Having the Scrum Master or PM make product priority calls that belong to the product owner, or vice versa
- Pulling work into a sprint mid-iteration to "help the stakeholder," breaking the sprint's protected commitment
- Confusing definition of ready (story can be started) with definition of done (story is complete and potentially shippable)
- Assuming agile means no documentation, no governance, and no contracts; it means just enough, fit for purpose
- Choosing pure agile or pure predictive when the scenario clues (regulation, fixed funding, evolving requirements) point to hybrid

**Deliverables**
- Delivery-approach decision tree for predictive, agile, and hybrid selection
- Product backlog with prioritized user stories, acceptance criteria, and release slices
- Hybrid governance model showing ceremonies, approvals, metrics, and escalation points
- 75 agile/hybrid questions with emphasis on role boundaries and servant-leader responses

## Chapter 8: Exam Strategy and Simulation
**Core questions:** Can you make good project decisions for four hours while the clock politely breathes down your neck?

**Key concepts**
- PMP question types: scenario, multiple choice, multiple response, matching, hotspot/point-and-click, pull-down, graphic-based, and case-style items
- Time strategy for 180 questions in 230-240 minutes, including review blocks and breaks based on the exam version and delivery modality
- Answer-choice traps: first action versus best action, escalate too soon, ignore the team, skip analysis, change without approval, over-document agile work, and solve the wrong problem beautifully
- Formula strategy, chart interpretation, and "good enough to move on" decision-making
- Final-week triage, rest, test-center or online-proctor logistics, and exam-day reset routines

**Applied skills**
- Predict the answer before reading options when the scenario is clear
- Eliminate choices that violate collaboration, ethics, governance, or value delivery
- Mark questions by uncertainty type and return only when the review will improve the answer

**Common traps**
- Picking the "first action" when the question asks for the best action, or doing something when the best move is to gather information
- Over-revising marked questions and changing correct answers out of nerves rather than new reasoning
- Burning the two scheduled breaks early and hitting the back third of the exam with no reset left
- Letting an unfamiliar drag-and-drop, hotspot, or matching item rattle pacing on the surrounding scenario questions
- Mismanaging the clock: 180 questions in 230 minutes is roughly 75 seconds each, so a single agonized item can quietly eat three

**Deliverables**
- Two full-length simulated PMP exams with domain score analysis
- Personal trap list with examples and corrected PMI-style reasoning
- Final 10-day study plan that balances weak areas, light review, rest, and logistics
- Exam-day checklist for ID, appointment, breaks, scratch tools, food, environment, and reset routines

## Capstone: The Project Leader Portfolio
By the end, learners assemble a portfolio that proves both exam readiness and workplace usefulness:

- PMP study charter, eligibility tracker, and final exam-day plan
- Stakeholder register, communication plan, team charter, and conflict-response memo
- Project charter, WBS, backlog, schedule network, cost baseline, and reserves rationale
- Change request packet, risk register, compliance checklist, procurement strategy, and quality dashboard
- Agile/hybrid tailoring decision tree, release plan, and metrics dashboard
- Two simulated exam reports with domain analysis and a targeted remediation plan

## Assessment Ideas
- Scenario-question sets tagged by People, Process, and Business Environment.
- Artifact drills that require a usable charter, stakeholder register, risk register, change request, or dashboard.
- Predictive/agile/hybrid comparison prompts where learners justify the delivery approach.
- Formula and chart checks for earned value, schedule, cost, and flow metrics.
- Full simulated PMP exams with domain analysis and a remediation backlog.
