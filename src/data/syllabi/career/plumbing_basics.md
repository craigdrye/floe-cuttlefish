# Home Plumbing: Fix It Yourself
Stop the leak, clear the clog, and know when to call a pro — confidence with the pipes behind your walls.
**ID**: `plumbingBasics` · **Discipline**: Home & Trades · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Most of us live our whole lives a few feet from plumbing we do not understand, and we only think about it when something goes wrong, usually at the worst possible moment. This course is for the curious adult who wants to demystify the water system in their own home: to know what those pipes actually do, fix the common problems themselves, and recognize the handful of situations that genuinely call for a professional.

The throughline is a single discipline that pros internalize early and amateurs learn the hard way: shutoff first. Before you touch anything, you stop the water. Build that reflex and most home plumbing stops being scary, because the worst-case outcome is now under your control. From there, the rest is understanding two simple systems — pressurized supply and gravity drainage — and a small set of repeatable repairs.

By the end you should be able to confidently fix a running toilet, clear a clogged drain, replace a worn washer or cartridge, understand your water heater and house pressure, prevent frozen pipes, and — just as importantly — know the bright lines where DIY ends and a licensed plumber or the gas utility begins. The goal is competence and good judgment, not bravado.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want practical, hands-on home maintenance rather than a trade certification or code-exam prep. The course assumes no prior knowledge and uses concrete household scenarios (a running toilet, a spraying supply line, a rumbling water heater) rather than abstract theory. Safety and shutoff-first discipline are woven through every chapter, not quarantined into one. Where a task crosses into gas, sewer mains, hidden leaks, or permitted DWV changes, the course explicitly hands off to a professional — teaching judgment about scope is treated as a core skill, not a disclaimer. Difficulty is mixed: foundational system literacy and tool recognition sit alongside diagnostic reasoning (matching a symptom to the right system) and a few integrative "what do you do first" emergency calls.

## Chapter 1: How Your Home Plumbing Works
**Core questions**: What are the two separate systems and how do they differ? Why do drains need vents and slope? What is a trap and why does every fixture have one? Where does water enter, and where does hot water come from?
**Key concepts**: supply vs drain-waste-vent (DWV), pressurized vs gravity flow, vents, trap water seal, drain slope (~1/4 in/ft), the main service entrance, on-site water heating.
**Applied skills**: trace a fixture back to its supply and drain; identify which system a pipe belongs to by size and behavior; explain a sewer smell as a dry trap.
**Common traps**: thinking all pipes are interchangeable; believing drains are pressurized; assuming hot water arrives from the street.

## Chapter 2: Shutoff Valves and Safety First
**Core questions**: What is the first move on any repair? Where is the main, and where are the fixture stops? How do you read a ball valve's position? What about electricity near water?
**Key concepts**: shutoff-first discipline, main shutoff, fixture stop (angle) valves, bleeding residual pressure, gate vs ball valves, valve position reading, breaker safety in wet work.
**Applied skills**: locate and test the main and every stop; isolate a single fixture; stop an overflowing toilet without a stop valve; de-energize a circuit before wet work.
**Common traps**: loosening a live pressurized joint; flushing an already-overflowing toilet; trusting a seized old gate valve; assuming no sparks means no shock risk.

## Chapter 3: Fixing a Running Toilet
**Core questions**: Why does a toilet run constantly? How do you confirm the cause? What if water spills into the overflow tube? Why is the flush weak?
**Key concepts**: flapper seal, fill valve, float and set water level, overflow tube, the dye test, rim jets and full-dump flush.
**Applied skills**: run a dye test to isolate the flapper; replace a flapper; adjust a float to stop overfill; clean rim jets to restore flush force.
**Common traps**: blaming the wax ring or house pressure for a run; replacing the flapper when the float is the problem; cranking the handle instead of fixing the seal.

## Chapter 4: Unclogging Drains
**Core questions**: Which plunger works on what? What actually makes plunging effective? When do you reach for a snake, and how does an auger work? How do you prevent clogs?
**Key concepts**: flange vs flat-cup plungers, water seal and push-pull technique, hand auger vs closet auger, mechanical vs chemical clearing, grease as the top kitchen culprit.
**Applied skills**: pick and use the right plunger; clean a P-trap; run a hand or closet auger safely; build grease-avoidance habits.
**Common traps**: using a flat-cup plunger on a toilet; plunging a dry fixture; defaulting to caustic chemicals; running a disposal with no water.

## Chapter 5: Leaky Faucets and Fixtures
**Core questions**: Why does an old two-handle faucet drip, and what fixes it? What does a modern single-handle faucet need? What does a base leak mean? How and where do you use thread tape?
**Key concepts**: compression washer and seat, cartridge faucets, spout O-rings, PTFE (Teflon) tape direction, threaded (NPT) vs compression/push-fit joints.
**Applied skills**: replace a washer and dress a seat; swap a brand-specific cartridge; replace a spout O-ring; wrap thread tape correctly and know when not to.
**Common traps**: overtightening a worn washer; looking for a washer in a cartridge faucet; taping a compression fitting; wrapping tape the wrong direction.

## Chapter 6: Pipe Materials and Tools
**Core questions**: What are PEX, copper, PVC, and ABS for, and how are they joined? Why are drains so much bigger than supply lines? Which wrench grips pipe, and which reaches faucet nuts?
**Key concepts**: PEX (flexible, supply-only, UV-sensitive), copper (soldered or push-fit), PVC (white, primer + cement) vs ABS (black, one-step), DWV sizing logic, pipe wrench vs adjustable vs basin wrench, push-fit fittings.
**Applied skills**: identify a pipe by material and color; choose the right cement; pick the right wrench for pipe vs chrome vs blind faucet nuts.
**Common traps**: using PEX for drains; gluing PVC straight to ABS; putting a toothed pipe wrench on chrome; reaching for the wrong wrench in a tight space.

## Chapter 7: Water Heaters and Water Pressure
**Core questions**: What is the T&P valve and why must it never be plugged? Why does a tank rumble? What temperature is safe? Why is high house pressure a problem, and what fixes it? What is water hammer?
**Key concepts**: temperature-and-pressure relief valve, sediment buildup and flushing, ~120 F setpoint (scald vs Legionella), 40-80 psi safe range, pressure-reducing valve (PRV), water hammer and arrestors.
**Applied skills**: identify and respect the T&P; flush a tank's sediment; set a safe temperature; gauge house pressure and diagnose a failing PRV; recognize and address water hammer.
**Common traps**: confusing the T&P with the drain valve; setting the tank dangerously hot or too cold; treating high pressure as a benefit; blaming bubbles for water hammer.

## Chapter 8: Frozen Pipes and Seasonal Care
**Core questions**: Why does a frozen pipe burst, and where? How do you prevent freezing in a cold snap? How do you thaw a pipe safely? How do you winterize an empty building?
**Key concepts**: trapped-pressure bursts downstream of ice, dripping faucets and warm-air circulation, gentle thawing from the faucet inward, draining and antifreezing traps for vacant homes.
**Applied skills**: protect vulnerable pipes during a freeze; thaw a frozen line without an open flame; fully winterize an unheated structure.
**Common traps**: assuming the pipe splits at the ice; turning heat off while away; thawing with a torch or boiling water on a sealed line; thinking a closed main alone protects the system.

## Chapter 9: When to DIY vs Call a Pro
**Core questions**: What do you do if you smell gas? What does sewage backing up into several drains mean? When do permits and code apply? How do you handle a hidden in-wall leak?
**Key concepts**: gas-leak evacuation (no flames/switches, call utility), main-line blockage vs single-fixture clog, permits and code for DWV/vent changes, hidden leaks and structural damage.
**Applied skills**: respond correctly to a gas smell; distinguish a main-line problem from a local clog; recognize permit-worthy work; act early on a spreading damp stain.
**Common traps**: relighting into a gas leak or flipping switches; snaking a main-line backup yourself; assuming residential work is exempt from code; painting over an active leak.

## Chapter 10: Putting It Together
**Core questions**: What is the first move in any uncontrolled leak? What belongs in a starter kit? How do you trace a mystery leak by timing? Why does overtightening backfire, and why not mix metals?
**Key concepts**: shutoff-first emergency reflex, a practical beginner toolkit, leak diagnosis by flow timing (drain vs supply), overtightening and cracked plastic, galvanic corrosion and dielectric unions, the shutoff audit.
**Applied skills**: stop a burst at the main; assemble a sensible tool kit; localize a leak by when it appears; tighten to the right torque; join dissimilar metals safely.
**Common traps**: taping or bucketing before shutting the water; buying advanced gear before learning shutoffs; cranking slip nuts to maximum; joining copper to steel without a dielectric union.

## Capstone
Do a real walk-through of your own home (or a detailed imagined one) and produce a household plumbing readiness brief: (1) a map of the main shutoff and every fixture stop, with each one tested and any seized valve flagged for replacement; (2) a labeled inventory of pipe materials and the water heater's type, temperature, and T&P/drain locations; (3) a measured house water-pressure reading with a verdict on whether a PRV is needed; (4) a written emergency plan covering a burst line, an overflowing toilet, a gas smell, and a sewage backup, each with the correct first move and a clear DIY-or-pro call; and (5) a seasonal checklist for freeze prevention and, if relevant, winterizing. The brief should demonstrate shutoff-first discipline, correct symptom-to-system diagnosis, and sound judgment about where competent DIY ends.
