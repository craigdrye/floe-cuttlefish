# MPRE: Ethics Before You Earn the Bar
**ID**: `mpre` · **Discipline**: Law (United States)

## Course Aim
The Multistate Professional Responsibility Examination is not a vibe check. It is sixty scored multiple-choice questions delivered in two hours by the National Conference of Bar Examiners, scaled 50-150, and almost every U.S. jurisdiction requires a score of 80, 85, or 86 to sit for or be admitted to the bar. The fact pattern will name an attorney, name a client, name a third party, name a tribunal, and quietly bury one dispositive sentence in the middle. The question stem will read "Is the attorney subject to discipline?" or "Was the lawyer's conduct proper?" — and four polished answer choices will compete for your attention while exactly one of them tracks the ABA Model Rules.

This course trains the rule-and-rules detective. You do not reason from what feels fair, from what your bar-prep professor said in passing, or from what your firm actually does. You reason from the text of the ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct (Rules 1.0-8.5), the ABA Model Code of Judicial Conduct (Canons 1-4), and the federal practice rules the NCBE explicitly tests — grand-jury secrecy, federal disqualification statutes, agency-specific overlays. When the rule says "shall," "may," "shall not," or "should," that word controls the answer.

The discipline this exam rewards is unforgiving of approximations. "Confidentiality" is not "privilege." A "conflict" is not a "violation" without analysis of consent and writing. "Subject to discipline" is not the same call as "subject to civil liability" or "subject to disqualification." Every answer turns on which call the stem is making — and on whether the underlying rule was written with a hard "shall" or a permissive "may."

The aim of this course is operational fluency. By the end you should be able to read a one-paragraph fact pattern, name the controlling Rule (or Canon) by number, identify the duty that is engaged, apply the right consent or writing requirement, and pick the answer that an experienced ethics-committee lawyer would defend at a disciplinary hearing — calmly, in two minutes, sixty times in a row.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions to this track only when they test the MPRE-specific subject matter: the ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct, the ABA Model Code of Judicial Conduct, and the narrow set of federal rules NCBE tests. This is distinct from the general `professionalEthics` track. That track surveys ethical decision-making across professions; MPRE is rule-specific, jurisdictional, exam-tactical, and bound to NCBE's subject-matter outline. Always reward the candidate who reads the call of the question with surgical care: "may," "must," "subject to discipline," "subject to disqualification," and "subject to civil liability" each demand a distinct analysis. Every question should expose a Rule number, a duty, and a discriminating fact — never an ambient "judgment call."

## Chapter 1: Regulation of the Legal Profession
**Core questions:** Who admits, disciplines, supervises, and reaches across borders for lawyers? When does conduct in one state expose a lawyer to discipline elsewhere, and what does each duty actually require?

**Key concepts**
- Bar admission and candor in the application (Rule 8.1); duty to update during pendency
- Reporting another lawyer's misconduct (Rule 8.3) and its narrow Rule 1.6 carve-out
- Lawyer misconduct categories (Rule 8.4): dishonesty, criminal acts reflecting on fitness, prejudice in conduct, bias/harassment in connection with practice (8.4(g))
- Unauthorized practice and multijurisdictional practice (Rule 5.5): the four temporary-practice safe harbors plus systematic-and-continuous practice rules
- Choice of law in disciplinary proceedings (Rule 8.5): tribunal rule vs. predominant-effect rule

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish "shall" report from "may" report; identify what Rule 1.6 protections actually cover
- Read a multijurisdictional fact pattern and decide which of 5.5(c)(1)-(4) applies (associated local counsel, ADR, pro hac vice, related to home practice)
- Map a fact pattern to 8.4(a)-(g) categories without overreach

**Common traps**
- Treating Rule 8.3 reporting as merely permissive (it is mandatory for misconduct raising a substantial question as to honesty, trustworthiness, or fitness)
- Confusing 8.3(c) — which exempts only Rule 1.6 confidential information and lawyer assistance program information — with a generalized discretion to stay silent
- Assuming temporary out-of-state work always requires pro hac vice (it does not; some 5.5(c) safe harbors do not require any application)
- Conflating UPL (5.5(a)) with multijurisdictional practice (5.5(c)); the first is a prohibition, the second is a permission

**Authoritative sources:** ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct, Rules 5.5, 8.1, 8.3, 8.4, 8.5; NCBE MPRE Subject Matter Outline; ABA Standing Committee on Ethics and Professional Responsibility formal opinions.

## Chapter 2: The Client-Lawyer Relationship
**Core questions:** When does representation begin, who is the client, what scope did the lawyer actually agree to, and when must the lawyer withdraw?

**Key concepts**
- Formation; prospective clients (Rule 1.18) and what protections survive non-engagement
- Scope and allocation of authority between lawyer and client (Rule 1.2); limited-scope representation (Rule 1.2(c)) with informed consent
- Diligence (Rule 1.3) and communication (Rule 1.4) — failure to communicate is a leading discipline trigger
- Fees (Rule 1.5): reasonableness factor list (1.5(a)); writing requirement for contingent fees (1.5(c)); prohibited contingencies in criminal and domestic relations matters (1.5(d)); fee-splitting among lawyers in different firms (1.5(e))
- Diminished-capacity clients (Rule 1.14) — protective action and confidentiality limits
- Termination (Rule 1.16): mandatory vs. permissive withdrawal; tribunal permission; refund of unearned fees and return of file

**Applied skills**
- Identify when Rule 1.18 confidentiality and disqualification attach to a non-client consultation
- Draft mentally an engagement letter that satisfies 1.5(b), 1.5(c), and 1.2(c)
- Walk a withdrawal scenario through 1.16(a) (must), 1.16(b) (may), and 1.16(c) (tribunal authority)

**Common traps**
- Treating an oral contingent fee as enforceable (1.5(c) requires a writing signed by the client)
- Splitting a contingent fee with a referring lawyer without client consent and proportional responsibility (1.5(e))
- Treating Rule 1.4 communication failures as merely "bad service"; they are routinely the basis for discipline
- Assuming withdrawal mid-trial is automatic without court permission (1.16(c))
- Holding unearned fees after termination (1.16(d) requires prompt refund)

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 1.2, 1.3, 1.4, 1.5, 1.14, 1.16, 1.18; ABA Formal Opinion 10-457 (limited-scope) and related state opinions.

## Chapter 3: Client Confidentiality
**Core questions:** What information must the lawyer protect, what may the lawyer reveal, and how is Rule 1.6 different from the attorney-client privilege?

**Key concepts**
- Rule 1.6(a): broad duty covering all information relating to the representation, whatever the source
- Rule 1.6(b) exceptions, every clause: (1) prevent reasonably certain death or substantial bodily harm; (2)-(3) prevent or mitigate client crime/fraud causing substantial financial injury where the lawyer's services were used; (4) secure ethics advice; (5) self-defense in claims and proceedings; (6) comply with law or court order; (7) detect conflicts in a firm transition
- Rule 1.6(c): reasonable efforts to prevent inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure (technology competence)
- Attorney-client privilege as a narrow evidentiary rule distinct from the broader confidentiality duty
- Inadvertent disclosure handling (Rule 4.4(b)): prompt notice to sender
- Confidentiality after termination and after client death (continues)

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish "privileged" from "confidential under 1.6" with a two-column scenario test
- Apply 1.6(b)(1) versus 1.6(b)(2)/(3) — bodily-harm exception is broader than financial-injury exception (financial-injury requires lawyer's services were used)
- Recognize that Rule 1.6 exceptions are almost all permissive ("may") not mandatory; a few jurisdictions deviate

**Common traps**
- Confidentiality (Rule 1.6) vs. attorney-client privilege are NOT the same. Confidentiality is broad ethics duty covering ALL info relating to representation; privilege is narrow evidentiary rule. Disclosure exceptions: prevent reasonably certain death/substantial bodily harm permissive (1.6(b)(1)); to comply with court order or law; to obtain ethics advice; to defend self.
- Treating Rule 1.6 as overridden by mere relevance in litigation (a court order is required)
- Disclosing financial-fraud exception without confirming the lawyer's services were used to further the fraud
- Forgetting that 1.6(c) imposes affirmative duties on email, file storage, and vendor security

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rule 1.6 and Comments; Rule 1.18; Rule 4.4(b); ABA Formal Opinion 477R (electronic communications); Upjohn v. United States (privilege).

## Chapter 4: Conflicts of Interest
**Core questions:** Whose loyalty is being split, can informed consent cure it, must the consent be in writing, and does the conflict impute to the firm?

**Key concepts**
- Current-client conflicts (Rule 1.7(a)): direct adversity OR significant risk of material limitation
- The 1.7(b) cure: (1) reasonable belief of competent and diligent representation; (2) not prohibited by law; (3) not the assertion of a claim by one client against another in the same proceeding; (4) each affected client gives INFORMED CONSENT, CONFIRMED IN WRITING
- Specific conflicts (Rule 1.8): business transactions with clients (1.8(a)), use of information (1.8(b)), gifts (1.8(c)), literary rights (1.8(d)), financial assistance to client (1.8(e)), payment by third party (1.8(f)), aggregate settlements (1.8(g)), prospective malpractice limits (1.8(h)), proprietary interest in litigation (1.8(i)), sexual relations (1.8(j) — barred unless pre-existing consensual relationship)
- Former-client conflicts (Rule 1.9): same or substantially related matter, materially adverse interests
- Imputation (Rule 1.10) and screening — when allowed for laterals (1.10(a)(2)), former government lawyers (1.11), former judges/neutrals (1.12), prospective clients (1.18)
- Organization as client (Rule 1.13): client is the entity, not its constituents; reporting up (1.13(b)) and reporting out (1.13(c))

**Applied skills**
- Diagram a "three-client triangle" for joint representation and identify the writing required
- Distinguish 1.10 imputation (nonconsentable conflicts spread to the firm) from non-imputed personal-interest conflicts (1.10(a)(1))
- Decide whether a former-client matter is "substantially related" using the playbook test (information learned would materially advance the new matter)

**Common traps**
- Believing concurrent direct adversity to a current client is curable without each client's INFORMED CONSENT IN WRITING (it is not — confirmed-in-writing is the floor)
- Assuming firms can always screen laterals; some jurisdictions deviate from 1.10(a)(2)
- Treating "substantially related" as factual identity; the test is overlap of confidential information
- Forgetting 1.8(j) prohibits sex with a client unless a consensual relationship pre-existed the representation
- Failing to spot 1.13's "up the ladder" duty when an organization's agent is breaking the law in a way that harms the entity

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 1.7, 1.8, 1.9, 1.10, 1.11, 1.12, 1.13, 1.18; Restatement (Third) of the Law Governing Lawyers §§ 121-135.

## Chapter 5: Competence, Diligence, and Civil Liability
**Core questions:** What does competence require in 2026, and where do disciplinary, civil, and criminal liability part ways for the same conduct?

**Key concepts**
- Rule 1.1 competence — legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, preparation; technology competence per Comment 8
- Rule 1.3 diligence; Rule 1.4 communication; the trio that catches most discipline cases
- Legal malpractice elements (state law): duty, breach (standard of care), causation, damages — separate from discipline
- Three columns: disciplinary liability (Model Rules), civil liability (malpractice/breach of fiduciary duty), criminal liability (e.g., obstruction, fraud)
- Limitation of malpractice liability (Rule 1.8(h)) — only if client independently represented; settlement of malpractice claim requires advice to seek independent counsel

**Applied skills**
- Take a single fact pattern and identify all three liability buckets that may apply
- Recognize when a fee dispute slides into a malpractice or fiduciary-duty exposure

**Common traps**
- Treating discipline and malpractice as the same; the standards and remedies differ
- Assuming technology incompetence is a soft issue; ABA Comment 8 made it part of Rule 1.1 in 2012
- Failing to spot that 1.8(h) requires independent counsel before limiting prospective malpractice liability

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 1.1, 1.3, 1.4, 1.8(h); Restatement (Third) of the Law Governing Lawyers §§ 48-58.

## Chapter 6: Litigation and Other Forms of Advocacy
**Core questions:** How far can the lawyer push for the client without breaking the tribunal, the witness, the jury, the press, or the prosecutor's special duties?

**Key concepts**
- Meritorious claims and contentions (Rule 3.1); expediting litigation (Rule 3.2)
- Candor toward the tribunal (Rule 3.3): no false statement of law or fact to tribunal; disclosure of directly adverse legal authority in the controlling jurisdiction not disclosed by opposing counsel; remedial measures including disclosure if necessary if the lawyer knows of false evidence offered (3.3(a)(3))
- Fairness to opposing party and counsel (Rule 3.4): no obstruction, no unlawful destruction of evidence, no falsification, no improper inducement of witnesses
- Impartiality and decorum (Rule 3.5): no ex parte except as authorized; juror contact rules
- Trial publicity (Rule 3.6): substantial-likelihood-of-material-prejudice standard; permitted statements list (3.6(b)); right-of-reply (3.6(c))
- Lawyer as witness (Rule 3.7): generally cannot act as advocate at a trial in which the lawyer is likely to be a necessary witness, with exceptions for uncontested matters, value of services, and substantial hardship to client
- Special responsibilities of a prosecutor (Rule 3.8): probable cause, right to counsel warnings, timely disclosure of exculpatory and mitigating evidence (3.8(d)), restrictions on subpoenas to defense lawyers, remedying convictions on new evidence (3.8(g)-(h))

**Applied skills**
- Walk a known-perjury scenario through Rule 3.3(a)(3) and the Comments' remedial-measures sequence (remonstrate, then withdraw, then disclose if necessary)
- Recognize "directly adverse legal authority in the controlling jurisdiction" — the only disclosure of adverse law required of an advocate

**Common traps**
- Treating Rule 3.3 candor as overridden by Rule 1.6 confidentiality (3.3(c) explicitly says it applies even when disclosure would otherwise be protected by 1.6)
- Believing the lawyer-witness disqualification under 3.7 imputes broadly to the firm (it does not — only the testifying lawyer is generally disqualified)
- Failing to disclose adverse controlling authority in a brief because opposing counsel "should have found it" (3.3(a)(2) requires disclosure)
- Prosecutor delaying Brady-equivalent disclosure under 3.8(d) until trial (the duty is timely)

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 3.1-3.9; Nix v. Whiteside (perjury); Brady v. Maryland (related, not identical to 3.8(d)).

## Chapter 7: Transactions and Communications with Persons Other Than Clients
**Core questions:** What can a lawyer say to non-clients, and when must the lawyer either say more or say nothing at all?

**Key concepts**
- Truthfulness in statements to others (Rule 4.1): no false statement of material fact or law; affirmative disclosure required where necessary to avoid assisting client crime/fraud unless prohibited by 1.6
- Communicating with represented persons (Rule 4.2): the "no-contact rule"; narrow exceptions for prior consent, court authorization, or law-authorized contact
- Dealing with unrepresented persons (Rule 4.3): no false impression of disinterest; advise to seek counsel where appropriate
- Respect for rights of third persons (Rule 4.4): no means that have no substantial purpose other than to embarrass, delay, or burden a third person; inadvertent disclosure prompt-notice duty (4.4(b))

**Applied skills**
- Apply Rule 4.2 to current and former employees of a represented organization (constituents who supervise, direct, or whose conduct may bind the organization are off-limits without consent)
- Recognize when Rule 4.1(b) compels disclosure to a non-client to avoid assisting client fraud and how 1.6 interacts

**Common traps**
- Treating Rule 4.2 as a prohibition on all contact (it covers communication about the matter; unrelated communications are not barred)
- Reading Rule 4.3 to require the lawyer to actually give legal advice to an unrepresented person (the rule prohibits giving advice other than to obtain counsel where interests conflict)
- Believing 4.4(b) requires return or destruction of inadvertently sent material (the Model Rule requires only prompt notice; some states add more)

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, 4.4; ABA Formal Opinion 11-461 (communicating with constituents); ABA Formal Opinion 06-442 (metadata).

## Chapter 8: Law Firms, Supervision, Different Roles
**Core questions:** When does a partner own a subordinate's misconduct, and when does a supervisory silence become its own violation?

**Key concepts**
- Responsibilities of partners, managers, and supervisory lawyers (Rule 5.1): reasonable measures to ensure conformity to the Rules; direct responsibility for ordering, ratifying, or knowingly failing to mitigate
- Responsibilities of subordinate lawyers (Rule 5.2): bound by Rules even if acting on direction; safe harbor only for arguable questions of professional duty resolved by supervisor
- Responsibilities for nonlawyer assistants (Rule 5.3)
- Professional independence (Rule 5.4): no fee-sharing with nonlawyers (narrow exceptions for retirement, court-awarded fees to nonprofit); no nonlawyer ownership/direction
- Unauthorized practice (Rule 5.5)
- Restrictions on right to practice (Rule 5.6): no partnership/employment agreements restricting practice post-departure (except retirement benefits); no settlement agreements restricting plaintiff's lawyer from future practice
- Different roles: advisor (Rule 2.1) — independent professional judgment, candid advice; evaluator (Rule 2.3); third-party neutral (Rule 2.4)

**Applied skills**
- Map a supervisory-lawyer scenario to 5.1(a) (firm-wide measures), 5.1(b) (direct supervisor), 5.1(c) (direct responsibility)
- Recognize a 5.6 violation in a settlement term that conditions payment on the plaintiff's lawyer never representing similar plaintiffs

**Common traps**
- Treating 5.2 as a "just following orders" safe harbor in all cases (it applies only when the question of duty is arguable)
- Failing to spot 5.4 violations in alternative business structures (most U.S. jurisdictions still prohibit nonlawyer ownership; Arizona and D.C. are notable exceptions)
- Believing 5.6 only restricts firm noncompetes (it also reaches settlement agreements)

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 2.1, 2.3, 2.4, 5.1, 5.2, 5.3, 5.4, 5.5, 5.6.

## Chapter 9: Safekeeping Funds and Property
**Core questions:** How does a lawyer hold client and third-party money without commingling, mishandling, or being disciplined for negligent recordkeeping?

**Key concepts**
- Rule 1.15(a): hold property of clients or third persons separate from the lawyer's own; clearly identified; client trust account in the state where the office is situated (typically IOLTA); records preserved for five years
- Rule 1.15(b): permitted lawyer funds in trust only to pay bank service charges
- Rule 1.15(c): legal fees and expenses paid in advance shall be deposited into a client trust account and withdrawn only as earned/incurred
- Rule 1.15(d): prompt notice and prompt delivery of funds or property the client is entitled to receive; rendering full accounting on request
- Rule 1.15(e): where funds are in dispute, the disputed portion must be held in trust until resolved

**Applied skills**
- Recognize commingling even when the lawyer is "borrowing" client money with intent to repay (this is misappropriation, not merely commingling)
- Identify when an advance fee retainer must be held in trust (almost always) and when a true non-refundable engagement fee is permissible (jurisdiction-dependent; many disallow)

**Common traps**
- Treating "earned-upon-receipt" retainers as universally permissible (the Model Rule and many states disagree)
- Holding disputed fees in the operating account "because the client is being unreasonable" (1.15(e) requires segregation until resolved)
- Failing to render accountings under 1.15(d) on client request

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rule 1.15; ABA Formal Opinions on IOLTA; state-specific trust account rules (the MPRE tests Model Rule positions).

## Chapter 10: Communications about Legal Services
**Core questions:** What may a lawyer say in marketing, who may a lawyer contact for business, and when does a truthful statement still mislead?

**Key concepts**
- Rule 7.1: no false or misleading communication about the lawyer or the lawyer's services; communications are misleading if material omissions or unjustified expectations
- Rule 7.2 (revised in 2018): permits advertising; identifies prohibited and permitted methods of paying for recommendations; lawyer-referral service rules
- Rule 7.3 (revised 2018): "Solicitation" defined as communication initiated by or on behalf of a lawyer directed to a specific person known to need legal services; live person-to-person solicitation prohibited when a significant motive is pecuniary gain, with carve-outs for other lawyers, family/close personal/prior professional relationships, and routine business-client contexts
- Rule 7.6 (judicial appointments-related): no political contributions to obtain legal engagements
- Comparative and specialization claims; mandatory disclaimers; Bates v. State Bar (1977) constitutional baseline

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish targeted real-time solicitation (regulated) from general advertising (broadly protected)
- Identify "false or misleading" by omission (Rule 7.1 Comment) — a literally true statement that creates a misleading impression violates the rule

**Common traps**
- Reading Rule 7.3 as a flat ban on all in-person contact (it has carve-outs and only applies when pecuniary gain is a significant motive)
- Believing all superlatives violate 7.1 (the test is whether the statement is false or misleading and can be factually substantiated)
- Forgetting that paying for recommendations beyond the limited 7.2(b) categories (advertising costs, lawyer referral service charges, qualified-plan business-development fees, reciprocal nonexclusive referral agreements) is a violation

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 7.1, 7.2, 7.3, 7.6; Bates v. State Bar of Arizona; Florida Bar v. Went For It; In re Primus.

## Chapter 11: Lawyers' Duties to the Public and the Legal System
**Core questions:** What obligations does a lawyer owe outside the client relationship — to courts, pro bono service, legal-services organizations, and the public's confidence in the bar?

**Key concepts**
- Pro bono service (Rule 6.1): aspirational 50 hours per year, primarily to persons of limited means or organizations addressing their needs
- Accepting appointments (Rule 6.2): good cause required to decline a court appointment
- Membership in legal services organizations (Rule 6.3): conflicts management
- Law-reform activities affecting client interests (Rule 6.4): disclosure when client interests are materially benefited
- Limited-scope nonprofit and court-annexed programs (Rule 6.5): relaxed 1.7/1.9 conflicts checking

**Applied skills**
- Recognize when Rule 6.5 allows short-term limited-scope legal services without full conflict screening, and when it does not (the lawyer must not knowingly violate 1.7/1.9)

**Common traps**
- Treating Rule 6.1 as binding (it is aspirational, not enforceable as discipline in most jurisdictions)
- Refusing court appointments under Rule 6.2 for reasons of inconvenience rather than the enumerated good causes

**Authoritative sources:** Model Rules 6.1-6.5.

## Chapter 12: Judicial Conduct
**Core questions:** What does the Model Code of Judicial Conduct require of judges, and how do those requirements affect the lawyers who appear before them?

**Key concepts**
- Canon 1: uphold integrity and independence of the judiciary; avoid impropriety and the appearance of impropriety
- Canon 2: perform duties impartially, competently, diligently
- Canon 3: conduct extrajudicial activities to minimize the risk of conflict
- Canon 4: refrain from political and campaign activity that is inconsistent with judicial independence
- Disqualification (Rule 2.11): personal bias or knowledge; economic interest in subject matter or party; family member as party/lawyer/material witness; prior service as lawyer/material witness in the matter; campaign contributions threshold (Caperton)
- Ex parte communications (Rule 2.9): generally prohibited; narrow exceptions for scheduling, emergencies, settlement conferences with consent
- Extrajudicial activities and gifts (Rule 3.13); financial activities (Rule 3.11)
- Reporting another judge's or lawyer's misconduct (Rule 2.15)

**Applied skills**
- Recognize when a judge's prior law firm representation requires disqualification under Rule 2.11(A)(6)
- Apply Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal: due-process disqualification beyond Code requirements when probability of bias is high (e.g., outsized campaign contributions)

**Common traps**
- Treating disqualification as waivable in every case (Rule 2.11(C) permits remittal of disqualification only after disclosure; some grounds are nonwaivable)
- Allowing ex parte settlement discussions without each party's consent under 2.9(A)(4)
- Failing to spot a judge's reporting duty under 2.15 when learning of lawyer or judicial misconduct

**Authoritative sources:** ABA Model Code of Judicial Conduct (2007, as amended); Caperton v. A.T. Massey Coal Co.; 28 U.S.C. § 455 (federal disqualification statute, separately tested).

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
**Pacing.** Sixty scored questions in 120 minutes is exactly 2:00 per question. Plan 1:50 per question with a 10-minute buffer for review. Never spend more than 3:00 on any single question on the first pass — flag and return.

**Reading the call.** Underline (mentally) the precise question stem: "Is the attorney subject to discipline?" tests Model Rules violations. "Was the conduct proper?" tests whether the action was permitted, not whether it was discipline-worthy. "Subject to civil liability" tests malpractice/fiduciary-duty doctrine, which is separate. "Subject to disqualification" tests motion-practice consequences, which is separate. Mismatching the call is the most common preventable error.

**Triage.** First pass answers every question; flag only true uncertainties; second pass returns to flagged items only. Never leave a question unanswered — there is no penalty for guessing.

**Heuristics that work.** "Shall" answers usually win when the rule contains "shall." Permissive "may" answers usually win when the rule contains "may." Answers that name a specific rule (1.6, 1.7, 3.3) and a specific duty almost always beat answers that invoke "common sense" or "fairness." Answers that introduce a fact not in the stem are wrong.

**Heuristics that fail.** "Whichever feels most ethical" is not a rule. "What my firm actually does" is not a rule. Confidentiality (1.6) and privilege are not the same. Discipline (Rules) and disqualification (case law plus 3.7/1.9) are not the same.
