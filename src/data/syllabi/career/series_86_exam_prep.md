# Series 86 Exam Prep
**ID**: `series86` · **Discipline**: Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
The Series 86 is the analytical half of FINRA's Research Analyst qualification, and it tests something narrower and harder than "do you know finance." It tests whether you can take raw company and market data, verify it, turn it into a forecast someone could disagree with, and value the result with a method that fits the business — fast, under time pressure, and without the calculation slips that quietly sink scores. The communications and conduct half lives on the Series 87. This course owns the numbers: information quality, three-statement mechanics, ratio interpretation, driver-based forecasting, and valuation that triangulates rather than guesses.

The exam rewards a specific kind of discipline. Most wrong answers on the Series 86 are not knowledge gaps — they are arithmetic errors, misread question stems, the wrong valuation metric paired with the wrong value base, or a forecast that is internally inconsistent. So this syllabus is built around two parallel tracks. One builds the analytical content the outline demands, organized to the current three-function structure. The other builds exam-taking mechanics: formula fluency, calculation triage, distractor recognition, and a failed-question loop that turns every miss into a labeled, repeatable drill. The point is not to read more; it is to be reliably right on the questions you already understand and to recover the ones you don't.

By the end, a learner should be able to walk into a timed mixed set and handle whatever the exam throws: trace a transaction through all three statements without flinching, read a ratio change and say whether it is operational or accounting-driven, build a forecast from price/volume/mix drivers and stress the one assumption that moves EPS most, value a company two ways and sanity-check the answer, and triage 95 questions across four and a half hours so that time is spent where it earns points. The deliverable of the course is exam readiness measured honestly — accuracy by function, error type by frequency, and a remediation plan that closes the specific gaps that are costing marks.

## Course Design Notes
The chapters follow the analyst's actual workflow, which is also the structure of the current Series 86 outline (effective May 15, 2023): gather and judge data (Function 1, ~18 scored questions), verify and analyze it (Function 2, ~28 scored questions), then forecast and value (Function 3, ~39 scored questions — the largest and most heavily weighted block). The exam is 85 scored plus 10 unscored pretest questions (95 total), 4 hours 30 minutes, and the SIE is a co-requisite. Function 3's weight is the single most important planning fact in this course: valuation and forecasting deserve the most practice, and the early chapters exist largely to make that block answerable.

Route questions here when they test research analysis mechanics: source quality, financial statements and accounting linkages, ratios and quality of earnings, forecasting and earnings drivers, valuation methods and target-price support, and the analytical economics/industry context that feeds an estimate. Conduct, disclosures, conflicts, public appearances, supervision, and recordkeeping belong to `series87` — keep those out of scope here. Numerical examples use round figures so learners reason about mechanics, not arithmetic, and every chapter flags Common traps because on this exam, recognizing the seductive-but-wrong answer is most of the score. The course also integrates Floe's internal Series 86 assets: the study manual as reference and the curated failed-question import as active-recall and calculation practice (see Internal Resources).

## Chapter 1: Exam Map and the Research-Analyst Workflow
**Key concepts**: the three Series 86 functions and their weights (Information and Data Collection ~18, Data Verification and Analysis ~28, Valuation and Forecasting ~39), 85 scored + 10 pretest questions, 4.5-hour timing, 70% passing standard, SIE co-requisite, the Series 86 vs. Series 87 split, and the analyst's gather → verify → analyze → forecast → value → conclude loop.

**Core questions**
- Which function is a given question testing — data quality, analysis, or valuation/forecasting — and where do most of the points actually sit?
- Is this an analysis question (Series 86) or a conduct/communication question (Series 87) that won't appear on this exam?
- Given 95 questions in 270 minutes, what is the per-question time budget, and which question types should be skipped and returned to first?

**Applied skills**
- Build a study tracker keyed to the three functions, with columns for accuracy, error type, and confidence so practice data drives the plan.
- Set a timing rule (roughly 2.8 minutes per question with a reserve) and a triage protocol for multi-step calculation items.

**Common traps**
- Studying every topic to equal depth when Function 3 carries nearly half the scored weight.
- Treating pretest questions as a knowable category — you can't identify them, so answer all 95 as if they count.
- Drilling conduct and disclosure material that belongs to the Series 87, not the 86.

## Chapter 2: Function 1 — Information, Data, and Source Quality
**Key concepts**: company filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy), industry and market data, management commentary and guidance, third-party and consensus data, source reliability and independence, data recency, comparable-period and seasonality hygiene, and distinguishing fact from estimate from opinion.

**Core questions**
- Which source best supports the conclusion, and is it primary, independent, and current?
- Is this datapoint representative, or is it stale, seasonally distorted, conflicted, or cherry-picked?
- Are we comparing like with like — same period, same accounting basis, same definition of the metric?

**Applied skills**
- Build a source-quality checklist for an earnings preview: filing date, period covered, GAAP vs. adjusted basis, independence of the source, and known revisions.
- Reconcile two conflicting datapoints and decide which to trust and why.

**Common traps**
- Trusting a precise-looking number without checking the period or accounting basis it was computed on.
- Treating management's framing as data rather than as a claim to be verified against the filings.
- Comparing a trailing figure to a forward estimate, or a quarter to an annual, and calling it a trend.

## Chapter 3: Function 2 — Three-Statement Linkages and Accounting Mechanics
**Key concepts**: income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement and how they connect; net income flowing to retained earnings and to the top of the cash flow statement; accruals vs. cash; working-capital changes; depreciation and capex; deferred revenue; debt and interest; revenue-recognition basics; and how a single transaction ripples through all three statements.

**Core questions**
- How does this transaction affect all three statements, and does the balance sheet still balance after it?
- What does the cash flow statement reveal that reported earnings hides — or vice versa?
- Where is the accrual that explains the gap between net income and operating cash flow?

**Applied skills**
- Run a three-statement linkage drill: trace several transactions (a sale on credit, a capex purchase, a debt drawdown, depreciation) end to end with journal-style notes.
- Build operating cash flow from net income via the indirect method and explain each adjustment.

**Common traps**
- Forgetting that depreciation is a non-cash expense added back in operating cash flow but still hits the income statement.
- Letting the balance sheet fail to balance after a transaction and not noticing — always check both sides.
- Confusing the cash impact of capex (investing, full amount up front) with its income impact (depreciation, spread over years).

## Chapter 4: Function 2 — Ratios, Margins, and Quality of Earnings
**Key concepts**: gross/operating/net margins, ROE and ROIC and the DuPont decomposition, leverage and coverage, liquidity (current, quick), turnover ratios (inventory, receivables, asset), days metrics (DSO, DIO, DPO), and quality-of-earnings flags where accruals or one-offs flatter the headline.

**Core questions**
- Which ratio explains the change, and does it trace to operations, mix, leverage, or accounting?
- Is an earnings improvement real, or is it a lower tax rate, a one-time gain, or aggressive accrual recognition?
- What does the ratio dashboard reveal as the company's actual red flag this period?

**Applied skills**
- Build a ratio dashboard for a fictional company and write interpretation notes that separate operational from accounting-driven changes.
- Run a DuPont decomposition to locate whether an ROE move came from margin, turnover, or leverage.

**Common traps**
- Reading a rising margin as good news when it was driven by a one-time gain or a deferred cost.
- Mixing enterprise and equity perspectives — pairing an EV-based numerator with an equity-based denominator.
- Treating receivables or inventory growth that outpaces sales as benign rather than as a quality-of-earnings warning.

## Chapter 5: Function 3 — Forecasting and Earnings Drivers
**Key concepts**: driver-based revenue builds (price × volume, mix, units × ASP, cohort/retention), cost structure and operating leverage, incremental margins, the bridge from revenue to operating income to EPS, share count, buybacks and dilution, tax rate, and positioning estimates relative to consensus.

**Core questions**
- Which single assumption moves EPS the most, and how sensitive is the bottom line to it?
- If margin compresses or a driver slows, is the forecast still internally consistent?
- Where do these estimates differ from consensus, and what fact justifies the gap?

**Applied skills**
- Build a driver-based forecast (revenue from price/volume/mix, costs from operating-leverage logic) down to EPS and free cash flow.
- Produce a sensitivity table showing EPS under high/base/low cases for the most important driver.

**Common traps**
- Painting one growth rate across every line instead of building revenue from drivers.
- Forecasting margin expansion with no operating-leverage or mix rationale to support it.
- Forgetting that buybacks, dilution, and the tax rate move EPS even when operating estimates don't change.

## Chapter 6: Function 3 — Valuation Methods and Target-Price Support
**Key concepts**: relative valuation (P/E, EV/EBITDA, EV/Sales, PEG) and matching the metric to enterprise vs. equity value; the DCF (free cash flow, WACC, terminal value, discounting); precedent transactions and comparable companies; the multiple × estimate target-price build; and triangulating methods rather than trusting one.

**Core questions**
- Which valuation method fits this business, and what justifies the multiple — growth, returns, or just the historical average?
- What assumption drives the DCF, and how much does the answer move if WACC or the terminal growth rate shifts?
- How is the target price actually built, and does it match the business model and the forecast?

**Applied skills**
- Build a target price as forward estimate × a justified multiple, and cross-check it against a simple DCF.
- Write a valuation summary that triangulates comps and DCF and explains any divergence.

**Common traps**
- Pairing an enterprise-value metric (EV/EBITDA) with a post-interest, equity-level figure like net income.
- Comparing P/E across companies with very different leverage or growth and calling one "cheap."
- Letting terminal value dominate the DCF, or reverse-engineering a multiple to hit a target you already wanted.

## Chapter 7: Function 2 — Economic, Market, and Industry Analysis
**Key concepts**: supply and demand, interest rates, inflation, FX, the business and credit cycles, cyclicality vs. defensiveness, industry structure (suppliers, buyers, substitutes, rivalry, regulation), competitive advantage, and how a macro or industry variable feeds back into the estimate.

**Core questions**
- Which macro or industry variable actually matters to this company's forecast?
- Is the company cyclical or defensive, and how should that change the earnings path and the multiple?
- Which industry driver, if it turns, changes the thesis rather than just the noise?

**Applied skills**
- Build an industry driver map linking macro and structural factors to catalysts and risks for a specific company.
- Translate one macro change (a rate move, an FX swing) into its effect on revenue, margin, and valuation.

**Common traps**
- Reciting macro context that never connects back to a number in the model.
- Treating every company as cyclical (or every one as defensive) instead of reading the actual revenue sensitivity.
- Confusing a strong industry with a strong company — sector tailwinds don't guarantee the firm captures them.

## Chapter 8: Function 3 — Research Logic and the Investment Conclusion
**Key concepts**: the thesis, variant perception, catalysts and timing, risk factors, estimate revisions, how the forecast and valuation support a target price, and the chain that ties evidence to estimate to value to recommendation.

**Core questions**
- What must be true for this recommendation to be right, and what single fact would disconfirm it?
- Does the valuation conclusion follow from the forecast, or were they built backward from a desired call?
- Which risk is genuinely material to the thesis versus boilerplate?

**Applied skills**
- Write a one-page equity note: thesis, variant view, valuation/target, catalysts, risks, and conclusion.
- Trace one piece of new evidence through to its effect on the estimate, the value, and the rating.

**Common traps**
- Stating a "variant perception" that is actually consensus dressed up.
- Building a conclusion the model doesn't support, or a model the conclusion doesn't follow from.
- Burying the call so the thesis, catalyst, and risk aren't visible in the first read.

## Chapter 9: Exam Execution, Calculation Triage, and the Failed-Question Loop
**Key concepts**: formula fluency, calculation triage, distractor and trap-answer recognition, time management across 95 questions, the skip-and-return protocol, active recall, timed mixed sets, and a structured error-review loop using Floe's imported failed-question set.

**Core questions**
- Was this miss conceptual, arithmetic, a misread stem, or a timing failure — and what is the fix?
- Which formulas still need repetition until they are automatic under pressure?
- What clue did the question stem give that pointed to the right method or the trap answer?

**Applied skills**
- Run timed mixed sets weighted to the function distribution (more Function 3), then label every error by type.
- Maintain a failed-question log built on the imported Floe review questions: re-derive the answer, name the error class, and schedule the re-drill.

**Common traps**
- Logging that a question was missed without diagnosing why, so the same error recurs.
- Spending unlimited time on one hard calculation and starving easy points later in the section.
- Practicing untimed and being surprised by the clock on exam day.

## Capstone: Timed Mock and Remediation Plan
Complete a full timed mixed Series 86 practice set weighted to the real function distribution, then deliver a readiness package:
- a scored result broken down by function (Information and Data, Verification and Analysis, Valuation and Forecasting) and by error type (conceptual, arithmetic, misread, timing);
- a three-statement linkage drill and a ratio/quality-of-earnings read on a provided company packet;
- a driver-based forecast with a sensitivity table and a two-method valuation (multiple × estimate cross-checked against a DCF);
- a one-page mini equity note built from the packet (thesis, valuation, catalysts, risks, conclusion);
- a correction log mapping each miss to its error class; and
- a prioritized list of the next three study drills, weighted toward the heaviest-scoring and weakest-accuracy areas.

## Assessment Ideas
- Three-statement linkage drills graded on balance integrity and journal-level precision.
- Ratio and quality-of-earnings exercises graded on operational-vs-accounting interpretation.
- Forecasting builds graded on driver logic and sensitivity reasoning.
- Valuation exercises graded on method selection, enterprise-vs-equity discipline, and triangulation.
- Timed mixed sets graded on accuracy by function and time management.
- Capstone graded on timed accuracy, error diagnosis, and the quality of the remediation plan.
