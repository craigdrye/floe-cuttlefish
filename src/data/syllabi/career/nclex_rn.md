# NCLEX-RN: The Safe-Practice Reflex
**ID**: `nclexRn` · **Discipline**: Nursing

## Course Aim
NCLEX-RN is not a memory test wearing scrubs. It is a licensure decision rendered as adaptive items, and it asks one quiet question over and over: would this candidate keep a patient safe right now? The exam rewards the nurse who hears "the potassium is 6.8" and reaches for calcium gluconate before the dysrhythmia arrives — who knows when to assess and when to intervene, when an LPN can take the dressing change and when the RN must stay, when to call the rapid response team five minutes before the code is called. Floe trains those reflexes alongside the content, because content without judgment is just trivia, and judgment without content is hand-waving.

What you are building here is the clinical mind of a brand-new RN ready for entry-level practice. Brand new means imperfect; entry-level means safe. The bar is not "diagnose this rare disease" or "interpret this exotic ABG"; the bar is "did you protect airway, breathing, circulation, and patient dignity in that order, with the least escalation that still does the job?" Every chapter is shaped around that bar. The voice is reassuring on purpose. This exam is survivable. The patients who will meet you on the other side of it deserve a calm nurse, not a panicked one.

The Clinical Judgment Measurement Model is the spine of the Next Generation NCLEX, and it is also the spine of this syllabus. You will practice recognizing cues (which finding in this chart actually matters?), analyzing cues (does this cluster point to fluid overload, sepsis, or anaphylaxis?), prioritizing hypotheses, generating solutions, taking action, and evaluating outcomes. Every question is an opportunity to rehearse that loop. By exam day, the loop should feel less like a checklist and more like a habit.

A word on the test itself before the syllabus begins: the NCLEX-RN computerized adaptive test asks between 75 and 145 items over up to five hours, calibrating difficulty to your demonstrated ability and stopping the moment the algorithm is statistically confident — passing or failing — within a small margin of error. You cannot tell from the screen whether you are winning or losing, and that is the design. Trust the algorithm, answer each item as if it matters (because it does), and let the machine do the deciding. Floe will get you ready for that moment.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test the NCSBN 2023 Test Plan: prioritization and delegation, safe medication administration, infection control, clinical assessment across the lifespan, therapeutic communication, and the six-step Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (recognize cues, analyze cues, prioritize hypotheses, generate solutions, take action, evaluate outcomes). The voice should make a high-stakes exam feel survivable without softening the patient-safety bar.

This course differs from a straight content-review app on three axes. First, **priority beats recall**: knowing that magnesium toxicity causes loss of deep tendon reflexes is necessary but not sufficient; the exam wants to know whether you would check reflexes before the next infusion. Second, **delegation logic is fair game on every item**: every shift has tasks that belong to the RN, tasks for the LPN/LVN, and tasks for the UAP, and the test rewards candidates who hold initial assessment, IV push, blood, and teaching at the RN level while letting stable ADLs, vitals on stable patients, and ambulation flow to the UAP. Third, **safety outranks comfort, and acute outranks chronic**: when in doubt, the unstable patient with an ABC threat is the first patient you see, even if a more stable patient is louder.

Floe will pull NCLEX-RN questions when the candidate is preparing for licensure, when the topic is squarely nursing scope of practice, or when the prompt invokes priority/delegation/safety framing. Adjacent banks (NCLEX-PN for practical-nurse scope, USMLE/PANCE for the provider role, hospital floor ops for non-licensure operational judgment) carry their own logic; mixing them dilutes the licensure signal.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The NCSBN 2023 Test Plan publishes ranges, not promises. Allocate effort by blueprint weight, by error-log frequency, and by your personal weak organ systems and weak cognitive steps.

| Client Needs Category | Exam Weight | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| Management of Care | 15-21% | Highest single slice; drill delegation, scope, advocacy, informed consent, case management |
| Safety and Infection Control | 10-16% | Drill isolation precautions, error prevention, equipment safety, emergency response |
| Health Promotion and Maintenance | 6-12% | Span the life cycle: prenatal, peds, adult, geriatric screening, immunizations |
| Psychosocial Integrity | 6-12% | Therapeutic communication, mental health, grief, end-of-life, abuse, substance withdrawal |
| Basic Care and Comfort | 6-12% | ADLs, mobility, nutrition, elimination, rest, non-pharm pain, palliation |
| Pharmacological and Parenteral Therapies | 13-19% | The largest medication slice on any nursing exam; drill calculations, reactions, antidotes |
| Reduction of Risk Potential | 9-15% | Lab values, diagnostic prep, complication recognition, monitoring after procedures |
| Physiological Adaptation | 11-17% | Acute, chronic, and emergent pathophysiology with high-acuity interventions |

**Test format (April 2023 onward).** The Next Generation NCLEX integrates traditional multiple-choice items with six new NGN item types: extended drag-and-drop (rank or order interventions), extended multiple response (Select N of M with partial credit), matrix/grid (rate each option as expected/unexpected or appropriate/contraindicated), highlight in text (click the relevant findings in a chart note), Select All That Apply (legacy SATA with no partial credit), and bow-tie (one action in the middle, supporting cues on the left, anticipated outcomes on the right). NGN case studies present a chart of cues that evolves across six linked questions, one per step of the Clinical Judgment Measurement Model. Stand-alone items still appear and still matter.

**The CAT mechanics, in plain language.** The first item is calibrated below the passing standard; if you answer correctly, the next item is harder, if not, easier. The algorithm scores continuously and stops when your ability estimate is reliably above or below the passing standard, or at 145 items, or at the 5-hour limit. The number of items you get is not a signal — short tests pass and short tests fail; long tests pass and long tests fail. You cannot skip items and you cannot return to earlier items.

**Study cadence.** Six to twelve weeks of focused preparation, four to six hours of work per day, with roughly 75 mixed-format items completed daily and full written rationales on every miss. Use UWorld (or Saunders/Lippincott) as the spine, not the supplement. Maintain a miss matrix tagged by client need, organ system, and CJMM step. Reading without questions is wallpaper; questions without rationale review is gambling. In the last two weeks shift toward two timed 85-item simulations per week with a strict break protocol that mimics test day.

## Chapter 1: Prioritization, Delegation, and Scope of Practice
**Core questions:** Of these four patients, who do I assess first? Which task can I hand to the LPN/LVN or UAP, and which must stay with the RN? Where does the chain of command go when a provider's order is unsafe?

**Key concepts**
- ABCs, then Maslow physiological > safety/security > love and belonging > esteem > self-actualization
- Stable vs unstable; acute vs chronic; expected vs unexpected; real vs potential
- RN-only tasks: initial assessment, teaching, IV push, blood administration, unstable patients, first-time sterile dressing change, evaluation of outcomes
- LPN/LVN scope: stable patients, reinforce teaching, oral and most IM/SC meds, routine sterile dressing changes, ostomy care, foley insertion
- UAP scope: ADLs, vital signs on stable patients, ambulation, intake/output, repositioning, feeding stable patients
- Five rights of delegation: right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction/communication, right supervision/evaluation
- Chain of command, informed consent witnessing, advance directives, against-medical-advice discharge

**Applied clinical skills**
- Triage a four-patient assignment in under 90 seconds
- Match each task on a shift to the lowest competent license level
- Document a refusal of care or AMA discharge defensibly

**Common traps**
- "Assess vs intervene": when both options appear, assess wins UNLESS airway/breathing/circulation/safety is already acutely compromised — then intervene first (e.g., apnea, active bleeding, anaphylaxis).
- ABCs always outrank psychosocial concerns; do not pick "explore the patient's feelings" over a low SpO2.
- A "stable" patient with a normal-range vital sign is rarely the first one to see; an "unexpected" finding usually beats an "expected" one.
- UAP can do stable, expected, and unchanging; the moment a task involves judgment, education, or initial data, it returns to the RN.

**Authoritative sources**
NCSBN 2023 Test Plan; ANA Scope and Standards of Practice; state Nurse Practice Acts; The Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals; Saunders Comprehensive Review.

## Chapter 2: Safety, Infection Control, and Error Prevention
**Core questions:** Which precaution does this organism require? What is the single most effective intervention to prevent this hospital-acquired infection? When does this near-miss require a safety event report?

**Key concepts**
- Standard precautions for all; contact (C. difficile — soap and water, not alcohol; MRSA, VRE, scabies, RSV); droplet (influenza, pertussis, meningococcus, mumps, rubella); airborne (TB, measles, varicella — negative-pressure room and N95)
- Hand hygiene is the highest-yield single intervention; PPE donning order gown-mask-goggles-gloves; doffing gloves-goggles-gown-mask
- National Patient Safety Goals: two identifiers, time-out, fall risk assessment, restraint policy (least-restrictive, time-limited, q15 min monitoring, q2h release and reassessment), fire safety (RACE — Rescue, Alarm, Confine, Extinguish; PASS — Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep)
- Just culture, sentinel events, root cause analysis, incident reporting (factual, objective, not in the medical record)
- Equipment safety: oxygen and electrical hazards, MRI screening, latex allergy precautions

**Applied clinical skills**
- Choose the correct isolation room and PPE for an undifferentiated febrile rash
- Reduce CAUTI, CLABSI, VAP, and SSI risk using bundle interventions (head-of-bed >30°, daily sedation interruption, chlorhexidine oral care, prompt foley removal)
- Respond to fire, code, active shooter, and disaster scenarios in priority order

**Common traps**
- C. difficile requires soap and water, not alcohol-based hand rub; alcohol does not kill spores.
- Restraints require a provider order renewed every 24 hours (medical) or 4/2/1 hours (behavioral, by age), continuous monitoring, and documented attempts at less-restrictive measures.
- A near-miss still requires an incident report; the report goes to risk management, not into the chart.
- Triage in mass-casualty events uses START: Red immediate (life-threat, salvageable), Yellow delayed, Green minor/walking, Black expectant.

**Authoritative sources**
CDC Isolation Precautions Guidelines; The Joint Commission NPSGs; OSHA bloodborne pathogen standard; NCSBN test plan; AHRQ patient safety primers.

## Chapter 3: Pharmacology and Parenteral Therapies
**Core questions:** Is the dose safe? What labs do I check before giving this drug? Which adverse effect requires holding the next dose and calling the provider?

**Key concepts**
- High-alert medications (ISMP list): insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, neuromuscular blockers, concentrated electrolytes (KCl, hypertonic saline), chemotherapy, magnesium sulfate
- Therapeutic drug levels: digoxin 0.5-2.0 ng/mL, lithium 0.6-1.2 mEq/L (acute up to 1.5), phenytoin 10-20 mcg/mL, vancomycin trough 15-20 mcg/mL, theophylline 10-20 mcg/mL
- Insulin types: rapid (lispro, aspart, glulisine — onset 5-15 min, peak 1-2 h); short (regular — onset 30 min, peak 2-4 h); intermediate (NPH — onset 1-2 h, peak 4-12 h); long (glargine, detemir, degludec — no peak, do NOT mix with other insulins)
- Anticoagulation: heparin aPTT 1.5-2.5× control (protamine sulfate reverses); warfarin INR 2-3 (2.5-3.5 for valves; vitamin K reverses; narrow therapeutic window; consistent vitamin K intake matters more than avoidance); DOACs — fewer monitoring needs, idarucizumab reverses dabigatran, andexanet alfa reverses factor Xa inhibitors; HIT appears 5-10 days in with platelet drop >50%
- Look-alike/sound-alike pairs (hydralazine vs hydroxyzine, heparin vs Hespan, glipizide vs glyburide, celecoxib vs citalopram)
- Pediatric weight-based dosing, geriatric Beers criteria, pregnancy categories, narrow therapeutic index drugs
- IV compatibility, infiltration vs extravasation, blood transfusion reactions (acute hemolytic, febrile non-hemolytic, allergic, TRALI, TACO)
- TPN: central line only, slow titration on and off, glucose q4-6h, never abruptly stop (rebound hypoglycemia — hang D10W if bag runs out)

**Applied clinical skills**
- Calculate dopamine, heparin, and insulin drip rates without panic
- Recognize anaphylaxis, serotonin syndrome, NMS, and acute dystonia by cluster
- Teach a patient warfarin, metformin, or albuterol home use in plain language

**Common traps**
- Digoxin toxicity is potentiated by hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and renal impairment; hold for HR <60 in adults, <70 in children, <90 in infants; classic toxicity = bradycardia + GI symptoms + yellow halos.
- Lithium toxicity: tremor (coarse, not the fine baseline tremor), ataxia, GI upset, confusion; dehydration and low-sodium diets raise levels. Avoid NSAIDs and thiazides with lithium.
- Magnesium sulfate toxicity: first sign is loss of deep tendon reflexes; then respiratory depression (RR <12); then cardiac arrest. Antidote: calcium gluconate IV. Therapeutic range for preeclampsia 4-7 mEq/L.
- Glargine and detemir cannot be mixed with any other insulin in the same syringe. When mixing regular and NPH, draw clear (regular) before cloudy (NPH).

**Authoritative sources**
Lehne/Burchum Pharmacology for Nursing Care; ISMP high-alert medication list; UWorld pharm rationales; Saunders pharm appendix; FDA Black Box warnings.

## Chapter 4: Cardiovascular and Hematologic Care
**Core questions:** Is this rhythm life-threatening? Which lab swing changes my next move? Who needs the code cart and who needs reassurance?

**Key concepts**
- ACS spectrum: STEMI (door-to-balloon 90 min), NSTEMI, unstable angina; troponin rises 3-6 h, peaks 12-24 h, normalizes 7-14 days; ECG localization — anterior V1-V4 (LAD), inferior II/III/aVF (RCA, watch for bradycardia and avoid nitrates if RV involvement), lateral I/aVL/V5-V6 (LCX)
- Heart failure: HFrEF (EF ≤40%) vs HFpEF (EF ≥50%); GDMT ladder ARNI > ACEi/ARB + beta-blocker + diuretic + SGLT2 inhibitor + MRA (spironolactone); daily weights, low-sodium diet, fluid restriction in advanced disease
- Dysrhythmias: atrial fibrillation (rate vs rhythm control; CHA2DS2-VASc for anticoagulation; HAS-BLED for bleeding risk); SVT (vagal then adenosine); VT with pulse (amiodarone or cardioversion); pulseless VT/VF (defibrillate); asystole/PEA (CPR + epinephrine, no shock); heart blocks (atropine for symptomatic bradycardia, then pacing)
- Anticoagulation monitoring as above (Chapter 3); HIT and warfarin-induced skin necrosis are board favorites
- Anemia by MCV: microcytic (iron deficiency, thalassemia, anemia of chronic disease, lead); normocytic (acute blood loss, hemolysis, chronic disease, renal); macrocytic (B12, folate, hypothyroid, alcohol use, drugs)
- Sickle cell crisis (hydration, oxygen, analgesia; watch for acute chest syndrome); DIC (lab pattern — high D-dimer, low platelets, low fibrinogen, prolonged PT/aPTT); thrombocytopenia thresholds for bleeding and transfusion
- Hyperkalemia ECG progression: peaked T waves → wide QRS → loss of P → sine wave → cardiac arrest. Treatment order: calcium gluconate (stabilize membrane) → insulin/D50 (shift) → albuterol (shift) → loop diuretic, kayexalate, or dialysis (remove)
- Hypokalemia ECG: flat or inverted T, U waves, ST depression; replace cautiously, never IV push KCl (rate ≤10 mEq/h peripheral, ≤20 mEq/h central with telemetry)

**Applied clinical skills**
- Read a six-second rhythm strip and pick the next intervention
- Recognize cardiogenic vs hypovolemic vs distributive vs obstructive shock from vitals
- Manage chest pain using MONA-B sequencing where appropriate (M = morphine if not relieved, O = oxygen if hypoxic, N = nitrates if no RV infarct, A = aspirin chewed, B = beta-blocker after stabilization)

**Common traps**
- In inferior MI with right ventricular involvement, nitroglycerin can precipitate profound hypotension; volume resuscitate first.
- Calcium gluconate does NOT lower the potassium level; it protects the heart while other agents work. The order matters.
- Atropine is for symptomatic bradycardia and AV blocks above the level of the AV node; it is ineffective and may worsen Mobitz II and third-degree blocks — go straight to transcutaneous pacing.
- HIT is a clotting problem, not a bleeding problem; the platelet drop is paradoxical and demands stopping all heparin (including flushes) and starting argatroban or bivalirudin.

**Authoritative sources**
ACLS Provider Manual (current cycle); AHA/ACC guidelines; UWorld cardiology; Saunders cardiovascular review; NCSBN test plan.

## Chapter 5: Respiratory, Renal, and Acid-Base
**Core questions:** Is this hypoxia or hypercapnia? Which ABG picture is this, and is compensation underway? When does this AKI need dialysis now?

**Key concepts**
- COPD vs asthma: COPD = chronic obstruction, irreversible, with chronic bronchitis ("blue bloater," cough/sputum/cyanosis) and emphysema ("pink puffer," barrel chest, hyperinflation) phenotypes; GOLD stages by FEV1; treatment LABA + LAMA for moderate-severe, ICS only in select phenotype/exacerbators. Asthma = reversible obstruction, ICS is first-line controller plus SABA reliever
- Pneumonia, PE, ARDS, pneumothorax, chest tube management; tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis — tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds, hypotension — and treated with needle decompression before imaging
- Oxygen delivery: nasal cannula 1-6 L (24-44% FiO2), simple mask 6-10 L, non-rebreather 10-15 L (up to 90+% FiO2), venturi for COPD precision; high-flow nasal cannula; BiPAP for COPD exacerbation and CHF; CPAP for OSA
- ABG interpretation: pH (<7.35 acidosis, >7.45 alkalosis), PaCO2 (respiratory if direction opposes pH), HCO3 (metabolic if direction matches pH); compensation if the second buffer is also abnormal in the direction that would normalize pH
- AKI vs CKD: prerenal (BUN:Cr >20:1, urine Na <20, FENa <1%), intrinsic (muddy brown casts in ATN, eosinophiluria in AIN), postrenal (obstruction); CKD stages by eGFR; dialysis indications "AEIOU" — Acidosis refractory, Electrolytes (especially K+), Ingestions, Overload, Uremia
- Fluid and electrolyte derangements: hyponatremia (correct slowly to avoid central pontine myelinolysis), hypernatremia (correct slowly to avoid cerebral edema); calcium and magnesium with the ECG/neuro picture
- Acute COPD exacerbation: target SpO2 88-92% — over-oxygenation can blunt the hypoxic drive in retainers and worsen hypercapnia

**Applied clinical skills**
- Choose appropriate oxygen device and target SpO2 for COPD vs non-COPD
- Identify tension pneumothorax in 30 seconds and act before imaging
- Read a basic metabolic panel and rank the three things to do first

**Common traps**
- A chest tube with continuous bubbling in the water-seal chamber suggests an air leak; absent tidaling suggests obstruction or lung re-expansion. Never clamp a chest tube routinely — tension pneumothorax risk.
- For COPD on home oxygen, do not crank to 6 L because they "look bad"; titrate to 88-92% SpO2.
- IV potassium is never pushed; concentrated KCl is a high-alert medication and one of the most-cited sentinel events.
- Hypocalcemia and hypomagnesemia both cause Chvostek/Trousseau signs and tetany; check magnesium when calcium replacement isn't working.

**Authoritative sources**
GOLD COPD report; GINA asthma guidelines; KDIGO AKI/CKD; Lippincott Manual of Nursing Practice; Saunders.

## Chapter 6: Maternal-Newborn and Pediatric Care
**Core questions:** Is this fetal heart strip reassuring? Which developmental milestone matters here? Which immunization is missing today?

**Key concepts**
- Stages of labor (1st: latent 0-6 cm, active 6-10 cm; 2nd: pushing; 3rd: placenta; 4th: first 1-4 h postpartum); contraction patterns; fetal monitoring VEAL CHOP (Variable–Cord compression, Early–Head compression, Acceleration–OK, Late–Placental insufficiency)
- Late decelerations are non-reassuring: turn mother to left lateral, increase IV fluids, give O2 by non-rebreather, stop oxytocin, notify provider
- Preeclampsia (>140/90 + proteinuria after 20 weeks); severe features include BP >160/110, headache, visual disturbances, RUQ pain, thrombocytopenia, elevated liver enzymes (HELLP). Magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis (4-7 mEq/L); antidote calcium gluconate
- Postpartum hemorrhage order: fundal massage first → empty bladder → oxytocin (Pitocin) → methylergonovine (Methergine — CONTRAINDICATED in HTN/preeclampsia) → carboprost (Hemabate — CONTRAINDICATED in asthma) → misoprostol → uterine balloon → surgery
- Tocolytics for preterm labor: magnesium first-line (also for fetal neuroprotection <32 weeks), then terbutaline, nifedipine, indomethacin (<32 wks only)
- Pediatric growth and milestones: 2 mo social smile, 4 mo rolls over, 6 mo sits with support, 9 mo crawls/pulls to stand, 12 mo first steps and first words, 15-18 mo walks well, 2 y 2-word sentences and runs, 3 y tricycle, 4 y hops on one foot, 5 y skips
- Common pediatric illnesses: croup (barking cough, racemic epinephrine), epiglottitis (drooling, tripod — DO NOT examine throat, do not agitate), RSV (supportive, possible ribavirin in severe), Kawasaki (fever ≥5 days + 4/5 criteria, treat IVIG + aspirin), sickle cell crisis, intussusception ("currant jelly" stools)
- Pediatric safety: SIDS prevention (supine sleep, no soft bedding, room-sharing not bed-sharing, no smoke exposure), car seats by age/weight, lead screening, accidental ingestion poison control
- Immunization basics (live vaccines avoided in pregnancy and severe immunocompromise: MMR, varicella, rotavirus, intranasal flu, yellow fever)

**Applied clinical skills**
- Interpret category I, II, III fetal heart tracings and act
- Identify postpartum hemorrhage and execute the medication ladder
- De-escalate an agitated parent without losing the child's safety thread

**Common traps**
- Methergine: avoid in HTN/preeclampsia. Carboprost: avoid in asthma. Memorize the contraindications, because the postpartum hemorrhage item often hinges on them.
- Magnesium sulfate first sign of toxicity is loss of patellar reflexes, not respiratory depression — that comes next.
- A bulging fontanelle in an infant is a sign of increased ICP (meningitis, hydrocephalus, abuse); a sunken fontanelle is a sign of dehydration.
- For croup, racemic epinephrine works fast but the child must be observed for rebound; corticosteroids (dexamethasone) prevent the rebound.

**Authoritative sources**
ACOG and AWHONN practice bulletins; AAP Bright Futures; CDC immunization schedule; Saunders maternal-newborn and peds.

## Chapter 7: Psychosocial Integrity and Mental Health
**Core questions:** Is this patient at imminent risk to self or others? Which therapeutic communication move opens the door instead of closing it? When is this side effect dangerous, not just annoying?

**Key concepts**
- Suicide risk assessment (Columbia Protocol; SAD PERSONS in older curricula): plan + means + intent = high risk → continuous observation, remove means, no-suicide contracts are NOT protective; safety is environmental
- Mood disorders: MDD (SIG E CAPS — Sleep, Interest, Guilt, Energy, Concentration, Appetite, Psychomotor, Suicide; ≥5 for ≥2 weeks); bipolar I (mania) vs II (hypomania + depression); SSRIs take 2-6 weeks; never start an SSRI in unrecognized bipolar (precipitates mania)
- Anxiety, OCD, PTSD: SSRIs/SNRIs first-line; benzodiazepines for short-term acute use only
- Schizophrenia: positive (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech) vs negative (flat affect, avolition, anhedonia); atypicals (risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole) preferred; clozapine for refractory (agranulocytosis — weekly CBC initially)
- Antidepressant lethality in overdose: MAOIs and TCAs > SSRIs (SSRIs the safest in overdose, hence first-line)
- Serotonin syndrome triad: cognitive (confusion, agitation), autonomic (hyperthermia, tachycardia, diaphoresis), neuromuscular (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor); discontinue serotonergic agents, supportive care, cyproheptadine in severe
- Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (from antipsychotics): high fever, lead-pipe rigidity, autonomic instability, altered mental status, elevated CK; stop the antipsychotic, cool, dantrolene/bromocriptine
- Eating disorders: refeeding syndrome (phosphate, K+, Mg fall as carbohydrate intake resumes — start slow, replete); bulimia/binge purge — dental erosion, Russell sign, hypokalemic alkalosis
- Substance withdrawal: alcohol (CIWA-Ar scoring, benzodiazepines, thiamine BEFORE glucose to prevent Wernicke), opioids (clonidine, buprenorphine, methadone; not life-threatening but miserable), benzodiazepines (life-threatening seizures, taper slowly)
- Therapeutic communication: use reflection, open-ended questions, silence; AVOID false reassurance ("everything will be fine"), "why" questions, advice-giving, minimizing, changing the subject, asking closed yes/no questions on emotional topics

**Applied clinical skills**
- Use a structured suicide assessment and translate it into a safety plan
- Recognize alcohol, opioid, and benzodiazepine withdrawal by timing and cluster
- De-escalate an agitated patient using least-restrictive intervention first; restraint is last

**Common traps**
- Lithium narrow therapeutic window 0.6-1.2 mEq/L: dehydration, low-salt diets, NSAIDs, and ACE inhibitors raise levels. SIADH-like protection comes from steady sodium intake. Lithium can also cause hypothyroidism.
- Wernicke's encephalopathy in alcohol withdrawal needs thiamine BEFORE any glucose-containing fluid — glucose first will precipitate or worsen Wernicke's.
- Acute dystonia (oculogyric crisis, torticollis) within hours of an antipsychotic dose = IM diphenhydramine or benztropine, not "wait and reassess."
- "Why" questions sound caring but feel accusatory to many patients; reframe to "tell me more about…"

**Authoritative sources**
DSM-5-TR; APA practice guidelines; SAMHSA TIP series; Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale; Saunders psychosocial.

## Chapter 8: Reduction of Risk and Physiological Adaptation
**Core questions:** What lab or vital change comes first when this complication is forming? Which post-op finding crosses the threshold from expected to emergent? Which patient on this floor decompensates next if I don't intervene?

**Key concepts**
- Pre- and post-procedure care: NPO times, anticoagulation hold windows, sedation monitoring, post-op bleeding/airway/pain/output assessment
- Recognizing early sepsis: temperature instability + tachycardia + hypotension + altered mental status; lactate >2 — aggressive fluid resuscitation (30 mL/kg crystalloid in first 3 h), blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics, broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour
- Diabetic emergencies: DKA (T1 typical, gap acidosis, ketones, glucose usually 300-800) — IV fluids first, then insulin drip, replace K+ before insulin if K <3.3; HHS (T2 typical, glucose >600, profound dehydration, minimal ketones) — fluids are even more central
- Common post-op complications timeline: atelectasis (first 24 h), pneumonia (48-72 h), wound infection (3-7 d), DVT/PE (5-7 d)
- Increased ICP: Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations); elevate HOB 30°, avoid hip flexion and neck rotation, mannitol, hyperventilation only as bridge
- Burn management: rule of nines, Parkland formula (4 mL × kg × %TBSA, half in first 8 h from time of injury), inhalation injury watch, escharotomy for circumferential
- Compartment syndrome: pain out of proportion + pain with passive stretch + paresthesia → fasciotomy (the 6 P's; pulses are LATE)

**Applied clinical skills**
- Identify the first failing organ in an evolving sepsis case and act in the first hour
- Run the DKA management order without flipping insulin and potassium
- Recognize a deteriorating neuro exam before the Cushing's triad appears

**Common traps**
- In DKA, do NOT start insulin if K+ is <3.3 — insulin will drive K+ further down and precipitate dysrhythmia. Replace K+ first.
- Lactated Ringer's is contraindicated in liver failure with hyperkalemia; choose NS instead.
- Cushing's triad is a LATE sign of increased ICP; the earliest sign is a decline in level of consciousness.
- A patient asking "am I dying?" or becoming suddenly anxious post-op is a classic stem for early PE; check SpO2, vitals, and order a CTA.

**Authoritative sources**
Surviving Sepsis Campaign; ADA standards; Brain Trauma Foundation guidelines; American Burn Association; Saunders.

## Chapter 9: Health Promotion, Communication, and Lifespan
**Core questions:** Which screening is due now? Which client statement shows the teaching landed? When does cultural humility change the plan?

**Key concepts**
- USPSTF Grade A/B screening highlights: blood pressure (adults 18+), colorectal 45-75 (FIT annual, colonoscopy q10y, etc.), mammography 40-74 q2y, cervical (Pap q3y 21-29; co-test q5y 30-65), AAA one-time 65-75 ever-smoker men, osteoporosis women 65+ or younger high-risk, lung LDCT 50-80 with 20-pack-year + current/quit ≤15y, lipid 40-75
- Immunization schedule: childhood DTaP, IPV, MMR, varicella, Hib, PCV, hepatitis A/B, rotavirus, HPV (start 9-12, catch-up to 26); adult Tdap q10y, annual flu, COVID per schedule, shingles 50+, pneumococcal 65+ (or younger high-risk), HPV through 26
- Lifespan development: Erikson's stages — trust vs mistrust (infant), autonomy vs shame (toddler), initiative vs guilt (preschool), industry vs inferiority (school age), identity vs role confusion (adolescent), intimacy vs isolation (young adult), generativity vs stagnation (middle adult), integrity vs despair (older adult)
- Geriatric: polypharmacy, Beers criteria (avoid first-generation antihistamines, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics in older adults), fall prevention, delirium vs dementia (delirium is acute and fluctuating)
- Cultural humility: ask, do not assume; use interpreters (not family) for medical discussions; honor dietary and end-of-life preferences

**Applied clinical skills**
- Build an age-appropriate health-maintenance plan from a single chart review
- Re-write a closed therapeutic statement into an open one
- Adapt teaching for low literacy, hearing impairment, or limited English proficiency

**Common traps**
- "I understand" from a patient is not evidence of learning; ask teach-back ("Tell me how you'll take this med at home").
- Use professional medical interpreters; family interpreters introduce bias and consent risk.
- Delirium often gets missed as "just dementia"; an acute change in mental status in an older adult is delirium until proven otherwise.

**Authoritative sources**

## Chapter 10: Capstone — Exam-Day Discipline and the NGN
**Core questions:** How do I unfreeze on a Select-N or unfolding case study? How do I read a chart-style item without scrolling forever? How do I stay grounded when the CAT keeps testing me past 75 items?

**Key concepts**
- NGN item types in detail: extended drag-and-drop (order interventions), extended multiple response (Select N of M, partial credit possible), matrix/grid (rate each option as expected/unexpected or appropriate/contraindicated), highlight in text, bow-tie, traditional SATA (no partial credit)
- Clinical Judgment Measurement Model in practice: read the chart, recognize cues (highlight what is abnormal), analyze cues (cluster into hypotheses), prioritize hypotheses (which one will kill the patient first?), generate solutions (what would I do?), take action (which is the safest least-escalation first move?), evaluate outcomes (did it work, and what is the next signal?)
- CAT mechanics: first item is below passing standard, ability estimate ± standard error updates after each response, exam stops when the estimate is reliably on one side of the passing standard or at 145 items or 5 hours. Cannot skip; cannot return
- Pacing: ~1 minute average, but spend up to 2-3 minutes on NGN case studies; abandon spirals; trust your first read on safety items unless a second reading reveals a missed cue

**Applied clinical skills**
- Convert a SATA item to a "is this safe?" filter on each option
- Walk through an unfolding case from cues to evaluation without skipping a phase
- Reset after a brutal-feeling item without losing the next four

**Common traps**
- Counting items does nothing; the test ends when it ends. If you feel the items getting harder, that is a good sign — the algorithm is testing whether you can handle them.
- On SATA, choose each option in isolation: "is this safe and indicated for this patient?" — yes or no. Do not pick a count first.
- When two answers look right, pick the broader/safer one (assessment over intervention unless ABC is failing) or the one that addresses the priority client need (safety > recall).
- Take the breaks. Hydration and a brief walk reset the prefrontal cortex; a wall of items without breaks degrades judgment.
