# USMLE Step 1: The Basic Sciences Boss Fight
**ID**: `usmleStep1` · **Discipline**: Medicine

## Course Aim
Step 1 stopped reporting a three-digit score in January 2022, but the exam itself did not get any kinder. It became a one-day, seven-block, pass-or-fail gate that asks one question over and over in two hundred and eighty different costumes: *can you reason from a molecule to a person?* The vignettes are tight, the distractors are mean, and the right answer is almost always the one that names a mechanism, an organism, a pathway, or a single high-yield buzzword the rest of the stem was built around.

The temperament this exam rewards is that of a basic-scientist-detective. You read the four-line vignette and your eye lands on one detail — *bite cells*, *bullous myringitis*, *non-bilious projectile vomiting*, *rust-colored sputum*, *cherry-red macula without hepatosplenomegaly* — and your brain already knows the enzyme, the organism, the inheritance, the cofactor, and the textbook treatment. Step 1 is not really a clinical exam; it is a *recognition* exam dressed up in clinical clothing, and the best preparation builds dense, fluent, retrievable knowledge of the mechanisms that hold the rest of medicine up.

This syllabus treats the course as a year-long apprenticeship in that fluency. We anchor on mechanism (the *why*), index by site/organ/system (the *where*), and rehearse with vignettes until the buzzwords land before the last line of the stem does. Biochemistry shows up dressed as a sick infant. Microbiology shows up dressed as a febrile traveler. Pharmacology shows up disguised as a side effect nobody warned the patient about. Pathology shows up as a stained slide with one diagnostic feature. The point is to make the costumes thin enough to see through within fifteen seconds.

The aim is to build a working preclinical brain that is confident on pathways, comfortable with organisms by site and virulence factor, fluent in drug mechanisms and class-level toxicity, fast on histology and gross specimens, and unflappable when a vignette buries the diagnosis under three red herrings and a lab value with no units. We will respect the pass/fail outcome — but we will not let it tempt us to a shallow pass. A confident pass requires *deep* mechanism understanding, because that is the same understanding that will carry you through Step 2 CK, Step 3, residency, and every rounds question that begins with "and why does that happen?"

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a question into this course when it tests preclinical content the NBME prioritizes: general principles (biochem, genetics, molecular biology), all organ systems from cardiovascular through behavioral, biostatistics and epidemiology, and the multisystem topics (genetics, aging, immunology) that thread everything together. The voice is mechanism-first, kind, and retrieval-heavy. It should make a notoriously brutal exam feel survivable without trivializing the science.

Step 1 has been pass/fail since January 2022, which shifted the consequences of failure but not the depth required to pass. The exam still tests the same conceptual range; the change simply moved the residency-application weight onto Step 2 CK. Treat the new format as freedom from chasing a single number, not as permission to study shallowly. Pass-level questions still ask for the rate-limiting enzyme of gluconeogenesis, the toxin that causes gas gangrene, the inheritance pattern of OTC deficiency, and the one drug that lyses fresh thrombus. The fastest route to a comfortable pass is the same as the fastest route to a strong Step 2 score: build the mechanisms deeply, the first time.

The exam delivers approximately 280 multiple-choice questions over seven 60-minute blocks of around 40 questions each, with a total of 45 minutes of break time across the day. ### Suggested Study Cadence
The single most validated rhythm is a **dedicated study block of 4-8 weeks immediately after preclinical coursework**, layered on top of a strong M1-M2 foundation. During the dedicated period, plan on roughly **12 hours per day**, six days per week, with one full day off for sleep and life. A typical day blends:

- One UWorld block (~40 questions timed and tutor mode), followed by detailed review of every question — right and wrong
- Daily Anki maintenance from First Aid and a focused deck (e.g., AnKing, Zanki, Lightyear)
- Pathoma video plus reading on the day's organ system, then Sketchy Micro and Sketchy Pharm where relevant
- A short, mixed retrieval block (cold recall, 20-30 minutes) at the end of the day

Self-assessment rhythm: one NBME or UWSA self-assessment every 7-10 days, with a written postmortem on every missed question.

## Chapter 1: Biochemistry and Molecular Genetics
**Core questions:** Which step in the pathway is broken, where does the substrate accumulate, and what does the patient look like when that step fails?

**Key concepts**
- Glycolysis (PFK-1 as the major rate-limiting step, fructose-2,6-bisphosphate as its key allosteric activator)
- Gluconeogenesis: the four irreversible enzymes (pyruvate carboxylase, PEPCK, fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, glucose-6-phosphatase), the mitochondrial-to-cytosolic shuttle
- TCA cycle (acetyl-CoA → 2 CO2 + 3 NADH + 1 FADH2 + 1 GTP per turn) and electron transport chain (complexes I-V; metformin's mild complex I inhibition; cyanide and complex IV)
- Pentose phosphate pathway and G6PD deficiency (oxidant triggers: dapsone, primaquine, sulfa drugs, nitrofurantoin, fava beans; bite cells and Heinz bodies on smear)
- Urea cycle: CPS1 is the first committed step with N-acetylglutamate as obligate activator; OTC deficiency is the only X-linked urea defect and the only one with orotic acid in the urine without megaloblastic anemia
- Amino acid disorders: PKU, alkaptonuria, homocystinuria, maple syrup urine disease, cystinuria
- Lysosomal storage diseases by enzyme: Tay-Sachs (hexosaminidase A) with cherry-red macula and *no* hepatosplenomegaly; Niemann-Pick (sphingomyelinase) with cherry-red macula *and* hepatosplenomegaly; Gaucher (glucocerebrosidase) with crinkled-paper macrophages
- Glycogen storage diseases: von Gierke (type I, G6Pase) severe fasting hypoglycemia; Pompe (type II, acid α-glucosidase) cardiomegaly; Cori (type III, debranching); McArdle (type V, muscle phosphorylase) exercise intolerance
- DNA repair: nucleotide excision repair (xeroderma pigmentosum), mismatch repair (Lynch/HNPCC), double-strand break repair via homologous recombination (BRCA1/2) and non-homologous end joining (ataxia-telangiectasia)

**Applied skills**
- Map a clinical phenotype back to the deficient enzyme and accumulated substrate
- Predict lab abnormalities (lactate, ammonia, ketones, urine orotic acid, pH) from a broken pathway
- Differentiate storage diseases by enzyme, organ involvement, smear finding, and inheritance

**Common traps**
- *Tay-Sachs vs Niemann-Pick:* both have a cherry-red macula in an infant. Niemann-Pick adds hepatosplenomegaly; Tay-Sachs spares the spleen.
- *OTC deficiency vs orotic aciduria:* both elevate orotic acid in urine, but orotic aciduria has megaloblastic anemia and OTC does not.
- *Wernicke triad COA — confusion, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia — comes BEFORE Korsakoff (anterograde amnesia + confabulation). Give thiamine BEFORE glucose in chronic alcohol use disorder, or glucose drains the last thiamine reserves and precipitates Wernicke.*
- *Glucokinase vs hexokinase:* glucokinase is liver/β-cell, low affinity, high Vmax, *not* feedback-inhibited by G6P; hexokinase is everywhere else, high affinity, low Vmax, feedback-inhibited.

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid for the USMLE Step 1, Boards & Beyond (Biochemistry), Pathoma (Chapter 1-2), Sketchy Biochem, UWorld.

## Chapter 2: Microbiology
**Core questions:** Which organism colonizes which site, which virulence factor explains the syndrome, and which drug class kills it?

**Key concepts**
- Bacteria by Gram stain, shape, oxygen tolerance, and special media
- Organisms by clinical clue: lobar pneumonia with rusty sputum → *S. pneumoniae*; cavitary lesion with currant-jelly sputum → *Klebsiella*; aspiration → oral anaerobes; walking pneumonia with positive cold agglutinins → *Mycoplasma*; bullous myringitis → *Mycoplasma*; gas gangrene with crepitus → *C. perfringens* (alpha toxin / lecithinase); rose-gardener thorn → *Sporothrix* with lymphangitic spread; pigeon droppings + India ink positive → *Cryptococcus*; cat scratch → *Bartonella*
- Tick-borne disease cluster: *Borrelia burgdorferi* (Lyme), *Babesia*, *Ehrlichia/Anaplasma*, *Rickettsia rickettsii* (RMSF), *Francisella* (tularemia)
- Virulence factors worth memorizing cold: *S. aureus* protein A binds Fc and disables opsonization; *S. pneumoniae* polysaccharide capsule; *H. influenzae* IgA protease; *Pseudomonas* exotoxin A ADP-ribosylates EF-2 (same mechanism as diphtheria toxin); TSST-1 superantigen (toxic shock)
- Antibiotics by mechanism — cell wall: β-lactams bind PBPs (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, monobactams), vancomycin binds D-Ala-D-Ala. Protein synthesis: 30S (aminoglycosides, tetracyclines — "buy AT 30") and 50S (macrolides, clindamycin, chloramphenicol, linezolid — "CCEL at 50"). DNA: fluoroquinolones (gyrase and topo IV); metronidazole (free radicals in anaerobes). RNA: rifampin (RNA polymerase). Folate: sulfa (DHPS), trimethoprim (DHFR)
- Resistance mechanisms: MRSA via altered PBP2a (mecA gene), VRE via D-Ala-D-Lac (vanA), ESBL and AmpC β-lactamases, NDM-1 carbapenemase

**Applied skills**
- Pick the most likely organism from age, site, exposure, and host factors
- Match a Gram-stain morphology to a clinical syndrome in under fifteen seconds
- Choose the correct antibiotic *mechanism* class from a clinical scenario

**Common traps**
- *Exotoxin A (Pseudomonas) and diphtheria toxin* share a mechanism (ADP-ribosylation of EF-2); the giveaway is the host (burn patient, neutropenic, cystic fibrosis → Pseudomonas; pseudomembranous pharyngitis with bull neck → diphtheria).
- *Cold agglutinins on the question stem* almost always means Mycoplasma — but the same lab finding also appears in EBV.
- *Catalase-positive organisms (S. aureus, Nocardia, Serratia, Burkholderia, Aspergillus)* are the ones chronic granulomatous disease patients cannot clear — because the patient's neutrophils can no longer compensate for the bug's own catalase.
- *VRE vs MRSA mechanism:* VRE changes the target (D-Ala-D-Lac), MRSA changes the binding protein (PBP2a). Don't mix.

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid Step 1, Sketchy Micro, Pathoma, UWorld micro tag.

## Chapter 3: Immunology
**Core questions:** Which cell type or molecule is missing, and which recurrent infection or autoimmune pattern is the patient stuck with?

**Key concepts**
- Helper T-cell subsets: Th1 (IFN-γ, IL-12; macrophage activation, cell-mediated immunity); Th2 (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13; B-cell class switching to IgE, eosinophils); Th17 (IL-17, IL-22; neutrophil recruitment, mucosal defense); Treg (FoxP3, IL-10, TGF-β)
- MHC class I (on all nucleated cells, presents endogenous antigens to CD8 T cells) vs class II (on APCs, presents exogenous antigens to CD4 T cells)
- Complement: C5-C9 deficiency → recurrent *Neisseria* infections; C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency → hereditary angioedema (do *not* give ACE inhibitors); CD55/CD59 deficiency → paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria
- Hypersensitivity types: I (IgE-mediated; anaphylaxis, atopy); II (antibody-mediated against fixed antigen; Goodpasture, autoimmune hemolytic anemia, ITP); III (immune complex; SLE, serum sickness, poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis); IV (T-cell delayed; contact dermatitis, TB skin test, MS)
- Primary immunodeficiencies: Bruton agammaglobulinemia (XLA, BTK; boys with recurrent encapsulated infections after maternal IgG wanes ~6 mo); SCID (both arms of adaptive immunity); DiGeorge (22q11 deletion; CATCH-22: Cardiac, Abnormal facies, Thymic aplasia, Cleft palate, Hypocalcemia); Wiskott-Aldrich (WAS gene; TIE: Thrombocytopenia, Immunodeficiency, Eczema); chronic granulomatous disease (NADPH oxidase defect; negative nitroblue tetrazolium / abnormal DHR flow; catalase-positive organisms)

**Applied skills**
- Match a hypersensitivity mechanism to a clinical syndrome (anaphylaxis, hemolytic disease, serum sickness, contact dermatitis)
- Recognize immunodeficiency patterns by which infections recur
- Read a flow cytometry vignette and predict the missing population

**Common traps**
- *Bruton XLA boys* present **after** 6 months of age because maternal IgG protects them in the first half-year.
- *Wiskott-Aldrich (WAS)* is the only major immunodeficiency with **thrombocytopenia with small platelets** — that's the discriminator from other immunodeficiencies on a smear.
- *Hereditary angioedema (C1 inhibitor deficiency)* presents with face/airway swelling **without urticaria** — and ACE inhibitors will tip it into crisis.
- *Type II vs III hypersensitivity:* Type II is antibody **against a fixed tissue antigen**; Type III is antibody **bound to a soluble antigen forming a circulating immune complex** that deposits later.

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid Step 1, Pathoma Chapter 2, Sketchy Micro/Immuno, UWorld immunology tag.

## Chapter 4: Pharmacology — Mechanisms and Toxicology
**Core questions:** What does this drug do at the receptor level, what does it ruin when it works too well, and what antidote or workaround exists?

**Key concepts**
- Receptors: M1-M5 cholinergic; α1, α2, β1, β2, β3, D1, D2; 5-HT subtypes; H1/H2. Sympathetic mnemonic: "1 heart, 2 lungs, Q for vessels" (β1 cardiac, β2 bronchial, α1 vascular).
- SLUDGE-M cholinergic toxicity (Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis, Miosis); anticholinergic toxidrome (hot as a hare, dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter).
- CYP450 **inducers** (CRAP-S): Carbamazepine, Rifampin, Alcohol (chronic), Phenytoin, Phenobarbital, St. John's wort.
- CYP450 **inhibitors** (G-PACMAN): Grapefruit, Protease inhibitors, Azoles, Cimetidine, Macrolides (not azithromycin), Amiodarone, Non-DHP CCBs.
- Pharmacokinetics: Vd, clearance, half-life, zero-order vs first-order kinetics, loading vs maintenance dose. Receptor pharmacology: partial agonist (lowers max effect), inverse agonist, competitive antagonist (Vmax unchanged, apparent Km up) vs non-competitive (Vmax down).
- Antidotes to memorize cold: acetaminophen → NAC; benzodiazepines → flumazenil; opioids → naloxone; β-blocker → glucagon (then atropine); calcium channel blocker → calcium plus glucagon; warfarin → vitamin K and FFP; heparin → protamine; tPA → aminocaproic acid; iron → deferoxamine; lead → succimer (or EDTA/dimercaprol for severe); cyanide → hydroxocobalamin (or nitrite plus thiosulfate); methemoglobinemia → methylene blue; salicylate → urinary alkalinization and dialysis; methanol or ethylene glycol → fomepizole and dialysis.

**Applied skills**
- Predict the side-effect profile from the mechanism rather than memorizing a list
- Identify a toxidrome and choose the antidote
- Reason about loading dose, maintenance dose, and clearance changes in disease

**Common traps**
- *β-blocker overdose → glucagon first*, not more atropine. Glucagon bypasses the β-receptor via cAMP.
- *Pheochromocytoma:* α-block (phenoxybenzamine) **before** β-block. β first leaves α1 unopposed and provokes hypertensive crisis.
- *Acetaminophen overdose:* NAC works best within the first 8-10 hours; the Rumack-Matthew nomogram starts at the 4-hour level, not on arrival.
- *Methemoglobinemia:* SpO2 reads ~85% no matter the PaO2; chocolate-brown blood; methylene blue is the antidote unless the patient has G6PD deficiency, in which case methylene blue itself oxidizes hemoglobin.

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid Step 1 pharmacology section, Sketchy Pharm, Lippincott Pharmacology, UWorld pharm tag.

## Chapter 5: Pathology and Histology
**Core questions:** What cell injury pattern is this, what does the tissue look like, and what disease does the morphology fit?

**Key concepts**
- Cell injury and death: reversible vs irreversible, necrosis types (coagulative, liquefactive, caseous, fat, fibrinoid, gangrenous), apoptosis
- Inflammation: acute (neutrophils, vascular changes) vs chronic (lymphocytes, macrophages, fibrosis), granuloma formation
- Hemodynamic disorders: edema, hyperemia, congestion, hemorrhage, thrombosis, embolism, infarction, shock
- Neoplasia: oncogenes, tumor suppressors (Rb, p53, APC, NF1, NF2, VHL, BRCA), tumor markers, paraneoplastic syndromes, metastasis biology
- Genetic disease, environmental and nutritional disease, developmental pathology

**Applied skills**
- Read a histology image for the named pattern (granuloma, Mallory body, Reed-Sternberg cell, signet-ring cell, psammoma body)
- Connect a paraneoplastic syndrome back to the tumor type and ectopic hormone
- Distinguish benign from malignant features on a slide or gross specimen

**Common traps**
- *Reed-Sternberg cells* are Hodgkin lymphoma — owl-eye nuclei. Non-Hodgkin lacks them.
- *Psammoma bodies* show up in **PSaMMoma**: Papillary thyroid carcinoma, Serous ovarian cystadenocarcinoma, Meningioma, Mesothelioma.
- *Coagulative necrosis everywhere except the brain*; the brain liquefies. *Caseous* = TB or fungal granulomas. *Fat necrosis* = pancreas or breast trauma (saponification with Ca²⁺).
- *Squamous cell lung cancer* causes hypercalcemia via PTHrP; *small cell* causes SIADH and ACTH ectopic secretion.

**Authoritative sources:** Robbins Basic Pathology, Pathoma (Sattar), First Aid Step 1, UWorld path tag.

## Chapter 6: Cardiovascular, Respiratory, and Renal Physiology
**Core questions:** Where on the curve is this patient, what gradient is driving the flow, and which intervention moves the system in the right direction?

**Key concepts**
- Cardiac: Frank-Starling, preload/afterload/contractility, pressure-volume loop changes per disease (AS = pressure overload concentric hypertrophy; AR = volume overload eccentric hypertrophy; MR = volume overload backward). Murmurs by maneuver: Valsalva ↓ preload → HCM louder, MVP click earlier; squat ↑ preload → opposite; handgrip ↑ afterload → AR and MR louder, AS softer.
- Renal: RAAS, ANP/BNP, JG apparatus, FENa <1% prerenal vs >2% ATN. Urine osm >300 in SIADH vs <300 in DI given the same plasma osm.
- Pulmonary: FEV1/FVC for obstruction vs restriction; A-a gradient; DLCO interpretation; V/Q mismatch vs shunt physiology.
- Hematology overlap: PT (extrinsic, factor VII); PTT (intrinsic, factors VIII, IX, XI, XII); ristocetin cofactor activity in von Willebrand disease.
- Endocrine integration: HPA axis and dexamethasone suppression test interpretation (low-dose suppress → normal; not suppress → Cushing's of any cause; high-dose suppress → pituitary Cushing's; high-dose not suppress → ectopic or adrenal). ACTH high → pituitary or ectopic; ACTH low → adrenal.

**Applied skills**
- Move points on PV loops, Starling curves, and oxygen dissociation curves with named perturbations
- Solve a mixed acid-base disorder using the anion gap and Winters formula
- Localize a tubular defect to a nephron segment from urinalysis findings

**Common traps**
- *Valsalva makes HCM LOUDER* — because ↓ preload makes the LV smaller, which makes the outflow obstruction worse. Most murmurs get softer with Valsalva.
- *FENa <1% does not rule out ATN* if the patient has been on diuretics; in that case use FEUrea (<35% prerenal).
- *Winters formula:* expected PaCO2 = 1.5 × HCO3 + 8 ± 2. Miss this and you'll miss the mixed disorder.
- *DI vs SIADH urine osm:* both can give the same serum sodium direction, but DI urine is dilute and SIADH urine is concentrated. The kidney always tells the truth.

**Authoritative sources:** Costanzo Physiology, BRS Physiology, First Aid Step 1, Boards & Beyond physiology.

## Chapter 7: Neuroscience and Behavioral Health
**Core questions:** Where is the lesion, what neurotransmitter system is involved, and which drug class fits this picture?

**Key concepts**
- Neuroanatomy: cortical localization, brainstem nuclei, spinal cord tracts (dorsal columns, spinothalamic, corticospinal), cranial nerves, vascular territories
- Neuropathology: stroke patterns by vascular territory, demyelinating disease (MS), neurodegenerative disease (Alzheimer's = β-amyloid plaques and tau tangles; Parkinson's = α-synuclein Lewy bodies; Huntington's = CAG repeat, atrophy of caudate; ALS = anterior horn and corticospinal)
- Psychiatric disorders: mood, anxiety, psychotic, personality, neurodevelopmental, substance use
- Psychopharmacology: SSRIs, SNRIs, atypical antidepressants (bupropion lowers seizure threshold; mirtazapine sedating + appetite), antipsychotic typical vs atypical, mood stabilizers (lithium, valproate, lamotrigine, carbamazepine), stimulants
- Embryology: neural tube closure, neural crest derivatives, BMP/SHH/FGF signaling, branchial arches

**Applied skills**
- Localize a lesion from a single sentence of physical exam findings
- Match a neurodegenerative disease to its hallmark inclusion and affected region
- Choose a psychiatric medication that fits both the diagnosis and the side-effect tolerance

**Common traps**
- *MCA stroke vs ACA stroke:* MCA hits face + arm > leg, dominant hemisphere aphasia, non-dominant neglect. ACA hits leg > arm, frontal personality changes.
- *Wernicke (receptive, fluent, nonsense) vs Broca (expressive, non-fluent, frustrated patient).* Conduction aphasia = arcuate fasciculus, fluent with repetition deficit.
- *Lewy body dementia* = visual hallucinations + parkinsonism + cognitive fluctuation; *don't give typical antipsychotics* — extreme sensitivity.
- *Serotonin syndrome (clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor, autonomic surge) vs NMS (lead-pipe rigidity, hyperthermia, autonomic instability).* The muscle finding is the discriminator.

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid Step 1, Blueprints Neurology, Sketchy Pharm (psych), UWorld neuro/psych tags.

## Chapter 8: Endocrine, Reproductive, GI, MSK, Skin, and Multisystem
**Core questions:** Which axis is broken, which feedback loop explains the labs, and which multisystem disease ties the findings together?

**Key concepts**
- Endocrine axes (HPA, HPT, HPG, growth hormone, prolactin) and autoimmune endocrinopathies (Hashimoto's = anti-TPO; Graves' = TSI; type 1 DM = anti-GAD/IA-2; Addison's = autoimmune adrenalitis)
- Reproductive: embryology of urogenital tract, menstrual cycle, pregnancy pathology (placenta previa vs abruption vs accreta), McCune-Albright (fibrous dysplasia + café-au-lait + precocious puberty), Turner (XO, short, webbed neck, coarctation, bicuspid aortic valve), Klinefelter (XXY, tall, gynecomastia, small firm testes)
- GI: anatomy, hormones (gastrin, secretin, CCK, GIP), hepatology, malabsorption (celiac anti-tTG, tropical sprue, Whipple PAS-positive macrophages)
- MSK and skin: connective tissue disease (Marfan FBN1, Ehlers-Danlos collagen), autoimmune rheumatology (RA anti-CCP, SLE anti-dsDNA/anti-Sm, scleroderma anti-Scl-70/anti-centromere)
- Multisystem genetics: MEN syndromes (MEN1 = 3 P's parathyroid/pituitary/pancreas; MEN2A = parathyroid/medullary thyroid/pheo; MEN2B = mucosal neuromas/medullary thyroid/pheo/marfanoid), neurofibromatosis (NF1 = neurofibromin/RAS-GAP; NF2 = merlin), tuberous sclerosis (TSC1/TSC2)

**Applied skills**
- Read a hormone panel and place the lesion at hypothalamus, pituitary, or end organ
- Recognize a multisystem genetic syndrome from two clues
- Match an autoimmune antibody to its disease and target tissue

**Common traps**
- *Primary vs secondary vs tertiary hyperparathyroidism:* primary = high Ca + high PTH (adenoma); secondary = low Ca + high PTH (CKD); tertiary = high Ca + high PTH after long-standing secondary (autonomous gland).
- *Sheehan syndrome:* postpartum pituitary infarction; first sign is failure to lactate (prolactin), then loss of menses.
- *21-hydroxylase deficiency* (most common CAH): low cortisol, low aldosterone, high androgens — salt-wasting, virilization of females, hypotension.
- *Pheochromocytoma rule of 10s:* 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% malignant, 10% familial. Always α-block before β-block.

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid Step 1, Pathoma, Sketchy Pharm endocrine, UWorld endocrine/repro tags.

## Chapter 9: Biostatistics, Epidemiology, and Behavioral Science
**Core questions:** What does this number mean, what study design produced it, and what bias is quietly distorting the answer?

**Key concepts**
- Study designs: case-control (odds ratio), cohort (relative risk, hazard ratio), cross-sectional (prevalence), randomized controlled trial (causal inference), systematic review and meta-analysis
- Measures: sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, likelihood ratios, ROC curve and AUC, number needed to treat (NNT = 1/ARR)
- Bias: selection (Berkson, healthy worker), recall, observer (Hawthorne), lead-time, length, attrition (loss to follow-up); confounding vs effect modification
- Ethical principles (autonomy, beneficence, nonmaleficence, justice), informed consent, capacity, surrogate hierarchy, advance directives, HIPAA, mandatory reporting

**Applied skills**
- Compute sensitivity, specificity, and predictive values from a 2x2 table without panic
- Name the bias in a one-sentence study description
- Decide whether a confidence interval supports a clinically meaningful conclusion

**Common traps**
- *Sensitivity and specificity do not change with prevalence; PPV and NPV do.*
- *Lead-time bias vs length bias:* lead-time = screening just moves the diagnosis date earlier without changing outcome; length = screening preferentially catches slow-growing disease that would have done well anyway.
- *Confounding vs effect modification:* confounding distorts the estimate (adjust for it); effect modification means the effect *truly differs* across strata (report stratified, do not pool).
- *Odds ratio approximates relative risk only when the disease is rare.*

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid Step 1 biostats, UWorld biostats tag, BRS Behavioral Science, *The Medical Letter* for cadence on screening.

## Chapter 10: Capstone — Exam-Day Discipline
The basic sciences are only half the test; the other half is operating under time pressure for seven hours. This capstone consolidates the test-day execution plan that turns *knowing it* into *getting it right*.

**Pacing**
- ~1.5 minutes per question, 40 questions per block, 60 minutes per block. Build a per-block pacing checkpoint: at minute 20 you should be at question 13-14; at minute 40 you should be at question 26-27.
- **Never skip blindly.** Make your best educated guess on the first pass, flag if uncertain, and only return to flagged questions if time remains at the end of the block. Most question changes on review hurt rather than help.

**Attacking a vignette**
1. Read the **last line** first — that tells you what the question is actually asking.
2. Scan **demographics, vitals, key labs/imaging** in that order.
3. Generate a one-sentence differential before reading the full stem.
4. Read the full stem only if the differential is not yet narrowed to a single answer.
5. Match the answer choices to the differential; eliminate before you choose.

**Breaks**
- ~45 minutes total break time across the day, distributed across six breaks. Use them.
- Plan a long break after blocks 3 and 4 with food, water, and a quick walk. Sugar and caffeine in moderation; nothing experimental on test day.

**Test-day prep**
- Sleep is the single most under-rated study tool the last 72 hours before the exam.
- No new content the day before; review your own error log and the highest-yield First Aid pages.
- Light, familiar breakfast. Pack snacks. Arrive 30 minutes early. Bring earplugs.
- After the exam: do not debrief with peers within the first hour. Walk away, eat, sleep, and let the day be over.

The exam is winnable. It is also designed to feel unwinnable around questions 25-32 of the third block. That feeling is universal and not diagnostic. Trust the preparation, work the next question, and finish the day.
