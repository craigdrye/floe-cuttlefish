# USMLE Step 2 CK: Clinical Reasoning Under the Clock
**ID**: `usmleStep2Ck` · **Discipline**: Medicine

## Course Aim
Step 2 CK is the exam where preclinical pattern recognition meets bedside decision-making. The vignettes get longer, the lab strips get richer, and the question stem stops asking *what is the mechanism* and starts asking *what do you do next*. This syllabus treats the test as the bridge it actually is: from a student who can name a disease to a junior clinician who can pick the next best step in management without freezing.

The temperament Step 2 CK rewards is that of a thoughtful end-of-medical-school clinician working through a five-step cognitive ladder on every patient: **diagnosis → next best step → definitive management → prognosis → prevention.** A perfect answer is rarely the most aggressive or the most cautious one; it is the one that matches the *stage of care* the question is asking about. "Best initial test" is not the same as "most accurate test." "Definitive treatment" is not the same as "initial step." "Most appropriate disposition" is not the same as "most common cause." The exam is exquisitely sensitive to which verb sits in the stem, and your reading speed should be tuned to catch that verb every time.

Because Step 1 went pass/fail in January 2022, Step 2 CK has quietly become the **most important numbered metric on a residency application** for most specialties. Program directors who used to filter applicants on Step 1 scores now filter on Step 2 CK. That makes the depth required higher than it once was, even as the content remains rotation-flavored. You are studying for both a licensure exam and a residency-application gateway in the same sitting. Treat it that way.

The aim is to build durable clinical judgment across the core rotations — internal medicine (the exam's center of gravity at 50-60% of items), surgery, OB/GYN, pediatrics, psychiatry, family medicine — and the cross-cutting topics of ethics, biostatistics, quality improvement, and patient safety. You should leave fluent in initial workups, definitive treatments, screening intervals, and the small phrases ("most likely diagnosis," "best initial test," "most accurate test," "definitive management," "most appropriate next step") that decide which answer is right.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a question into this course when it tests clinical knowledge the way real patients present: complaints, vitals, exam findings, labs and imaging, then a single decision. The voice is rotation-flavored, kind, and retrieval-heavy. It should make the heaviest test on the licensing path feel reasonable rather than punishing.

The exam is now the residency-application **filter** that Step 1 used to be. Programs use it to triage hundreds of applications, and many program directors openly cite Step 2 CK as their first numeric screen. That has practical consequences for how you study: do not just chase a pass; chase the same depth that strong residency applicants chase, because the same exam decides both outcomes. The students who score best are not the ones who memorize the most facts. They are the ones who develop a *clinical reflex*: a vignette appears, and the next step appears with it.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The exam delivers approximately **318 multiple-choice questions over eight 60-minute blocks of ~40 questions each**, with about 45 minutes of total break time across the day. The full session runs roughly **9 hours**. Pace works out to ~**1.7 minutes per question** — slightly more time per item than Step 1, because vignettes are longer and require more synthesis.

NBME publishes content ranges that shift slightly year to year. Allocate by published range, rotation experience, and personal error log.

### Suggested Study Cadence
The most validated rhythm is a **dedicated 4-6 week block immediately after the major clerkships**, with roughly **12 hours per day** six days per week. A typical day blends:

- One UWorld block (40 questions, timed) followed by detailed review of every question
- One AMBOSS block or AMBOSS qbank deep-dive on weaker topics
- Online MedEd (Dustyn Williams) video for the day's organ system, with the corresponding chapter of *Step-Up to Medicine*
- Daily ten-minute biostats and ethics drill
- Mixed retrieval block at the end of the day

Self-assessment: one NBME CCSSA, UWSA, or Free 120 every 7-10 days. Treat every wrong answer as a learning object — write a one-sentence rule into your error log before moving on.

## Chapter 1: Internal Medicine, Part One — Cardio, Pulm, Renal, Endo
**Core questions:** What is the diagnosis, what is the next best test, what is the definitive treatment, and what does the answer change to if the patient is older / pregnant / on dialysis?

**Key concepts**
- **Cardio:** ACS pathway (STEMI → primary PCI within 90-120 min, fibrinolytics if PCI not available); NSTEMI risk-stratify and treat medically with possible cath; atrial fibrillation — rate vs rhythm control (rhythm if young, symptomatic, or HF-related; rate otherwise; AFFIRM trial); CHA2DS2-VASc for anticoagulation; aortic stenosis severity (mean gradient >40 mmHg, peak velocity >4 m/s, AVA <1 cm²; TAVR vs SAVR); HFpEF management with SGLT2i (EMPEROR-Preserved), spironolactone (TOPCAT), and ARNI (PARAGON)
- **Pulm:** COPD exacerbation (bronchodilators + steroids + antibiotics if any 2 of 3 Anthonisen criteria + controlled O2 to 88-92%); asthma severity classification and step therapy; PE diagnostic pathway (Wells, PERC, D-dimer, CTPA); pneumonia severity (CURB-65, PSI)
- **Renal:** AKI workup (FENa, FEUrea if on diuretics); CKD staging and complications; glomerular disease (nephritic vs nephrotic); electrolyte and acid-base
- **Endo:** DM type 1 vs 2 management ladder (metformin → GLP-1 RA or SGLT2i in CV/renal risk → others); DKA management (fluids → insulin → potassium; bicarb only for pH <6.9); HHS in T2DM; thyroid storm (PTU acutely, β-block, steroids, cooling, iodine ≥1 hr after thionamide); adrenal disorders

**Applied skills**
- Choose the next best test from history and exam alone before reaching for imaging
- Stratify chest pain by HEART score and pretest probability
- Adjust medications for renal function and pregnancy without harming the patient

**Common traps**
- *Aortic stenosis criteria for intervention:* you intervene when the patient is **symptomatic** with severe AS, or asymptomatic with EF <50%. Stable asymptomatic severe AS gets serial surveillance.
- *DKA potassium:* total body K is **depleted** even when serum K looks normal/high. Once K drops below 5.3, start replacement *before* it crashes after insulin shifts.
- *COPD oxygen:* target SpO2 **88-92%**, not 100%. Over-oxygenation worsens hypercapnia through V/Q mismatch and the Haldane effect, not just hypoxic drive.
- *Atrial fibrillation in hemodynamic instability:* synchronized cardioversion *first*, regardless of duration.

**Authoritative sources:** *Step-Up to Medicine* (Agabegi), Online MedEd, UWorld, AMBOSS, First Aid for the USMLE Step 2 CK.

## Chapter 2: Internal Medicine, Part Two — GI, Heme/Onc, ID, Rheum
**Core questions:** What pattern matches this complaint, which urgent diagnosis must be excluded, and what is the disposition?

**Key concepts**
- **GI:** upper vs lower GI bleed (BUN/Cr ratio >36 favors upper); liver enzyme patterns (ALT > AST = viral hepatitis; AST/ALT >2 = alcoholic; ALP/GGT elevation = cholestatic); cirrhosis complications (SBP — diagnose if PMN >250 on ascitic tap; HE — lactulose and rifaximin; varices — band ligation + non-selective β-blocker for prophylaxis); IBD (UC vs Crohn distribution); acute pancreatitis (Atlanta classification; Ranson; treat IV fluids + pain + early enteral nutrition); colorectal cancer screening
- **Heme/Onc:** anemia algorithm by MCV and reticulocyte count; *heparin-induced thrombocytopenia type II* (IgG against PF4-heparin complex, platelet drop ≥50% by day 5-10, paradoxical thrombosis; switch to argatroban, bivalirudin, or fondaparinux — NOT warfarin first); transfusion reactions; leukemias and lymphomas; plasma cell disorders
- **ID:** sepsis bundle (lactate, cultures, broad-spectrum antibiotics, 30 mL/kg fluids within the first hour); meningitis empiric coverage by age; HIV/OI prophylaxis thresholds (PCP at CD4 <200, toxoplasmosis at CD4 <100, MAC at CD4 <50); endocarditis (Duke criteria; native vs prosthetic valve)
- **Rheum:** RA (anti-CCP, DMARD ladder — methotrexate first); SLE (anti-dsDNA, anti-Sm; nephritis classes); vasculitides (large = giant cell/Takayasu, medium = polyarteritis nodosa/Kawasaki, small = ANCA-associated GPA/EGPA/MPA and immune-complex HSP/cryoglobulinemia); crystal arthropathies; *giant cell arteritis — start steroids before biopsy*

**Applied skills**
- Decide when a GI bleed needs the ICU, the endoscopist, or both, and in what order
- Match an autoantibody to a disease and to the next confirmatory test
- Pick empirical antibiotics by site, severity, host factors, and local resistance patterns

**Common traps**
- *HIT type II:* the platelet drop is **modest** (often still >100k); the threshold is the 50% drop, not an absolute count. Heparin must come off; warfarin alone is contraindicated until platelets recover (skin necrosis risk from protein C depletion).
- *Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis:* PMN >250/μL on ascites tap is diagnostic *before* the culture returns. Treat empirically with ceftriaxone and give albumin to reduce hepatorenal syndrome.
- *Giant cell arteritis:* steroids start *before* biopsy. Biopsy yield holds up for 1-2 weeks; vision loss is irreversible.
- *Iron deficiency in a postmenopausal woman or older man:* must work up for occult GI malignancy. The diagnosis is not "anemia," it is "anemia from cancer until proven otherwise."

**Authoritative sources:** Online MedEd, *Step-Up to Medicine*, UWorld, AMBOSS, *Pocket Medicine* (Sabatine), First Aid Step 2 CK.

## Chapter 3: Surgery, Trauma, and Perioperative Care
**Core questions:** Is this an operative problem, what is the resuscitation priority, and what is the postoperative complication you must not miss?

**Key concepts**
- **Acute abdomen by quadrant:** RUQ (cholecystitis, hepatitis), RLQ (appendicitis, ectopic, ovarian torsion), LLQ (diverticulitis, ovarian), epigastric (peptic ulcer, pancreatitis, MI). Appendicitis exam: McBurney point, Rovsing, obturator, psoas signs; image with CT in adults, US in pregnancy and children; OR is the answer for uncomplicated appendicitis
- **Bowel obstruction:** SBO vs LBO; **closed-loop obstruction is a surgical emergency**; conservative trial 24-48 hr if partial and simple; surgery for complete, strangulated, or closed-loop
- **Biliary disease:** acute cholecystitis (Murphy sign; **RUQ US first**, HIDA if equivocal; laparoscopic cholecystectomy is definitive); ascending cholangitis (Charcot triad of fever, jaundice, RUQ pain; Reynolds pentad adds altered mental status + shock; ERCP + antibiotics)
- **Vascular:** AAA screening for men 65-75 who ever smoked (one-time abdominal US); aortic dissection (CT angiography; type A = surgery, type B = medical β-blockade); DVT (Wells, US, anticoagulation)
- **Trauma:** ATLS ABCDE primary survey; FAST exam; permissive hypotension in penetrating trauma until OR; **burns** (Parkland 4 mL/kg/%TBSA over 24 hr, half in first 8 hr from time of burn)
- **Pediatric surgery:** pyloric stenosis (3-6 wk, olive mass, non-bilious projectile vomiting, hypochloremic alkalosis); intussusception (6 mo - 2 yr, currant-jelly stool, sausage-shaped mass; air or contrast enema reduction); malrotation with midgut volvulus (**bilious emesis in a newborn is malrotation until proven otherwise**); necrotizing enterocolitis (premature, pneumatosis intestinalis on KUB)

**Applied skills**
- Run a structured ABCDE survey on a trauma vignette without skipping the easy step
- Choose between resuscitation, imaging, and operating room from a one-paragraph stem
- Identify the day-of-postoperative-fever cause by timing and exam findings (the 5 W's: Wind, Water, Walking, Wound, Wonder drugs)

**Common traps**
- *Ottawa ankle and knee rules* exist to **reduce unnecessary imaging**. Patients often pressure for an X-ray; the rule is your defense. Image only if specific criteria are met (e.g., bony tenderness at posterior edge or tip of malleolus, inability to bear weight 4 steps).
- *Bilious emesis in a newborn = malrotation with midgut volvulus until proven otherwise.* Get an upper GI study urgently. Time to OR is bowel-saving.
- *Cholangitis vs cholecystitis:* both are RUQ pain with fever, but cholangitis adds **jaundice** (because the bile duct is obstructed) — Charcot triad.
- *Burns first 8 hours:* half of the Parkland total volume, measured from the **time of the burn**, not the time of arrival.

**Authoritative sources:** *Surgical Recall*, Pestana's *Surgery Notes*, NMS Surgery, UWorld, AMBOSS, Online MedEd Surgery.

## Chapter 4: Obstetrics and Gynecology
**Core questions:** Is the mother or fetus in danger, what is the gestational age expectation, and what is the standard of care for this complaint?

**Key concepts**
- **Prenatal screening:** first-trimester labs (CBC, blood type/Rh, antibody screen, rubella, syphilis, HIV, hepatitis B, urinalysis, urine culture; cell-free fetal DNA optional); GBS swab at 35-37 weeks; gestational diabetes screen 24-28 weeks (50 g GCT → 100 g 3-hr GTT)
- **Hypertensive disorders:** chronic vs gestational vs preeclampsia (≥140/90 + proteinuria, or severe features regardless of proteinuria) vs HELLP. Magnesium for seizure prophylaxis in severe features; labetalol, hydralazine, or nifedipine for BP control; delivery is definitive
- **Bleeding in pregnancy:** placenta previa (painless bleeding, no digital exam), abruption (painful, often after trauma or cocaine), vasa previa (rupture of membranes → fetal exsanguination)
- **Postpartum hemorrhage:** atony first and most common (the 4 T's: **Tone, Trauma, Tissue, Thrombin**); ladder of management: massage + oxytocin → methylergonovine (avoid in HTN) or carboprost (avoid in asthma) → balloon tamponade → uterine artery embolization or surgery
- **Ectopic pregnancy:** medical methotrexate (stable, no fetal cardiac activity, β-hCG <5000, mass <3.5 cm, can follow up) vs surgical (salpingostomy or salpingectomy)
- **Gyn:** abnormal uterine bleeding (PALM-COEIN); contraception eligibility (USMEC); PCOS (Rotterdam criteria — 2 of 3 of oligo/anovulation, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries); endometriosis vs adenomyosis; cervical cancer screening per USPSTF; fetal heart rate categories I/II/III

**Applied skills**
- Read a fetal heart tracing for category and the appropriate intervention
- Choose contraception by patient preference, contraindications, and effectiveness
- Stage and triage a breast or pelvic complaint without losing the urgent diagnosis

**Common traps**
- *Painless third-trimester bleeding* = previa until imaging proves otherwise. **No digital cervical exam** until previa is excluded by ultrasound.
- *Postpartum hemorrhage 4 T's order — Tone is first and most common.* Reflex to uterine massage and oxytocin first; reach for the second-line drugs by contraindication.
- *Magnesium toxicity:* loss of deep tendon reflexes is the first sign, then respiratory depression. Antidote is **calcium gluconate**.
- *Methotrexate for ectopic:* requires stable patient, no fetal cardiac activity, β-hCG below ~5000, mass under ~3.5 cm, and reliable follow-up. Otherwise OR.

**Authoritative sources:** Blueprints OB/GYN, Beckmann's *Obstetrics and Gynecology*, Online MedEd OB/GYN, UWorld, AMBOSS.

## Chapter 5: Pediatrics and Developmental Care
**Core questions:** Is the child meeting milestones, what congenital condition explains this finding, and what infection is age-appropriate?

**Key concepts**
- Developmental milestones and red flags (loss of milestones is always abnormal)
- Vaccine schedule (CDC); routine vs catch-up; live-vaccine contraindications
- Respiratory distress: **croup vs epiglottitis vs bacterial tracheitis vs foreign body**. Croup = barking cough, steeple sign on neck X-ray, racemic epinephrine + dexamethasone. Epiglottitis = drooling, tripod, thumbprint sign, do **not** examine the throat; OR for airway. Foreign body = sudden onset, unilateral wheeze
- Asthma severity classification and step therapy
- ADHD diagnosis (symptoms in ≥2 settings, onset before age 12); stimulant pharmacotherapy
- **Cyanotic congenital heart disease — the 5 T's:** Truncus arteriosus, Transposition of the great arteries, Tricuspid atresia, Tetralogy of Fallot, Total anomalous pulmonary venous return
- **Kawasaki disease:** ≥5 days of fever + 4 of 5 features (rash, mucocutaneous extremity changes, conjunctivitis, oral changes / strawberry tongue, cervical lymphadenopathy ≥1.5 cm). Treat with IVIG + aspirin to prevent coronary aneurysms
- **Henoch-Schönlein purpura (IgA vasculitis):** purpura of lower extremities, abdominal pain, arthritis/arthralgia, IgA nephropathy. Supportive in most; steroids for severe GI or renal disease
- JIA classifications, child abuse recognition (pathognomonic patterns: spiral fractures in non-ambulatory infants, posterior rib fractures, retinal hemorrhages in shaken baby)

**Applied skills**
- Recognize a milestone delay from a single age-and-finding pair
- Match a congenital heart lesion to its murmur, cyanosis pattern, and intervention
- Identify child abuse red flags without delaying urgent care

**Common traps**
- *Epiglottitis:* do **not** examine the throat in the ED. Take the child to the OR with anesthesia ready for definitive airway management.
- *Pyloric stenosis labs:* hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis. Correct electrolytes *before* pyloromyotomy.
- *Kawasaki vs scarlet fever:* both can have strawberry tongue and rash. Kawasaki adds prolonged fever (≥5 d), conjunctivitis, cervical adenopathy, and **extremity changes** (palmar erythema, desquamation).
- *IVIG in Kawasaki* should be given within the first 10 days of illness; aspirin is continued at low dose until inflammatory markers normalize.

**Authoritative sources:** Blueprints Pediatrics, Nelson Essentials, Online MedEd Pediatrics, UWorld, AMBOSS.

## Chapter 6: Psychiatry and Behavioral Health
**Core questions:** Which DSM-5 criteria does this picture meet, which medication fits, and how acute is the risk?

**Key concepts**
- **DSM-5 duration cutoffs:** Major depressive disorder — ≥2 weeks of 5 of 9 SIGECAPS (sleep, interest, guilt, energy, concentration, appetite, psychomotor, suicidality, depressed mood). Bipolar I — ≥1 week of mania (or any duration if hospitalized). GAD — ≥6 months of excessive worry. PTSD — ≥1 month of intrusive/avoidant/cognitive/arousal symptoms. ADHD — symptoms in ≥2 settings with onset before age 12. Anorexia — BMI <17.5 (or significantly low weight) + restriction + distorted body image
- **Substance use:** alcohol withdrawal (peaks at 24-72 hr; DTs by 72 hr; benzodiazepines first-line + thiamine before glucose); opioid withdrawal (uncomfortable but rarely lethal; buprenorphine or methadone); benzodiazepine withdrawal (seizures, can be lethal; long taper)
- **First-break psychosis workup and rule-outs:** TSH, UDS, electrolytes, CBC, CMP, HIV, RPR, head imaging if focal signs or atypical age
- **Serotonin syndrome vs NMS:** serotonin = clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor, autonomic surge; NMS = lead-pipe rigidity, hyperthermia, autonomic instability. Treat both with discontinuation of agent + supportive care; cyproheptadine for serotonin syndrome, dantrolene/bromocriptine for NMS
- **Suicide risk assessment:** SAD PERSONS or Columbia scale; access to means is the most modifiable risk factor

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish bipolar I, II, cyclothymia, and unipolar depression from one paragraph of history
- Manage acute withdrawal (alcohol, opioid, benzodiazepine) with correct medication and monitoring
- Assess suicide risk and decide on level of care without flinching

**Common traps**
- *Hospitalization or psychosis in a manic episode bumps Bipolar II up to Bipolar I.* The presence of full mania is the discriminator.
- *Bupropion and tramadol both lower seizure threshold;* in eating disorders or seizure history, avoid bupropion.
- *Lithium toxicity:* tremor, ataxia, hyperreflexia, seizures. Dialysis if serum >4.0 or severe symptoms.
- *Switching antidepressants:* fluoxetine has the longest half-life — needs a wash-out before MAO inhibitor.

**Authoritative sources:** DSM-5-TR criteria sheets, First Aid Step 2 CK Psychiatry, Online MedEd Psychiatry, UWorld, AMBOSS.

## Chapter 7: Family Medicine, Prevention, and Ambulatory Care
**Core questions:** What is the recommended screening, what is the appropriate counseling, and what is the longitudinal management plan?

**Key concepts**
- **USPSTF screening grades** (A and B are routinely covered): colorectal cancer screening starts at age **45** (Grade B); cervical cancer per current guidance; breast cancer (biennial mammography 50-74, individualized 40-49); lung cancer with low-dose CT for adults 50-80 with ≥20 pack-year history who quit ≤15 years ago; AAA one-time US in men 65-75 who ever smoked; depression and unhealthy alcohol use screening universally
- **Adult vaccinations across the lifespan:** Tdap (one dose adult, plus each pregnancy 27-36 wk); zoster recombinant for adults ≥50; pneumococcal PCV20 or PCV15 → PPSV23 sequence for adults ≥65 (or earlier with conditions); annual influenza; COVID per current guidance
- **Smoking cessation:** **5 A's** (Ask, Advise, Assess, Assist, Arrange); first-line meds — varenicline, bupropion SR, or nicotine replacement (combination patch + short-acting is most effective)
- **Alcohol screening:** AUDIT-C is the validated brief tool. Pharmacotherapy for AUD — naltrexone (oral or LAI) or acamprosate first-line; disulfiram for highly motivated and supervised
- **Opioid use disorder:** medication-assisted treatment with buprenorphine, methadone, or extended-release naltrexone; combine with behavioral therapy; harm reduction
- Common ambulatory complaints: headache (red flags = thunderclap, worst-of-life, new pattern over 50, focal signs), low back pain (red flags = trauma, malignancy history, fever, bowel/bladder, saddle anesthesia), fatigue, dyspepsia, dizziness
- Chronic disease management: hypertension (start with thiazide, ACE/ARB, or CCB; combination for stage 2; ACE/ARB preferred in DM and CKD); diabetes (metformin first; GLP-1 RA or SGLT2i preferred add-on for CV/renal risk); hyperlipidemia (statin by ASCVD risk)

**Applied skills**
- Build an age-appropriate screening list for a new adult patient in under a minute
- Counsel on cessation using brief motivational interviewing structure
- Manage chronic disease with the right intensification step rather than the showiest medication

**Common traps**
- *Colorectal cancer screening now starts at 45*, not 50, per 2021 USPSTF update.
- *Lung cancer screening eligibility:* age 50-80, ≥20 pack-years, current smoker or quit ≤15 years ago. Both criteria must be met.
- *AAA screening* is for men 65-75 who **ever smoked**, one-time abdominal ultrasound. The screening for never-smoking men is shared decision-making.
- *Tdap each pregnancy* (between 27 and 36 weeks), regardless of prior Tdap history, to pass antibodies passively to the newborn.

**Authoritative sources:** USPSTF recommendation database, CDC adult vaccination schedule, *AAFP Essentials*, UWorld FM tag, AMBOSS.

## Chapter 8: Biostatistics, Ethics, Patient Safety, and Quality Improvement
**Core questions:** What does this statistic mean, what is the ethically correct action, and which system improvement prevents the next event?

**Key concepts**
- **Test characteristics:** sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, likelihood ratios, ROC/AUC. **NNT = 1/ARR.** Sensitivity and specificity are intrinsic to the test; PPV and NPV depend on prevalence
- **Study designs:** case-control (rare disease, odds ratio), cohort (relative risk, hazard ratio), cross-sectional (prevalence), RCT (causal inference), systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Bias types:** selection (Berkson, healthy worker), recall, observer (Hawthorne), lead-time, length, attrition (loss to follow-up). Confounding vs effect modification — adjust for confounders; report stratified results when there's true effect modification
- **Ethics:** informed consent (capacity vs competence; capacity is decision-specific), advance directives + surrogate hierarchy, HIPAA, mandatory reporting (suspected elder abuse, child abuse, certain infectious diseases, gunshot wounds in most states), end-of-life — palliative vs hospice, futility, conscientious objection, AMA ethics
- **Patient safety taxonomy:** near miss (caught before reaching patient), adverse event without harm, adverse event with harm, sentinel event (serious harm or death). Just culture focuses on system-level prevention rather than punishment
- **QI tools:** PDSA cycles, run charts, control charts, root cause analysis, common-cause vs special-cause variation, Swiss cheese model of error

**Applied skills**
- Read a 2x2 table and produce sensitivity, specificity, and predictive values quickly
- Apply the four principles (autonomy, beneficence, nonmaleficence, justice) in a one-paragraph ethics vignette
- Diagnose a safety event and propose a system-level fix rather than a punitive one

**Common traps**
- *A diagnosis of dementia does not automatically remove capacity.* Capacity is **decision-specific** and requires the patient to communicate a choice, understand, appreciate, and reason.
- *Sentinel events* are unexpected occurrences involving **death or serious physical/psychological injury**. An intercepted error is a near miss, not a sentinel event.
- *Mandatory reporting:* you report suspected abuse — you do not need to confirm it.
- *Odds ratio ≈ relative risk only when the disease is rare* (roughly <10%).

**Authoritative sources:** First Aid Step 2 CK biostatistics/ethics, USMLE Easy biostats, AHRQ Patient Safety primers, UWorld biostats/ethics tags.

## Chapter 9: Capstone — Exam-Day Discipline
The clinical knowledge is only half the test; the other half is operating under nine hours of cognitive load. This capstone consolidates the test-day execution plan that turns *knowing it* into *getting it right*.

**Pacing**
- ~1.7 minutes per question, 40 questions per block, 60 minutes per block. Slightly more breathing room than Step 1 — but the vignettes are longer, so it does not feel that way.
- Build a per-block pacing checkpoint: at minute 20 you should be at question 12, at minute 40 you should be at question 24.
- **Never skip blindly.** Make your best educated guess on the first pass, flag if uncertain, and only return to flagged questions if time remains. Most question changes on review hurt more than they help.

**Attacking a vignette**
1. **Read the last line first.** The verb in the stem determines the answer family ("most likely diagnosis," "best initial test," "most accurate test," "definitive treatment," "most appropriate next step in management").
2. Scan demographics, vitals, and the key labs/imaging in that order.
3. Generate a one-sentence differential before reading the full stem.
4. Read the full stem only if the differential is not yet narrowed to a single answer.
5. Match the answer choices to the differential; eliminate before you choose.

**Breaks**
- ~45 minutes total break time across the day, distributed across seven breaks (between the eight blocks). Use them.
- Plan longer breaks after blocks 3 and 5 with food, water, and a quick walk. Eat what your stomach already tolerates; nothing experimental.

**Test-day prep**
- Sleep in the last 72 hours matters more than any cramming you can do in the same window.
- No new content the day before; review your error log and the highest-yield Step-Up to Medicine or First Aid pages.
- Light familiar breakfast, snacks packed, arrive 30 minutes early, bring earplugs.
- After the exam: do not debrief with peers in the first hour. Walk away, eat, sleep, and let the day be over.

The exam is winnable. It is also designed to feel unwinnable somewhere around blocks 5 and 6, when fatigue and the long vignettes compound. That feeling is universal and not diagnostic. Trust the preparation, work the next question, and finish the day.
