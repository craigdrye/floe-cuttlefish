# NALA CP: Certified Paralegal Exam Preparation
**ID**: `nalaCp` · **Discipline**: Law

## Course Aim
The NALA Certified Paralegal credential is the American paralegal generalist's calling card and a serious test of scope-of-practice awareness. The exam, delivered through Prometric on a rolling-administration basis, consists of approximately 120 multiple-choice questions plus a Skills Section assessing five critical thinking competencies, sat across four hours. The passing standard is 70%. NALA tests both substantive legal knowledge and the daily reflexes of a working paralegal — knowing what a paralegal does competently, and knowing, just as firmly, what a paralegal must never do.

Paralegals can do almost everything an attorney can do EXCEPT (1) give legal advice, (2) set fees, (3) accept cases, and (4) represent clients in court (with the narrow exception of certain administrative agencies such as the Social Security Administration). This unauthorized-practice-of-law boundary is the spine of the NALA exam. It is tested explicitly in the ethics questions and implicitly throughout — a question that asks "what should the paralegal do?" almost always rewards the answer that routes substantive legal judgment to the supervising attorney.

This syllabus treats the NALA exam content outline as the map and trains the habits a working American paralegal actually needs: clean client communications, precise terminology, rigorous Bluebook citation, defensible legal research using primary then secondary sources, and quick recognition of the UPL trap. The substantive coverage is broad — administrative law, bankruptcy, business organizations and commercial law, civil litigation, contracts, criminal law, family law, intellectual property, real estate, tort law, plus estate planning and probate — and the questions punish the candidate who specialises into a corner.

The voice of this course is a senior paralegal who is firm about ethics, calm under deadline pressure, intolerant of guesswork, and unimpressed by paralegals who drift into giving legal advice when an attorney is unreachable. Use US terminology and citation styles throughout: plaintiff not claimant, complaint not particulars of claim, motion for summary judgment under FRCP 56, demand for jury trial, Bluebook citation form for cases and statutes. If you cannot cite the rule by name and number, you cannot rely on it.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test NALA exam content: paralegal ethics under NALA's Code of Ethics and Professional Responsibility and the Model Standards and Guidelines for Utilization of Paralegals, communications skills, legal research methodology, judgment and analytical reasoning, and substantive legal terminology across the named disciplines. Use US terminology consistently: plaintiff not claimant, complaint not particulars of claim, deposition not examination for discovery, jury trial demand, motion for summary judgment, opposing counsel.

Floe presents four options per question — one correct, three plausible distractors — and the right answer is the one a competent paralegal under attorney supervision would actually take. Each distractor should map to a real workplace mistake: giving advice when none was sought, missing a Bluebook signal, drafting in the attorney's name without supervision, citing secondary authority where primary controls.

## Chapter 1: Paralegal Ethics and the Scope of Practice
**Core questions:** Where exactly is the line between competent paralegal assistance and the unauthorized practice of law? When the supervising attorney is unavailable, what may a paralegal do?

**Key concepts**
- NALA Code of Ethics and Professional Responsibility (eight canons)
- NALA Model Standards and Guidelines for the Utilization of Paralegals
- ABA Model Rule 5.3 on supervisory responsibilities regarding nonlawyer assistants
- ABA Model Rule 5.5 on UPL applied to paralegals via the supervising attorney
- Activities prohibited to paralegals: legal advice, setting fees, accepting cases, court appearance except in authorized administrative settings
- Confidentiality, conflicts of interest, and imputation of conflicts to the firm
- State-specific UPL definitions and exceptions (varies materially between jurisdictions)

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Decline to answer a substantive legal question without breaking rapport
- Identify a conflict during intake before the engagement letter is signed
- Recognize when administrative agency rules permit nonlawyer representation (e.g. Social Security under 42 USC 406)

**Common traps**
- Paralegals can do everything an attorney can do EXCEPT (1) give legal advice, (2) set fees, (3) accept cases, and (4) represent in court (except certain administrative agencies). Framing advice as "personal opinion" or "what I would do" does not cure UPL
- Treating client-facing conversations as automatically protected by attorney-client privilege — privilege requires the communication to be in the scope of the legal representation and confidentially maintained
- Failing to disclose paralegal status when the client assumes you are an attorney — NALA Code Canon 5 and Model Standard 5 require affirmative status disclosure
- Drafting and signing a substantive court document in the attorney's name without authority — UPL in nearly every jurisdiction

**Authoritative sources**
- NALA Code of Ethics and Professional Responsibility; NALA Model Standards and Guidelines
- ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct 5.3 and 5.5
- State bar UPL guidance for the paralegal's working jurisdiction

**Practice deliverables**
- UPL trap deck: 30 scenarios labeled "permitted," "prohibited," or "needs supervising attorney"
- One-page sheet on what to say when a client asks "what should I do?"

## Chapter 2: Communications for Paralegals
**Core questions:** How do you write a letter, email, or memo that informs without advising, supports the attorney's strategy, and protects privilege?

**Key concepts**
- Audience analysis: client, court, opposing counsel, agency, internal team
- Plain English drafting and the danger of legal jargon used wrongly
- Attorney-client privilege and the work-product doctrine under FRCP 26(b)(3)
- Settlement communications under FRE 408 ("For Settlement Purposes Only")
- Email risks: accidental waiver, reply-all, metadata, BCC mistakes
- Telephone discipline and message documentation

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Draft a status letter that updates without advising
- Write a memo to file that records a phone call accurately
- Spot privilege risks in an email chain before sending

**Common traps**
- Labeling a letter to opposing counsel as "Attorney-Client Privileged" — privilege protects communications between the client and counsel, not adversarial correspondence
- Forwarding a privileged email to a non-client third party and assuming privilege survives — waiver is the default
- Using "Without Prejudice" (English convention) instead of "For Settlement Purposes Only" (US convention under FRE 408)
- Promising an outcome to a worried client — moves from communications to legal advice

**Authoritative sources**
- FRE 408 (compromise offers); FRCP 26(b)(3) (work product)
- NALA Manual for Paralegals (West) chapters on legal writing

**Practice deliverables**
- Five mock client letters across litigation, probate, and family law
- Phone log template with required fields

## Chapter 3: Legal Research and Citation Discipline
**Core questions:** Where do you start when an attorney asks "is this still good law?" How do you record the trail so the attorney can rely on it?

**Key concepts**
- Primary versus secondary authority; mandatory versus persuasive authority
- Statute first, then regulations, then cases, then commentary
- Westlaw, Lexis, Bloomberg Law, Fastcase, free sources like CourtListener and Google Scholar
- Citators: Shepard's (Lexis) and KeyCite (Westlaw); treatment indicators (red, yellow, green)
- Bluebook basics for cases, statutes, regulations, and secondary sources

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Build a research log that captures query, sources, results, and date checked
- Convert a slip opinion citation into proper Bluebook form
- Update a five-year-old memo by checking each authority's current treatment

**Common traps**
- Citing a treatise or legal encyclopedia (Am. Jur., C.J.S.) as a substitute for primary authority — secondary sources are guides, not citations
- Treating an out-of-circuit federal case as binding on the working district — federal appellate decisions are mandatory only within their own circuit
- Missing a yellow Shepard's flag indicating the case has been criticized or distinguished — material to the attorney's reliance
- Citing the wrong reporter for a state case (e.g. citing the regional reporter only where parallel state citation is required under local rules)

**Authoritative sources**
- The Bluebook: A Uniform System of Citation (current edition)
- Westlaw, Lexis Bloomberg Law platform training; NALA Manual research chapters

**Practice deliverables**
- Three worked research memos with full citation trails
- Citation drill set: 40 raw citations to be Bluebooked correctly

## Chapter 4: Judgment, Analytical Ability, and Reading the Question
**Core questions:** When a 12-paragraph fact pattern hides one decisive detail, how do you find it quickly? How do you distinguish what the question asks from what the question seems to ask?

**Key concepts**
- Issue spotting frameworks: who, what, when, where, why, how
- Inference from indirect evidence
- Eliminating distractors that are technically true but not responsive
- Tracking time on long reading passages — roughly 2 minutes per MCQ
- Recognizing trap patterns: wrong jurisdiction, wrong era, wrong party

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Mark up a long fact pattern in under 60 seconds
- Build a personal "flag and return" rule for tough items
- Convert each missed practice question into an error-log entry

**Common traps**
- Reading a fact pattern set in state court and applying FRCP timelines — federal and state procedures diverge in service, discovery, and motions practice
- Failing to notice a date that places the question before a recent statutory amendment (e.g. pre-2018 TCJA alimony tax treatment)
- Selecting the most authoritative-sounding option rather than the option that matches the facts
- Spending more than 3 minutes on a single MCQ — recovery is statistically very hard

**Authoritative sources**
- NALA published practice exams; NALA Manual for Paralegals (West)
- Floe error-log workflow

**Practice deliverables**
- Two timed reading-comprehension sets per week
- Error log sorted by issue, distractor family, and time spent

## Chapter 5: Civil Litigation and the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure
**Core questions:** From complaint to verdict, what is the paralegal's role at each stage? When does state practice diverge meaningfully from the FRCP?

**Key concepts**
- FRCP pleadings: complaint (Rule 8 short and plain statement), answer, counterclaim, crossclaim, third-party
- Service under FRCP 4 and waiver under FRCP 4(d) (60 days to answer if waived)
- FRCP 12 motions: 12(b)(1) jurisdiction, 12(b)(6) failure to state a claim, 12(b)(7) failure to join
- Discovery under FRCP 26-37: interrogatories, document requests, depositions, requests for admission
- E-discovery duties under FRCP 26(f) conference and proportionality under FRCP 26(b)(1)
- Summary judgment under FRCP 56 (no genuine dispute of material fact); motions in limine
- Federal vs state jurisdiction; diversity under 28 USC 1332; federal question under 28 USC 1331
- Trial preparation: exhibits, witness binders, jury instructions

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Calendar deadlines under FRCP 6 including the additional 3 days for service by mail under Rule 6(d)
- Prepare a deposition outline that supports the attorney without leading
- Build a privilege log that survives a motion to compel

**Common traps**
- Treating a 12(b)(6) motion as testing evidence — the motion accepts the complaint's allegations as true and tests legal sufficiency only
- Forgetting that diversity jurisdiction requires complete diversity (Strawbridge v Curtiss) and an amount in controversy over $75,000
- Filing a privilege log that produces the document for opposing counsel to review — production defeats the privilege claim
- Calculating FRCP 6 deadlines from the wrong trigger date — service of the discovery request, not receipt by the attorney

**Authoritative sources**
- Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (current); 28 USC 1331, 1332, 1391
- Federal Practice and Procedure (Wright & Miller); NALA Manual litigation chapters

**Practice deliverables**
- FRCP timeline poster from filing to post-trial motions
- Sample privilege log with 20 entries and rationale

## Chapter 6: Contracts, Business Organizations, and Bankruptcy
**Core questions:** How are private agreements formed and broken? What entity should a small business client choose, and what happens when the business cannot pay its debts?

**Key concepts**
- Contract formation: offer, acceptance, consideration, mutual assent, capacity, legality
- Statute of frauds (sale of goods over $500 under UCC 2-201; real estate; one-year rule; suretyship)
- Parol evidence rule; conditions; breach (material vs minor); remedies (expectation, reliance, restitution; specific performance for unique goods)
- UCC Article 2 sales: merchants (UCC 2-104), perfect tender (UCC 2-601), gap fillers, warranties (express, merchantability 2-314, fitness for particular purpose 2-315)
- Entity choice: sole proprietorship, general partnership (UPA / RUPA), LP, LLP, LLC, S corp, C corp
- Federal tax classification: pass-through (partnership, LLC default, S corp) vs C corp double taxation
- Bankruptcy: Chapter 7 liquidation, Chapter 11 reorganization, Chapter 13 wage-earner plan
- Automatic stay under 11 USC 362 (with limited exceptions in subsection (b)); dischargeable vs nondischargeable debts under 11 USC 523
- Means test under 11 USC 707(b) for Chapter 7 eligibility

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Assemble a Chapter 7 petition package without advising on the choice of chapter
- Compare entity options on a single chart for an attorney-client meeting
- Draft a simple LLC operating agreement template for attorney review

**Common traps**
- Treating the statute of frauds as requiring formal contracts — a memorandum signed by the party to be charged often suffices, and the UCC merchant exception (2-201(2)) substitutes for the signature in some merchant contexts
- Confusing material breach (excuses non-performance and grants damages) with minor breach (only damages)
- Telling a client which chapter of bankruptcy to file — chapter choice is legal advice
- Calculating an S corp's eligibility without checking the 100-shareholder limit and the single-class-of-stock requirement under 26 USC 1361

**Authoritative sources**
- Restatement (Second) of Contracts; UCC Article 2; 11 USC (Bankruptcy Code)
- 26 USC subchapter S; state LLC statutes (e.g. Delaware LLC Act, ULLCA)

**Practice deliverables**
- Side-by-side entity comparison chart
- Bankruptcy intake checklist by chapter

## Chapter 7: Criminal Law, Family Law, and Administrative Law
**Core questions:** Where does the paralegal add value in matters that touch a client's freedom, family, or government benefits?

**Key concepts**
- Criminal law mental states: general intent vs specific intent vs strict liability
- Classification: felonies, misdemeanors, infractions; federal vs state offenses
- Criminal procedure: arrest, charging, arraignment, plea, trial, sentencing, appeal
- Fourth Amendment search and seizure; Fifth Amendment Miranda (Miranda v Arizona) and double jeopardy; Sixth Amendment counsel and confrontation
- Criminal defenses: self-defense, insanity (M'Naghten, MPC, irresistible impulse depending on jurisdiction), duress, intoxication
- Family law: marriage, dissolution (no-fault vs fault grounds by state), property division (equitable distribution vs community property), custody (best interests standard), child support guidelines, alimony / spousal support
- Post-2018 TCJA (Tax Cuts and Jobs Act): alimony is no longer deductible to the payor or taxable to the recipient for divorces finalized after December 31, 2018
- UCCJEA and interstate jurisdiction in family cases (home-state preference)
- Prenuptial agreements under the Uniform Premarital Agreement Act (UPAA); adoption
- Administrative law: Administrative Procedure Act (5 USC), notice-and-comment rulemaking under 5 USC 553, formal adjudication, judicial review under 5 USC 706
- Nonlawyer representation permitted in some agencies (Social Security, IRS within scope of representation)

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Prepare a suppression motion outline for attorney review without arguing it
- Draft a parenting plan template that respects state-specific factors
- Represent a claimant at a Social Security hearing where rules permit

**Common traps**
- Treating Miranda as required at the point of arrest — Miranda attaches to custodial interrogation, not to arrest itself
- Forgetting that alimony tax treatment for divorces finalized on or before December 31, 2018 remains deductible/taxable under prior law unless modified after that date with the parties electing TCJA treatment
- Applying UCCJEA continuing jurisdiction without confirming the issuing state retains a significant connection
- Treating notice-and-comment rulemaking as mandatory for all agency action — interpretive rules and policy statements are exempt under 5 USC 553(b)(A)

**Authoritative sources**
- Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure; Model Penal Code; Administrative Procedure Act
- UCCJEA; UPAA; state family-law statutes; NALA Manual for Paralegals

**Practice deliverables**
- Criminal procedure timeline from arrest through appeal
- Family law intake form covering jurisdiction, assets, and children

## Chapter 8: Real Estate, Estate Planning, Probate, and Intellectual Property
**Core questions:** How does title pass cleanly from seller to buyer? How does property pass cleanly from decedent to heir or beneficiary? How are intangible creations protected as IP?

**Key concepts**
- Real estate: purchase agreement, title search, title insurance, deed types (general warranty, special warranty, quitclaim), recording acts (race, notice, race-notice)
- Financing: mortgage, deed of trust, promissory note, RESPA, TILA disclosures
- Closing process: Closing Disclosure (TRID) replacing HUD-1
- Estate planning: wills, revocable and irrevocable trusts, durable powers of attorney, healthcare proxies, advance directives
- Probate administration: opening the estate, notice to creditors, inventory, accounting, distribution
- Intestate succession variations across community property (e.g. CA, TX, WA) and common-law states
- Federal estate tax exemption (currently $13.61 million per individual for 2024, scheduled to sunset)
- Patents under 35 USC 101 (utility), 102 (novelty), 103 (nonobviousness), 112 (specification); design and plant patents
- Copyrights under 17 USC; original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium; fair use four factors under 17 USC 107
- Trademarks under the Lanham Act (15 USC 1051 et seq.); distinctiveness spectrum (generic, descriptive with secondary meaning, suggestive, arbitrary, fanciful)
- Trade secrets under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA, 18 USC 1836) and state UTSA

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Assemble a closing binder with every required signature tab
- Draft a simple will under attorney supervision using the firm's template
- Prepare a probate inventory that reconciles to bank statements

**Common traps**
- Treating a quitclaim deed as conveying full warranty — a quitclaim conveys only whatever interest the grantor has, with no warranty of title
- Recording acts: applying race jurisdiction logic in a notice-recording state — recording rules vary by state
- Distributing estate assets before the creditor-notice period has run — exposes the personal representative to personal liability
- Treating fair use as a defense without analyzing all four 17 USC 107 factors (purpose, nature of work, amount and substantiality, market effect)
- Confusing distinctiveness with strength: descriptive marks require secondary meaning to be protectable; generic terms are never protectable

**Authoritative sources**
- 35 USC (Patents); 17 USC (Copyright); 15 USC 1051 et seq. (Lanham Act); 18 USC 1836 (DTSA)
- Restatement (Third) of Property; Uniform Probate Code; state-specific recording statutes

**Practice deliverables**
- Closing checklist from contract to recorded deed
- Probate timeline with statutory deadlines flagged
- IP intake checklist by patent, copyright, trademark, trade secret

## Chapter 9: Tort Law in Practice
**Core questions:** What does a paralegal need to know about intentional torts, negligence, strict liability, and products liability to support tort litigation from intake through trial?

**Key concepts**
- Intentional torts: battery, assault, false imprisonment, IIED, trespass to land and chattels, conversion
- Negligence elements: duty, breach, factual causation (but-for), proximate causation (foreseeable scope), damages
- Defenses: contributory negligence (a minority of states), comparative negligence (pure vs modified)
- Strict liability: abnormally dangerous activities (Restatement Second 519-520); ultrahazardous activities
- Products liability: manufacturing defect, design defect (consumer expectations vs risk-utility), failure to warn; theories include negligence, strict liability under Restatement Second 402A, breach of warranty
- Vicarious liability: respondeat superior, independent contractor exceptions
- Damages: compensatory (general and special), punitive (limited by BMW v Gore and State Farm v Campbell ratios), collateral source rule

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Assemble a personal injury intake file with medical records, wage loss, and witness contact
- Draft a damages summary for the attorney's settlement letter
- Build a chronology of treatment and earnings for discovery production

**Common traps**
- Confusing assault (apprehension of imminent harmful or offensive contact) with battery (actual contact)
- Applying contributory-negligence rules in a comparative-negligence state — pure comparative reduces damages but does not bar recovery; modified comparative bars recovery above the threshold
- Treating proximate cause as identical to factual cause — proximate cause limits scope of liability to foreseeable harm even where but-for causation exists
- Forgetting that punitive damages must satisfy due process — single-digit ratios are the practical ceiling under State Farm v Campbell

**Authoritative sources**
- Restatement (Second) of Torts; Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability
- State pattern jury instructions; NALA Manual tort chapters

**Practice deliverables**
- Personal injury intake template with medical authorization release
- Damages chart with general, special, and projected categories

## Chapter 10: Capstone — Exam Day Strategy and Skills Section
**Core questions:** How do you pace four hours, 120 MCQs, and the Skills Section across multiple practice areas in one sitting without your judgment fraying? How do you apply the five critical thinking competencies under exam pressure?

**Key concepts**
- MCQ pacing target of roughly 2 minutes per question
- Skills Section: assesses critical thinking, communications, ethics, legal research, and judgment through applied scenarios
- Two-pass strategy: answer easy items first, then return to flagged ones
- Distractor pattern recognition: right rule wrong facts, right facts wrong rule, secondary authority masquerading as primary
- Ethics override: when scope-of-practice quietly changes the answer
- Test-day logistics: ID, breaks, snacks, sleep, travel timing, Prometric-specific procedures

**Applied paralegal skills**
- Build a personal pacing plan based on practice-test data
- Convert each practice miss into a one-sentence rule on a flashcard
- Decide when to guess and move versus flag and return

**Common traps**
- Spending more than 3 minutes on a single MCQ — net negative across the section
- Changing first-instinct answers without specific contrary reasoning — research consistently shows this lowers scores
- Reading the Skills Section as factual recall — it tests the application of skills, not raw knowledge
- Ignoring the ethics overlay in non-ethics questions — UPL traps appear inside civil litigation, family law, and real estate questions

**Authoritative sources**
- NALA published practice exams and content outline
- NALA Manual for Paralegals (West); West Paralegal Series
- Floe error-log workflow

**Practice deliverables**
- One full simulated exam two weeks before test day
- Error log sorted by topic and distractor family
