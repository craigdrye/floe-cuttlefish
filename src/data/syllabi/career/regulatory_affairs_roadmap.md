# Regulatory Affairs Roadmap
**ID**: `regulatoryAffairsRoadmap` - **Discipline**: Healthcare - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This is the on-ramp for someone walking into a regulatory affairs (RA) function in life sciences for the first time — a new RA associate, a scientist or engineer moving from the bench into submissions, a quality or clinical professional picking up regulatory duties, or a med-device/pharma startup employee who suddenly owns "the FDA stuff." Regulatory affairs is the discipline of turning a product and its evidence into a case the right authority will accept, and then keeping that case true for the rest of the product's life. The work is not paperwork for its own sake: every document is an argument that a specific product, with a specific intended use, is safe and effective (or substantially equivalent) enough to be sold — and that the company can prove it. This course teaches the shape of that argument so a beginner can step into a submission team and understand the machine they've joined rather than just filling in a template they don't yet trust.

The spine of the course is a single repeating decision: **what is this product, what claim are we making, which pathway does that force, what evidence does that pathway demand, and can a reviewer find it.** Everything downstream — the submission, the label, the changes, the post-market reports — flows from how that question is answered up front. We build on the frameworks that actually govern the work: the US drug and biologic pathways (IND, NDA under 505(b)(1)/(b)(2), ANDA, BLA, the 351(k) biosimilar route) and the device pathways (510(k), De Novo, PMA, and the Q-Submission/pre-sub program), the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act and 21 CFR, the EU's split system (the centralised/MAA drug route under EMA and the medical-device regime under EU MDR 2017/745 / IVDR 2017/746 with Notified Bodies), and the common-sense rule that the **intended use you claim is the intended use you must support**. Two questions sit under every decision in this course: **does the evidence support the claim, and is the record defensible to a reviewer or an inspector?**

By the end you should be able to write a product profile and intended-use statement, classify a product and select a defensible pathway with precedent, plan the evidence a submission needs and find the gaps before a reviewer does, assemble a reviewer-usable submission (eCTD for drugs, the relevant device format), review a label and a promotional claim against what the evidence and the approved labeling actually support, judge whether a change requires a new submission, and triage a post-market signal into the right report on the right clock. This is the practical foundation of an entry-level RA role and the floor under the RAC (Regulatory Affairs Certification). For the adjacent depth — the day-to-day pharmacovigilance loop, trial-site conduct, or the compliance/quality system that surrounds RA — pair this roadmap with the **Pharma Drug Safety**, **Clinical Research**, and **Healthcare Compliance** tracks.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests entry-level regulatory-affairs judgment: defining intended use and product classification; choosing a submission pathway and supporting it with precedent; planning evidence and finding submission gaps; assembling and formatting a submission for review; interacting with the agency (meetings, pre-subs, questions, deficiency responses); labeling and promotional-claim review; change control and the "does this need a new submission?" decision; and post-market obligations (complaints, adverse events, field actions, periodic reports). The bar is *making the next defensible regulatory decision and documenting why* — not memorizing every form number or guidance clause. Where the work is shared with safety, the protocol, or the quality system, keep the RA lens (pathway, claim, submission, defensibility) and send the deeper safety/site/QMS detail to the sibling tracks.

The voice is a calm senior regulatory lead orienting a sharp new associate: concrete, unhurried, every acronym tied to a real submission task the first time it appears. Where a fact appears it must be current and correct (the US drug/biologic and device pathways, 21 CFR, eCTD, EU MDR/IVDR and the centralised route), because a beginner who learns a wrong pathway or a wrong reporting clock carries the error into a filing. Distractors should be the genuine beginner mistakes — claiming an indication the data doesn't support, forcing a product into 510(k) when there's no real predicate, treating "the FDA approved it" as covering a market it never touched, calling a labeling change "minor" to dodge a submission, sitting on a reportable event — each encoding one specific failure mode. When a scenario is ambiguous, the right answer is the one that keeps the claim honest and the record reconstructable.

### Where to start
There is one named credential behind much of this — the **RAC (Regulatory Affairs Certification)** from RAPS — but the real exam is day-one competence on a submission team. Work the chapters in order, because RA is sequential: you cannot choose a pathway before you've pinned the intended use, plan evidence before you know the pathway's demands, or reason about a change before you understand what was originally cleared. After each chapter, do its applied skill on a real or invented product rather than re-reading; RA work is production (writing the intended-use statement, the pathway memo, the gap table, the labeling redline), not recognition. Drill the Common traps as their own set, because nearly every clinical hold, refuse-to-file, deficiency letter, and warning letter maps to one of them.

## Chapter 1: The RA Function, Intended Use, and the Product Profile
- **Core questions**: What does regulatory affairs actually own, and where does it sit between R&D, clinical, quality, and marketing? What *is* this product, in regulatory terms — and what exactly are we claiming it does? Which single decision (the intended use / indication) silently determines every downstream obligation?
- **Key concepts**: RA as the owner of the regulatory strategy and the agency relationship; the difference between **intended use** (what the maker claims/promotes) and **indication for use** (the specific clinical condition/population); the product profile (what it is, who it's for, the claims, the markets, the risk, the evidence it will need); how a single word in the claim ("treats" vs. "supports," "cure" vs. "manage") changes the product's regulatory class; the principle that the company defines the intended use — and is then bound by it.
- **Applied skills**: write an intended-use / indication statement that is specific enough to support and narrow enough to win; build a one-page product profile (intended use, target market, key claims, risk, anticipated evidence) for a real or invented product; spot where marketing's draft claim is broader than what RA can defend.
- **Common traps**: writing a vague or aspirational intended use that the evidence can't support; conflating intended use with indication and mis-scoping the submission; letting marketing's language quietly expand the claim beyond the approved use; treating RA as a document-filing service rather than the owner of strategy and agency contact.

## Chapter 2: Classification — Drug, Biologic, Device, or Combination
- **Core questions**: Is this a drug, a biologic, a device, an IVD, or a combination product — and who at the agency owns it? What risk class does it fall into, and what does that class trigger? When the answer is genuinely unclear, how do you get a binding determination?
- **Key concepts**: the statutory definitions (drug vs. device vs. biological product) and why the boundary matters; **device risk classes** (US Class I/II/III; EU MDR Class I/IIa/IIb/III) and how class maps to required controls and pathway; **combination products** and the **primary mode of action (PMOA)** that decides the lead FDA center (CDER, CBER, or CDRH); the **Request for Designation (RFD)** when jurisdiction is contested; software as a medical device (SaMD) as a live classification frontier.
- **Applied skills**: classify a product (and a borderline combination product) and name the lead center and likely risk class; for a contested case, decide whether an RFD is warranted and state the PMOA argument; map a product's risk class to the controls and pathway it implies.
- **Common traps**: assuming "device" because it's hardware when the PMOA is actually a drug (or vice versa); guessing a risk class instead of reasoning from the rule and intended use; ignoring that EU and US classification can differ for the same product; missing that software or an accessory can be a regulated device in its own right.

## Chapter 3: Pathways and Precedent — Choosing the Route
- **Core questions**: Which submission pathway does this product and claim force? What precedent (predicate, reference product, prior approval) supports it? What is the fastest *defensible* route — not just the fastest one?
- **Key concepts**: the **US drug/biologic pathways** — IND (to begin clinical trials), NDA via 505(b)(1) (full data) vs. 505(b)(2) (relying partly on others' data), ANDA (generics), BLA, and the 351(k) biosimilar route; the **US device pathways** — 510(k) (substantial equivalence to a **predicate**), De Novo (low/moderate-risk, no predicate), PMA (Class III, full safety/effectiveness evidence), and exemptions; the **EU routes** — the centralised/MAA drug procedure via EMA, and the MDR/IVDR device route through a **Notified Body** with CE marking; expedited programs (Breakthrough, Fast Track, Accelerated Approval; device Breakthrough) and what they do and don't change; precedent as the backbone of any pathway argument.
- **Applied skills**: select a pathway for a given product/claim and justify it with a named precedent or reference; identify when there is *no* valid predicate and a 510(k) must become a De Novo or PMA; write a short pathway memo comparing two routes with their assumptions, evidence burden, timeline, and risk.
- **Common traps**: forcing a 510(k) when no genuinely equivalent predicate exists; assuming a 505(b)(2) can borrow data it has no right to reference; treating an expedited designation as lowering the evidence bar rather than changing the interaction/timeline; choosing the pathway before the intended use and risk class are settled.

## Chapter 4: Evidence Planning and the Submission Gap
- **Core questions**: What evidence does this pathway actually require, and do we have it? Which missing piece blocks the submission — and who owns closing it? Does the evidence support the *claim*, or just exist?
- **Key concepts**: the evidence pillars — **nonclinical** (toxicology, biocompatibility, bench/performance testing), **clinical** (trials, real-world evidence, or for many 510(k)s, none), and **quality/CMC** (chemistry-manufacturing-controls, or device design/manufacturing); the GxP foundations the data must rest on (GLP, GCP, GMP) and why **data integrity / ALCOA+** decides whether evidence counts; the gap analysis — comparing required evidence to available evidence before filing; **refuse-to-file (RTF)** and clinical-hold risk as the cost of filing with a gap; evidence traceability so a claim maps to a study maps to a dataset.
- **Applied skills**: build an evidence-gap table (required item, source document, owner, status, risk, remediation) for a planned submission; trace a specific claim back to the study and data that support it; decide whether a gap is a blocker (don't file) or a manageable risk (file with a rationale).
- **Common traps**: filing into a known gap and absorbing an RTF or a deficiency letter that costs more than the delay would have; collecting evidence that exists but doesn't actually support the specific claim; ignoring data-integrity problems in otherwise complete data; leaving a gap with no owner so it's still open at filing.

## Chapter 5: Building the Submission — Format, Structure, and the Reviewer
- **Core questions**: How is this submission physically structured, and can a reviewer navigate it without a map? Is the argument findable — does every claim point to its support? What does this specific submission type require in its sections?
- **Key concepts**: the **eCTD** (electronic Common Technical Document) and its modules (M1 regional/admin, M2 summaries, M3 quality, M4 nonclinical, M5 clinical) as the standard structure for US/EU drug submissions; the analogous structure of a **510(k)/PMA** device submission and the EU MDR **technical documentation**; the summary documents (e.g., the clinical/nonclinical overviews) that frame the reviewer's first read; cross-referencing and the cover letter as navigation; **reviewer usability** — the discipline of making the answer easy to find, because a reviewer who can't find support treats it as absent.
- **Applied skills**: lay out where a given piece of evidence belongs in an eCTD (or device dossier) and how the summary points to it; draft a cover letter / submission roadmap that tells the reviewer what's inside and where; sanity-check that every key claim in the summary is traceable to a section that supports it.
- **Common traps**: a complete-but-unnavigable submission where the data exists but the reviewer can't find it; summaries that overclaim relative to the underlying sections; putting content in the wrong module and triggering a technical-rejection or RTF; treating volume ("we sent everything") as the same as a clear argument.

## Chapter 6: Agency Interactions — Meetings, Pre-Subs, and Responses
- **Core questions**: What do we actually want from the agency, and what's the right venue to ask? What single question, if answered, most de-risks the program? When a deficiency letter or hold arrives, how do we respond so it closes rather than escalates?
- **Key concepts**: the structured **meeting types** (FDA Type A/B/C, and the device **Q-Submission / pre-sub** program; EU **scientific advice**) and matching the question to the venue; the **meeting package / briefing document** as the thing that determines the meeting's value; getting agency agreement on pathway, endpoints, or design *before* spending the money; deficiency mechanisms — the **clinical hold**, the **refuse-to-file**, the **Complete Response Letter (CRL)**, the device **Additional Information (AI) request** — and how to respond directly and completely; documenting agency feedback as a binding-ish commitment.
- **Applied skills**: draft the focused list of questions for a pre-sub or Type C meeting (the questions whose answers change the plan); write a deficiency-response plan that addresses each point head-on with evidence rather than argument; capture an agency commitment in a memo the team can rely on.
- **Common traps**: bringing vague or unanswerable questions to a costly meeting; treating informal agency feedback as either worthless or absolutely binding when it's neither; responding to a CRL/AI request by re-arguing instead of supplying what was asked; not documenting what the agency actually said and re-litigating it later.

## Chapter 7: Labeling, Claims, and Promotional Review
- **Core questions**: Does this wording stay inside the approved intended use, or does it quietly expand it? Is every claim substantiated by the labeling or the data? Where's the line between truthful promotion and off-label or misleading promotion?
- **Key concepts**: the **label / labeling / IFU** (instructions for use) as a regulated document, not marketing copy; the approved indication, contraindications, warnings/precautions, and boxed warning as boundaries; **substantiation** — every efficacy/comparative/safety claim must be supported; **off-label promotion** and **misbranding** risk; fair balance and the prohibition on misleading claims; promotional review (the US **OPDP** lens; the medical/legal/regulatory — **MLR** — review) as the gate before anything external ships; consistency between the label, the promotional piece, and the cleared/approved use.
- **Applied skills**: review a draft claim or label change against the approved indication and the supporting data, and mark each line supported / unsupported / off-label; redline a promotional piece for unsubstantiated or expansive claims; write a claims-review table (proposed language, support, risk, decision).
- **Common traps**: approving a claim that subtly broadens the indication beyond what was cleared; promoting an off-label use because "physicians do it anyway"; comparative claims with no head-to-head evidence; dropping or shrinking risk information to make the piece read better (loss of fair balance).

## Chapter 8: Change Control and Lifecycle Maintenance
- **Core questions**: We're changing the product, process, or label — does this change require a new submission, a supplement, or just a documented internal change? What was *originally* cleared/approved, and does this change still fit inside it? Could an inspector reconstruct why we decided what we decided?
- **Key concepts**: change types — **design**, **manufacturing/process**, **labeling**, **supplier/site** — and their regulatory impact; the US decision frameworks for "does this need a filing" (drug/biologic **PAS / CBE-30 / CBE-0 / annual report** under 21 CFR 314.70; device "**when to submit a new 510(k)**" guidance; EU MDR significant-change rules); the **regulatory impact assessment** as the documented reasoning behind the call; keeping the approved file (the "regulatory baseline") current so the dossier still matches the marketed product; versioning and traceability of changes.
- **Applied skills**: assess a proposed change and decide the filing route (new submission / supplement / annual report / no filing) with a written rationale; map a manufacturing or labeling change against the original clearance to test whether it still fits; document a change so the next reviewer/inspector can follow the logic.
- **Common traps**: calling a change "minor" to dodge a filing it actually requires; making the change first and assessing the regulatory impact afterward; letting the marketed product drift away from the approved dossier; an undocumented "we decided it was fine" with no impact assessment behind it.

## Chapter 9: Post-Market Surveillance and Reporting
- **Core questions**: Is this complaint, malfunction, or adverse event **reportable** — and if so, to whom and on what clock? Does this warrant a **field action** (recall/correction)? Do our periodic reports tell one coherent, current safety story?
- **Key concepts**: the post-market machinery — **complaints**, **adverse events**, malfunctions, and the reportability decision; US device **Medical Device Reporting (MDR, 21 CFR 803)** and the drug adverse-event/FAERS route, with their **timelines** (e.g., 30-day vs. 5-day device reports); **recalls / corrections and removals** and field-safety actions; **periodic/aggregate reports** (device PMS reports / EU MDR PSUR; drug periodic reports) as the cumulative benefit-risk narrative; how a post-market signal can feed back into a label change, a change-control filing, or a field action; the line between RA's reportability call and the safety function's case processing (the deep loop lives in the Pharma Drug Safety track).
- **Applied skills**: make a reportability decision for a complaint/AE — reportable or not, recipient, clock, Day 0, rationale — and log it; decide whether a problem rises to a field action and what triggers it; check that a periodic report's narrative matches the underlying event data and the current label.
- **Common traps**: under-reporting a malfunction or AE by deciding privately it "wasn't really related"; starting the reporting clock from the wrong date; treating a recall as a PR problem rather than a regulatory obligation with its own process; periodic reports that contradict the label or the individual reports beneath them.

## Capstone
Take one fictional product — pick a moderate-risk medical device, a 505(b)(2) drug, or a combination product — and carry it through the full regulatory arc, producing the records each stage generates. **Start with identity and claim**: write the intended-use / indication statement and a one-page product profile, then classify the product (lead center, risk class, and — if it's a combination product — the PMOA argument). **Choose the route**: write a pathway memo selecting the submission route, naming the precedent (predicate, reference product, or comparable approval) and stating why a faster route doesn't work. **Plan the evidence**: build an evidence-gap table tying each required item to its source, owner, and risk, and flag the one gap that would block filing. **Prepare to engage and submit**: draft the focused question list for a pre-sub / Type C meeting, and sketch where the key evidence sits in the eCTD or device dossier with a one-paragraph cover-letter roadmap. **Defend the claim**: take one proposed marketing claim and review it against the indication and the data, marking it supported, unsupported, or off-label, with a fix. **Maintain the product**: a post-launch manufacturing change is proposed — write the regulatory-impact assessment and the filing decision (new submission / supplement / annual report / none) with rationale. **Watch it in the field**: a field complaint of a malfunction with a patient injury arrives — make the reportability call (reportable? recipient? clock? Day 0?) and decide whether it triggers a field action. The deliverable is a single ordered regulatory worklog — identity and claim first, then classification, pathway, evidence, submission/agency, labeling, change control, and post-market. The grade is simple: at every step, does the evidence support the claim, and would the record survive a reviewer and an inspector?
