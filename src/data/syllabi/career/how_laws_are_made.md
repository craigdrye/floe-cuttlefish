# How Laws & Decisions Get Made
*Stop arguing about politics and start understanding the machine. Follow a real bill from a member's hopper to the President's desk — and learn where it actually dies.*

**ID**: `howLawsAreMade` · **Discipline**: Civics & Government · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Almost everyone has opinions about what the government should do, and almost no one can describe how it actually does anything. That gap shows up everywhere: in confused headlines ("the bill passed!" — passed what, exactly?), in arguments that mistake an executive order for a law, and in a general sense that decisions happen in a black box. This course opens the box. It is for the curious adult who wants to follow the real process — calmly, concretely, and without partisanship — so the news finally makes sense.

We build it the way the process actually runs. You'll trace a bill from the moment a member introduces it, through the committee where most legislation quietly dies, into markup, onto the floor, against the Senate's filibuster and the 60-vote wall of cloture, around it through budget reconciliation, into a conference committee to reconcile two chambers' clashing versions, and finally onto the President's desk to be signed, vetoed, ignored into law, or pocket-vetoed. Then we widen out to the parts of government that shape law without being "laws" at all: agency rulemaking and regulations, executive orders, and the Supreme Court's power to interpret and strike down statutes.

The throughline is judgment, not trivia. By the end you should be able to look at any government action and ask the right questions: Who is the actor — Congress, an agency, the President, a court? What kind of thing is this — a statute, a regulation, an order, a ruling? Which step is it at, and who controls the next one? We keep it strictly nonpartisan and process-first, because the mechanics are the same no matter who is in power — and understanding them is what turns a spectator into an informed citizen.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want to genuinely understand how government decisions get made: someone following a contentious bill, a new citizen or first-time voter, a professional whose work touches regulation, or anyone tired of nodding along to civics they never actually learned. This is a "follow the actual process" course, not exam prep — it favors mechanism and decision-making over dates and names to memorize. Questions are scenario-driven ("a bill has 54 votes but can't pass — why?", "a program is authorized but unfunded — what's missing?") because that's how these confusions show up in real life. The tone is deliberately nonpartisan: every example is structural, and the mechanics are presented as they work regardless of party. Difficulty is mixed — foundational "what is a veto" items sit beside multi-step puzzles about chokepoints, reconciliation, and the authorization-versus-appropriation distinction that trips up even attentive news readers.

## Chapter 1: From Idea to Introduced Bill
- **Core questions**: Who can actually introduce a bill? What's the difference between a bill and a resolution? Why must revenue bills start in the House? What are companion bills?
- **Key concepts**: sponsorship, the member-only power to introduce, bills vs simple/concurrent resolutions, the Origination Clause, companion bills.
- **Applied skills**: separating "wrote the draft" from "introduced the bill"; recognizing when a measure carries no force of law; spotting a chamber-of-origin problem.
- **Common traps**: thinking the President or a lobbyist can introduce a bill; treating an honorary resolution as a stalled law; assuming senators can lead on revenue bills.

## Chapter 2: Committees & Markup
- **Core questions**: Why do most bills die in committee? What actually happens in a markup? How much power does a committee chair have? How can a majority force a stuck bill out?
- **Key concepts**: committee referral, the chair's agenda-setting power, markup and amendments, reporting a bill out, the discharge petition.
- **Applied skills**: explaining why "referred to committee" often means "shelved"; describing markup as the real editing stage; naming the chair as the gatekeeper.
- **Common traps**: imagining an automatic timer that forces bills to the floor; confusing markup with the final floor vote; thinking a sponsor can override a chair's inaction.

## Chapter 3: The Filibuster & Cloture
- **Core questions**: What does a filibuster actually do? How many votes does cloture take? What is the "nuclear option"? Why does the House have no filibuster?
- **Key concepts**: the filibuster as a block on the final vote, the 60-vote cloture threshold, the gap between 60 (to proceed) and 51 (to pass), the nuclear option, the House Rules Committee's debate limits.
- **Applied skills**: explaining why a 54-vote majority can still fail; distinguishing "pass the bill" from "end debate"; knowing the filibuster is a Senate tradition, not a constitutional rule.
- **Common traps**: confusing cloture (60) with passage (51) or with override (two-thirds); assuming the Constitution mandates the filibuster; thinking the House has one too.

## Chapter 4: Budget Tools — Reconciliation, Authorization & Appropriations
- **Core questions**: What is reconciliation for, and how does it dodge the filibuster? What does the Byrd Rule strip out? How does authorization differ from appropriation? What causes a shutdown, and what's a continuing resolution?
- **Key concepts**: budget reconciliation as a filibuster-proof simple-majority track, the Byrd Rule, authorization (the ceiling) vs appropriation (the check), government shutdowns, continuing resolutions, the debt ceiling as a separate thing.
- **Applied skills**: knowing why narrow majorities reach for reconciliation; explaining a "funded but not really" program; distinguishing a shutdown from a debt-ceiling crisis.
- **Common traps**: treating authorization as funding; blaming a shutdown on a veto; confusing the debt ceiling (borrowing) with appropriations (spending authority).

## Chapter 5: Both Chambers Agree — Conference & Final Passage
- **Core questions**: Why can't two different versions become law? What does a conference committee do? Why can't members amend a conference report?
- **Key concepts**: bicameralism and the identical-text requirement, conference committees, the conference report as a take-it-or-leave-it package, amendment exchanges between chambers.
- **Applied skills**: explaining why both chambers must pass the same words; identifying where clashing versions get reconciled; recognizing the up-or-down nature of a final report.
- **Common traps**: thinking two separate approvals are enough; assuming one chamber's version automatically wins; believing a conference report can be re-amended on the floor.

## Chapter 6: The President — Signature, Veto & Override
- **Core questions**: What are the President's real options? What does an override take? What happens if the President does nothing — in session, and after adjournment? What is a signing statement?
- **Key concepts**: sign vs veto (no line-item veto federally), the two-thirds override, the ten-day rule (becomes law in session), the pocket veto (dies after adjournment), signing statements.
- **Applied skills**: stating the President's choices precisely; computing why most vetoes stand; reading the timing of inaction; distinguishing a signing statement from a veto or order.
- **Common traps**: thinking the President can edit a bill; confusing cloture (60) with override (two-thirds); assuming inaction always kills a bill; mistaking a signing statement for a line-item veto.

## Chapter 7: Laws vs Rules — Rulemaking & Executive Orders
- **Core questions**: Why do agencies make rules? What is notice-and-comment? When a regulation conflicts with a statute, which wins? What can — and can't — an executive order do? How are orders and rules undone differently?
- **Key concepts**: delegated authority and rulemaking, regulations with the force of law, notice-and-comment under the APA, the Constitution > statute > regulation hierarchy, executive orders as directives to the executive branch, the asymmetry of rescinding orders vs repealing rules.
- **Applied skills**: identifying the actor (Congress, agency, President) behind any action; explaining why a rule can't override its statute; predicting how durable an order or rule is.
- **Common traps**: thinking agencies "pass laws"; assuming a newer regulation beats a statute; believing an executive order creates new law or can be erased as easily as a finalized rule.

## Chapter 8: The Courts Shape the Law
- **Core questions**: What is judicial review? What is stare decisis? How do cases reach the Supreme Court? How does interpreting a statute shape policy?
- **Key concepts**: judicial review (Marbury v. Madison), precedent and stare decisis, the writ of certiorari and a discretionary docket, statutory interpretation as binding meaning.
- **Applied skills**: naming judicial review when a court strikes a law; distinguishing following precedent from striking laws; explaining how the Court sets its own agenda via cert; seeing interpretation as policy-shaping.
- **Common traps**: calling a court ruling a "veto"; confusing judicial review with stare decisis; thinking courts amend or write statutes rather than interpret them.

## Chapter 9: Influence — Lobbying & Interest Groups
- **Core questions**: What is lobbying, really, and how does it differ from bribery? Why does information give lobbyists influence? What is a PAC? What is the revolving door?
- **Key concepts**: lobbying as protected petitioning, the informational basis of influence, registration and disclosure, PACs and super PACs, the revolving door and cooling-off rules.
- **Applied skills**: distinguishing lawful advocacy from corruption; explaining why thin staffs lean on outside expertise; placing PACs and lobbying in the money-and-access picture.
- **Common traps**: equating all lobbying with bribery; thinking lobbyists can introduce bills or vote; confusing lobbying (advocacy) with PACs (campaign money) or the revolving door (careers).

## Chapter 10: How States & the System Differ
- **Core questions**: Do all state legislatures look like Congress? Why can a governor strike single spending lines? What is a ballot initiative? When state and federal law clash, which wins?
- **Key concepts**: bicameral norm and Nebraska's unicameral exception, the governor's line-item veto (which the President lacks), ballot initiatives and direct democracy, the Supremacy Clause and federal preemption.
- **Applied skills**: spotting where the federal model is common but not universal; explaining a line-item veto at the state level; recognizing direct-democracy tools; resolving a federal-state conflict.
- **Common traps**: assuming every state copies Congress; thinking the President has a line-item veto; confusing an initiative with a discharge petition; believing recency or "closeness" beats federal law.

## Chapter 11: Following the Whole Process
- **Core questions**: What's the standard order of steps? At which stage do most bills die? How do you tell statute, regulation, and executive order apart? What does "passed the House" really mean? When is 51 not enough? Why is a program funded vs authorized?
- **Key concepts**: the introduction-to-President skeleton, committee as the great die-off, naming the right tool, single-chamber passage, the filibuster wall on non-budget bills, the authorization/appropriation split.
- **Applied skills**: locating where any bill is stuck; reading headlines process-aware; matching each chokepoint to who controls it; running an "is it authorized and funded?" check.
- **Common traps**: misordering the steps; overweighting vetoes and filibusters as the usual killers; treating single-chamber passage as law; conflating funding with authorization.

## Chapter 12: Deeper Cuts & Common Confusions
- **Core questions**: What are riders, earmarks, and omnibus bills? What is the Congressional Review Act? How do treaties differ from executive agreements? How is the Constitution amended? What's a quorum? What are the committee types? What does the House Rules Committee do?
- **Key concepts**: riders and earmarks, omnibus packages, the Congressional Review Act, treaties (two-thirds Senate) vs executive agreements, the amendment process, quorum vs vote thresholds, standing/select/joint/conference committees, the House Rules Committee.
- **Applied skills**: spotting how controversial items hitch rides; distinguishing the CRA from other tools; explaining why constitutional change is rare; separating "enough present" (quorum) from "enough votes."
- **Common traps**: confusing a rider with an earmark or a CR; thinking an ordinary law can override the Constitution; mixing up quorum with cloture or a supermajority; conflating committee types.

## Capstone
Pick a real or imagined government action — a bill in the news, a regulation, an executive order, a court ruling — and narrate it the way this whole course teaches. First, name the actor and the kind of thing it is: statute, regulation, order, ruling, resolution. Then locate it on the process map. If it's a bill, trace where it actually is and where it's most likely to die: did it clear committee, survive the floor, beat a filibuster (or use reconciliation), reconcile with the other chamber, and reach the President? Identify the current chokepoint and exactly who controls the next step — a committee chair, 60 senators, both chambers, the President, or a two-thirds override. If it's a regulation, ask what statute authorizes it and whether notice-and-comment was followed; if an order, ask what authority it rests on and how durable it is; if a ruling, ask whether it's interpretation or judicial review. State plainly what would have to happen next for it to take effect or be undone. The goal isn't to predict the outcome — it's to demonstrate the judgment this course builds: name the actor, name the tool, find the chokepoint, and follow the actual process instead of the headline.
