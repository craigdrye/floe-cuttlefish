# CRCM Banking Compliance
**ID**: `crcm` | **Discipline**: Finance | **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course prepares you to think and work like a bank compliance manager — and, along the way, to pass the American Bankers Association's Certified Regulatory Compliance Manager (CRCM) exam — without pretending those are two different skills. The job is not memorizing regulations; it is making a bank's products, processes, and people defensible against a stack of consumer-protection rules that change faster than any single person can track. A compliance manager's real output is judgment with a paper trail: this product touches these rules, here is the control that satisfies each one, here is the evidence it worked, and here is what I escalated when it didn't. The learner who can do that reasoning is the one who passes the exam *and* survives the exam that matters more — the regulatory examination of a real bank.

The course follows the actual logic of the role rather than the table of contents of a regulation. You will start with the compliance management system (CMS) — the scaffolding examiners grade first — then learn to map any bank product to the obligations it triggers across its lifecycle. From there you walk the substantive rule families a compliance manager owns: the core consumer regulations (Truth in Lending, ECOA, EFTA, RESPA, expedited funds availability, deposit and overdraft disclosures), the unfair-deceptive-abusive (UDAP/UDAAP) and complaints lens, fair lending and community obligations (HMDA, CRA, the redlining and pricing risks, plus SCRA and MLA), and the privacy and information-protection rules where consumer compliance touches financial crime. Then come the manager's craft skills the exam weights heavily: monitoring and testing, issue management, regulatory-change management, and reporting to senior management and the board. BSA/AML and sanctions get treated as an *interface* here — what the compliance manager must coordinate and report — because the deep financial-crimes analyst track lives in separate Floe courses.

By the end you should be able to take a product, a complaint trend, a fair-lending statistic, or a new rule and do four things a hiring manager and an examiner both want: identify which obligation is actually in play, name the control and the evidence that prove it is met, decide what is a finding and what gets escalated, and write it up so a board member, an auditor, and a regulator reach the same conclusion from the same record. Tools and core systems differ between banks; this reasoning, and the discipline of the defensible record behind every decision, is portable.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when learners want the *consumer-regulatory compliance-manager* track: CMS design, product-to-rule mapping, the consumer-protection regulation families (Reg Z, B, E, CC, DD; RESPA; HMDA; CRA; UDAP/UDAAP; SCRA; MLA; GLBA privacy and safeguarding; FCRA/FACT Act), complaint and fair-lending analytics, monitoring and independent testing, issue and regulatory-change management, board reporting, and examiner readiness — framed around the ABA CRCM exam. Keep it distinct from `bankComplianceAmlRoadmap` (the analyst on-ramp into bank financial-crimes work) and `amlCompliance` (ACAMS/CAMS exam and money-laundering typologies); when a question is purely about KYC, sanctions screening, alert triage, or SAR mechanics, it belongs in those courses, and this one should treat BSA/AML only as a coordination-and-reporting interface.

The voice is a seasoned bank compliance officer mentoring someone moving from line work into the compliance function: concrete (a real product, a real complaint, a real exam finding), plain-language about what each rule is *for*, no acronym left undefined on first use, and a relentless focus on the *defensible decision and the record behind it*. Every chapter surfaces Common traps, because in consumer compliance the costly error is almost never exotic — it is the manager who confuses a written policy with a working control, treats a disclosure as compliance with the substance behind it, calls a fair-lending statistic a violation, files a complaint away without spotting the trend, or reports activity volume to the board as if it were risk. Keep facts current and frame the moving parts honestly: this is a politically and legally volatile area, and the right habit is to teach the durable principle and flag what is contested. As of 2026, several recent CFPB rules have been reversed — the credit-card late-fee rule (the proposed $8 safe harbor) was **vacated in April 2025**, leaving the existing Reg Z late-fee safe harbors in force, and the December 2024 overdraft rule was **repealed under the Congressional Review Act (P.L. 119-10)**, meaning no substantially similar rule may be reissued without new legislation. The Section 1071 small-business lending data rule (Reg B) was **re-finalized on May 1, 2026** with a narrower scope (a 1,000-covered-transaction threshold) and a single January 1, 2028 compliance date, and remains subject to litigation; the Section 1033 open-banking rule is unsettled. Teach the principle; never present a contested specific as permanently settled. Distractors should be plausible-but-flawed, the calls a real compliance officer might mistakenly make — not silly.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The CRCM is a 200-question, multiple-choice exam delivered over four hours, scored on the ABA's scaled 200–800 model with 500 required to pass. For the content outline effective **June 2026**, the ABA reorganized the exam into three domains:
- **Core Compliance (37%)** — the role-critical heavy hitters: Regulation E (EFTA), Regulation Z (TILA), Regulation B (ECOA), CRA, HMDA, flood insurance, UDAP/UDAAP, and the BSA/OFAC interface.
- **Foundational Compliance (37%)** — the broader rule set, split between primary rules (~23%) and ancillary rules (~14%): RESPA, Reg CC, Reg DD, GLBA privacy and safeguarding, FCRA/FACT Act, SCRA, MLA, advertising rules, and others.
- **Compliance Management System (26%)** — risk assessment, monitoring and testing, issue and change management, complaint management, training, and board reporting.

Two implications for how you study. First, weight your effort toward *reasoning* in the Core domain rather than rote coverage of every ancillary rule. Second, the CMS domain is more than a quarter of the exam and is where line-experienced candidates lose points — they know the rules but not the manager's machinery (testing methodology, issue rating, escalation, examiner readiness). A workable rhythm: master the CMS scaffolding first (Chapters 1, 7, 8) so every rule has somewhere to live; then learn product-to-rule mapping (Chapter 2) as your retrieval framework; then take the rule families (Chapters 3–6) in passes, each pass tied to a fact pattern rather than a definition. Use released-style scenario questions, not flashcards — the exam rewards "which obligation is triggered and what does it require," not "define APR."

## Chapter 1: The Compliance Management System
- **Core questions**: What is the bank actually controlling for, and who owns each control? How does an examiner decide whether a compliance program is adequate before reading a single disclosure? Where does compliance set the rule versus own the risk?
- **Key concepts**: the compliance management system (CMS) as board and senior-management oversight plus a working compliance program (policies, procedures, training, monitoring, and consumer-complaint response); the three lines of defense (the business owns and operates the control; compliance sets standards and monitors; internal audit independently tests); the difference between a policy, a procedure, and a control that actually fires; risk assessment as the engine that points compliance resources at the highest-harm products and rules; the regulatory expectation that the CMS scales to the bank's size, complexity, and risk profile.
- **Applied skills**: draw a one-page CMS map for a product that links each obligation to its control, the control's owner, the evidence the control produces, and the escalation path; distinguish "we have a policy" from "we have a control" for a given requirement.
- **Common traps**: treating a written policy as proof a control works; assuming compliance "owns" a risk the line of business actually owns; building a risk assessment that scores every rule the same and therefore prioritizes nothing; treating examiner readiness as a project you do before an exam rather than a steady-state property of good documentation.

## Chapter 2: Product-to-Rule Mapping
- **Core questions**: Which rules attach to *this* product, and at which moment in the customer's journey does each obligation arise? What system owns the data that proves the obligation was met? When the product changes, which controls must change with it?
- **Key concepts**: building a rule inventory for a product (a checking account, a mortgage, a credit card, an auto loan); mapping obligations to lifecycle events (marketing, application, underwriting, account opening, disclosure delivery, servicing, error resolution, collection, closure); the idea that timing is itself a requirement (many rules specify *when* a disclosure or notice must be delivered, not just what it says); change triggers (a new fee, a new channel, a new vendor, a product redesign) that should force a re-mapping; data lineage — knowing which system of record produces the evidence a control depends on.
- **Applied skills**: produce a product-rule matrix for one bank product listing each requirement, the lifecycle trigger, the responsible owner, the control, and the evidence; identify the rule-mapping change forced by a single product modification (e.g., adding an overdraft option or a digital application channel).
- **Common traps**: mapping rules to the product in the abstract instead of to the moments in the customer journey where they bite; ignoring servicing and collection because they feel post-sale; forgetting that a third-party vendor performing a function does not transfer the bank's compliance obligation; treating the matrix as a one-time artifact rather than something a change trigger must revise.

## Chapter 3: Core Lending Disclosures — Reg Z, Reg B, and RESPA
- **Core questions**: What is each disclosure regime actually trying to make the customer able to see or do? Which trigger creates the obligation, and what is the deadline? When does a pricing or underwriting decision require an adverse-action notice — and what must it say?
- **Key concepts**: the Truth in Lending Act / Regulation Z (cost-of-credit disclosure, APR and finance charge, the TRID integrated mortgage disclosures, the right of rescission, credit-card and advertising rules, and the late-fee safe harbor — noting that the proposed $8 cap was vacated in 2025 and the existing safe harbor framework stands); the Equal Credit Opportunity Act / Regulation B (the adverse-action notice, its timing, and the specific-reasons requirement; the prohibition on discouraging applicants); RESPA (the prohibition on kickbacks and unearned fees under Section 8, escrow and servicing rules, and its overlap with TRID); the difference between a disclosure obligation and a substantive prohibition.
- **Applied skills**: given a credit transaction, name which disclosure or notice is required, its content, and its deadline; write the reasoning for an adverse-action notice that states specific, accurate principal reasons; spot a RESPA Section 8 risk in a marketing-services or referral arrangement.
- **Common traps**: confusing the APR with the interest rate (the APR includes certain finance charges and is the comparison metric the law cares about); treating a delivered disclosure as compliance when the underlying conduct or timing was wrong; writing an adverse-action reason so generic ("does not meet our standards") that it fails Reg B's specificity requirement; missing that RESPA Section 8 can be violated by a thing of value exchanged for referrals even when a disclosure was given.

## Chapter 4: Deposit, Payments, and Funds Rules — Reg E, Reg CC, and Reg DD
- **Core questions**: When a customer disputes an electronic transaction, what does the bank owe them and on what clock? What must a deposit account disclose before and during the relationship? When must funds be made available?
- **Key concepts**: the Electronic Fund Transfer Act / Regulation E (consumer liability limits for unauthorized EFTs, the error-resolution procedure and its investigation timelines, provisional credit, and the application to debit cards and P2P payments); the expedited-funds rules under Regulation CC (availability schedules, exception holds, and the notices that must accompany them); the Truth in Savings Act / Regulation DD (account disclosures, the annual percentage yield, and fee and overdraft disclosure — noting that the December 2024 CFPB overdraft rule was repealed under the Congressional Review Act, so the prior disclosure-and-opt-in framework governs); how these rules interlock at the point of a disputed or held transaction.
- **Applied skills**: walk a Reg E error claim through intake, the investigation deadline, provisional credit, and resolution, identifying the bank's obligations at each step; determine the funds-availability outcome and required notice for a deposit under Reg CC; check a deposit-account disclosure for Reg DD completeness.
- **Common traps**: applying the wrong Reg E liability tier because the timing of the customer's report was misread; treating a Reg E "error" narrowly and missing that the definition includes more than fraud; placing an exception hold under Reg CC without the required notice; assuming an overdraft program is fine because a fee was disclosed, without confirming the opt-in and disclosure mechanics that remain in force.

## Chapter 5: UDAP/UDAAP, Complaints, and Customer Harm
- **Core questions**: Is there actual or likely customer harm here, and does the practice cross into unfair, deceptive, or abusive territory even if every technical disclosure was correct? What does a single complaint tell me, and what does the *trend* tell me?
- **Key concepts**: the unfair-or-deceptive standard (FTC Act Section 5) and the added "abusive" prong under Dodd-Frank's UDAAP; the three-part unfairness test (substantial injury, not reasonably avoidable, not outweighed by benefits), the deception standard (a representation likely to mislead a reasonable consumer to their detriment), and what "abusive" adds (taking unreasonable advantage of a consumer's lack of understanding or inability to protect their interests); complaint management as both a control and an early-warning radar; root-cause analysis and remediation; the heightened scrutiny of practices affecting vulnerable customers, fees, and add-on products.
- **Applied skills**: run a practice or marketing claim through the unfairness and deception tests and state the conclusion with reasons; turn a cluster of complaints into a trend memo with root cause, affected population, a UDAAP analysis, and a recommended remediation; decide what to escalate.
- **Common traps**: assuming a fully disclosed practice cannot be UDAAP (disclosure does not cure substantive unfairness); treating complaints as customer-service tickets to close rather than data to aggregate; doing root-cause analysis on the individual complaint but never on the pattern; recommending remediation for the complainant while leaving the unharmed-but-affected population untouched.

## Chapter 6: Fair Lending and Community Obligations
- **Core questions**: Which protected-class risk does this pattern actually present, and is it disparate treatment, disparate impact, or neither? Does a statistical disparity prove a violation, or only frame a question? Is the bank meeting its community and servicemember obligations?
- **Key concepts**: the fair-lending framework under ECOA/Reg B and the Fair Housing Act; the three methods of proof (overt discrimination, disparate treatment, disparate impact); redlining and steering; pricing and underwriting disparity analysis; HMDA data collection and reporting and how it fuels fair-lending and redlining review (and the separate, recently re-finalized Section 1071 small-business lending data rule under Reg B — narrower scope, a 1,000-transaction threshold, a January 1, 2028 compliance date, and ongoing litigation, so treat its specifics as live); the Community Reinvestment Act (assessment areas, performance evaluation); the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA) interest-rate cap and protections and the Military Lending Act (MLA) coverage and the 36% Military APR.
- **Applied skills**: classify a fair-lending fact pattern by method of proof and name the data that would confirm or rebut it; read a pricing or denial disparity and state what it does and does not establish; check an SCRA or MLA obligation against a specific account; build a fair-lending and community-risk dashboard with metrics, exceptions, and follow-ups.
- **Common traps**: calling a statistical disparity a violation without testing for a legitimate, non-discriminatory explanation; analyzing disparate treatment and forgetting disparate impact (or vice versa); confusing SCRA (active-duty protections, the 6% cap on pre-service debt) with MLA (the 36% MAPR on certain consumer credit) and applying the wrong one; treating HMDA as a reporting chore rather than the evidence base examiners use to open a redlining inquiry.

## Chapter 7: Privacy, Information Protection, and the Financial-Crimes Interface
- **Core questions**: What customer information is protected and how must it be safeguarded and shared? Which credit-reporting obligations attach when the bank uses or furnishes consumer-report data? Where does consumer compliance hand off to BSA/AML, and what must the compliance manager coordinate and report?
- **Key concepts**: the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) Privacy Rule (privacy notices, the opt-out for sharing with nonaffiliated third parties) and the Safeguards Rule (the information-security program expectation); the Fair Credit Reporting Act and FACT Act (permissible purpose, adverse-action and risk-based-pricing notices, dispute and furnisher accuracy duties, the Red Flags identity-theft rules); the BSA/AML and OFAC sanctions program as an *interface* the compliance manager coordinates — CIP, customer due diligence and beneficial ownership, SAR/CTR reporting, and sanctions screening live operationally in the financial-crimes function (see the AML courses), but the CRCM owns the coordination, the consumer-facing disclosures, and the board reporting that tie them together.
- **Applied skills**: identify which privacy notice or safeguard applies to a data-sharing or vendor scenario; determine the FCRA notice triggered by a use of consumer-report data; draw a cross-functional control map showing where consumer compliance, privacy, information security, and financial crimes hand off and who reports what to the board.
- **Common traps**: conflating the GLBA Privacy Rule (sharing and notice) with the Safeguards Rule (security program) and applying the wrong one; missing a risk-based-pricing or adverse-action notice obligation under FCRA; assuming BSA/AML is "someone else's problem" and failing to report the interface risk upward; treating a vendor's handling of protected information as outside the bank's obligation.

## Chapter 8: Monitoring, Testing, and Issue Management
- **Core questions**: What should be tested, how large a sample proves the point, and does the evidence support closing the finding? Is the control failing in design or only in execution? How do I rate and track an issue so remediation actually happens?
- **Key concepts**: the difference between monitoring (the line and compliance checking their own work, ongoing) and independent testing (a separate, objective review); risk-based scoping and sampling (population, sample size, judgmental versus statistical sampling); testing control *design* versus control *operating effectiveness*; findings, root cause, and corrective-action plans; issue rating and aggregation; validating remediation before closing a finding; how monitoring and testing feed the risk assessment and the board report.
- **Applied skills**: write a monitoring or test plan with scope, population, sample, test steps, expected evidence, exceptions, an issue rating, and a remediation tracker; decide whether an exception is a design failure or an execution failure and route it accordingly; judge whether evidence supports closing a finding or whether the fix was cosmetic.
- **Common traps**: testing a sample too small or too convenient to support the conclusion; confusing a control that is well-designed but not operating with one that is operating but poorly designed; rating every issue the same and burying the material one; closing a finding on the promise of a fix rather than evidence the fix works; testing the same low-risk things every quarter because they are easy while high-risk areas go untested.

## Chapter 9: Regulatory-Change Management and Board Reporting
- **Core questions**: What actually changed, which products and controls does it touch, and what is the bank's implementation path and deadline? What does senior management and the board need to know — and how do I report risk without drowning them in activity?
- **Key concepts**: regulatory-change management (horizon scanning, impact assessment against the product-rule matrix, implementation planning, policy and procedure updates, and validation); distinguishing a rule that is final and in force from one that is proposed, stayed, vacated, or repealed — and reporting that status honestly (the 2025–2026 reversals of the late-fee and overdraft rules, the re-finalized Section 1071 rule, and the unsettled Section 1033 open-banking rule are live examples of why status, not headlines, drives the work); board and senior-management reporting (a concise risk-and-status page: what changed, exposure, control status, metrics, open issues, and decisions needed); the difference between activity metrics and risk metrics; examiner readiness as the by-product of all of the above.
- **Applied skills**: write a regulatory-change impact memo that states what changed, the products and controls affected, the implementation plan, and the deadline; build a one-page board report that pairs each volume metric with a quality or risk metric and surfaces the decisions leadership must actually make; assess and communicate the *status* of a contested rule without overstating its certainty.
- **Common traps**: implementing to a headline or a proposed rule before it is final (or treating a vacated/repealed rule as still binding); assessing a change's impact in the abstract instead of against the product-rule matrix; reporting activity ("we reviewed 4,000 complaints") to the board as if it were risk; presenting a contested or shifting rule to leadership as settled; assuming examiner readiness is a pre-exam scramble rather than a steady state.

## Capstone
Take a single fictional bank and one of its consumer products — say, a checking account with an optional overdraft service, or a consumer mortgage — from rule-mapping through a board report, producing the work product at each stage. Build: (1) a one-page CMS map placing the product in the three lines of defense and linking obligations to controls, owners, evidence, and escalation; (2) a product-rule matrix tying each requirement to its lifecycle trigger, owner, control, and evidence; (3) a complaint-and-UDAAP trend memo for a cluster of complaints about the product, with root cause, affected population, the unfairness/deception/abusive analysis, and recommended remediation; (4) a fair-lending and community-risk read on the product's pricing or underwriting that states what any disparity does and does not establish and checks any SCRA/MLA exposure; (5) a cross-functional control map showing where consumer compliance, privacy, information security, and the BSA/AML interface hand off and who reports what; (6) a monitoring or test plan with scope, sample, test steps, an issue rating, and a remediation tracker; and (7) a regulatory-change impact memo and a one-page board report that pairs volume with risk metrics and states honestly the *status* of one contested rule affecting the product (e.g., overdraft, late fees, or Section 1071). Close with one paragraph naming the single piece of evidence that, if it surfaced, would change your overall risk conclusion for the product — the habit that separates a compliance manager who reports a defensible position from one who reports a confident one.
