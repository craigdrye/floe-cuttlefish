# How Voting & Elections Work
The mechanics behind the ballot: how US elections are actually run, counted, and decided.

**ID**: `votingElections` · **Discipline**: Government

## Course Aim
Most of us learn election trivia in fragments, an Electoral College fact here, a gerrymandering headline there, and never get the full machine. This course is for the curious adult who wants to understand how US elections genuinely operate, end to end, from the moment you register to the instant Congress declares a president. It is deliberately nonpartisan and mechanism-focused: not who should win, but how winning is determined.

The big surprise for most learners is how decentralized the whole system is. There is no national election agency, no single voter roll, and no uniform set of rules. Your county runs your polling place, your state sets your registration deadline and ID requirements, and the federal layer mostly sets outer limits and regulates campaign money. Once that clicks, a lot of confusing news, why results take weeks, why swing states dominate, why two parties persist, suddenly makes sense.

By the end, you should be able to read any election story and locate it: Is this about registration, nomination, the Electoral College, counting, districts, methods, money, or direct democracy? You will know which body is responsible, what the mechanism is, and where the common misconceptions hide. The aim is durable civic literacy you can return to every cycle.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test the mechanics of US elections: voter registration and eligibility, primaries vs caucuses, the Electoral College, vote counting and certification, recounts and audits, apportionment, redistricting and gerrymandering, ballot types and voting methods (mail, early, in-person), ranked-choice and runoffs, midterm vs presidential cycles, federal vs local administration, the FEC and campaign-finance basics, and ballot initiatives, referenda, and recalls. Keep the tone clear, friendly, and rigorous, never partisan. Prefer concrete scenarios over abstractions, and always name the responsible body (state, county, Congress, FEC, courts) so learners build an accurate mental map of who controls what.

## Chapter 1: Registering and Eligibility
**Core questions:** Who decides whether you can vote, and how do you get on the rolls? Why do the rules feel so different from state to state?

**Key concepts**
- State and county administration; no national voter roll
- The NVRA ("Motor Voter"), registration deadlines, same-day registration
- Voter ID variation, felony disenfranchisement, list maintenance and purges
- Provisional ballots as a safety net

**Applied skills**
- Look up your own state's registration deadline and ID rule before assuming
- Decide what to do if your name is missing from the roll
- Tell the difference between lawful list maintenance and improper removal

**Common traps**
- Assuming a single national voter list or federal ID standard exists
- Confusing the FEC (money) with election administration (states)
- Believing same-day registration is available everywhere

## Chapter 2: Primaries, Caucuses, and Nominations
**Core questions:** How does each party choose its nominee before the general election, and why do delegate counts matter more than raw votes?

**Key concepts**
- Primary (secret ballot) vs caucus (in-person meeting)
- Open, closed, semi-closed, and top-two ("jungle") primaries
- Delegates, conventions, and how nominations are formally won

**Applied skills**
- Predict whether an independent can vote in a given primary
- Read delegate math instead of fixating on popular-vote percentages
- Distinguish convention delegates from Electoral College electors

**Common traps**
- Mixing up open and closed primaries
- Thinking primaries elect officials rather than nominate candidates
- Confusing party-run caucuses with state-run primaries

## Chapter 3: The Electoral College
**Core questions:** Why don't Americans elect the president by national popular vote, and how does the elector system actually allocate power?

**Key concepts**
- Electoral votes = House seats + 2 senators; 270 to win; 269 ties go to the House
- Winner-take-all (48 states + DC) vs district allocation (Maine, Nebraska)
- Historical origins as a federalism compromise
- Faithless electors and Chiafalo v. Washington

**Applied skills**
- Compute a state's electoral votes from its congressional delegation
- Explain how a candidate can win the popular vote yet lose
- Identify when an election would go to the House

**Common traps**
- Thinking 269 is enough, or that a plurality wins
- Assuming the popular-vote winner always wins
- Believing electors are always free agents

## Chapter 4: Casting Your Ballot
**Core questions:** What are your real options for voting, and what safeguards protect the integrity and secrecy of your ballot?

**Key concepts**
- Absentee vs no-excuse mail-in voting; vote-by-mail states
- In-person early voting and Election Day voting
- The secret (Australian) ballot; drop boxes; signature verification and curing

**Applied skills**
- Choose the most reliable way to return a mail ballot on a tight timeline
- Explain why the secret ballot defeats vote buying and coercion
- Recognize legitimate identity safeguards that never reveal your choices

**Common traps**
- Treating "absentee" and "mail-in" as fundamentally different mechanisms
- Assuming emailing or phoning in a vote is a normal option
- Confusing signature verification with checking how you voted

## Chapter 5: Counting, Certifying, and Recounts
**Core questions:** Why do official results take so long after the networks "call" a race, and what makes a result final and trustworthy?

**Key concepts**
- Media projections vs official certified counts
- The canvass, certification, and the order before inauguration
- Recount triggers (margin thresholds, requests) and risk-limiting audits
- Voter-verifiable paper trails

**Applied skills**
- Explain the gap between election-night calls and certified totals
- Identify when an automatic recount kicks in
- Describe how a risk-limiting audit confirms a winner by sampling

**Common traps**
- Believing the FEC certifies results or that concessions are required
- Assuming full hand recounts are routine
- Thinking paper records slow things down rather than enabling audits

## Chapter 6: Districts, Apportionment, and Gerrymandering
**Core questions:** How are the lines that decide your representation drawn, and how can the same votes produce very different seat outcomes?

**Key concepts**
- Census-driven apportionment of 435 House seats vs within-state redistricting
- Gerrymandering; packing and cracking
- Independent/bipartisan commissions
- Rucho v. Common Cause and state-court challenges

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish apportionment (how many seats) from redistricting (where lines go)
- Spot packing and cracking in a described map
- Identify where partisan gerrymandering can still be challenged

**Common traps**
- Confusing apportionment with redistricting
- Thinking the FEC or the President draws districts
- Assuming federal courts must strike down any partisan map

## Chapter 7: Voting Methods and Ranked-Choice
**Core questions:** How do different vote-counting rules change who wins, and what problems do ranked-choice and runoffs try to solve?

**Key concepts**
- Plurality / first-past-the-post and the spoiler effect
- Ranked-choice (instant-runoff) voting and elimination rounds
- Traditional majority-runoff (two-round) systems
- Duverger's law and the two-party tendency

**Applied skills**
- Determine a plurality winner and recognize when no one has a majority
- Walk through an instant-runoff elimination round
- Explain why plurality tends toward two dominant parties

**Common traps**
- Assuming plurality requires a majority
- Confusing instant-runoff (one ballot) with traditional runoffs (separate election)
- Thinking RCV prevents a majority winner rather than manufacturing one

## Chapter 8: Cycles, Offices, and Federalism
**Core questions:** What is actually on the ballot in different years, and who runs the elections at each level of government?

**Key concepts**
- Midterm vs presidential cycles; turnout differences
- House (2-year, all seats) vs Senate (6-year, staggered thirds)
- Off-year state and local elections; special elections
- Local administration of a single combined ballot

**Applied skills**
- Explain why midterm turnout is lower than presidential turnout
- State how often each chamber faces voters
- Identify who administers your ballot in any given year

**Common traps**
- Thinking midterms are minor or local-only
- Assuming federal and local races are run by separate agencies
- Believing the federal government runs polling places

## Chapter 9: Money, the FEC, and Campaign Finance
**Core questions:** Where does campaign money come from, what limits apply, and what does the FEC actually do?

**Key concepts**
- The FEC's role: contribution limits, disclosure, enforcement (not running elections)
- Direct contribution limits vs PACs and super PACs
- Citizens United / SpeechNow and independent expenditures
- Disclosure and "dark money"

**Applied skills**
- Explain why a donor can't give a candidate unlimited money directly
- Distinguish a PAC from a super PAC by what each can do
- Describe what made spending change after Citizens United

**Common traps**
- Thinking the FEC runs elections or counts votes
- Believing super PACs can donate directly to candidates
- Assuming Citizens United removed direct contribution limits or disclosure

## Chapter 10: Direct Democracy and Ballot Measures
**Core questions:** When do voters make law directly, and how do initiatives, referenda, and recalls differ?

**Key concepts**
- Ballot initiatives (citizen-originated law)
- Referenda (voters approve/reject a measure)
- Recall elections (removing an official)
- No federal initiative or referendum process

**Applied skills**
- Tell an initiative apart from a referendum
- Distinguish a recall (voters remove an official) from impeachment (legislature removes)
- Explain why there is no national ballot measure

**Common traps**
- Swapping the definitions of initiative and referendum
- Confusing a recall with impeachment or a referendum
- Assuming direct democracy exists at the federal level

## Capstone
Trace one full election cycle for a hypothetical voter. Start by registering them correctly under a specific state's rules; walk through whether they can vote in that state's primary; have them cast a ballot using a chosen method with its safeguards; follow their single combined ballot through local administration, counting, certification, and any audit; map their House and Senate races onto the right term cycles; locate a ballot initiative and a recall on their ballot and classify each correctly; and finally follow the presidential contest through winner-take-all allocation to the 270 threshold and Congress's joint-session count. At each step, name the responsible body and call out one common misconception. A learner who can narrate this cleanly has the mental map this course is built to deliver.
