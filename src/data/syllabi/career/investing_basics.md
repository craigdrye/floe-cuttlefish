# Investing: From Zero to Index Fund
Demystify the market, skip the hype, and build the quiet habits that actually grow money over a lifetime.
**ID**: `investingBasics` · **Discipline**: Personal Finance · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Most people are never taught how investing actually works, so it stays a fog of jargon, scary headlines, and stock tips from confident strangers. This course is for the curious adult who wants to clear that fog: to understand what stocks, bonds, and funds really are, why a boring index fund quietly beats most clever strategies, and how to start with whatever they have today.

The throughline is a single liberating idea that the evidence keeps confirming: simple, low-cost, diversified, and consistent beats complicated, expensive, and reactive. You do not need to predict the market, pick winners, or have a fortune to begin. You need to understand a handful of concepts well enough to make a confident default decision and then mostly leave it alone.

By the end you should be able to tell ownership from lending, index from active, an ETF from a mutual fund, and an account from an investment; explain compounding, diversification, and dollar-cost averaging in plain words; spot the fees and myths that quietly drain returns; and sketch a sensible starter plan for your own timeline. This is education, not personalized financial advice — the goal is literacy and good judgment, not a tip.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want foundational investing literacy as a curious beginner, not exam prep or professional finance training. The course assumes zero prior knowledge and leans on concrete, everyday scenarios (a 401(k) match, a scary crash headline, a 0.05% vs 0.80% fund) rather than formulas or market forecasting. The voice is warm and plain — Crash-Course energy — but never dumbed-down-wrong. Difficulty is mixed: definitional literacy (what is a bond?) sits alongside judgment calls (where should a 2-year house fund go?) and behavioral traps (what do you do when the market plunges?). Safety and the "this is not personalized advice" boundary are woven through, and the course explicitly hands off complex, personal situations to a fee-only fiduciary rather than pretending to replace one. Every distractor carries a bespoke explanation so wrong answers teach as much as right ones.

## Chapter 1: What You Can Actually Own
**Core questions**: What do you really own when you buy a share? How is a bond different from a stock? Why is cash a hidden risk over decades? What is a fund?
**Key concepts**: stock as ownership, bond as a loan with interest, the risk-return difference between them, funds as baskets of many investments, inflation eroding cash.
**Common traps**: thinking a stock guarantees gains; confusing a bond with a savings account; assuming cash is risk-free; believing a fund is a single hand-picked stock.

## Chapter 2: Index vs Active, ETFs vs Mutual Funds
**Core questions**: What does an index fund try to do? Why do most active funds lose to it? How do ETFs and mutual funds differ? What is the S&P 500?
**Key concepts**: indexing (match, don't beat), active management and its fee drag, ETF intraday trading vs mutual fund daily pricing, broad market indexes, total-market vs sector exposure.
**Common traps**: thinking active managers are simply better; believing ETFs are riskier because they trade more; assuming mutual funds are always free; mistaking one sector for diversification.

## Chapter 3: Diversification and Risk
**Core questions**: What is diversification actually for? Why is one beloved stock dangerous? How are risk and return linked? Why mix stocks and bonds?
**Key concepts**: spreading risk, single-stock failure, the risk-return tradeoff, blending assets to smooth the ride, paper (unrealized) vs realized losses.
**Common traps**: expecting diversification to guarantee no loss; thinking careful research removes single-stock risk; believing higher returns can come risk-free; treating a price dip as a locked-in loss.

## Chapter 4: Time, Compounding, and Dollar-Cost Averaging
**Core questions**: What is compounding really? Why does starting early matter so much? How fast does money double? What is dollar-cost averaging and why automate it?
**Key concepts**: growth on growth, time in the market, the Rule of 72, dollar-cost averaging, the behavioral edge of automation.
**Common traps**: confusing your own contributions with compounding; thinking young investors get higher returns; misapplying the Rule of 72; believing DCA always beats a lump sum or removes risk.

## Chapter 5: Asset Allocation and Rebalancing
**Core questions**: What is asset allocation and why does it matter most? How should time horizon shape your mix? What is rebalancing and why bother? What does a target-date fund do?
**Key concepts**: the stock/bond/cash split, horizon-driven risk, portfolio drift, rebalancing as automatic sell-high/buy-low, target-date glide paths.
**Common traps**: confusing allocation with stock picking; holding all cash with decades to invest; thinking a mix stays put on its own; expecting a target-date fund to guarantee a sum.

## Chapter 6: Accounts and Where to Hold Investments
**Core questions**: What is the difference between an account and an investment? How do Roth and traditional differ on taxes? Why grab an employer match? When is a taxable brokerage right?
**Key concepts**: accounts as containers (401k/IRA/Roth/brokerage), pay-now vs pay-later taxes, the employer match as free money, taxable flexibility, a common starting order.
**Common traps**: treating an IRA as an investment itself; swapping the Roth and traditional tax rules; skipping a free match; thinking a brokerage account is illegal or always inferior.

## Chapter 7: Fees, Costs, and What They Steal
**Core questions**: What does an expense ratio mean in dollars? Why does a 1% fee hurt so much over decades? What is a sales load? What hidden costs come from trading?
**Key concepts**: the annual expense ratio, fees compounding against you, front-end loads, trading commissions and bid-ask spreads, comparing identical funds on cost.
**Common traps**: reading 0.05% as a one-time or 5% charge; dismissing 1% as trivial; thinking a load is a bonus; assuming higher fees buy better index performance.

## Chapter 8: Myths and Beginner Mistakes
**Core questions**: Does market timing work? Why is chasing last year's winner risky? How should you treat a hot tip or a crash? Do you need a lot of money or a complex plan?
**Key concepts**: time in the market vs timing, performance chasing, FOMO and hype red flags, panic selling, low minimums and fractional shares, simplicity as a strength, total return vs dividends alone.
**Common traps**: believing amateurs can time dips; buying on past returns; gambling the emergency fund on hype; selling at the bottom; waiting for "enough" money; equating complexity with skill.

## Chapter 9: Putting It All Together
**Core questions**: Where should a beginner with debt actually start? What is a sensible one-fund starter portfolio? How does horizon set risk? How do you respond to scary news, and when is an advisor worth it?
**Key concepts**: debt and emergency fund first, a broad low-cost starter holding, horizon-driven risk for short-term goals, ignoring media noise, the line between education and personalized fiduciary advice.
**Common traps**: investing before clearing high-interest debt; over-diversifying with overlapping funds; putting near-term money in stocks; reacting to headlines; expecting an advisor to guarantee market-beating returns.

## Capstone
Build a one-page personal investing starter brief (for yourself or a detailed imagined person) that demonstrates the course's whole arc: (1) a short foundation check — emergency fund status and any high-interest debt to clear first; (2) the correct account order for the situation, including capturing any employer 401(k) match and a clear Roth-vs-traditional reasoning; (3) a target asset allocation justified by time horizon, with a separate, lower-risk plan for any money needed within a few years; (4) a specific, simple, low-cost holding (a broad index or target-date fund) with its expense ratio noted and compared against a pricier alternative; (5) a written "rules of the road" pledge covering automatic contributions, a rebalancing cadence, and exactly how you will respond to the next crash headline. The brief should show plain-language understanding of ownership vs lending, diversification, compounding, fees, and the discipline to ignore noise — and should explicitly note that it is a personal education exercise, not formal financial advice.
