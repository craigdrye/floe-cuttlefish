# Physician Practice Roadmap
**ID**: `physicianPracticeRoadmap` - **Discipline**: Healthcare - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
An outpatient physician practice succeeds or fails on the same axis: can a patient who needs care get a useful, well-documented, correctly-paid visit without the clinic losing money or breaking a rule along the way? This course trains the operational judgment that makes that happen. It is written for practice managers, office leads, front-desk and revenue-cycle staff, and clinicians stepping into administrative roles who need to see the clinic as a system rather than a pile of tasks.

The throughline is the patient's path: a request for care becomes a scheduled slot, a verified eligibility, a roomed and documented encounter, a captured charge, a clean claim, a paid balance, and a closed-loop follow-up. At every handoff something can leak — access is squeezed by template design, value is lost at an uncaptured charge, a denial is born at a registration typo, and a complaint is created by silence in the lobby. Good practice management is the discipline of finding the handoff that is failing and fixing the system, not blaming the person standing closest to the failure.

This is also a course about rules, because outpatient medicine is heavily regulated and the rules carry real money and real risk. Learners leave able to reason about E/M code selection under the 2021 rules, the No Surprises Act good-faith-estimate duty, incident-to billing, prior-authorization workflow under the 2024 CMS rule, HIPAA right-of-access timelines, and the OIG's seven compliance elements — not as trivia, but as constraints that shape how a solvent, defensible clinic actually runs.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test clinic workflow, appointment access and templates, front-desk and visit flow, clinical support and provider productivity, charge capture and the revenue cycle, documentation and coding judgment, compliance and patient-rights rules, and practice-manager decision-making. Keep scenarios close to the day-to-day work of keeping a clinic useful, defensible, and solvent.

Distractors should model the real mistakes practice staff make: blaming a person for a system defect, treating a one-time heroic fix as a process, optimizing speed at the expense of quality or safety, ignoring a rule because it is inconvenient, and "fixing" the back end while the front-end cause keeps generating the same failure. Where a question turns on a code, rule, timeframe, or threshold, the fact must be exam-grade correct (E/M 2021, NSA GFE $400 dispute threshold, prior-auth 7-day/72-hour windows, HIPAA 30-day access, incident-to 100%-vs-85%). Prefer judgment over recall, but anchor judgment in correct facts. Voice: adult, concrete, respectful of frontline staff, allergic to buzzwords.

## Chapter 1: Practice Model and Patient Access
- **Core questions**: Can the patient who needs care actually get in, in time, to the right visit type? Where does the template create artificial scarcity?
- **Key concepts**: specialty vs primary care models, panel and panel complexity, new vs established patient, appointment template and visit types, third next available appointment (TNAA), supply-demand matching, no-show and cancellation rates, advanced/open access, slot release rules.
- **Applied skills**: read an access dashboard (demand, capacity, TNAA, no-show rate, next-available); diagnose whether an access problem is demand, capacity, template design, or behavior; design a waitlist and slot-release rule.
- **Common traps**: treating raw panel size as workload while ignoring acuity; "fixing" access with posters or bigger buttons instead of template rules; hiding open capacity to look calm; blaming clinician effort for a structural template problem.

## Chapter 2: Front Desk and Visit Flow
- **Core questions**: What must be true before the patient arrives, and where is the handoff from scheduling to checkout actually failing today?
- **Key concepts**: registration and demographics, insurance eligibility/benefit verification, referral capture, copay collection, rooming and the rooming standard, checkout and follow-up booking, patient portal, pre-visit work vs day-of exceptions, closed-loop referral.
- **Applied skills**: build a visit-flow checklist from scheduling through checkout; move predictable administrative work upstream while preserving a same-day exception path; identify the single handoff to fix first.
- **Common traps**: punishing patient time (arrive two hours early) instead of resequencing work; skipping eligibility and creating downstream denials; inconsistent late-arrival policy applied person-by-person; counting a referral "done" when it is only ordered.

## Chapter 3: Clinical Support and Provider Productivity
- **Core questions**: Which work truly requires a clinician, what can be protocol-driven within scope, and what is slowing the provider down?
- **Key concepts**: medical assistant and nurse roles, scope of practice, standing orders and their contraindications, nurse triage, inbox/in-basket management, refill and result protocols, prior-authorization workload, documentation burden, panel management, RVU as a productivity unit, cold-chain/vaccine handling.
- **Applied skills**: map clinic workload to task owner and bottleneck; design a delegation protocol with required data checks, escalation rules, and provider review thresholds; handle a standing-order exception or a temperature excursion safely.
- **Common traps**: letting each MA invent a personal protocol (the source of variation); delegating by dumping rather than by defined scope; optimizing rooming speed while missing vitals; bypassing standing-order contraindications under schedule pressure.

## Chapter 4: Revenue Cycle and Charge Capture
- **Core questions**: Did the work get captured, will the claim be clean, and where will payment leak or denials originate?
- **Key concepts**: the revenue cycle from scheduling to paid balance, charge capture and reconciliation, superbill/charge ticket, clean claim, front-end edits, denial vs rejection, medical necessity, payer rules, the feedback loop from back-end denials to front-end fixes, aged-receivables segmentation.
- **Applied skills**: trace a denial to its true upstream cause; build a charge-capture workflow linking clinical documentation, supply/inventory use, coding, and reconciliation; prioritize an AR backlog by payer, age, dollar value, and root cause.
- **Common traps**: relying on memory or intuition for charge capture; manually fixing a structural front-end bug forever instead of correcting it upstream; indiscriminate write-offs; speculative "just in case" billing.

## Chapter 5: Documentation and Coding Compliance
- **Core questions**: Does the documentation reflect the encounter that actually happened, and does it support the code submitted?
- **Key concepts**: E/M office-visit coding under the 2021 rules (MDM vs total time, history/exam no longer level-driving), 99202-99215, modifier 25 (significant, separately identifiable E/M with a same-day procedure), incident-to billing (100% vs 85% PFS, established patient, direct supervision), copy-forward/cloning risk, unsigned-note impact, audit readiness.
- **Applied skills**: choose between time-based and MDM-based E/M leveling; decide whether modifier 25 is justified and separately documented; judge whether an encounter qualifies for incident-to billing.
- **Common traps**: thinking volume of history/exam still drives the E/M level; appending modifier 25 to bill an E/M bundled into a minor procedure; billing incident-to for a new patient or new problem; treating copy-forward of an exam that did not happen as harmless.

## Chapter 6: Patient Rights, Privacy, and Practice Compliance
- **Core questions**: What does the law require the practice to do for patients and payers, and how does a small practice stay defensible?
- **Key concepts**: No Surprises Act good-faith estimate for uninsured/self-pay (timing windows, $400 patient-provider dispute threshold), HIPAA right of access (30 days, one 30-day extension, cost-based fee, no search/retrieval charge), HIPAA minimum necessary, OIG seven elements of an effective compliance program, CMS 2024 interoperability/prior-authorization rule (7 calendar days standard, 72 hours expedited).
- **Applied skills**: determine when a GFE is owed and within what window; respond to a records request within the HIPAA timeline and fee limits; map a practice's compliance program to the seven OIG elements; reason about prior-authorization decision timeframes.
- **Common traps**: assuming GFE duties apply only to large facilities; charging search/retrieval fees for record copies; treating compliance as a binder no one uses; confusing prior-auth standard vs expedited timeframes.

## Chapter 7: Service, Staffing, and Practice Improvement
- **Core questions**: What is the real complaint, what does the data show changed, and what small, measurable change can be tested before scaling?
- **Key concepts**: patient complaint vs root cause, service recovery, cross-training and single-person dependency, standard work, coverage planning, PDSA/small tests of change, baseline-and-measure, change adoption, balancing measures.
- **Applied skills**: run a service-recovery conversation; design a small test of change with a clear measure, patient selection, and staff workflow before scaling; build a practice-improvement plan with issue, baseline, action, owner, and measure.
- **Common traps**: scale-first rollouts that reveal problems at maximum cost; "improvement" measured only by whether the manager likes it; cancelling vacations instead of cross-training; service recovery that walks away or over-promises.

## Capstone
Build a physician practice-manager packet for a busy clinic month. Include: (1) an access review using TNAA, no-show rate, and template analysis with one concrete template change; (2) a visit-flow map naming the one failing handoff and its upstream fix; (3) a clinical-workload and delegation map with a scoped protocol and escalation rule; (4) a revenue-cycle risk list tracing two denials to their upstream causes plus a charge-capture fix; (5) a documentation/coding judgment note covering one E/M leveling decision and one modifier-25 or incident-to call; (6) a compliance check confirming GFE practice, HIPAA right-of-access process, and which OIG elements are in place; and (7) a service-improvement plan structured as a single small test of change with baseline, action, owner, and measure. The packet should read like something a real practice could act on Monday morning.
