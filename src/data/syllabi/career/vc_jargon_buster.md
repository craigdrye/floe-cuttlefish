# VC Jargon Buster
**ID**: `vcJargon` - **Discipline**: Finance - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Venture capital runs on shorthand. A partner says "the ARR is soft," "watch the signaling," "model the preference stack," or "this is a bridge, not a reset," and expects you to know exactly which analysis they want — and to come back with evidence, not vibes. This course teaches you to translate that shorthand into the real diligence task underneath it, and to do the task to a standard a partner would actually underwrite.

The course is built for the person who is new to a venture or growth fund, an operator stepping into corporate development or startup finance, or a founder who needs to read a term sheet without a translator. It covers the full vocabulary stack: revenue quality and SaaS unit economics, customer and cohort evidence, market and moat, burn and financing risk, the cap table and term-sheet mechanics that decide who actually gets paid, and the fund-level economics that explain why your partner cares about ownership and reserves at all.

The voice can be dry and funny, because the jargon is often funny. But the learning goal is ruthlessly practical: hear the phrase, name the underlying question, do the right work, and state a conclusion in plain English. Every chapter ends not with a definition you can recite but with a next action you could take on a live deal Monday morning.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test startup and fund diligence language: revenue durability, customer evidence, market sizing, defensibility, burn and runway, dilution, term-sheet economics and control, fund metrics, or the difference between a partner's metaphor and the literal task. Questions should reward the learner who can distinguish a vanity number from a load-bearing one, a standard term from a predatory one, and genuine demand from politely caffeinated pilot energy.

Two question styles work well: (1) "translate the partner-speak" — a colleague says something jargon-heavy and the learner picks the real ask; and (2) "know the mechanic" — a definitional or applied question testing whether the learner actually understands how a SAFE converts, what a 1x participating preference pays, or what DPI measures. Keep distractors plausible to a beginner: the wrong answers should be the misunderstandings real juniors actually hold, not obvious jokes. Use 2024–2026 market norms (post-money SAFEs dominant, broad-based weighted-average anti-dilution standard, NDR 120%+ as best-in-class) rather than dated rules of thumb.

## Chapter 1: Revenue Quality and Unit Economics
- **Core questions**: Which reported number is durable recurring revenue and which is noise? Do the unit economics support venture-scale growth?
- **Key concepts**: ARR vs MRR vs contracted vs booked revenue; gross retention vs net dollar retention (NDR); CAC, LTV, CAC payback period; gross margin; the Rule of 40.
- **Applied skills**: Strip one-time and services revenue out of an ARR figure; compute NDR and read it against the 120% best-in-class bar; judge whether a 12-month CAC payback is healthy.
- **Common traps**: Treating bookings or pipeline as revenue; quoting net retention to hide gross churn; ignoring that aggressive markups and "annualized last month × 12" inflate ARR.

## Chapter 2: Customers, Cohorts, and Evidence
- **Core questions**: Is demand real pull or shallow pilot enthusiasm? Would these customers renew, and are they expanding or just present?
- **Key concepts**: cohort retention curves, logo vs dollar churn, expansion/upsell, design partners vs paying customers, reference checks, implementation friction, switching costs.
- **Applied skills**: Build a customer-call guide covering renewal intent, depth of usage, budget owner, and switching cost; read a cohort chart for a leaky bucket; distinguish a marquee logo from a marquee dollar.
- **Common traps**: Confusing a famous logo with revenue concentration safety; mistaking free pilots for committed demand; accepting "everyone loves it" without asking who renews.

## Chapter 3: Market, Moat, and Defensibility
- **Core questions**: Is the market estimate grounded in real buyers? Is the moat durable or just a nice feature with a head start?
- **Key concepts**: TAM/SAM/SOM (top-down vs bottom-up), competitive intensity, defensibility, network effects, switching costs, data moats, "wedge vs platform."
- **Applied skills**: Rebuild a top-down TAM as a bottom-up (buyers × price) estimate; map a competitive landscape to spot a crowded lane; classify a claimed moat as durable or decorative.
- **Common traps**: Accepting "1% of a huge market" sizing; calling a feature a moat; assuming first-mover status is defensibility.

## Chapter 4: Burn, Runway, and Financing Risk
- **Core questions**: When does cash run out, and what happens if growth misses plan? Is the next raise a bridge or a reset?
- **Key concepts**: gross vs net burn, runway, default-alive vs default-dead, hiring plan, financing cliff, bridge round, base vs downside case, milestones for the next round.
- **Applied skills**: Build a base/downside runway model with hiring and revenue sensitivities; identify the milestone the company must hit to raise the next round; distinguish a true bridge from distress.
- **Common traps**: Using gross burn when net burn is what matters (or vice versa); assuming the plan case; forgetting that a "bridge to nowhere" is just slow death.

## Chapter 5: The Cap Table and Dilution Mechanics
- **Core questions**: After this round and the existing instruments convert, who owns what? Where is hidden dilution buried?
- **Key concepts**: pre-money vs post-money valuation; SAFEs and convertible notes; valuation cap and discount; pre-money vs post-money SAFE dilution; the option pool shuffle; fully diluted ownership; anti-dilution (broad-based weighted average vs full ratchet).
- **Applied skills**: Convert a stack of post-money SAFEs and show founder ownership at the priced round; quantify how a pre-money option pool top-up lowers the effective valuation; estimate the impact of a broad-based weighted-average adjustment in a down round.
- **Common traps**: Reading the headline pre-money as your ownership; missing that a pre-money pool dilutes only existing holders; assuming all SAFEs dilute the same way.

## Chapter 6: Term Sheets — Economics and Control
- **Core questions**: Who actually gets paid in a mediocre exit, and who controls key decisions? Is this term standard or predatory?
- **Key concepts**: liquidation preference (1x, multiple, participating vs non-participating), the payout waterfall, the zone of indifference, pro rata and super pro rata rights, protective provisions, board composition, pay-to-play, drag-along, no-shop.
- **Applied skills**: Run a simple exit waterfall for 1x non-participating vs 1x participating; flag a non-standard term (2x participating, full ratchet, super pro rata) for the partner; summarize a term sheet's economics and control in plain English.
- **Common traps**: Clapping at a high headline valuation that hides a 2x participating preference; treating protective provisions as harmless boilerplate; confusing economic terms with control terms.

## Chapter 7: Fund Economics and Portfolio Strategy
- **Core questions**: Why does my partner care about ownership, reserves, and "the fund returner"? How is the fund actually judged?
- **Key concepts**: LP/GP, committed capital, 2-and-20 (management fee and carry), hurdle rate, reserves and follow-on, ownership targets, power law and the "fund returner," signaling risk.
- **Applied skills**: Explain why a fund sizes a check to an ownership target; reason about whether to deploy reserves (pro rata) into a weak company; explain why an insider passing on its pro rata is a negative signal.
- **Common traps**: Thinking the management fee is the GP's payday; ignoring reserves when judging an initial check; missing that insider follow-on behavior signals information to the market.

## Chapter 8: Reading Performance — Fund and Deal Metrics
- **Core questions**: Is this return real cash or a paper markup? Is the partner's number realized or unrealized?
- **Key concepts**: MOIC, IRR, DPI, TVPI, RVPI (TVPI = DPI + RVPI); realized vs unrealized value; markups; J-curve; gross vs net returns.
- **Applied skills**: Translate a TVPI into its realized (DPI) and unrealized (RVPI) parts; explain why a great IRR with low DPI may be a markup mirage; separate gross-of-fees from net-to-LP performance.
- **Common traps**: Celebrating TVPI as if it were cash; trusting IRR early in a fund's life (J-curve); ignoring that markups are self-reported.

## Capstone
Take a messy, jargon-stuffed partner request — for example: "The ARR looks soft and one whale is wearing a fake mustache, the SAFE stack is doing something rude to the founders, the preference stack scares me below a celestial exit, and I'm not sure our reserves justify the signaling we'd send by passing." Translate it into a one-page diligence workplan: the real questions to answer, the evidence to gather, the model tabs to build (revenue bridge, cohort retention, runway base/downside, cap-table conversion, exit waterfall), and the three risks to flag for the investment committee — each stated in plain English with a recommended next action.
