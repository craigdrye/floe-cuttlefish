# IB Jargon Buster
**ID**: `ibJargon` - **Discipline**: Finance - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Investment banking runs on a private dialect. A senior banker drops a phrase into a 9pm email — "scrub the comps," "the QoE has a hole in it," "the sponsor is teeing up a retrade" — and a junior is expected to translate it into a concrete deliverable within the hour. This course teaches that translation: hearing live-deal language and instantly knowing the actual task, the underlying mechanic, and the trap that ends careers when missed. It is built for analysts, associates, and adjacent professionals (lawyers, accountants, corp-dev, founders) who need to operate fluently in M&A and financing conversations without asking someone to slow down.

The course is deliberately split between two skills that beginners conflate. The first is decoding tone and metaphor — the playful, hedged, deniable way bankers actually talk. The second is the hard technical substance underneath: how an enterprise-to-equity bridge works, why a working-capital peg moves the wire by millions, what separates an IOI from an LOI, when a deal is accretive, and which closing condition can blow up a signing. Jargon without mechanics produces analysts who nod and then build the wrong thing; mechanics without jargon produce analysts who are correct but always a beat behind the room. We train both.

The voice is direct and a little dry, because the job is. Every question maps a real phrase or situation to the right next action, and every wrong answer models a specific, plausible way smart juniors get it wrong — taking a metaphor literally, confusing two adjacent process documents, adjusting the wrong line in the bridge, or treating polite seller language as a binding commitment. By the end, a learner should be able to read a messy deal email chain and produce the real task list without supervision.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests: banking shorthand and deal-desk tone, the sell-side and buy-side process sequence, M&A valuation mechanics (comps, precedents, EV/equity bridge, accretion/dilution), quality-of-earnings and diligence, purchase-agreement economics (NWC peg, locked box, indemnity, escrow, RWI), and the judgment calls around buyer seriousness, retrade risk, and closing readiness. Prefer questions where the learner must hear a phrase and identify the deliverable, OR define a precise term and distinguish it from a near-neighbor. Distractors should be the genuine errors juniors make, not absurd ones. Keep it adult, fast, and unforgiving of hand-waving. Two complementary item styles coexist: "decode the colleague's request" narrative items, and crisp technical-definition items. Both belong; balance them across chapters.

## Chapter 1: Deal-Desk Dialect — Tone, Metaphor, and the Real Ask
- **Core questions**: A senior uses a hedged or metaphorical phrase; what concrete deliverable is being requested?
- **Key concepts**: polite-but-urgent phrasing ("when you get a chance" = now), metaphor vs literal instruction, "scrub / foot / tie out," deniable asks, who actually owns the output.
- **Applied skills**: translate a vague or jokey instruction into a scoped task with an owner and a deadline.
- **Common traps**: taking metaphor literally; mistaking softened phrasing for low priority; producing flair when accuracy was requested.

## Chapter 2: Valuation and Market Read
- **Core questions**: Which comparable is relevant, which adjustment moves value, and what does the client need to see?
- **Key concepts**: trading comps vs transaction (precedent) comps, control premium, NTM vs LTM multiples, EV/EBITDA vs P/E, the EV-to-equity bridge (subtract net debt, preferred, minority interest), football field, valuation range, "calendarization" and "spreading."
- **Applied skills**: build a short valuation bridge stating source, adjustment, and implication; pick a defensible comp set.
- **Common traps**: anchoring on the flattering top of the range; adding the wrong sign in the bridge; using spiritually-similar but numerically-irrelevant precedents.

## Chapter 3: Quality of Earnings and Diligence
- **Core questions**: Is the add-back supportable? What must the buyer verify? Which finding actually changes price?
- **Key concepts**: QoE, normalized/adjusted EBITDA, add-backs (one-time vs recurring), revenue recognition, customer concentration, working-capital trends, "EBITDA bridge," management-vs-QoE EBITDA gap.
- **Applied skills**: build a diligence tracker (issue, owner, evidence, risk, next step) and flag price-relevant findings.
- **Common traps**: accepting add-backs at face value; treating a presentation problem as a substance problem (or vice versa); ignoring buried items in the data room.

## Chapter 4: Buyer Process and Deal Tension
- **Core questions**: Is buyer interest real? What makes a process competitive? What should be escalated?
- **Key concepts**: process sequence — teaser → NDA → CIM → IOI → management presentation → LOI/exclusivity → confirmatory diligence → sign/close; strategics vs financial sponsors; non-binding vs binding; standstill; retrade vs renegotiation; "stalking horse."
- **Applied skills**: write a buyer-status summary with seriousness, open items, and next action; rank outreach by strategic fit.
- **Common traps**: confusing IOI with LOI; reading warm adjectives as momentum; treating an LOI as a deal rather than the start of exclusivity; missing a building retrade.

## Chapter 5: Purchase-Agreement Economics
- **Core questions**: What term moves the wire? Where is risk allocated between buyer and seller?
- **Key concepts**: SPA/APA, cash-free/debt-free, net-working-capital peg and true-up, completion accounts vs locked box (and leakage), reps & warranties, indemnification basket (deductible vs tipping), cap, escrow/holdback, RWI and retention, MAC/MAE, earnout.
- **Applied skills**: read a term and state who bears the risk and how it changes proceeds; distinguish a cosmetic redline from a value-shifting one.
- **Common traps**: ignoring the NWC peg until it costs millions; confusing a deductible basket with a tipping basket; assuming RWI means zero seller liability; treating a locked box as if a true-up still applies.

## Chapter 6: Financing, Accretion/Dilution, and the Model
- **Core questions**: Does the capital structure close? Is the deal accretive? Does the credit story survive a bad quarter?
- **Key concepts**: leverage (Debt/EBITDA), covenants (maintenance vs incurrence), sources & uses, sponsor equity, accretion/dilution (all-stock rule of thumb: accretive if acquirer P/E > target P/E, absent premium/synergies), synergy ownership and credibility, downside/stress case.
- **Applied skills**: judge whether financing is feasible; build a downside; sanity-check an accretion claim.
- **Common traps**: balancing debt "on vibes"; quoting the all-stock P/E shortcut while ignoring premium and synergies; booking unowned synergies as fact.

## Chapter 7: Materials, Markups, and Client Readiness
- **Core questions**: What changed? What must be reconciled? Who needs the next clean draft?
- **Key concepts**: management presentation, board book, CIM, comment turn, redline/blackline, version control, talking points, "wall-crossing," "tombstone."
- **Applied skills**: maintain a clean comment-response log and a deck-readiness checklist; reconcile conflicting comment turns.
- **Common traps**: losing version control across turns; reconciling numbers in the deck but not the model; coaching the CEO toward buzzwords instead of credible answers.

## Chapter 8: Signing, Closing, and Chaos Control
- **Core questions**: What blocks signing? What blocks closing? Who owns each open item?
- **Key concepts**: signing vs closing, conditions precedent, disclosure schedules, regulatory approvals (HSR/antitrust, CFIUS), third-party consents, funds flow/wire mechanics, closing checklist, simultaneous vs deferred close.
- **Applied skills**: build a closing issue list with owner, deadline, dependency, and risk level; distinguish signing blockers from closing blockers.
- **Common traps**: treating admin items (consents, wires) as harmless because economics are agreed; conflating signing and closing conditions; missing a regulatory long-pole.

## Capstone
Take a simulated Friday-night deal email chain — laced with metaphor, hedged asks, and three half-finished workstreams — and produce the real task list: the valuation update and bridge, the diligence asks, the buyer-seriousness readout, the purchase-agreement terms that move proceeds, the client/CEO prep, and the closing-risk list, each with an owner and the next concrete action. Then write the two-line plain-English status the MD will actually forward to the client.
