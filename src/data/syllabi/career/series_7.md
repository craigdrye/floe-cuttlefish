# Series 7: The General Securities Representative's Gauntlet
**ID**: `series7` · **Discipline**: Finance

## Course Aim
The Series 7 is FINRA's flagship retail license — the qualification that lets a registered representative recommend and execute trades in essentially the full universe of securities a US investor can hold: equities, corporate and municipal debt, options, mutual funds, ETFs, UITs, REITs, DPPs, variable contracts, structured products, and government securities. Pass it (paired with the SIE) and you are a "General Securities Representative" — the GS Rep. Fail it and your career on a retail or wirehouse desk does not start. It is wide, it is long, it is product-dense, and it has a reputation for stamina precisely because the exam refuses to be narrow.

The Series 7 expects you to *recommend*, not just *recite*. The SIE asked "what is a covered call." The Series 7 asks "Maria, 64, retired teacher with $410K IRA balance and an $80K taxable account, wants supplemental income — does a covered call on her dividend ETF fit her stated objective, and what disclosure must precede the trade?" Roughly 73% of the 125 scored items live in the "Provides Information and Recommendations" job function. Reading that as "product depth" is accurate but underestimates it — the exam is really testing whether you can take a fact pattern, identify the controlling rule, apply the suitability framework (Reg BI for BDs), and pick the answer a thoughtful supervisor would defend.

What you leave with is not just a license. You leave able to construct option strategies on paper, walk a margin account through a price move and produce SMA / buying power / equity figures, compare a Roth conversion to a backdoor strategy for a high-earner, explain why a muni fits one client and ruins another, and recognize a breakpoint sale on instinct. The Series 7 is the professional product-fluency exam in retail securities; that fluency is what makes the rest of the job (compliance reviews, branch audits, supervisory conversations) feel survivable.

The Floe Series 7 voice is more serious than SIE. We assume you are working at a firm — possibly already shadowing the desk — and we use named clients, specific dollar amounts, and real ticker-level scenarios. Stamina matters: the actual exam is 3 hours 45 minutes, so we drill in 30- and 60-minute blocks deliberately.

## Course Design Notes
Send any question about packaged products, options strategies, debt math, retirement suitability, margin mechanics, written communications, or trade execution and reporting here. The Series 7 voice should be technical, practical, and decisively suitability-framed. Always specify: client name, age, account type, balance, and stated objective. Distractors should mirror the kind of plausible-but-wrong recommendations a green associate might make. Lean heavily on options payoff diagrams and bond math. Use scratch-paper-friendly numbers (50%, 25%, 100 shares, $1,000 par).

Where the SIE is "what is a security," Series 7 is "which security for this client." Where Series 63 is "which state law applies," Series 7 is "which federal rule and which suitability finding." Where Series 79 is "how is this deal structured," Series 7 is "how do I sell the resulting security to a retail customer."

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The Series 7 has **125 scored questions plus 10 unscored pretest items** over 3 hours 45 minutes, broken into two 1h 52m sessions with an optional 30-minute break. Passing is 72%. FINRA organizes the exam by four job functions:

| Topic | Exam Weight | Scored Qs | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Seeks Business from Customers and Prospects | 7% | 9 | Quick win; learn FINRA 2210 communications and prospecting rules cold |
| Opens Accounts and Evaluates Profile | 9% | 11 | Account types, suitability, profiling — light overlap with SIE |
| Provides Information, Makes Recommendations, Transfers Assets, Maintains Records | 73% | 91 | The whole exam essentially lives here — products, strategies, recommendations |
| Obtains/Verifies Instructions, Processes Transactions | 11% | 14 | Order tickets, trade reporting, settlement (T+1), error handling |

**Study cadence: 8-12 weeks at 10-15 hours per week** is realistic for a candidate working full-time. Compress to 6-8 weeks at 20+ hours per week only if you have a finance background and uninterrupted study time.

- **6-week sprint (finance background, full-time study)**: Weeks 1-2 — equities/debt/munis with bond math drills; Weeks 3-4 — options strategies (one strategy per day with payoff diagrams); Week 5 — packaged products + retirement + margin; Week 6 — two full-length practice exams, weak-area drill, communications + execution mop-up.
- **10-week standard (working full-time)**: 1 week per content cluster, 30 questions/day from week 2, two full-length exams in the last two weeks. Drill options strategies *daily* starting week 4 — they decay fastest.
- **12-week deep prep**: Add a third practice exam, schedule a dedicated "muni week" and "options week," and use the spare weeks for retirement scenarios (Roth conversion, RMD math, ERISA edge cases).

Open the question bank in week 1 and never close it. Options strategies and bond math should be drilled *daily* for the last month — these are the highest-decay topics. Schedule the actual exam at the start of your prep so the deadline is fixed.

## Chapter 1: Prospecting, Communications, and Public Appearances
**Core questions:** What can a registered rep say, write, post, or text — and what supervisor approval is required first?

**Key concepts**
- FINRA Rule 2210 categories: retail (>25 retail recipients), institutional, correspondence (≤25 retail in 30 days)
- Principal pre-approval requirements; filing with FINRA Advertising Regulation Department
- Public appearances, social media, testimonials (now permitted with disclosures post-2021 SEC marketing rule amendments)
- Performance claims: must show net of fees, include risk disclosure, comparable benchmarks
- Telemarketing: do-not-call registry, 8 AM-9 PM local time window
- Reg BI Care Obligation in recommendations

**Applied skills**
- Classify a communication as retail / institutional / correspondence and determine the approval and filing rule
- Identify a violation in a draft email, LinkedIn post, or radio script
- Apply Reg BI to a recommendation pitch

**Common traps**
- The 2021 SEC marketing rule replaced the old IA cash solicitation rule and *now permits* testimonials and endorsements for IAs — but with required disclosures (compensation, conflicts).
- A Series 7 holder cannot send research-style emails to 25+ retail customers without principal pre-approval. The 25-recipient threshold is "Bright line A" of Rule 2210.
- Testimonials in BD advertising remain governed by FINRA Rule 2210 — disclosure rules apply, and *paid* testimonials require the additional disclosures.
- Do-not-call: even existing customers must be on the firm's internal DNC if they request it. National DNC alone is not sufficient.

**Authoritative sources:** FINRA Rule 2210 and Regulatory Notice 17-18; SEC IA Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1, effective Nov 2022); TCPA do-not-call rules.

## Chapter 2: Account Opening and Customer Profiling
**Core questions:** Which form, signature, and disclosure is required for each account type, and when does suitability shift?

**Key concepts**
- New account form requirements, CIP under USA PATRIOT Act, Customer Due Diligence and beneficial ownership (FinCEN)
- Discretionary accounts (FINRA 3260), fiduciary accounts, margin (loan consent, hypothecation, credit agreement), options (level 1-4), prime brokerage
- Reg BI Care Obligation, Form CRS delivery (initial and amendment)
- Account transfers via ACATS (1 business day validation + 3 business days transfer = 4 total)
- Death of customer: cancel all open orders, mark account "Estate of," request death certificate, letters testamentary, inheritance tax waivers

**Applied skills**
- Identify documentation gaps before approving an account
- Determine which option approval level a strategy requires
- Process an ACATS transfer with the correct timeline

**Common traps**
- Discretionary authority requires *written* customer authorization (FINRA 3260) **before** any discretionary trade. "Time and price" is *not* discretion (it's an exception).
- Reg BI does **not** make a broker a fiduciary. Care, Disclosure, Conflict, and Compliance obligations apply at the time of recommendation.
- An options account requires the customer to be approved at the correct level *before* the trade — selling cash-secured puts requires Level 2 minimum at most firms.
- Form CRS must be delivered at or before the start of the relationship for retail; amendments must be re-delivered when materially changed.

**Authoritative sources:** FINRA Rule 3260 (discretion), Rule 2090 (KYC), Rule 2111 (suitability); SEC Reg BI Adopting Release; ACATS operating procedures.

## Chapter 3: Equity Products and Recommendations
**Core questions:** When is a common stock suitable, when does a preferred make more sense, and how do corporate actions reshape positions?

**Key concepts**
- Stock splits (forward, reverse), stock dividends, spinoffs, mergers, tender offers
- Rights offerings (cum-rights, ex-rights, value of a right), warrants (long-dated, often sweeteners)
- ADRs, foreign ordinaries, currency translation risk
- Recommendation factors: time horizon, risk tolerance, tax situation, liquidity needs, concentration
- Tax lots: FIFO default, specific-ID, average cost (mutual funds), HIFO

**Applied skills**
- Adjust position cost basis after a 3:2 forward split or 1-for-10 reverse split
- Compute rights subscription cost and theoretical ex-rights price
- Pair a client risk profile with a suitable equity allocation under Reg BI

**Common traps**
- Theoretical value of a right *cum-rights*: (Market − Subscription) / (Rights to subscribe + 1). *Ex-rights*: (Market − Subscription) / Rights to subscribe. The "+1" is the most common arithmetic miss.
- A wash sale disallows the loss if a substantially identical security is purchased within 30 days *before or after* the sale (61-day window). The disallowed loss adds to the replacement security's basis.
- Currency depreciation in the ADR's home country *reduces* the dollar value of dividends and the underlying share — even if the foreign-currency price rises.

**Authoritative sources:** IRS Pub 550 (wash sales, capital gains); FINRA suitability rule 2111; Bloomberg ADR primer.

## Chapter 4: Debt Products, Municipals, and Bond Math
**Core questions:** How do you price, yield, and compare bonds, and when does a muni beat a corporate after tax?

**Key concepts**
- Premium and discount bonds; accretion of discount (added to basis annually), amortization of premium (subtracted from basis annually)
- Yield to worst: YTC on premium callable, YTM on discount callable
- Municipal bonds: GO (full faith and credit), revenue (project-specific), AMT bonds (private activity, may trigger AMT for high earners), BABs (taxable munis), pre-refunded/escrowed-to-maturity
- MSRB rules: G-17 (fair dealing), G-37 (political contributions, $250 de minimis), G-38 (solicitor compensation)
- Treasury auctions: competitive (specify yield) vs non-competitive (accept stop yield, $5M cap); regular reopen schedule
- TRACE reporting (corporates, agencies) within 15 minutes; RTRS for munis

**Applied skills**
- Compute taxable equivalent yield for a client in a given federal and state bracket
- Determine which yield to quote on a premium callable bond (YTC) vs discount callable (YTM)
- Identify the source of repayment for a revenue bond (project cash flows, not taxing power)

**Common traps**
- **TEY = muni yield / (1 − marginal tax rate)**. Adding state tax requires either (1 − federal − state) if state tax is on the muni (out-of-state muni) or just (1 − federal) for in-state munis exempt from state tax.
- Pre-refunded munis become essentially Treasury-collateralized; their credit ratings often rise to AAA regardless of the issuer's underlying rating. They are *no longer* GO/revenue from a credit perspective.
- AMT bonds are typically private activity revenue bonds (airports, stadiums) — interest is federally tax-exempt but is an AMT preference item. Many high-earner clients should avoid.
- G-37 prohibits MSRB-regulated reps from soliciting muni business for two years after a non-de-minimis political contribution to an issuer official with influence.

**Authoritative sources:** MSRB Rule G-17, G-37, G-38; TreasuryDirect auction documentation; Bloomberg muni primers; IRS AMT rules.

## Chapter 5: Options Strategies and Risk
**Core questions:** What is the maximum gain, maximum loss, and breakeven of every basic and intermediate options strategy?

**Key concepts**
- Single-leg: long/short call, long/short put — know the four payoff diagrams cold
- Vertical spreads: bull call (debit, max gain = spread − debit), bear call (credit, max gain = credit), bull put (credit), bear put (debit)
- Combinations: straddle (same strike, same expiry — direction-neutral, vol-positive), strangle (different strikes — cheaper, needs bigger move)
- Income strategies: covered call (sell call against long stock — caps upside), cash-secured put (sell put with cash reserved — bullish or willing to acquire)
- Protective: protective put (long stock + long put = insurance), collar (long stock + long put + short call — cheap insurance with capped upside)
- Defined-risk multi-leg: iron condor (4 legs, range-bound + low vol), butterfly (3 strikes, pinned-stock target), calendar spread (long-dated long + short-dated short, same strike)
- LEAPS (long-dated equity options), index options (cash-settled, European-style), exercise/assignment, OCC

**Applied skills**
- Draw payoff diagrams for any two-leg strategy
- Compute breakeven for a long straddle (lower BE = strike − total premium; upper BE = strike + total premium)
- Identify the right strategy for a stated outlook ("expects volatility but no direction" → straddle/strangle; "expects pinned at $50" → iron butterfly)

**Common traps**
- A short straddle has *unlimited upside risk* (short call) and substantial downside risk (short put). Many candidates miss the unlimited-loss leg.
- Covered calls cap upside but do not protect downside below (cost basis − premium). They are *not* a hedge.
- Collar = long put + short call against long stock. It is the cheapest insurance because the short call premium offsets the put premium — sometimes free ("zero-cost collar").
- An iron condor profits if the stock stays *between* the inner strikes. Iron butterfly profits at a single pinned strike. They look similar but resolve very differently.

**Authoritative sources:** OCC Options Disclosure Document; CBOE strategy guides; Sheldon Natenberg, *Option Volatility and Pricing*.

## Chapter 6: Packaged Products and Variable Contracts
**Core questions:** Which packaged product fits the client's tax and liquidity needs, and what disclosures are required?

**Key concepts**
- Open-end mutual funds: breakpoints, Letter of Intent (LOI, 13 months), Rights of Accumulation (ROA)
- Closed-end funds (trade at premium/discount to NAV), UITs (fixed portfolio, fixed termination)
- ETFs (in-kind creation/redemption — tax-efficient), ETNs (unsecured debt — issuer credit risk)
- Variable annuities: accumulation units (pre-annuitization), annuity units (after); 1035 exchanges (life→life, life→annuity, annuity→annuity, NOT annuity→life), surrender charges
- Variable life insurance, 529 plans, ABLE accounts
- Mutual fund share classes: A (front load, breakpoints), B (back load, converts to A), C (level 1% load — best for short hold), I (institutional)

**Applied skills**
- Identify a breakpoint sale violation (selling just under a breakpoint to retain higher commission)
- Compare a Class A vs Class C share for a 10-year holding period
- Determine 1035 exchange tax treatment

**Common traps**
- A breakpoint sale is a violation regardless of intent — a $24,500 Class A purchase when a breakpoint sits at $25K is a fact-pattern violation.
- Class B shares convert to Class A after the back-load tail (usually 6-8 years). For a 20-year hold, A typically beats C; for under 5 years, C beats A.
- A 529 plan can be used for K-12 tuition up to $10K/year (since TCJA), apprenticeship programs, and up to $10K student loan repayment (lifetime) under SECURE 2.0.
- ETN ≠ ETF. ETN is unsecured *debt* of an issuer (commodity or volatility-linked) — if the issuer goes bankrupt (Lehman 2008), the ETN value approaches zero regardless of the underlying.

**Authoritative sources:** FINRA Rule 2342 (breakpoint sales); IRS Section 1035 rules; IRS Pub 970 (529 plans); SECURE 2.0 Act provisions.

## Chapter 7: Retirement Plans and Education Accounts
**Core questions:** Which retirement vehicle matches the client's employment status, income, and tax goals?

**Key concepts**
- Traditional IRA, Roth IRA: 2024 contribution limit $7,000 ($8,000 catch-up for 50+)
- Roth IRA income phase-outs (2024 single $146-$161K, MFJ $230-$240K)
- SEP IRA (employer-only, 25% of compensation up to $69K 2024), SIMPLE IRA ($16K + $3.5K catch-up)
- 401(k) limits: $23K + $7.5K catch-up 2024; Roth 401(k) no income limit
- RMDs at age 73 (rising to 75 in 2033 under SECURE 2.0); 25% excise tax on missed RMD (reduced from 50%, can drop to 10% if corrected timely)
- 60-day rollover rule (one per 12-month period across all IRAs), direct transfer (unlimited)
- 10% early-withdrawal penalty and exceptions: SEPP/72(t), first-time home ($10K), higher ed, $1K emergency (SECURE 2.0), birth/adoption ($5K)
- ERISA: fiduciary duty, prohibited transactions, vesting schedules
- 529 vs Coverdell ESA ($2K/year cap, age 30 spend-down)
- Backdoor Roth: non-deductible Traditional IRA contribution → Roth conversion (watch pro-rata rule)

**Applied skills**
- Determine whether a high-income client can contribute to a Roth IRA directly (phase-out math)
- Apply the 10% early-withdrawal penalty and identify exceptions
- Recommend the optimal rollover path for a job changer (direct rollover beats 60-day rollover)

**Common traps**
- The 60-day rollover rule limits to *one per 12-month rolling window* across all the taxpayer's IRAs, per Bobrow v. Commissioner. Direct trustee-to-trustee transfers are unlimited.
- Backdoor Roth's pro-rata rule (IRC §408(d)(2)) aggregates **all** Traditional IRA balances when determining the taxable portion of a conversion. A large existing pre-tax IRA can poison the strategy.
- Roth conversions are no longer reversible (recharacterization was eliminated by TCJA in 2018).
- RMD age is 73 now (SECURE 2.0); 75 starting 2033. Pre-2023 RMD age (72) is obsolete.

**Authoritative sources:** IRS Pub 590-A and 590-B; SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022; ERISA §404 and §406; Bobrow v. Commissioner (2014).

## Chapter 8: Margin, Long and Short
**Core questions:** What is the initial deposit, maintenance level, and SMA available on this account today?

**Key concepts**
- Reg T initial margin: 50% on long equity, 50% on short equity (since 1974)
- FINRA maintenance: 25% long, 30% short (higher for low-priced or concentrated positions)
- Special Memorandum Account (SMA): records excess equity; "memo" not cash; can be used to buy 2x its value of new securities
- Margin call (federal call, Reg T), maintenance call (house call before FINRA call typically)
- Pattern day trader (PDT): 4+ day trades in 5 business days where day-trade activity > 6% of total → $25,000 minimum equity, 4× day-trading buying power
- Hypothecation and rehypothecation; loan consent

**Applied skills**
- Compute equity, SMA, and buying power after a price move
- Determine margin call amount required for a maintenance call (Long: 4/3 × debit − market value; Short: market value − 30%×market)
- Identify a pattern day trader and apply the $25K minimum

**Common traps**
- Long maintenance formula: minimum equity = market value × 0.25. Maintenance call = (Debit × 4/3) − Market value. If market drops, equity drops; if equity < 25% of market value, call is triggered.
- PDT $25,000 is a *minimum equity* requirement, not a deposit. Falling below $25K freezes day-trading buying power until restored. Cash deposits to meet the minimum are *not* withdrawable for 2 business days.
- SMA is *not cash*. It is a credit line tracked at the firm. Withdrawing SMA in cash requires the account to still meet maintenance after withdrawal.
- Short sale margin: initial 50% of short proceeds plus the short proceeds themselves are credited. Maintenance margin is 30% (with the higher of 30% or $5/share for low-priced stocks under $5).

**Authoritative sources:** Reg T (12 CFR §220); FINRA Rule 4210 (margin); FINRA Rule 4210(f)(8) (PDT).

## Chapter 9: Trading, Execution, and Reporting
**Core questions:** Which order type, market venue, and reporting obligation applies to this trade?

**Key concepts**
- Order types: market, limit, stop, stop-limit, MOC/LOC, GTC, day, IOC, FOK, AON, NH (not held)
- Bid/ask, NBBO, Reg NMS Rule 611 (Order Protection — no trade-throughs), Rule 605/606 disclosures
- Payment for order flow (PFOF) — must be disclosed; SEC has scrutinized in 2022-2023
- Trade reporting: TRACE for corporates/agencies (within 15 minutes), RTRS for munis, consolidated tape for NMS
- Settlement: T+1 for equities, corporates, munis (since May 28, 2024); T+0 for Treasury bills; cash settlement same-day
- Regulation SHO Rules 200-204: locate requirement, threshold securities, close-out

**Applied skills**
- Choose the correct order type for a defensive client objective (stop-loss vs limit)
- Identify the trade reporting venue for a given security
- Calculate settlement date including holidays (skip exchange holidays + weekends)

**Common traps**
- A *stop* order becomes a *market* order when triggered. A *stop-limit* becomes a *limit* order — and may not execute if it gaps past the limit.
- TRACE reporting is **15 minutes** from execution for most corporate bonds. Late reports trigger MSRB / FINRA fines.
- Reg NMS Order Protection prohibits *trade-throughs* — executing at a price worse than the NBBO when a protected quote is displayed at the better price. Internal routing must respect this.
- T+1 since May 28, 2024 — most prep materials published before then say T+2. Cash trades are same-day; T-bill auctions settle next-business-day.

**Authoritative sources:** SEC Reg NMS (Rules 600-612); FINRA TRACE manual; SEC T+1 Adopting Release (Feb 2023); FINRA Rule 6700 series.

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
225 minutes (3h 45m) for 125 scored items leaves 108 seconds per question, but options and bond math will eat extra time — bank seconds on definitions to spend on calculations. The exam is split into two sessions of ~62 items with an optional 30-minute break between.

- **Calculator**: on-screen four-function only. Scratch paper provided. No bringing your own.
- **The 30-minute break**: take it. Eat protein, not sugar (a hard-boiled egg, not a granola bar). Hydrate. The second-session fatigue is real and almost always shows up in options and margin questions.
- **Use scratch paper religiously**: draw every option payoff diagram. Write every margin T-account. "Thinking it through" mentally is how rushed S7 candidates fail the math cluster.
- **When two answers look right**: choose the one a senior advisor would defend to a compliance officer. Reg BI's Care Obligation favors the more conservative, more disclosed, more documented recommendation.
- **Wrong-area panic**: if you hit a 5-question options cluster cold, mark them all, move on, and come back with a clear head. Do not let one weak topic eat 15 minutes that the rest of the exam will need.
- **Day-before discipline**: review your error log + options payoff cheat sheet + bond math formulas + 20-question mixed quiz. Sleep. No marathon study.

If your last three full-length exams average 75%+ across all four job functions, you are ready. If any single job function trails by more than 10 points, spend the day-before window on that one area.
