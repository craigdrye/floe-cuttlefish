# Consulting Cases
**ID**: `consultingCases` · **Discipline**: Career Hopper · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course prepares you to handle management-consulting case interviews the way the firms actually grade them: as a live decision-making conversation, not a memorized recital of frameworks. The case interview is a proxy for the job. The interviewer is asking, "If I put this person in front of a client tomorrow, would they clarify the real problem, structure it cleanly, do honest math under pressure, read the data without overclaiming, and tell me what to do?" Everything in this course points at those five behaviors.

The center of gravity is judgment, not jargon. A candidate who recites a textbook profitability tree but cannot adapt when the interviewer says "revenue is fine, focus on cost" will lose to a candidate who structures from first principles and stays flexible. So the course drills the mechanics that have to be automatic — the profit equation, margin math, breakeven, market sizing, CAGR, payback, MECE — precisely so that working memory is free for the part that earns the offer: diagnosis, adaptation, and a crisp recommendation. We treat speed as a by-product of structure, not a goal in itself.

The voice is adult and direct because the stakes are real: these interviews gate six-figure offers and people walk in terrified. The course replaces that fear with a repeatable process. You will learn to open a case calmly, build a structure you can defend, run numbers you can sanity-check, respond to new information without panicking, and close with a recommendation a partner would actually carry into a client meeting.

## Course Design Notes
The content mirrors the publicly documented expectations of McKinsey, BCG, and Bain and the case-prep canon (Victor Cheng, Marc Cosentino, CaseCoach, RocketBlocks), while correcting two failure modes common in beginner prep. First, framework worship: learners memorize trees and then force every case into one. We teach structures as starting points to be customized and explicitly drill adaptation. Second, math fragility: learners freeze on arithmetic. We separate setup from calculation from interpretation so the math becomes a checklist, not a cliff.

Each chapter lists Core questions (the diagnostic prompts a learner should be able to answer), Key concepts (the named ideas and formulas), Applied skills (what the learner can now do live), and Common traps (the specific, recurring mistakes the questions are built to catch). Question difficulty spans recognition (name the right formula), application (run the number, read the chart), and judgment (choose the defensible move under ambiguity). Most items reward the operationally sound answer over the fast, flashy, or overconfident one.

## Chapter 1: What a Case Is Really Testing
- **Core questions**: What decision is the client actually trying to make? What would a complete answer need to prove? Which two or three facts are essential before any structure?
- **Key concepts**: business-problem framing, the objective/metric/timeline/decision clarification set, interviewer collaboration, hypothesis-led thinking, signal versus polish.
- **Applied skills**: open a case by restating the objective and confirming the success metric; ask focused clarifying questions; state an early, revisable hypothesis.
- **Common traps**: calculating before clarifying; jumping to a solution because a word in the prompt "sounds like" a function (growth → social media); demanding perfect information before committing to any view.

## Chapter 2: Structuring Without Sounding Robotic
- **Core questions**: Which structure fits this situation, and where might it miss the real cause? How do you adapt the tree after the interviewer adds a constraint? Is each branch mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive?
- **Key concepts**: issue trees, profit trees (Profit = Revenue − Cost; Revenue = Price × Volume), MECE (Barbara Minto, McKinsey), driver decomposition, customizing a frame rather than reciting one.
- **Applied skills**: build a tailored structure in under 90 seconds; label and justify assumptions out loud; restructure on the fly when the interviewer narrows or shifts the scope.
- **Common traps**: forcing a memorized framework onto a mismatched problem; overlapping or gap-leaving buckets (non-MECE); treating the tree as the answer instead of a map to the data.

## Chapter 3: Business Math Under Time Pressure
- **Core questions**: What assumptions drive this number? Does the result pass a real-world sanity check? Which unit conversion or operator could quietly break the math?
- **Key concepts**: revenue and cost build-up, gross and contribution margin, breakeven (fixed cost ÷ contribution per unit), CAGR = (End/Start)^(1/years) − 1, payback period = investment ÷ annual cash flow, weighted averages, percentage growth.
- **Applied skills**: state the formula before the arithmetic; carry units cleanly; estimate large products quickly; sanity-check every output against plausible reality.
- **Common traps**: confusing dollar margin with margin percentage, or cost ratio with margin ratio; using fixed cost × rate instead of ÷; reporting revenue as if it were profit; accepting an un-sanity-checked number.

## Chapter 4: Market Sizing and Estimation
- **Core questions**: Is this market better sized top-down or bottom-up? What is the cleanest population to start from? What implied per-person rate should make me pause?
- **Key concepts**: top-down sizing (start from a macro population and narrow), bottom-up sizing (build from a unit and scale up), segmentation, anchoring on defensible base rates, the sanity check as a required final step.
- **Applied skills**: choose an approach deliberately based on market size and homogeneity; lay out a transparent estimation chain; pressure-test the answer against known anchors.
- **Common traps**: defaulting to one approach reflexively; hiding assumptions instead of stating them; stacking estimates without checking the implied behavior at the end.

## Chapter 5: Charts, Exhibits, and Data Pulls
- **Core questions**: What does this exhibit actually say (title, axes, units) before I interpret it? What is the single most important comparison? What would I ask for next?
- **Key concepts**: read the frame before the data, trend versus level, segmentation that beats the blended average, variance and outliers, correlation versus causation, observation → implication → next action.
- **Applied skills**: summarize an exhibit in one sentence; turn a pattern into a focused hypothesis; resist the unsupported causal leap; identify which follow-up data would confirm or kill the hypothesis.
- **Common traps**: reading numbers aloud without interpreting; calling a correlation a cause; letting a flat average hide a segment tradeoff; pulling a conclusion the chart does not support.

## Chapter 6: Case Types and Economic Reasoning
- **Core questions**: What is the economic engine of this business? Where does value leak? Which stakeholder constraint binds hardest?
- **Key concepts**: profitability, market entry, growth, pricing (cost-plus vs competitor-based vs value-based), operations and bottlenecks (throughput set by the slowest step), price elasticity of demand, M&A value-creation thesis, public-sector and nonprofit outcome metrics, sunk cost versus relevant cost.
- **Applied skills**: recognize the case type fast and recall its likely drivers, math tools, and risks; pick a pricing logic that fits the situation; ignore sunk costs when evaluating a go-forward decision.
- **Common traps**: confusing elastic and inelastic demand; treating activity/input metrics as outcomes; chasing a deal on excitement with no synergy thesis; letting sunk costs drive a forward-looking choice.

## Chapter 7: Synthesis and Recommendation
- **Core questions**: What is your answer in one sentence? What evidence supports it? What is the key risk and the next step?
- **Key concepts**: top-down (answer-first) communication, the Pyramid Principle, evidence hierarchy, tradeoffs, conditional recommendations, naming risk as a strength, executive tone.
- **Applied skills**: deliver a 30-second synthesis with recommendation, two or three supporting reasons, the main risk, and a next step; qualify a recommendation conditionally without sounding indecisive.
- **Common traps**: reciting every analysis instead of synthesizing; refusing to commit under uncertainty; over-extrapolating a pilot result; hiding the risk to sound "crisp."

## Chapter 8: Handling Pushback, New Information, and Interview Readiness
- **Core questions**: What changes after this new fact? Which assumption should update? How do you recover from a math error gracefully and pace a full case?
- **Key concepts**: recalibration, integrating new data without abandoning structure, transparent error recovery, scenario pivots, pacing, note and whiteboard hygiene, behavioral/fit alignment, deliberate practice.
- **Applied skills**: absorb a curveball, update the logic, and keep the frame intact; correct an arithmetic slip openly and state the new implication; manage time across opening, structure, analysis, and synthesis; deliver a tight fit story.
- **Common traps**: refusing to update when new data arrives; panicking and discarding the structure; disguising or doubling down on an error; running out of time before reaching a recommendation.

## Capstone
Run two full mock cases end to end: one profitability or growth case, and one operations, pricing, market-sizing, or public-sector case. For each, submit the clarifying questions you asked, your structure, your math (with sanity checks), the exhibit interpretations, the final recommendation in answer-first form, and one paragraph of self-critique naming your single highest-leverage next drill. Pass condition: a partner could carry your recommendation into a client meeting without reworking it.

## Assessment Ideas
- Timed structure drills graded on fit and adaptability, not framework recall.
- Math drills graded on setup, correct operator, units, and an explicit sanity check.
- Exhibit drills graded on insight and on avoiding the correlation-causation trap.
- Final mock case scored across framing, math, synthesis, collaboration, and recovery.
