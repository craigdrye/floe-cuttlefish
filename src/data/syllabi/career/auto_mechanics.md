# Auto Mechanics: Know Your Car
*Stop fearing the dashboard. Understand what's under the hood, diagnose the noise, and walk into the shop as the smartest person in the room.*

**ID**: `autoMechanics` · **Discipline**: Practical Engineering · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Almost everyone drives, and almost no one understands the machine they're sitting in. That gap is expensive: it shows up as overpriced "urgent" repairs, ignored warning lights that turn small problems into engine-killers, and a low-grade anxiety every time something rattles. This course closes that gap for the curious adult who wants to actually understand their car — not to become a certified technician, but to know what's happening, why, and what to do about it.

We build from first principles. You'll learn how an engine turns controlled explosions into motion (the four-stroke cycle), what each fluid does and why running low on the wrong one can destroy the car, how brakes and tires keep you alive, and how the battery, alternator, and starter divide the electrical labor. Then we get practical: decoding warning lights and OBD trouble codes, translating mystery noises and smells into likely causes, and handling the genuinely safe DIY jobs (jump-start, flat tire, fluid checks) with confidence.

Just as importantly, you'll learn the boundaries. A big part of being competent with cars is knowing when *not* to touch something — airbag systems, brake hydraulics, suspension springs, refrigerant — and how to be a sharp, un-upsellable customer when the work goes to a professional. The throughline is judgment: matching symptoms to systems, going cheapest-and-most-likely first, and respecting the difference between "service soon" and "stop the car now."

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want practical, real-world car literacy: a new driver, a used-car buyer, someone tired of feeling helpless at the repair shop, or anyone who heard a noise this morning and wants to know if it's serious. This is a "smart owner / home mechanic" course, not an ASE certification prep track — it favors mechanism and decision-making over torque specs and teardown procedures. Questions are scenario-driven ("you hear X, you smell Y, the gauge does Z — now what?") because that's how car problems actually present themselves. Difficulty is mixed: foundational mechanism questions sit alongside multi-symptom diagnosis and safety-judgment items. Where a real safety rule exists (hot cooling systems, jack stands, flashing check-engine light), the course is firm about it.

## Chapter 1: How the Engine Works
- **Core questions**: What are the four strokes and which one squeezes the mixture? What does a spark plug actually do? What does "2.0L 4-cylinder" mean? Why does a misfire make the idle rough?
- **Key concepts**: four-stroke cycle (intake, compression, power, exhaust), combustion, spark plugs vs injectors, cylinders, displacement, compression, misfire.
- **Applied skills**: explaining "low compression" or "rough idle" in plain language; recognizing which under-hood systems affect how the engine runs.
- **Common traps**: confusing the spark plug's job with the injector's; thinking displacement means fuel-tank or oil capacity; blaming idle problems on unrelated systems like tires or brakes.

## Chapter 2: Oil & Fluids
- **Core questions**: What three jobs does engine oil do? What does "5W-30" mean? How do you read a dipstick correctly? How do you identify a fluid by color and smell? When should oil actually be changed?
- **Key concepts**: lubrication, heat transfer, contaminant suspension, viscosity grades, multigrade oil, the dipstick procedure, fluid identification, modern oil-change intervals.
- **Applied skills**: checking and topping up oil; identifying coolant vs oil vs brake vs washer fluid from a puddle; choosing the right oil interval for your driving.
- **Common traps**: the obsolete 3,000-mile rule; checking oil hot or on a slope; treating the red oil-pressure light as a change reminder; using the wrong oil grade.

## Chapter 3: Brakes
- **Core questions**: How do disc brakes stop the car? What does a brake squeal mean vs a grind? Why is a spongy pedal a hydraulic clue? What does the parking brake actually hold?
- **Key concepts**: hydraulic braking, calipers, pads, rotors, friction-to-heat, wear indicators, brake fluid, air in the lines, the parking-brake system.
- **Applied skills**: distinguishing pad-wear noise from a hydraulic problem; recognizing the early squeal before the expensive grind; understanding the parking brake as a separate rear system.
- **Common traps**: blaming brake symptoms on tires or the engine; mistaking a spongy pedal for worn pads; confusing the transmission parking pawl with the parking brake.

## Chapter 4: Tires & Pressure
- **Core questions**: Where do you find YOUR correct tire pressure? Why check pressure cold? How does the penny test work? Why rotate tires?
- **Key concepts**: door-jamb placard vs sidewall maximum, cold inflation, the gas law in a tire, tread depth, the penny test, tire rotation, even wear.
- **Applied skills**: setting correct pressure; reading tread for safety; understanding rotation as wear management, not magic.
- **Common traps**: using the sidewall max as the target; checking pressure right after driving; misreading the penny test (hidden head = good); expecting rotation to fix alignment or MPG.

## Chapter 5: Battery, Alternator & Starter
- **Core questions**: What powers the car once it's running? What does a click-no-crank mean? What's the safe jump-start order? What does the battery warning light really indicate?
- **Key concepts**: battery (starting), alternator (charging/running), starter (cranking), parasitic drain, terminal corrosion, jump-start sequence, the charging-system warning.
- **Applied skills**: jump-starting safely in the correct clamp order; diagnosing a no-start as battery vs starter; reading the battery light as a charging problem.
- **Common traps**: thinking the battery powers a running car; grounding the final jumper clamp on the dead battery; reading the battery light as "needs water" or "low fuel."

## Chapter 6: Cooling & Temperature
- **Core questions**: What does the thermostat do? What's the correct first move when the temp gauge hits red? Why is coolant not just water?
- **Key concepts**: thermostat as a temperature valve, operating temperature, overheating damage, pressurized cooling systems, antifreeze (boiling/freezing points, corrosion inhibitors), coolant mix ratio.
- **Applied skills**: responding safely to overheating; never opening a hot pressurized cap; topping up coolant with the right type and ratio.
- **Common traps**: opening a hot radiator cap; continuing to drive an overheating engine; pouring cold water on a hot block; using plain tap water as coolant.

## Chapter 7: Transmission, Belts & Filters
- **Core questions**: What does a transmission actually do? Manual vs automatic — what's the real difference? What does the serpentine belt drive, and why is the timing belt a big deal? Which filter causes which symptom?
- **Key concepts**: gear ratios matching engine RPM to wheels, clutch and shifting, torque converter/CVT, serpentine belt accessories, the interference-engine timing belt, engine vs cabin vs oil vs fuel filters.
- **Applied skills**: explaining gearing in plain terms; respecting the timing-belt interval; mapping weak A/C airflow to the cabin filter and poor performance to the engine filter.
- **Common traps**: confusing the serpentine belt with the timing belt; underestimating timing-belt failure on interference engines; mixing up the four filters and their symptoms.

## Chapter 8: Warning Lights & OBD Codes
- **Core questions**: Steady vs flashing check-engine — what changes? What does an OBD-II scanner actually do? What is the horseshoe TPMS light? How does the red oil-can light differ from the "service oil" reminder?
- **Key concepts**: malfunction indicator lamp, severe misfire / catalytic converter risk, OBD-II port and trouble codes (e.g., P0301), TPMS, red vs amber warning hierarchy, oil pressure vs oil-life.
- **Applied skills**: reading a trouble code yourself; triaging a flashing CEL; distinguishing emergency reds from "soon" ambers.
- **Common traps**: ignoring a flashing CEL as a "bulb glitch"; expecting a scanner to fix things; confusing the oil-pressure emergency with the oil-change reminder.

## Chapter 9: What's That Noise or Smell?
- **Core questions**: Sweet smell — coolant or oil? Burning rubber — what's touching what? Grinding only when braking — how bad? Tick that rises with RPM? Blue vs white vs black exhaust smoke? Highway steering shimmy?
- **Key concepts**: symptom-to-system mapping, smell signatures (sweet=coolant, acrid=oil, rotten-egg=battery), exhaust smoke color diagnosis, speed-dependent vibration, valvetrain/oil-starvation ticking.
- **Applied skills**: translating a sensory symptom into a likely system; checking the cheap cause first (oil level for a tick, balance for a shimmy).
- **Common traps**: misattributing smells across systems; calling thin white startup vapor a problem; assuming a shimmy is electrical or engine-related rather than wheel balance.

## Chapter 10: DIY Basics & When NOT to DIY
- **Core questions**: How do you get under a car safely? Why loosen lug nuts before jacking? Which jobs are safe DIY and which are leave-it-to-the-pros? Top up or flush? How do you act as a smart customer? Where's the real maintenance schedule?
- **Key concepts**: jack vs jack stands, wheel chocks, lug-nut sequence and torque, risk/reversibility as the DIY test, proportionate response (top up vs flush), shop-customer questions, manufacturer maintenance schedule, normal vs severe service.
- **Applied skills**: changing a tire and jump-starting safely; deciding DIY vs pro by risk; asking the right questions (symptom, code, parts, safety-critical) and getting second opinions.
- **Common traps**: trusting a jack alone or bricks; trying to loosen lugs on a lifted wheel; DIY-ing airbags/brake hydraulics/springs/refrigerant; blind trust or blanket distrust of shops; following a windshield sticker over the manual.

## Chapter 11: Putting It Together
- **Core questions**: Cranks but won't start — which system is least likely? Several lights on — which means stop NOW? What's the cheapest-first diagnostic habit? Can you match a symptom to the right system?
- **Key concepts**: symptom-precise diagnosis (cranks vs won't-crank), red-vs-amber urgency hierarchy, cheapest-and-most-likely-first reasoning, integrated symptom-to-system mapping.
- **Applied skills**: narrowing a no-start by naming the exact symptom; triaging multiple warnings by urgency; running a structured, money-saving first diagnosis.
- **Common traps**: parts-cannon guessing; jumping to the most expensive fix; ignoring symptoms until total failure; crossing wires between systems (e.g., blue smoke ≠ battery).

## Capstone
Pick a real or imagined car and a real or imagined problem, then think it all the way through like a competent owner. Start from the symptom exactly as it presents (the noise, the smell, the light, the gauge), name the most likely system and one or two alternates, and decide your cheapest-first checks. State whether it's a "stop now" or "service soon" situation. Decide what you'd safely do yourself versus take to a shop — and if it's going to the shop, write the three questions you'd ask to avoid being upsold (what symptom or code, what parts and labor, is it safety-critical or can it wait). The goal is not a perfect repair plan; it's demonstrating the judgment this whole course builds: understand the machine, read the symptom, respect the danger lines, and never walk in helpless.
