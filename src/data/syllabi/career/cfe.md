# Certified Fraud Examiner (CFE): The Investigator Who Reads Ledgers and Listens for Lies
**ID**: `cfe` · **Discipline**: Fraud Examination / Investigations

## Course Aim
A Certified Fraud Examiner is not an accountant who happens to spot anomalies, and not a detective who happens to read income statements. The CFE is the working hybrid the ACFE invented because real fraud cases need both: an investigator who can pull a journal entry apart by hand, structure an admission-seeking interview without contaminating it, and walk a chain of custody from a hard drive to a courtroom. The exam tests that whole stack — financial transactions, law, investigation methodology, and prevention — because the credential certifies a person, not a specialty.

The CFE Exam (Prometric, computer-based, open-book using the ACFE Fraud Examiners Manual) is four sections of 125 multiple-choice items each — Financial Transactions and Fraud Schemes, Law, Investigation, Fraud Prevention and Deterrence — taken separately, with a 75% passing score required in each. Four hours of total testing time across the four sections rewards prepared judgment, not exam-day improvisation. The Manual is searchable; treat it as a verification tool, not a study substitute.

This syllabus trains the four habits a working examiner uses every day: pattern recognition on the ACFE Fraud Tree (corruption, asset misappropriation, financial statement fraud), evidence hygiene that survives Daubert and the Federal Rules of Evidence, controlled skepticism that respects the rights of every person an examination touches, and the discipline to write reports that describe conduct without opining on legal guilt. The voice should reflect the seriousness of the work: fraud examiners testify, and the ACFE Code of Professional Standards (Professional Conduct/Ethics, Independence/Objectivity, Confidentiality, Professional Competence) is enforceable.

The goal is a working examiner toolkit and four passing scores: scheme fluency across the Fraud Tree, statute-by-statute familiarity with FCPA, FCA, RICO, SOX, Dodd-Frank, and the BSA, interviewing discipline rooted in the cognitive interview and admission-seeking phases, tracing skills using both Net Worth and Expenditures methods, and a calm relationship with privilege, predication, and chain of custody.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they cover ACFE exam content specifically — fraud investigations, not generic ethics or general accounting. CFE differs from CPA (financial reporting), CIA (internal audit broadly), and CAMS (AML specifically). Fraud examination is its own discipline: predicate-offense analysis, the Fraud Triangle/Diamond/Scale/Trapezoid lineage, occupational fraud schemes by category, and US-flavored evidence law. When a question is really an AML typology question (placement/layering/integration without a clear predicate fraud), route it to CAMS. When a question is really about GDPR or personal data, route it to CIPP/E.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
Four sections, 125 items each, 75% passing per section, ~1.9 minutes per item. The ACFE CFE Exam Prep Course (textbook + software) is the canonical preparation. Typical cadence: 6-12 months self-paced, ~150-200 hours per section, error log keyed to Fraud Tree branch and ACFE section.

## Chapter 1: The Fraud Triangle, Diamond, Scale, Trapezoid — and the Tree
**Core questions:** Why do otherwise ordinary employees commit occupational fraud, and how do those motives map to the schemes you will see on the exam?

**Key concepts**
- Cressey's Fraud Triangle (1953): perceived non-shareable pressure, perceived opportunity, rationalization
- Wolfe and Hermanson's Fraud Diamond (2004): adds capability — does the actor have the skills, position, and ego to pull it off?
- Albrecht's Fraud Scale: pressure, opportunity, and personal integrity on a continuum
- Choo's Fraud Trapezoid and Sutherland's Differential Association (criminology)
- ACFE Occupational Fraud Tree top branches: Corruption, Asset Misappropriation, Financial Statement Fraud — and their sub-branches
- Detection methods (ACFE Report to the Nations): tips lead, followed by internal audit and management review

**Applied skills**
- Place a fact pattern on the Fraud Tree within 30 seconds and name the sub-branch
- Translate "what was taken and how" into a scheme label the exam recognizes
- Identify the most likely tip source and most likely detective control by scheme

**Common traps**
- **Triangle vs Diamond vs Scale.** All three are widely cited; the exam distinguishes by who added what. Capability = Wolfe/Hermanson. Integrity = Albrecht.
- **Larceny vs misappropriation.** Larceny under the Fraud Tree is taking cash AFTER it has been recorded in the books; skimming is taking BEFORE recording. The umbrella is asset misappropriation; the specific sub-branch dictates the investigative path.
- **Rationalization is internal, not external.** A bonus that "should have been paid" is rationalization, not opportunity, even though it sounds situational.
- **Predication.** A reasonable basis to believe fraud may have occurred. Examiners do not investigate hunches; they investigate predicated suspicions.

**Authoritative sources:** ACFE Fraud Examiners Manual; ACFE Report to the Nations (current biennial edition).

## Chapter 2: Asset Misappropriation — Cash, Inventory, and Fraudulent Disbursements
**Core questions:** How do employees skim cash, steal inventory, and create fictitious vendors, and which controls actually stop them?

**Key concepts**
- Cash receipts: skimming (unrecorded sales, understated sales, refund schemes, lapping accounts receivable) vs cash larceny (post-recording theft)
- Fraudulent disbursements: billing schemes (shell company, non-accomplice vendor, personal purchases), check tampering (forged maker/endorsement, altered payee, concealed checks, authorized maker), payroll schemes (ghost employees, falsified hours, commission schemes), expense reimbursement (mischaracterized, overstated, fictitious, multiple reimbursement), register disbursements (false refunds and voids)
- Non-cash misappropriation: inventory and equipment shrinkage, asset transfers
- Controls that actually work: segregation of duties, mandatory vacations, surprise audits, vendor master file hygiene, positive pay, dual approval thresholds

**Applied skills**
- Trace a suspect disbursement from origination through bank clearing
- Reconstruct a lapping scheme from out-of-sequence customer payments
- Spot ghost employees through payroll/HR data joins (no W-4 changes, P.O. Box address, direct deposit to a shared account)

**Common traps**
- **Skimming vs larceny vs billing.** Skimming = pre-record (cash never hits the books). Larceny = post-record. Billing = AP-side fictitious or inflated invoice.
- **Check tampering vs billing.** Check tampering manipulates an issued check (forged maker/endorsement, altered payee). Billing manipulates the invoice that triggers a legitimately issued check.
- **Lapping vs kiting.** Lapping rolls customer payments forward in AR to hide skimming. Kiting exploits float between bank accounts. Different ledger, different control.
- **Ghost employees vs falsified hours.** Ghost = a person who does not exist or no longer works there. Falsified hours = a real employee inflating time. Different investigative steps.

**Authoritative sources:** ACFE Fraud Examiners Manual sections on Asset Misappropriation; AICPA AU-C 240.

## Chapter 3: Financial Statement Fraud — Cooking the Books
**Core questions:** How are revenues, expenses, assets, liabilities, and disclosures manipulated, and how do you spot it from the outside?

**Key concepts**
- Revenue overstatement: bill-and-hold, channel stuffing, round-tripping, cookie-jar reserve release, premature recognition, fictitious sales
- Expense and liability understatement: capitalizing operating expenses, omitting accruals, off-balance-sheet vehicles
- Improper asset valuations: inventory overstatement, AR aging manipulation, goodwill impairment delay
- Improper disclosures: related-party transactions, contingent liabilities, subsequent events
- Timing differences and earnings management vs fraud
- Analytical procedures: Beneish M-Score, Altman Z-Score, DSO and gross margin trend analysis, vertical/horizontal/ratio analysis

**Applied skills**
- Recompute revenue, gross margin, and DSO trends to spot anomalies
- Read three quarters of statements and pick the scheme

**Common traps**
- **Bill-and-hold legitimacy.** Sometimes legitimate (customer requested, separate storage, fixed delivery, no cancellation right). Cancellation right or open delivery date usually means control has not transferred.
- **Channel stuffing vs round-tripping.** Channel stuffing pushes inventory to distributors. Round-tripping is reciprocal sales between two parties for no economic substance.
- **Cookie jar reserves.** Over-accruing in good periods to release in bad ones. Direction matters: the fraud is the smoothing, not the accrual itself.
- **Cutoff issues.** Holding the books open past period end to capture next-period sales. Specific to revenue recognition timing.

**Authoritative sources:** ACFE Fraud Examiners Manual financial statement fraud sections; SEC AAERs; PCAOB AS 2401.

## Chapter 4: Corruption, Bribery, and the FCPA
**Core questions:** How do bribery, kickbacks, and conflicts of interest show up in the books, and what does the FCPA actually prohibit?

**Key concepts**
- Corruption sub-branches: bribery (commercial and official), illegal gratuities (reward after the fact), economic extortion (insider demands payment), conflicts of interest (undisclosed interest in a transaction)
- Bid-rigging variants: bid rotation, bid suppression, complementary (cover) bids, market division
- Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA): anti-bribery provisions (15 USC 78dd-1 et seq.) plus accounting provisions (books and records, internal controls)
- "Anything of value" — narrow exceptions for facilitation payments; due diligence on third-party intermediaries
- UK Bribery Act 2010 contrast: strict liability "failure to prevent," no facilitation-payment exception
- Red flags in vendor master and contracting

**Applied skills**
- Identify off-book bribery via vendor concentration, unusual commissions, and consultant agreements
- Map an FCPA fact pattern to anti-bribery vs accounting provision liability

**Common traps**
- **Illegal gratuity vs bribery.** Bribery is quid pro quo in advance to influence an act. Illegal gratuity is a reward for an official act after the fact. Different intent element.
- **Facilitation payments.** FCPA permits small payments to expedite routine, non-discretionary government action (e.g., processing visas). UK Bribery Act does not.
- **Books and records liability.** FCPA accounting provisions apply to issuers regardless of bribery — inaccurate records of payments alone violate.
- **Conflicts of interest vs bribery.** Conflict = undisclosed interest in a transaction the employee influences. No external payor required.

**Authoritative sources:** FCPA Resource Guide (DOJ/SEC); UK SFO guidance; ACFE Manual corruption sections.

## Chapter 5: Law for the Fraud Examiner
**Core questions:** What is the difference between a criminal prosecution and a civil recovery action, and what rules of evidence shape both?

**Key concepts**
- Criminal vs civil: beyond a reasonable doubt vs preponderance of the evidence (clear and convincing in fraud-related equitable actions)
- US Constitution: Fourth Amendment (state action; consent, exigent circumstances, plain view, Terry, automobile exceptions; probable cause); Fifth Amendment self-incrimination + immunity (transactional vs use); Sixth Amendment confrontation + counsel
- Privileges: attorney-client, work product (Hickman v. Taylor), Fifth Amendment
- Federal Rules of Evidence: FRE 401-403 relevance, FRE 404-406 character, FRE 801 hearsay + exceptions (FRE 803 business records, 804 unavailability), FRE 901 authentication, FRE 1001-1008 best evidence
- Expert witness: Daubert factors (testability, peer review, error rate, general acceptance)
- Major statutes: mail/wire fraud (18 USC 1341/1343), RICO 18 USC 1961-68 (predicate acts, enterprise, pattern of at least two predicates within 10 years), FCPA, False Claims Act (31 USC 3729 et seq., qui tam relator share 15-30%), Sarbanes-Oxley 302 (certification), 404 (ICFR), 802 (document retention), 906 (criminal certification), 1107 (whistleblower retaliation), Dodd-Frank Section 922 (SEC whistleblower bounty 10-30% of penalties over $1M, retaliation protection)
- AML statutes: BSA, USA PATRIOT Act, 18 USC 1956 (laundering) and 1957 (monetary transactions in criminally derived property over $10K)

**Applied skills**
- Decide when to involve counsel, run an investigation under privilege, or refer to government
- Lay a proper foundation for a business record in a written report
- Identify whether evidence will survive a Daubert challenge

**Common traps**
- **Fourth Amendment reach.** Restricts state action. Private employer searches generally fall outside its scope unless government involvement makes the private actor an agent of the state.
- **Hearsay business records.** FRE 803(6) requires record made at or near the time, by someone with knowledge, kept in the regular course, regular practice to make such records, established by custodian or other qualified witness.
- **Work product vs attorney-client.** Attorney-client protects communications for legal advice. Work product protects material prepared in anticipation of litigation — broader scope but qualified protection.
- **RICO pattern.** At least two predicate acts within 10 years; the predicates must form a pattern (related + continuous), not just two random events.
- **FCA qui tam relator share.** 15-25% if government intervenes, 25-30% if government declines and relator proceeds.

**Authoritative sources:** ACFE Fraud Examiners Manual Law section; FRE; relevant Supreme Court decisions.

## Chapter 6: Fraud Examination Methodology and Interviewing
**Core questions:** How do you build a case from least to most intrusive, and how do you conduct an admission-seeking interview without contaminating it?

**Key concepts**
- The fraud theory approach: analyze data, develop hypothesis, refine hypothesis, test against documents and people
- Predication before investigation
- Interview phases: introductory, informational, assessment, closing, admission-seeking
- Cognitive interview: open-ended recall, context reinstatement, change order, change perspective
- Question types: open vs closed, leading, double-negative trap, complex
- Admission-seeking interview: themes, transition statements, presenting evidence, handling denials, obtaining detailed confession (verbal then written), benchmark admission elements
- Behavioral cues used with care (no single tell is reliable)
- Sequence: least involved → most involved → subject

**Applied skills**
- Sequence witnesses from neutral to suspect-adjacent to subject
- Draft an admission-seeking opening that is firm, calm, and noncoercive
- Avoid contaminating an interview through compound or leading questions early on

**Common traps**
- **Admission-seeking comes last.** The admission-seeking interview is the final phase, after evidence is gathered. Going first wastes the one chance.
- **Open vs closed in the informational phase.** Use open-ended questions to elicit narrative. Closed questions are confirmatory; reserve for assessment phase.
- **Behavioral analysis is not a polygraph.** Cluster of indicators with caution; never the sole basis for a conclusion.
- **Themes vs accusations.** Themes minimize the moral significance of the act to permit the subject to admit; they do not minimize legal consequences.

**Authoritative sources:** ACFE Manual Investigation section; cognitive interview research (Fisher & Geiselman).

## Chapter 7: Tracing Illicit Funds, Digital Forensics, and Source Documents
**Core questions:** How do you follow funds across accounts, entities, and borders without losing chain of custody?

**Key concepts**
- Direct (specific-item) method: trace each transaction
- Indirect methods: Net Worth Method (assets - liabilities - prior net worth - known income = unexplained funds), Expenditures Method (source and application of funds), Bank Deposits Method
- Source documents: bank statements, wire records, invoices, journals, contracts, email, vendor master, payroll registers
- Digital forensics fundamentals: imaging (bit-for-bit), hashing (MD5/SHA), write-blockers, metadata, preservation; cloud and mobile considerations
- Chain of custody: who, when, what, where; sealed evidence bags; access logs
- Cryptocurrency tracing: address clustering, exchange off-ramps, mixers, peel chains
- OSINT and public records: corporate registries, court filings, property records, UCC filings

**Applied skills**
- Build a funds-flow chart that a jury could follow
- Choose between Net Worth and Expenditures methods based on what records you have
- Preserve digital evidence in a way that survives a Daubert challenge

**Common traps**
- **Net Worth vs Expenditures.** Net Worth requires accurate beginning net worth; if you don't have it, use Expenditures. Both indirect methods need a "likely source" theory.
- **Hashing.** Hashes prove a copy is identical to the original. Not encryption.
- **Chain of custody breaks.** A gap in custody is fatal to admissibility. Document every transfer.
- **Cryptocurrency anonymity vs pseudonymity.** Bitcoin is pseudonymous, not anonymous. Address clustering plus exchange KYC often deanonymizes.

**Authoritative sources:** ACFE Manual Investigation section; NIST SP 800-86 forensic guidance.

## Chapter 8: Prevention, Deterrence, COSO, and Fraud Risk Management
**Core questions:** What does a fraud risk management program look like, and how does it connect to COSO and the board?

**Key concepts**
- COSO Internal Control — Integrated Framework (2013): 5 components (Control Environment, Risk Assessment, Control Activities, Information and Communication, Monitoring Activities) and 17 principles
- COSO ERM (2017): governance and culture, strategy and objective-setting, performance, review and revision, information/communication/reporting
- Three Lines of Defense: business ownership (1st), risk and compliance (2nd), internal audit (3rd)
- Fraud risk assessment: identify schemes by branch, assess likelihood/impact, map controls, address gaps
- Anti-fraud programs: code of conduct, whistleblower hotline, training, tone at the top, governance, board's role, fraud risk register
- SOX anti-fraud provisions: 302 certification, 404 ICFR, 802 document retention, 906 criminal cert, 1107 whistleblower retaliation
- ACFE Managing the Business Risk of Fraud (with IIA/AICPA)

**Applied skills**
- Design a fraud risk assessment that names schemes, not generic risks
- Recommend layered controls (preventive, detective, corrective) by scheme
- Brief a board on a hotline tip with appropriate restraint

**Common traps**
- **COSO IC vs ERM.** IC is internal control over financial reporting and operations; ERM is enterprise-wide risk management. Different scope, overlapping vocabulary.
- **Three Lines model update.** The 2020 IIA update relabeled "Lines of Defense" as "Three Lines Model" with emphasis on alignment, not just defense.
- **Tone at the top vs tone in the middle.** Both matter; middle-management modeling often outweighs CEO statements in field studies.
- **Hotline design.** Anonymous, confidential, multi-channel, third-party operated, with documented disposition. Anonymous and confidential are not the same.

**Authoritative sources:** COSO Internal Control Integrated Framework; COSO ERM 2017; ACFE/IIA/AICPA Managing the Business Risk of Fraud.

## Chapter 9: ACFE Code of Professional Standards, Reporting, and Working with Stakeholders
**Core questions:** What does the ACFE Code require, and how do you write a report that helps rather than harms a case?

**Key concepts**
- ACFE Code of Professional Standards — four sections: Professional Conduct and Ethics (integrity, no conflicts, lawful means), Independence and Objectivity (no opinion of guilt), Confidentiality (no unauthorized disclosure), Professional Competence (only accept engagements within competence)
- Report writing: facts vs opinions, exhibits, summary of findings, recommendations; signed and dated
- No opinion on legal guilt: describe conduct, name the evidence, do not assert "X is guilty"
- Coordination with counsel, HR, law enforcement, insurers, external auditors
- Report distribution and privilege: written for litigation may attach work product

**Applied skills**
- Avoid stating an opinion on legal guilt; describe conduct and the evidence supporting findings
- Decide who receives the report and what attaches privilege

**Common traps**
- **"In my opinion, Jones committed fraud."** Violates the Code. Examiners describe; the trier of fact decides.
- **Confidentiality limits.** Confidentiality yields to lawful subpoena, regulator request, and engagement-authorized disclosures.
- **Competence boundary.** Accepting a healthcare fraud engagement with no healthcare-coding background may breach professional competence even if you can investigate "in general."

**Authoritative sources:** ACFE Code of Professional Standards; ACFE Code of Professional Ethics for Certified Fraud Examiners.

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
The four CFE sections can be scheduled separately and over time. Each section is open-book to the ACFE Fraud Examiners Manual — treat it as a verification tool, not a study substitute. Pacing target: ~1.9 minutes per item, with strategic flagging and return. Pass each section at 75%. Common failure mode: spending exam time looking up items that should have been known cold, leaving real lookups for the end when time has run out.

**Capstone deliverables**
- One mixed quiz per section in the final 2 weeks with written postmortem
- Personal exam-day checklist: manual access, breaks between sections, flag-and-return protocol
- Error log review keyed to Fraud Tree branch and ACFE section weakness
