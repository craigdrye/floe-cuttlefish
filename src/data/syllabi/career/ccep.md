# CCEP Compliance Program Design
**ID**: `ccep` - **Discipline**: Compliance - **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course prepares you for the Certified Compliance and Ethics Professional credential and, more importantly, for the judgment the credential is supposed to certify. The CCEP exam tests whether you understand the elements of an effective compliance and ethics program; the job tests whether you can build one that holds up when a regulator, a board, and a panicked business leader are all looking at the same incident and reaching different conclusions. Those are not the same skill, and this syllabus refuses to treat them as one. You will learn the framework precisely enough to answer 100 scored multiple-choice questions, and you will practice the underlying craft until the framework stops feeling like vocabulary and starts feeling like a way of seeing the organization.

The spine of the course is the modern consensus on what "effective" means: the seven elements of the U.S. Sentencing Guidelines (§8B2.1), the three fundamental questions the DOJ asks in its Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs — is the program *well designed*, is it *adequately resourced and empowered* to function, and does it *work in practice* — and the operational reality that a program lives or dies on standards, governance, education, communication, monitoring, enforcement, response, and prevention. We translate each of these into a deliverable a compliance officer actually produces: a charter, a risk assessment, an escalation matrix, an investigation memo, a monitoring dashboard, a board report. The recurring discipline is the gap between a policy that *reads* well and a control that *works*, because that gap is where programs fail and where examiners look first.

By the end you should be able to take a compliance failure or a design question and do four things a hiring manager and an examiner both want: locate the obligation and the control meant to satisfy it, judge whether the control is designed and resourced to actually do that, decide the proportionate response (escalate, investigate, remediate, report) with a defensible record behind it, and explain the program's health to a board in language a director can act on. Tools and org charts differ between companies; this reasoning is portable, and it is what the CCEP letters are meant to stand for.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when learners are preparing for the SCCE/CCB Certified Compliance and Ethics Professional exam or building a general corporate compliance-and-ethics program: the seven elements of an effective program, program governance and independence, risk assessment, policies and training, reporting channels and non-retaliation, investigations, monitoring and auditing, discipline and incentives, and board-ready reporting. Keep it distinct from the regulator-specific tracks — `bankComplianceAmlRoadmap` and `amlCompliance` (financial-crimes/BSA-AML), `crcm` (consumer-regulatory compliance management), and `aigp` (AI governance). When a question is industry-specific (healthcare fraud and abuse, banking AML, privacy), teach the cross-cutting principle here and let the specialist track carry the regulation.

The voice is a seasoned chief compliance officer mentoring a capable analyst toward the CCO chair — concrete, plain-language, no element left as a buzzword, and relentlessly focused on the *defensible decision and the evidence behind it*. Every chapter carries Common traps, because in compliance the expensive error is rarely exotic: it is the program that documents a control it does not operate, the investigation that prejudges the outcome, the discipline applied to the junior employee but not the senior one, or the board deck that reports activity volume as if it were risk reduction. Keep facts current and frame them honestly: the seven elements live in USSG §8B2.1; the DOJ ECCP's three fundamental questions were reinforced in the September 2024 update, which added explicit expectations on emerging-technology and AI risk, whistleblower encouragement and anti-retaliation, and whether compliance has *proportionate* resources and timely access to the company's data. The CCEP exam is currently 115 questions (100 scored) over two hours, with a separate eligibility path (a Compliance Professional designation plus continuing-education units) — flag that fees, item counts, and CEU rules are set by CCB and change, so verify the current handbook rather than memorizing a number. Distractors should be plausible-but-flawed (the policy-vs-control confusion, the resourced-on-paper program, the inconsistent enforcement), not silly.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
- **What the CCEP tests**: a content outline built around the elements of an effective compliance and ethics program and the compliance professional's role — standards and code, governance and oversight, risk assessment, training and communication, reporting and investigation, monitoring/auditing and enforcement, plus legal foundations (Sentencing Guidelines, DOJ ECCP, sector frameworks). It is judgment-in-context, not legal-citation recall: most items are "given this situation, what is the *most appropriate* action," and the trap answers are the ones that are defensible-sounding but second-best.
- **Format and logistics (verify against the current CCB handbook)**: roughly 115 questions with 100 scored over two hours; eligibility requires a Compliance Professional designation (full-time compliance experience or a set number of compliance work hours within the prior two years) and continuing-education units earned in the window before the exam. Treat every specific number here as a pointer to confirm, not gospel.
- **Study rhythm**: spend the first third internalizing the *why* of each of the seven elements and the three DOJ questions until you can recite them as a diagnostic, not a list. Spend the middle third running scenarios — "is this control designed, resourced, and working?" — against each element. Spend the last third on timing and trap discipline: practice picking the *most appropriate* action under a two-minute-per-question pace, and after every miss write one sentence on *why the better answer was better*. The exam rewards the candidate who reasons from program effectiveness, not the one who memorized definitions.

## Chapter 1: What "Effective" Means — The Seven Elements and the Three Questions
- **Core questions**: What separates a program that exists on paper from one that works? Which standard does an evaluator actually apply? Which element is this organization weakest on right now?
- **Key concepts**: the seven elements of an effective compliance and ethics program under USSG §8B2.1 (standards and procedures; high-level oversight and adequate resources/authority; reasonable efforts to keep bad actors out of authority; training and communication; monitoring, auditing, and a reporting system with non-retaliation; consistent enforcement and incentives; reasonable steps to respond and prevent recurrence); the DOJ ECCP's three fundamental questions — *well designed*, *adequately resourced and empowered to function*, *works in practice*; the difference between a program's *design*, its *implementation*, and its *operating effectiveness*; reasonable-efforts/good-faith as the legal standard rather than perfection.
- **Applied skills**: score a described program against the seven elements and name the single weakest one with evidence; restate a vague "we have a compliance program" claim as the three DOJ questions and identify which one is unanswered.
- **Common traps**: treating the seven elements as a checklist to tick rather than capabilities to demonstrate; assuming a well-designed program is automatically effective (design and operation are separate questions); confusing the existence of a policy with the existence of a control; thinking "effective" means flawless rather than reasonably designed and operating in good faith.

## Chapter 2: Standards — Code of Conduct, Policies, and Procedures
- **Core questions**: Where does a given expectation belong — code, policy, procedure, or training? Is this standard written for the lawyer or for the employee who has to follow it? Could a frontline worker actually do what this requires?
- **Key concepts**: the document hierarchy (code of conduct as the values-and-expectations apex; policy as the rule; procedure as the how; training and job aids as the delivery); plain-language drafting and readability; policy lifecycle and ownership (drafting, approval, version control, periodic review, retirement); the test that every standard maps to a real risk and a real owner; tone-at-the-top and tone-in-the-middle as the standard's enforcement context.
- **Applied skills**: take a single obligation and place each part of it correctly across code, policy, procedure, and training; rewrite a dense policy clause into a behavior an employee can perform and a manager can observe.
- **Common traps**: putting operational detail in the code (which then can't change without a board cycle) or putting binding rules only in training (which then aren't enforceable policy); drafting standards no one owns or reviews; writing for defensibility in litigation at the cost of comprehension by employees; assuming a published policy changes behavior without communication and reinforcement.

## Chapter 3: Governance, Independence, and Adequate Resources
- **Core questions**: Does compliance have enough authority, access, and budget to do its job — and how would you *prove* that to a regulator? Who hears the report the business does not want heard? Where could business pressure quietly distort a compliance decision?
- **Key concepts**: board and audit-committee oversight; senior-management accountability; the chief compliance officer's seniority, independence, and direct line to the board; the distinction between *authority* (can act and escalate) and *resources* (people, budget, data, technology); the DOJ's "proportionate resource allocation" lens — comparing what compliance has to what the rest of the business has; reporting structure (solid-line vs dotted-line to legal, the CEO, the board) and its effect on independence; access to data as a resource question, sharpened in the 2024 ECCP update.
- **Applied skills**: draw a reporting-line map showing board, audit committee, CCO, legal, HR, internal audit, and the business units, and mark the points where independence is strongest and weakest; build a short "is compliance adequately resourced?" argument using authority, budget, headcount, and data access as evidence.
- **Common traps**: equating a fancy title with real authority; burying compliance under the function it is supposed to check (e.g., reporting solely to the business it monitors); assuming "we have a CCO" answers the resourcing question; ignoring data access as a resource and then being unable to monitor anything in time to matter.

## Chapter 4: Risk Assessment and Control Prioritization
- **Core questions**: What are we actually trying to prevent, and how likely and how damaging is each failure? Which risks deserve scarce control effort first? Does this control reduce the risk or merely document it?
- **Key concepts**: inherent vs residual risk; likelihood and impact scoring; risk appetite as a business/board decision (not the analyst's); the risk-based principle that controls and resources follow the risk; control design vs control operation; control testing and gap identification; remediation ownership and dates; emerging-risk management (AI, new products, new geographies) as an explicit ECCP expectation; the risk assessment as a living document tied to the program, not an annual artifact filed and forgotten.
- **Applied skills**: build a ranked risk assessment that ties each top risk to a control, an owner, a gap, and a remediation date; distinguish a control that lowers residual risk from one that only creates evidence; flag an emerging risk and propose a proportionate first control.
- **Common traps**: rating risk by industry label rather than the organization's actual exposure and activity; spreading control effort evenly instead of concentrating it where residual risk is highest; treating the risk assessment as a compliance deliverable rather than a decision tool; mistaking "we wrote a policy about it" for "we reduced the risk."

## Chapter 5: Training, Communication, and Speak-Up Culture
- **Core questions**: Who needs to know this to do their job differently? Is the training changing behavior or just generating completion certificates? What message makes an employee willing to raise a hard concern?
- **Key concepts**: role-based, risk-targeted training vs generic annual click-through; behavior-specific learning objectives; certifications and attestations as evidence; manager toolkits and tone-in-the-middle; communication cadence and reinforcement; speak-up culture and psychological safety; non-retaliation messaging as both a legal protection and a cultural signal; the DOJ's heightened focus on whistleblower *encouragement* (not just a hotline's existence).
- **Applied skills**: design a training and communication plan that names the audience, the behavior to change, the channel, the reinforcement, and the evidence of completion; write speak-up messaging that lowers the perceived cost of reporting.
- **Common traps**: measuring training by completion rate rather than behavior change or comprehension; one-size-fits-all annual training that ignores role-specific risk; treating the existence of a hotline as proof of a speak-up culture; non-retaliation as a poster rather than a demonstrated, enforced commitment.

## Chapter 6: Reporting Channels, Triage, and Escalation
- **Core questions**: What *is* this — a question, a concern, an allegation, an emergency, or a control failure? Who must be told, by when, and with exactly what facts? What must not be guessed at or written down as conclusion?
- **Key concepts**: hotline and open-door channels; anonymity and confidentiality; non-retaliation in operation; intake triage and severity classification; routing and decision authority; escalation timelines; when legal privilege attaches and who can direct privileged work; documentation hygiene at intake (facts vs allegations vs assumptions); the difference between an intake decision and an investigative conclusion.
- **Applied skills**: build an escalation matrix mapping intake category to severity, routing, timeline, decision authority, and documentation requirement; triage a raw report into the right category and the right urgency, citing the facts that drove the call.
- **Common traps**: recording speculation as fact at intake; routing by org politics rather than severity and independence; treating every report as either an emergency or noise instead of classifying it; assuming anonymity equals confidentiality, or promising more confidentiality than the process can keep; missing the moment a concern became an urgent escalation.

## Chapter 7: Investigations and Case Management
- **Core questions**: What allegation, stated precisely, am I testing? What evidence would confirm it — and what would disprove it? Could an independent reviewer reach my conclusion from the file alone, and would they call the process fair?
- **Key concepts**: intake-to-plan conversion; investigation scope and the discipline of knowing when to stop; evidence preservation and chain of custody; interview planning and witness sequencing; confidentiality and conflict-of-interest screening (the investigator can't report to the subject); privilege management; the standard of proof for *substantiation* vs proof beyond doubt; corrective action; case closure and a defensible closing memo; consistency across cases as a fairness requirement.
- **Applied skills**: turn an allegation into an investigation plan with scope, evidence sources, interview order, and a substantiation standard; write a closing memo covering allegation, scope, evidence weighed, findings, residual uncertainty, remediation, and lessons learned.
- **Common traps**: anchoring on the first hypothesis and reading every fact as confirmation; letting scope sprawl until the investigation never closes; assigning an investigator with a conflict; recording the conclusion without the evidence that produced it; inconsistent handling that punishes the junior subject and protects the senior one.

## Chapter 8: Monitoring, Auditing, and Program Effectiveness
- **Core questions**: What should compliance monitor itself, and what belongs to internal audit? Which number is a real signal of risk, and which is just activity? How would I demonstrate the program *works in practice* rather than merely exists?
- **Key concepts**: the line between compliance monitoring (continuous, within the second line) and independent auditing (the third line); key risk indicators vs vanity metrics; testing schedules and sampling; data analytics and continuous monitoring; issue validation and root-cause analysis; the third DOJ question (does it work in practice) and the evidence that answers it; continuous improvement as a program property, not a project.
- **Applied skills**: build a monitoring dashboard with KRIs, a testing schedule, exceptions, root causes, and follow-up owners; pair an activity metric with a quality metric so the dashboard tells the truth (e.g., case volume alongside substantiation rate and timeliness).
- **Common traps**: reporting activity (hotline call counts, training completions) as if it measured risk reduction; blurring the independence line by having compliance "audit" its own controls; treating a falling backlog as success when it was cleared by lowering rigor; finding issues and never validating that remediation closed the root cause.

## Chapter 9: Enforcement, Incentives, and Discipline
- **Core questions**: Is discipline consistent with how comparable cases were handled? Do the company's incentives quietly reward the behavior the code forbids? Did the consequence reach the people with the most power, not just the least?
- **Key concepts**: consistent enforcement as a Sentencing-Guidelines and ECCP expectation; disciplinary matrices and comparability; the role of incentives and clawbacks (rewarding compliance, penalizing misconduct, including at senior levels); the DOJ's interest in whether compensation structures encourage or deter wrongdoing; documenting the rationale for disciplinary decisions; the credibility cost of enforcement that spares senior or high-performing employees.
- **Applied skills**: test a proposed disciplinary action against prior comparable cases and flag inconsistency; review an incentive or compensation structure for a behavior it inadvertently rewards and propose a fix.
- **Common traps**: inconsistent discipline across seniority or performance that destroys program credibility; incentive schemes that reward the outcome while the code forbids the means; treating enforcement as HR's silo with no compliance link to the underlying risk; failing to document why a given consequence was chosen, leaving it indefensible later.

## Chapter 10: Response, Remediation, and Board-Ready Reporting
- **Core questions**: Did the fix address the root cause or just the symptom? What does the board need to see versus what is management-level detail? Would this record survive a regulator's review a year from now?
- **Key concepts**: incident response and root-cause remediation (reasonable steps to prevent recurrence — the seventh element); self-disclosure considerations and cooperation credit at a conceptual level; the difference between board reporting (risk posture, significant matters, program effectiveness, decisions needed) and management reporting (operational detail); regulator-ready recordkeeping; the annual program assessment that answers the three DOJ questions; tailoring the message so a director can govern, not drown.
- **Applied skills**: write a board reporting pack with a risk summary, significant issues, investigation and trend data, remediation status, and a program-effectiveness narrative; turn a closed incident into a root-cause remediation plan with prevention steps and an owner.
- **Common traps**: remediating the incident but not the condition that produced it; flooding the board with operational noise so the one decision they must make is buried; reporting only good news (no significant issues ever) which itself signals a program that isn't looking; keeping records that can't reconstruct the decision when an examiner asks a year later.

## Capstone
Take one fictional organization — mid-size, growing fast, recently flagged in a regulator's industry sweep, with a CCO who reports through the General Counsel and a sales incentive plan everyone privately worries about — and build the program and the proof that it works. Produce: (1) a one-page program diagnosis scoring the organization against the seven elements and the three DOJ questions, naming the single weakest area with evidence; (2) a reporting-line and governance map that marks where independence and resourcing are strongest and weakest, with a recommendation on the CCO's line; (3) a ranked risk assessment tying the top risks to controls, owners, gaps, and remediation dates, including at least one emerging (e.g., AI or new-product) risk; (4) a training and speak-up plan targeting the highest-risk behavior; (5) an escalation matrix and the triage of one raw report through it; (6) an investigation plan and closing memo for that report, including the residual uncertainty; (7) a monitoring dashboard that pairs at least one activity metric with a quality metric; and (8) a one-page board report that states the program's effectiveness, the significant matters, and the single decision you need from the board. Close with one paragraph answering the question an examiner would ask last: of everything you built, which control would you most expect to *fail in practice*, how would you know, and what evidence would prove you wrong — the habit that separates a CCO who runs a program from one who merely owns the binder.

## Assessment Ideas
- Element-scoring exercises graded on whether the learner can distinguish design, resourcing, and operating effectiveness rather than tick a checklist.
- Escalation and triage scenarios graded on severity judgment, correct routing, and clean fact-vs-conclusion documentation.
- Investigation work graded on fairness, scope discipline, evidence weighing, and a conclusion an independent reviewer could reconstruct.
- Board-reporting drills graded on the move from activity to risk, clarity for a director, and the surfacing of significant matters rather than only good news.
- Capstone graded on program coverage, defensible governance and resourcing logic, evidence quality, and the candor of the "what would fail in practice" reflection.
