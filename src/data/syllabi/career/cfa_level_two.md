# CFA Level II: The Vignette Dojo
**ID**: `cfaLevelTwo` · **Discipline**: Finance

## Course Aim
CFA Level II is the exam where the formulas grow teeth. Level I asked whether you could recall definitions; Level II hands you a two-page vignette with a slightly evasive CFO, a footnote that does most of the real work, and four item-set questions whose right answers all depend on whether you read carefully. Pass Level II and you have proven you can pull a fact out of a 10-K, run it through a valuation model, defend the assumption you picked, and answer the question that was actually asked rather than the one you wanted to see. That is the difference between a junior analyst who pastes consensus into a model and one a CIO will eventually trust with sector coverage.

The exam itself is now 88 multiple-choice item-set questions across 22 vignettes, split into two 2-hour-12-minute sessions. Every vignette is roughly 1-2 pages with 4 questions attached. Most candidates report Level II is the hardest of the three because the volume of formulas is higher than Level I and the application is much harder than Level III — there is nowhere to hide behind generic IPS language; the numbers either reconcile or they do not. The credential gates the buy-side associate roles, sell-side analyst seats with publishing rights, FSA-heavy banking roles, and most fundamental long-only and long-short shops. Recruiters read "Level II passed" as "this person can build and defend a model."

What you leave with: fluency in equity, fixed-income, and derivatives valuation; comfort with multinational consolidation, pensions, and intercorporate investments; calm in front of regression output; calibration on item-set traps; and a working analyst's instinct for which footnote moves the answer. You will not finish memorizing every detail — nobody does — but you will know which model to reach for, how to spot the trap distractor, and how to read a vignette once carefully rather than four times anxiously.

The voice of this track should match what is actually being tested: a patient mentor with a red pen, willing to point at the sentence in the footnote that decides the answer, never afraid to say "the model is correct but the input is wrong," and allergic to the kind of memorize-and-pray habit that gets candidates into trouble at this level.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test item-set reasoning, mid-depth valuation, intercorporate-investment accounting, pension or multinational FSA, regression or time-series interpretation, term-structure and option-pricing models, swap or futures valuation, multifactor portfolio reasoning, alternative-investments depth, or the slightly meaner Ethics traps that show up once candidates have started pattern-matching. The signature Level II voice is **vignette stamina plus valuation rigor**: every prompt should resemble a fragment of a vignette (one named company, 1-3 facts, then the question) rather than a definitional ask. Distractors should reward close reading, not trivia.

If a question is purely definitional or single-formula, it belongs in Level I, not here. If a question is judgment-heavy ("recommend an allocation given the client's constraints") or essay-style, it belongs in Level III. Level II lives in the middle: enough application to be challenging, enough specificity to have a single defensible right answer.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The 2024+ Level II exam uses 22 vignettes with 4 item-set questions each (88 questions total) across two sessions of 2 hours 12 minutes. CFA Institute publishes topic weights as **ranges** that may shift between administrations; the table below gives current bands and a working study posture.

| Topic | Exam Weight | Study Posture |
|---|---:|---|
| Ethical and Professional Standards | 10-15% | Practice item-set Ethics weekly; expect multiple violations per vignette |
| Quantitative Methods | 5-10% | Multi-regression, time-series, ML intuition, big-data |
| Economics | 5-10% | Currencies, growth models, regulation, neutral rates |
| Financial Statement Analysis | 10-15% | Intercorp investments, pensions, multinational, quality of earnings |
| Corporate Issuers | 5-10% | Capital structure, ESG, M&A, restructurings, cost of capital |
| Equity Investments | 10-15% | DDM, FCFE/FCFF, residual income, market-based, private company |
| Fixed Income | 10-15% | Term structure, arbitrage-free pricing, OAS, MBS, credit |
| Derivatives | 5-10% | Forwards/futures, swaps, BSM and binomial trees, hedging |
| Alternative Investments | 5-10% | PE, real estate, hedge funds, commodities, infrastructure |
| Portfolio Management | 10-15% | Multifactor models, active management, trading, attribution |

**Realistic prep:** 300-400 hours over 4-6 months. Most candidates who pass put in 350+, and most retakes were 250-300 hour attempts the first time. A working professional's typical cadence is 15-20 hours/week.

**Sample 16-week plan (300-330 hours):**
- **Weeks 1-2** (40h): Ethics + Quant. Read both topics end-to-end; do 60 EOC questions.
- **Weeks 3-5** (60h): FSA — intercorporate investments, pensions, multinational, quality of earnings. This is the highest-yield topic; over-invest.
- **Week 6** (20h): Corporate Issuers + Economics. Lighter weight but easy points.
- **Weeks 7-8** (40h): Equity Valuation — DDMs, FCFE/FCFF, RI, market-based.
- **Weeks 9-10** (40h): Fixed Income — term structure, arbitrage-free pricing, OAS, MBS, credit.
- **Week 11** (20h): Derivatives — binomial, BSM, swaps.
- **Week 12** (15h): Alts + Portfolio Mgmt.
- **Week 13** (20h): First full mock + review. Expect 55-60%; do not panic.
- **Weeks 14-15** (40h): Targeted weak-area work + a second mock.
- **Week 16** (15h): Light review, third mock, Ethics re-read, calculator sanity check.

**Weekly cadence:** Mon-Wed reading and EOC; Thu-Fri item-set vignettes from a question bank; Sat full-topic timed block; Sun review error log and re-do any question missed twice. Every miss earns one error-log line that names the topic, the trap, and the exact sentence you should have re-read.

## Chapter 1: Ethics in Item-Set Form
**Core questions:** Which Standards are violated when the analyst, the firm, and the client all behave slightly badly in the same vignette? Where does GIPS reporting connect to ethical performance presentation?

**Key concepts**
- Code and Standards with item-set emphasis: research independence (I(B)), material nonpublic information (II(A)), fair dealing (III(B)), suitability (III(C)), performance presentation (III(D)), preservation of confidentiality (III(E)), record retention (V(C))
- Research Objectivity Standards and the Asset Manager Code where relevant
- GIPS principles: composite construction (one composite per strategy, all fee-paying discretionary portfolios included), input data, return calculation, presentation, verification (firm-wide, not composite-level)
- Soft Dollar Standards: mixed-use allocation, client commission as client property

**Applied skills**
- Diagnose multiple simultaneous violations in a long vignette and pick the most severe
- Distinguish disclosure cures from outright prohibitions
- Read a composite presentation and spot dispersion, hierarchy, or terminated-portfolio errors

**Common traps**
- Treating "I disclosed it" as a cure for every conflict — disclosure does not fix MNPI trading or front-running
- Confusing GIPS verification (firm-wide) with performance examination (composite-level)
- Removing terminated portfolios retroactively from a composite (survivor bias) — they must remain through their last full period
- Soft dollars for **mixed-use** products: must be **allocated** between research (client-paid) and non-research (firm-paid), not all-or-nothing
- Selective disclosure to "large clients first" before publishing — Fair Dealing (III(B)) violation regardless of how nice the dinner was

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute *Code and Standards* and *Standards of Practice Handbook*; *Global Investment Performance Standards* (current GIPS for Firms); Schweser Book 1; Wiley Ethics review.

## Chapter 2: Quantitative Methods for Item-Sets
**Core questions:** When a regression output sits in front of you, what does it tell you, what does it lie about, and what would a careful analyst test next?

**Key concepts**
- Multiple regression: coefficients, standard errors, t- and F-tests, R-squared vs adjusted R-squared, dummy variables, model misspecification
- Diagnostics: multicollinearity (high R-squared + insignificant t-stats), heteroskedasticity (Breusch-Pagan), serial correlation (Durbin-Watson)
- Time-series: trend, AR(p), unit roots and Dickey-Fuller, cointegration, seasonality, ARCH, RMSE comparison
- Machine-learning intuition: supervised vs unsupervised, overfitting, train/validation/test splits, big-data pipelines, fintech applications

**Applied skills**
- Read a regression table and decide which coefficient deserves a footnote rather than a headline
- Detect spurious regression in time-series output (high R-squared between unrelated trending series)
- Translate model risk into investment language a portfolio manager can act on

**Common traps**
- Confusing R-squared (fit) with predictive accuracy out of sample
- Reading a significant F with insignificant individual t-stats as "good model" — this is the multicollinearity signature
- Forgetting that a unit root makes standard inference invalid until first-differencing
- Heteroskedasticity inflates Type I error rates (false positives); use White-corrected SEs
- Confusing the **conditional** heteroskedasticity tested by ARCH with simple heteroskedasticity

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Quantitative Methods curriculum; Schweser Book 1; Wiley Quant Methods review; Brooks *Introductory Econometrics for Finance*.

## Chapter 3: Economics — Growth, Currencies, and Regulation
**Core questions:** How do exchange rates, growth differentials, and regulatory choices shape valuation inputs and capital flows?

**Key concepts**
- Currency exchange rates: spot/forward quotes, covered interest rate parity (CIP), uncovered IRP, PPP (absolute and relative), international Fisher relation, the Mundell-Fleming model, BoP-based currency forecasting
- Growth models: Solow (steady-state, golden rule), endogenous growth (R&D as factor), total factor productivity, demographic and capital-deepening drivers
- Economics of regulation: rationales (information asymmetry, externalities, market power), regulatory capture, costs of regulation, international cooperation
- Currency-cross arithmetic and forward-premium/discount sign conventions

**Applied skills**
- Apply CIP and UIP to cross-currency analysis and forward-rate calculation
- Translate macro views into discount-rate and free-cash-flow assumptions
- Evaluate when regulatory change is a tail risk vs base case for an issuer

**Common traps**
- Inverting the CIP formula (the higher-rate currency trades at a forward **discount**, not premium)
- Confusing absolute PPP (price-level equality) with relative PPP (rate of change)
- Treating UIP as empirically reliable — it routinely fails in short horizons (the carry-trade observation)
- Forgetting that a country with persistently higher real rates may attract capital inflows that **appreciate** the currency, opposite to PPP intuition over short horizons
- Misreading USD/EUR as "USD per 1 EUR" vs "EUR per 1 USD" — fail this on the test and the whole vignette dies

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Economics curriculum; Schweser Book 2; Wiley Economics review.

## Chapter 4: Financial Statement Analysis Deep Dive
**Core questions:** What does the analyst do when companies own pieces of each other, run pensions in multiple countries, or restate prior periods to disguise earnings momentum?

**Key concepts**
- Intercorporate investments: financial assets (FVPL, FVOCI, amortized cost), associates (equity method, 20-50%), joint ventures, business combinations and consolidation (>50% with NCI), special-purpose entities
- Employee compensation: defined-benefit vs defined-contribution post-employment plans (DBO/PBO, service cost, interest cost, expected return, prior service cost, actuarial gains/losses, settlements/curtailments); stock-based compensation (vesting, fair-value expense)
- Multinational operations: presentation vs functional currency; **current-rate method** (functional ≠ parent reporting → CTA in OCI) vs **temporal method** (functional = parent reporting → remeasurement gain/loss in income); hyperinflationary economies
- Quality of financial reports: accruals, conservatism vs aggressive recognition, earnings management red flags, Beneish M-score, Altman Z-score

**Applied skills**
- Adjust reported financials when comparing equity-method to consolidated peers (proportional adjustments)
- Convert reported pension expense to **economic pension expense** (current service cost + interest cost − actual return on plan assets ± plan amendments)
- Detect earnings-quality red flags hidden in footnotes (revenue recognition shifts, capitalized costs, related-party transactions)

**Common traps**
- Confusing CTA (current-rate, OCI) with remeasurement gain/loss (temporal, income statement) — the test loves this
- Believing the equity method recognizes dividends as income (it does not — dividends reduce carrying value)
- Treating prior service cost as immediate expense under US GAAP — amortized over remaining service life from OCI
- Forgetting that under IFRS, **net interest** on the net pension liability is the single P&L line; remeasurements (including actual vs expected return diff) go to OCI permanently
- Reading "associate" as triggering consolidation — it triggers the equity method

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute FSA curriculum (highest-yield reading); Schweser Books 2-3; Robinson *International Financial Statement Analysis*; Wiley FSA review.

## Chapter 5: Corporate Issuers
**Core questions:** How do capital-structure choices, dividend and buyback policies, ESG factors, and corporate-restructuring decisions create or destroy value?

**Key concepts**
- Capital structure theory: Modigliani-Miller without and with taxes, static trade-off (tax shield vs financial-distress costs), pecking order, target leverage
- Cost of capital: WACC under leverage changes, country risk premium for emerging markets, marginal vs average cost of capital
- ESG considerations in valuation and credit
- Corporate restructurings: spin-offs, split-offs, divestitures, leveraged buyouts, share repurchases (open market, fixed-price tender, Dutch auction, accelerated repurchase)
- Dividend policy: signaling, clientele, residual, stable, target payout

**Applied skills**
- Estimate WACC under different leverage assumptions using the Hamada equation
- Evaluate a buyback for EPS arithmetic, signaling, and value creation (E/P vs after-tax cost of debt)
- Score an M&A deal on synergies, breakup fees, form of payment (cash vs stock), and accretion/dilution

**Common traps**
- Treating EPS accretion as value creation — it is arithmetic, not value
- Confusing levered and unlevered beta when relevering across capital structures (Hamada)
- Ignoring that a buyback financed at after-tax cost of debt below the earnings yield is accretive even when it destroys value (and vice versa)
- Believing dividends are always value-relevant — Modigliani-Miller's dividend irrelevance applies in frictionless markets
- Forgetting that an LBO's IRR is driven by multiple expansion, debt paydown, and operational improvement — in that approximate order on a typical deal

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Corporate Issuers curriculum; Schweser Book 3; Wiley Corporate review; Damodaran *Investment Valuation* (selected chapters).

## Chapter 6: Equity Valuation
**Core questions:** What is this business worth, and which model gives the cleanest answer for this fact pattern?

**Key concepts**
- Discount dividend models: Gordon growth, two-stage, three-stage, H-model; PVGO (Price = E1/r + PVGO)
- Free cash flow: **FCFF** = NI + NCC + Int(1−t) − FCInv − WCInv (discounted at WACC); **FCFE** = NI + NCC − FCInv − WCInv + Net Borrowing (discounted at required return on equity)
- Residual income: V0 = B0 + Σ (ROE − r) × B(t-1) / (1+r)^t; useful when cash flows are negative or terminal value is unstable
- Market-based valuation: P/E (trailing vs forward), justified P/E from fundamentals (P0/E1 = (1−b)/(r−g)), EV/EBITDA, P/B, P/S
- Private company valuation: income approach, market approach, asset approach; control premiums and DLOC; marketability discounts (DLOM)

**Applied skills**
- Choose between FCFF (when capital structure is changing) and FCFE (when stable)
- Build a **justified** multiple from fundamentals rather than a peer median
- Use residual income when book-value-based franchises (banks, insurers) make DCF terminal values noisy

**Common traps**
- FCFF and FCFE both add back non-cash charges; FCFE **subtracts** net borrowing… wait — FCFE **adds** net borrowing. Many candidates flip the sign. Reread: FCFE = FCFF − Int(1−t) + Net Borrowing
- Discounting FCFF at the cost of equity (wrong — use WACC) or FCFE at WACC (wrong — use required return on equity)
- Computing g using ROE × b but forgetting g must be **sustainable** (cannot exceed the long-run economy growth rate)
- Treating PVGO as a free input — it is the residual after the no-growth value E1/r
- Forgetting that justified leading P/E uses E1, while trailing uses E0; the formulas differ by (1+g)

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Equity Investments curriculum; Schweser Book 4; Stowe *Equity Asset Valuation*; Damodaran *Investment Valuation*.

## Chapter 7: Fixed Income Mid-Depth
**Core questions:** How does the term structure of interest rates drive bond valuation, and how do embedded options change duration, convexity, and credit analysis?

**Key concepts**
- Term-structure theories (pure expectations, liquidity preference, market segmentation, preferred habitat); spot, forward, and par rates; bootstrapping the spot curve
- Arbitrage-free valuation, binomial interest-rate trees (calibrated to no-arbitrage), OAS (option-adjusted spread) for callable/putable bonds
- Mortgage-backed securities: prepayment (CPR, SMM, PSA), PAC vs support tranches, contraction risk (rates fall) vs extension risk (rates rise), CMOs
- Credit analysis: structural (Merton-style, equity as call) vs reduced-form models; credit migration; CDS pricing (premium leg vs protection leg, upfront vs running spread, ISDA conventions)
- Term structure of credit spreads, credit cycle, recovery rates

**Applied skills**
- Price a callable bond as straight bond − value of call; relate OAS to Z-spread by the value of the option
- Decompose CDS pricing into default probability and recovery assumptions
- Translate a yield curve shift (parallel, steepening, flattening, butterfly) into duration, key-rate duration, and convexity effects

**Common traps**
- For a **callable** bond, OAS < Z-spread (the call works against the investor); for a **putable** bond, OAS > Z-spread
- Treating modified duration as the right tool for an MBS — use effective duration because cash flows are path-dependent
- Forgetting that contraction risk (rates fall, prepayments accelerate) is the **bad** scenario for MBS investors because cash comes back at lower reinvestment rates
- Bootstrapping using YTM instead of par-bond yields — only par-bond yields bootstrap cleanly to spot rates
- Treating CDS upfront as the only cost — there is also a running spread

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Fixed Income curriculum; Schweser Books 4-5; Fabozzi *Fixed Income Analysis*; Tuckman & Serrat *Fixed Income Securities*.

## Chapter 8: Derivatives Pricing
**Core questions:** How do you price a forward, future, swap, or option without hand-waving — and how do hedging Greeks behave in the real world?

**Key concepts**
- Forward and futures pricing: cost-of-carry (F = S × e^((r−q+u)T) in continuous form), convenience yield, basis, contango vs backwardation, mark-to-market mechanics for futures
- Swap valuation: interest-rate swaps as a series of FRAs or as a portfolio of bonds (fixed-leg bond vs floating-leg bond); currency swaps; equity swaps (fixed-for-equity, floating-for-equity, equity-for-equity)
- Option pricing: one- and two-period binomial trees with risk-neutral probability p* = (1+r−d)/(u−d); Black-Scholes-Merton assumptions and inputs; put-call parity (C + PV(K) = P + S); the Greeks (delta, gamma, theta, vega, rho); implied volatility and the volatility smile/skew

**Applied skills**
- Build a one- or two-period binomial tree and reconcile with risk-neutral probability
- Decompose option value into intrinsic and time value; use delta-gamma for hedging
- Value an interest rate swap on a non-payment date as fixed-bond − floating-bond (floating resets to par on payment dates)

**Common traps**
- Confusing the **physical** probability with the **risk-neutral** probability — only the latter prices options
- Using simple compounding when the textbook expects continuous (e^rT) — read the question
- Forgetting that the floating leg of an IR swap is worth par at reset — between resets it is par + accrued floating
- Treating BSM as valid for American options without adjustment — early-exercise can dominate, especially for puts and dividend-paying calls
- Reading delta of 0.5 as "50% chance of finishing in the money" — delta is the hedge ratio and approximately the risk-neutral ITM probability for calls, but the two are subtly different

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Derivatives curriculum; Schweser Book 5; Hull *Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives* (the standard text).

## Chapter 9: Alternative Investments
**Core questions:** What drives returns, fees, and risks in private equity, real estate, commodities, hedge funds, and infrastructure — and which biases distort the reported track records?

**Key concepts**
- Private equity: GP/LP structure, 2-and-20, hurdle rate and catch-up, J-curve, vintage year, IRR vs MOIC vs TVPI vs DPI vs RVPI, PME comparison
- Real estate: direct ownership, REITs, NAV-based valuation (book vs market cap rates), capital indirect (CMBS), cap rate = NOI / value, GIM, DCF for real estate
- Hedge funds: equity L/S, market-neutral, global macro, event-driven, relative-value, managed futures; fee waterfall; lock-ups, side pockets, gates; survivorship and backfill biases
- Commodities: roll yield (contango = negative, backwardation = positive), total return = spot + roll + collateral
- Infrastructure: greenfield vs brownfield, regulated asset base

**Applied skills**
- Evaluate PE fund performance using PME (Kaplan-Schoar) to benchmark against a public index
- Decompose REIT returns into NOI growth, cap rate change, and leverage effects; stress-test cap rate assumptions
- Distinguish alpha from leverage in hedge-fund track records (annualized return / leverage ratio)

**Common traps**
- Treating IRR as comparable across vintages — different deployment pacing makes IRR misleading vs TVPI/DPI
- Forgetting the **J-curve**: PE IRR is negative in the first 2-3 years from fees and write-downs before exits begin
- Confusing **carried interest** (GP share of profits above hurdle, typically 20%) with **management fee** (2% on committed or invested capital)
- Reading reported hedge fund returns at face value despite **survivorship bias** (dead funds disappear) and **backfill bias** (only good early years reported when added to the index)
- Treating contango as inherently bad — roll yield matters only on the term structure at the time of roll, not the long-run mean

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Alternative Investments curriculum; Schweser Book 5; CAIA Level I/II readings (selective); Stowe and Robinson references.

## Chapter 10: Portfolio Management at Level II
**Core questions:** How does active management actually create value, and how is it measured, decomposed, and risk-budgeted?

**Key concepts**
- Multifactor models: macroeconomic, fundamental, statistical; Fama-French three-factor (market, SMB, HML); Carhart four-factor (adds momentum, UMD); Fama-French five-factor (adds profitability and investment)
- Active management math: information ratio = active return / active risk; fundamental law of active management IR ≈ IC × √breadth × TC (transfer coefficient)
- Trading strategy and execution; implementation shortfall
- Risk-budgeting: ex-ante vs ex-post tracking error; component risk vs marginal contribution to risk
- ETFs, smart beta, factor portfolios

**Applied skills**
- Decompose active return into factor exposures and stock selection
- Set an active risk budget consistent with an IR target and required confidence interval on alpha
- Distinguish skill from luck using transfer coefficient and breadth (more bets at lower IC can beat fewer bets at higher IC)

**Common traps**
- Confusing **information coefficient** (correlation between forecasted and realized active returns) with **information ratio** (annualized active return / active risk)
- Forgetting that the fundamental law's **breadth** counts **independent** decisions, not number of holdings
- Reading high tracking error as evidence of skill — tracking error is risk, not return
- Treating Fama-French SMB and HML loadings as alpha — they are factor exposures that can be replicated cheaply
- Conflating implementation shortfall with explicit trading costs — IS includes opportunity cost on unfilled orders

**Authoritative sources:** CFA Institute Portfolio Management curriculum; Schweser Book 6; Grinold & Kahn *Active Portfolio Management*; Ang *Asset Management*.

## Capstone: Exam-Day Discipline
Level II rewards the candidate who reads each vignette once carefully, annotates it (numbered facts in the margin), answers the exact question asked, and resists the urge to rebuild the analyst's entire model in their head. The math is:

- **Timing:** Roughly **90 seconds per question** on average (88 questions across 264 minutes). On hard vignettes you may spend 2-3 minutes per question and on easy ones 45 seconds — but never spend 4+ minutes on any single question without flagging and moving on.
- **Reading order:** Skim the vignette quickly first (30 seconds), then read the 4 questions, then re-read the vignette with the questions in mind. Most facts only matter once you know what is being asked.
- **Annotate:** Circle the named entity, underline the date, box the rates. The trap distractor often hinges on whether you used the right footnote.
- **Worst-section discipline:** If a vignette is going badly, get 1 of 4 correct (the easiest), guess the rest with a consistent letter, and move on. Sunk-cost time is the second-biggest reason candidates fail.
- **Calculator settings (BA II Plus):** **DEC = 9** (more decimal places, less rounding error), **P/Y = 1, C/Y = 1** (so N and I/Y are interpreted per the question without surprise compounding), clear TVM between problems, 2nd-FORMAT to verify settings before exam start.
- **Ethics last hour:** Ethics is anti-fatigue. Save 20 minutes for the Ethics vignettes when your judgment is freshest, not last when you are tired.

Build a one-page checklist for FSA adjustments and equity-valuation pivots, train the habit of walking past a question you cannot solve in 90 seconds, and accept that 65-70% raw is a passing exam. You do not need to be a genius. You need to be steady.
