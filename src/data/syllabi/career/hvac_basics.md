# HVAC: Heating, Cooling & Comfort
*Stop being a stranger to the loudest, most expensive box in your house.*

**ID**: `hvacBasics` · **Discipline**: Home Systems / Building Science · **Level**: Career / Adult Enrichment

## Course Aim
Every home has a heating and cooling system that quietly eats a large share of the energy bill, occasionally fails on the hottest or coldest day of the year, and is almost always a black box to the people who depend on it. This course opens the box. It teaches the curious adult how home HVAC actually works, from the refrigeration cycle humming in the backyard to the thermostat on the hallway wall, so that "the AC is broken" becomes a diagnosable situation rather than a panicked phone call.

The goal is not to turn you into a technician. It is to make you a smart homeowner and a sharp customer: someone who can tell a clogged filter from a dead capacitor, knows why "just add refrigerant" is a red flag, can read a SEER or AFUE rating without glazing over, and understands which jobs are safe DIY and which are firmly hands-off. That confidence is worth real money, in avoided service calls, better equipment decisions, and lower bills.

Underneath the practical tips sits a satisfying chunk of physics: heat moves, it is never created or destroyed by your air conditioner, and almost every comfort problem in a house is really a story about heat, airflow, moisture, or pressure. We will keep it concrete, scenario-driven, and a little fun, because once the mental model clicks, the mysterious machine in the closet becomes genuinely interesting.

## Course Design Notes
Floe routes a learner here when they want to understand home comfort systems as an owner or renter, not as a trade student: people buying a house, replacing a furnace, sizing an AC, fighting a hot back bedroom, deciding whether a heat pump makes sense, or just tired of feeling helpless when something breaks. It pairs well with home-ownership, energy, and building-science interests, and sits beside other practical "how the world works" Career tracks. Scenarios are written around real homeowner moments (water on the floor, ice on the coil, a furnace blowing cold) and every distractor names a specific misconception so a wrong answer teaches something. Safety content (carbon monoxide, gas leaks, the DIY/pro line) is treated as non-negotiable, not optional trivia.

## Chapter 1: The Refrigeration Cycle
- **Core questions**: What does an air conditioner physically do? Where does heat go? What are the four stages of the loop?
- **Key concepts**: cooling as heat removal (not "cold creation"), evaporator, compressor, condenser, expansion valve, the sealed refrigerant loop, condensate as dehumidification.
- **Applied skills**: trace heat from a room to the outdoors; identify which component does what from a symptom.
- **Common traps**: thinking AC "makes cold," confusing condensate water with a refrigerant leak, mixing up which coil absorbs vs rejects heat.

## Chapter 2: Heat Pumps and Furnaces
- **Core questions**: How can one unit both heat and cool? Why is a heat pump efficient? How does a gas furnace heat air without poisoning you?
- **Key concepts**: reversing valve, COP, auxiliary/emergency heat, gas vs electric furnaces, the heat exchanger, electronic vs pilot ignition.
- **Applied skills**: compare operating cost of heat pump vs resistance vs gas; explain why a heat pump runs long in deep cold.
- **Common traps**: assuming heat pumps burn fuel, thinking long winter run times mean failure, believing electric furnaces and heat pumps cost the same to run.

## Chapter 3: Thermostats and Controls
- **Core questions**: Does a lower setpoint cool faster? Why is one room always wrong? What makes a smart thermostat save money?
- **Key concepts**: setpoint vs capacity, single-stage on/off control, thermostat placement, scheduling and setbacks, the C-wire.
- **Applied skills**: diagnose a sun-baked thermostat; check for a C-wire before buying a smart thermostat; reason about setbacks vs constant temperature.
- **Common traps**: "setting it colder cools faster," the constant-temperature myth, blaming equipment for a placement problem.

## Chapter 4: Air Filters and Airflow
- **Core questions**: What does MERV mean? Can a filter be "too good"? How often should you change it?
- **Key concepts**: MERV ratings, filtration vs airflow restriction, static pressure, return vs supply vents, the airflow tradeoff.
- **Applied skills**: pick an appropriate MERV for a given system; recognize airflow starvation as a cause of icing; keep returns clear.
- **Common traps**: maxing out MERV and freezing the coil, treating MERV as a lifespan number, closing vents to "save money."

## Chapter 5: Ducts and Distribution
- **Core questions**: Why does duct leakage cost so much? Why is the far bedroom always uncomfortable? What seals a duct long-term?
- **Key concepts**: ducts in unconditioned space, leakage and pressure loss, balancing dampers, mastic and UL 181 tape (and why cloth "duct tape" fails).
- **Applied skills**: connect a comfort complaint to a distribution cause; choose the right sealing material; reason about return placement.
- **Common traps**: assuming a far room needs its own unit, using cloth duct tape, dismissing duct leaks as a noise issue.

## Chapter 6: Sizing and Capacity
- **Core questions**: What is a "ton"? Is bigger better? How should a pro size equipment?
- **Key concepts**: ton = 12,000 BTU/hr, BTU as a rate, oversizing and short cycling, dehumidification vs runtime, Manual J load calculation.
- **Applied skills**: read a tonnage spec; explain why an oversized AC leaves a clammy house; ask for a Manual J instead of a square-foot guess.
- **Common traps**: confusing tonnage with weight or refrigerant, "bigger to be safe," accepting rule-of-thumb sizing.

## Chapter 7: Efficiency Ratings
- **Core questions**: What does SEER measure? AFUE? HSPF? When is a high-efficiency unit worth the premium?
- **Key concepts**: SEER/SEER2 (cooling efficiency), AFUE (furnace fuel efficiency), HSPF (heat-pump heating efficiency), efficiency vs capacity, payback analysis.
- **Applied skills**: match the right rating to the right mode; estimate when higher SEER pays back given climate and usage.
- **Common traps**: confusing SEER with capacity, applying AFUE to a heat pump, assuming highest efficiency always pays off.

## Chapter 8: Common Problems and Troubleshooting
- **Core questions**: No cool air, what first? Why did the coil freeze? What is short cycling? Why is the furnace blowing cold?
- **Key concepts**: cheap-first diagnosis, frozen coil causes, short cycling causes, igniter/flame sensor failures, run capacitors, "low refrigerant means a leak."
- **Applied skills**: run a safe homeowner triage before calling a pro; thaw a coil correctly; recognize a capacitor failure signature.
- **Common traps**: DIY refrigerant, chipping ice off a coil, "running it harder" through a fault, accepting a top-off without a leak repair.

## Chapter 9: Maintenance — DIY vs Pro
- **Core questions**: What can I safely do myself? What needs a pro? When should I schedule service?
- **Key concepts**: safe DIY (filters, condenser clearance, drain flush), the EPA Section 608 refrigerant line, the value of a tune-up, seasonal timing.
- **Applied skills**: clean a condenser without wrecking the fins; flush a condensate drain; schedule cooling in spring and heating in fall.
- **Common traps**: opening sealed components, pressure-washing fins, buying DIY refrigerant, only servicing after a breakdown.

## Chapter 10: Safety and Carbon Monoxide
- **Core questions**: Why is CO so dangerous? Where do detectors go? What is a cracked heat exchanger? What do you do if you smell gas?
- **Key concepts**: CO as colorless/odorless, detector placement and lifespan, cracked heat exchangers and red-tags, the gas-leak evacuate-first rule, combustion air and clearance.
- **Applied skills**: place CO alarms correctly; respond to a gas smell safely; understand why a red-tag means shutdown.
- **Common traps**: thinking CO is detectable by smell, storing clutter around fuel appliances, investigating a gas leak instead of evacuating.

## Chapter 11: Comfort and Humidity
- **Core questions**: Why do two 76-degree rooms feel different? Fan ON or AUTO? What is the best comfort-per-dollar upgrade?
- **Key concepts**: relative humidity and perceived comfort, dehumidification via runtime, fan ON re-evaporation, the building envelope (insulation, air sealing, duct sealing).
- **Applied skills**: set fan to AUTO for better summer humidity; prioritize envelope fixes before equipment; target 40-50% indoor humidity.
- **Common traps**: ignoring humidity, leaving the fan on "ON" in summer, buying a high-SEER unit before sealing a leaky house.

## Capstone
Walk through your own (or a hypothetical) home as a smart homeowner. Identify the system type (furnace + AC, heat pump, dual-fuel) and its key components. Read the equipment ratings (tonnage, SEER/SEER2, AFUE or HSPF) and explain what each means. Build a maintenance plan separating monthly DIY tasks from annual pro service, and a seasonal schedule. Diagnose three classic problems from symptoms (no cool air, frozen coil, furnace blowing cold), naming the cheapest likely cause and the correct first action for each. Finally, produce a prioritized improvement list, envelope and duct sealing before equipment, with a one-line justification for each item, and verify CO detector placement against the safety checklist.

## Assessment Ideas
- Symptom-to-cause drills graded on naming the cheapest likely cause and the correct safe first action.
- Equipment-spec reading tasks graded on correctly matching SEER/AFUE/HSPF/tonnage to mode and meaning.
- Safety scenarios (CO alarm, gas smell, red-tagged furnace) graded pass/fail on the evacuate-and-call-first response.
- Capstone graded on diagnostic reasoning, correct DIY/pro boundaries, and sensible prioritization.
