# Customs Broker License
**ID**: `customsBroker` · **Discipline**: Operations · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
This course trains an adult learner to reason like a working customs broker and to pass U.S. Customs and Border Protection's Customs Broker License Examination (CBLE) without treating those as two different skills. A broker's job is to take a shipment that someone wants to import — a fact pattern of goods, invoices, contracts, and corporate relationships — and translate it into a defensible filing with the U.S. government: the right classification, the right declared value, the right country of origin, the right entry type, the right duties and fees, and a record that survives a CBP audit years later. Get any one of those wrong and the importer overpays, underpays (which is worse), misses a deadline, or inherits a penalty. The broker is the licensed professional who is supposed to exercise *reasonable care* on the importer's behalf, and the exam exists to prove you can.

The defining feature of both the exam and the job is that you work *from the references, not from memory*. The CBLE is open-book: you are tested on the Harmonized Tariff Schedule of the United States (HTSUS), Title 19 of the Code of Federal Regulations (19 CFR), and CBP directives and instructions, and the skill being measured is whether you can find and apply the controlling rule under time pressure. So this syllabus is built around reference navigation as a first-class competency, not an afterthought. You will internalize the structure of the HTSUS and the General Rules of Interpretation, the architecture of 19 CFR, and the handful of high-frequency provisions the exam returns to again and again — because a candidate who knows *where the answer lives* beats one who has memorized facts that turn out to be slightly out of date.

By the end you should be able to take an unfamiliar import scenario and do five things cleanly: classify the goods through the HTSUS with a cited GRI path, compute customs value with the right inclusions and exclusions, determine and document country of origin and any preference claim, select the correct entry type and compute total duties and fees, and decide what the broker must do to stay within reasonable care — file, correct, disclose, or escalate. Classification, valuation, origin, procedure, and reasonable care: that combination is what a working broker is paid for, and it is, not coincidentally, what the CBLE measures.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it tests tariff classification under the HTSUS and the General Rules of Interpretation; customs valuation (transaction value, additions and deductions, related-party tests); country of origin, marking, and preferential trade programs such as USMCA and GSP; entry process, entry types, entry summary, bonds, and liquidation; duties, fees (MPF and HMF), antidumping and countervailing duties, quotas, drawback, and foreign-trade zones; recordkeeping, post-entry corrections, prior disclosure, and penalties; the broker's licensing and conduct obligations under 19 CFR Part 111; or open-book reference navigation and CBLE exam strategy. For a general logistics or supply-chain overview, prefer a trade-operations roadmap; this is the certification-and-mastery track for the licensed-broker credential specifically.

The voice is a senior licensed broker mentoring a sharp new hire who is also studying for the exam: concrete shipment scenarios, every acronym defined on first use, and a relentless focus on the *controlling reference* and the *next defensible action*. Every chapter surfaces **Common traps**, because the highest-value skill in customs work is recognizing the seductive-but-wrong move — letting a commercial invoice's product name override the HTSUS legal text, treating "the seller invoiced it" as the customs value, or assuming a free-trade agreement's tariff benefit when the rule of origin was never met. Distractors should be the plausible mistakes real brokers and candidates make, not absurd ones.

Keep facts current and U.S.-accurate. The CBLE is administered electronically through Pearson VUE testing centers, is **open-book** (you may use the HTSUS, 19 CFR, and the cited CBP materials), and historically runs about **80 multiple-choice questions** with a **75% passing score** over roughly a half-day session; CBP sets the exact format, references, and editions for each administration, so always confirm the current Notice of Examination before sitting. Prefer post-2020 framing: **USMCA** replaced NAFTA effective July 1, 2020, with its own rules of origin and certification (no government-issued certificate form — the importer, exporter, or producer certifies); entry summaries and most CBP filings run through **ACE** (the Automated Commercial Environment); and tariff actions such as Section 301 (China) and Section 232 (steel/aluminum) remedies sit *on top of* the regular HTSUS duty and change frequently — teach the mechanism (where the extra duty attaches and how it interacts with value and origin) rather than memorizing volatile rates.

### Exam map (CBLE, CBP Notice of Examination)
CBP does not publish fixed percentage weights per topic, but decades of released exams cluster heavily and predictably. Build effort around where the questions actually fall:
- **Classification (HTSUS + GRIs)** — consistently one of the two largest blocks: Chapter 2, with the reference skills from Chapter 1.
- **Entry, broker compliance, and powers of attorney** — the other large block, drawn from 19 CFR Parts 111, 141, 142, and 143: Chapters 1, 5, and 7.
- **Valuation** — a reliable mid-size block: Chapter 3.
- **Origin, marking, and trade programs (USMCA, GSP)** — Chapter 4.
- **Duties, fees, AD/CVD, drawback, FTZs, quotas** — Chapter 6.
- **Recordkeeping, penalties, prior disclosure, liquidation/protests** — Chapters 6 and 7.
Practice on **released past exams with answer keys** (CBP publishes them) is the single highest-yield activity; Chapter 8 turns those into a structured drill.

### Study rhythm
A working professional should plan eight to twelve weeks. First, get *reference-fluent*: spend the early weeks learning the structure of the HTSUS and 19 CFR cold, so that finding a provision is reflex and not a scavenger hunt. Then read one chapter and immediately work a handful of released exam questions on that topic, *open-book, timed*, citing your reference for each answer rather than just picking a letter — the exam rewards citation discipline and so does the job. Keep a running error log sorted by reference, concept, and mistake type (Chapter 8). Sit at least one full released exam under true time pressure at the two-thirds mark to expose weak topics and, just as important, to calibrate pacing — running out of time is a more common failure than running out of knowledge.

## Chapter 1: The Broker's Role, Reasonable Care, and Reference Navigation
- **Core questions:** Who is legally responsible for this entry, and what does the broker owe them? Which reference controls this question — HTSUS, a part of 19 CFR, or a CBP directive — and how do I get to the answer fast? What is the broker's duty *here*, before any number is computed?
- **Key concepts:** the licensed customs broker and 19 CFR Part 111 (licensing, conduct, supervision, triennial status report, district vs national permit); the **importer of record** and who may act as one; **power of attorney** as the source of a broker's authority; the **reasonable care** standard and the broker's duty to advise; CBP's role and the broader trade-agency landscape (PGAs — partner government agencies such as FDA, USDA, EPA); the structure of the **HTSUS** (sections, chapters, headings, subheadings, notes) and of **19 CFR** (which Part governs what); open-book strategy — tabbing, the table of contents, and the index as your fastest path.
- **Applied skills:** Build a reference map that says, for each major question type, which document and which Part or chapter to open first. Given a fact pattern, name the *controlling reference* before attempting the answer. Draft a checklist of what a valid power of attorney must contain and what it does and does not authorize.
- **Common traps:** Treating the broker as the party liable for the entry when the *importer of record* bears primary legal responsibility — and missing that the broker still owes reasonable care and can be penalized for its breach. Searching the HTSUS for a rule that actually lives in 19 CFR (or vice versa). Burning exam minutes reading a long provision when the section or chapter *note* answers the question in one line. Assuming a power of attorney is open-ended when its scope is defined by its terms.

## Chapter 2: Classification With the HTSUS
- **Core questions:** What is the article, in the tariff's own terms, and which heading describes it most specifically? Which **General Rule of Interpretation** decides this, and which section or chapter **note** controls or excludes? What single fact, if changed, would move the goods to a different heading?
- **Key concepts:** the General Rules of Interpretation (**GRIs 1–6**) and their strict order of application; GRI 1 (terms of headings and the relative section/chapter notes govern first); GRI 2 (incomplete/unfinished articles; mixtures and composite goods); GRI 3 (most specific description, **essential character**, last-in-numerical-order tiebreaker); GRI 6 at the subheading level; the role of **section and chapter notes** (which can *define*, *include*, or *exclude*); parts vs accessories; sets put up for retail sale; the Additional U.S. Rules of Interpretation; the 10-digit statistical suffix; Explanatory Notes as persuasive (not binding) guidance.
- **Applied skills:** Classify a good by walking GRI 1 → 6 in order and writing the path (heading, governing note, GRI used, subheading). Resolve a two-competing-headings problem by applying GRI 3 in sequence (3(a) specificity, then 3(b) essential character, then 3(c)). Identify the section or chapter note that excludes a tempting-but-wrong heading.
- **Common traps:** Letting the **commercial or marketing name** on the invoice drive classification instead of the tariff's legal language. Jumping to GRI 3 (essential character) when GRI 1 and the notes already decide it — the GRIs apply *in order* and you stop at the first that resolves the issue. Ignoring a chapter note that *excludes* the article from the heading you like. Picking the 8-digit subheading without applying GRI 6 and the relevant subheading notes. Treating the Explanatory Notes as binding law.

## Chapter 3: Customs Valuation
- **Core questions:** What is the **transaction value** here, and is it even usable? What must be *added* to the price actually paid or payable, and what may be *deducted*? Is the buyer-seller relationship a problem, and if so, how is it tested?
- **Key concepts:** the hierarchy of valuation methods (transaction value first, then transaction value of identical/similar goods, deductive, computed, and the fallback "derived" method — applied **in order**); **price actually paid or payable**; statutory **additions** (packing costs, selling commissions, **assists**, royalties and license fees related to the goods as a condition of sale, proceeds of subsequent resale accruing to the seller); permissible **deductions/exclusions** (international freight and insurance, post-importation construction/assembly, U.S. duties and taxes, **buying** commissions); **related-party** transactions and the "circumstances of sale" and "test values" checks; currency conversion; first-sale concepts at a high level.
- **Applied skills:** Build a valuation worksheet that starts from the invoice price, lists each statutory addition with its basis, lists each permitted exclusion, and arrives at a cited customs value. Distinguish a dutiable **selling** commission from a non-dutiable **buying** commission. Decide whether a related-party price is acceptable and state the test you applied.
- **Common traps:** Treating the **invoice total** as the customs value without adding assists and royalties or removing international freight and U.S. duty. Confusing **buying** commissions (excluded) with **selling** commissions (included). Assuming a related-party price is automatically rejected — it is acceptable if the relationship did not influence price or it meets a test value. Skipping the method hierarchy and reaching for computed value when transaction value was available.

## Chapter 4: Country of Origin, Marking, and Trade Programs
- **Core questions:** What is the country of origin under the rule that applies, and what evidence proves it? Does the article qualify for a **preference program**, and was the rule of origin actually met? What **marking** does the law require, and who fixes a marking failure?
- **Key concepts:** **substantial transformation** (a new article with a different name, character, or use) for non-preferential origin; **tariff-shift** and **regional value content** rules under agreements; **country-of-origin marking** requirements and exceptions (the ultimate purchaser, the "J-list," containers); **USMCA** (replaced NAFTA July 1, 2020 — rules of origin, regional value content, the automotive labor-value content, and self-**certification** by importer/exporter/producer with no prescribed government form); **GSP** and other preference programs (note GSP's lapse-and-renewal history — confirm current authorization); the difference between **origin for duty preference**, **origin for marking**, and **origin for AD/CVD or trade-remedy scope**, which can diverge.
- **Applied skills:** Write a short origin memo: facts, the rule applied (substantial transformation or the agreement's tariff-shift/RVC rule), the conclusion, and the documentation needed to support it on audit. Determine the required marking for a given article and ultimate purchaser. Decide whether a USMCA preference claim is supportable and what certification must exist.
- **Common traps:** Claiming a free-trade-agreement tariff benefit because the goods *ship from* a member country, when the **rule of origin** was never satisfied. Looking for a NAFTA or USMCA "certificate of origin **form**" — USMCA uses a data-element certification, not a numbered government form. Assuming origin is one answer for all purposes when marking, preference, and trade-remedy scope can each reach a different country. Treating GSP as always in force without confirming its current authorization status.

## Chapter 5: Entry, Release, and the Entry Summary
- **Core questions:** Which **entry type** applies to this shipment and why? What is the deadline that controls — for filing, for the entry summary, for payment — and what document is missing to meet it? What happens at **liquidation**, and what are the importer's rights afterward?
- **Key concepts:** the difference between **cargo release** and the **entry summary** (the CBP Form **7501** data set); entry types (consumption, informal, warehouse, temporary importation under bond/TIB, transportation in bond, FTZ admission/entry); **bonds** (single-transaction vs continuous; the surety's role); required documents (commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading); **ACE** as the filing system; key timelines (entry summary and estimated duties generally due within **10 working days** of release; **liquidation** as CBP's final computation of duties); the **protest** right (generally within **180 days** of liquidation) and post-summary correction.
- **Applied skills:** Select the correct entry type for a scenario and justify it. Build an entry checklist that lists required documents, the responsible party, and each deadline. Walk a shipment from release through entry summary to liquidation and identify the importer's options if the duty was assessed wrong.
- **Common traps:** Confusing **release** (getting the goods) with the **entry summary** (declaring and paying) and missing that they are separate steps with separate deadlines. Choosing a consumption entry when a TIB or warehouse entry was the right tool. Missing the entry-summary or protest deadline — in customs, blown deadlines are a leading source of avoidable liability. Assuming liquidation is automatic and final with no recourse, when a timely protest exists.

## Chapter 6: Duties, Fees, AD/CVD, and Special Programs
- **Core questions:** What is the **total** the importer owes — duty plus every fee — and on what base is each computed? Are **antidumping or countervailing duties** in scope, and how would I know? Which special program (drawback, FTZ, exclusion) changes the calculation, and is the importer eligible?
- **Key concepts:** ad valorem, specific, and compound duty rates; the **Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF)** (ad valorem with statutory minimum/maximum, with informal-entry and FTA exceptions) and the **Harbor Maintenance Fee (HMF)** (ocean shipments); **antidumping (AD)** and **countervailing (CVD)** duties as separate, scope-defined remedies with deposit rates set by Commerce; trade-remedy tariffs layered on top of normal duty (**Section 301** China, **Section 232** steel/aluminum — teach the mechanism, confirm current rates); **quotas** (absolute vs tariff-rate); **drawback** (refund of duties on re-exported or destroyed goods); **foreign-trade zones (FTZs)**; duty preference recap from Chapter 4.
- **Applied skills:** Compute a landed-duty figure: classify, value, apply the HTSUS rate, add MPF and HMF where they apply, and layer any AD/CVD or Section 301/232 duty with the correct base. Decide whether a shipment is within an AD/CVD order's scope. Identify when drawback or an FTZ would lower total cost and what the importer must do to claim it.
- **Common traps:** Forgetting that **MPF and HMF** are owed on top of duty (and forgetting MPF's floor and ceiling, or that informal entries and some FTAs are exempt). Computing a trade-remedy duty on the wrong base or assuming it replaces rather than **adds to** the normal HTSUS duty. Assuming AD/CVD applies to anything from a named country, when scope is defined by the specific order, not by country alone. Treating drawback as automatic rather than a claim with its own filing and proof requirements.

## Chapter 7: Recordkeeping, Corrections, Penalties, and Broker Conduct
- **Core questions:** What record proves this entry was right, and how long must it be kept? When should a **correction** or **prior disclosure** be filed, and what does each accomplish? What conduct creates **penalty** exposure for the importer or the broker?
- **Key concepts:** the recordkeeping requirement (the **(a)(1)(A) list**; the general **five-year** retention from date of entry); **post-summary correction (PSC)** vs **protest** (different tools, different timing); **prior disclosure** and how it limits penalties; the **§1592** penalty framework (fraud, gross negligence, negligence) and the role of **reasonable care**; broker discipline under **19 CFR Part 111** (revocation, suspension, monetary penalty, the triennial status report); **confidentiality** of importer information.
- **Applied skills:** Build a compliance file checklist mapping each required record to the claim it supports. Use a decision tree to choose among PSC, protest, and prior disclosure for a given error and timing. Identify, in a fact pattern, the act that would expose the importer or broker to a §1592 penalty and the reasonable-care step that would have prevented it.
- **Common traps:** Confusing the post-summary **correction** (pre-liquidation) with a **protest** (post-liquidation) and using the wrong tool or missing the window. Treating **prior disclosure** as an admission to avoid, when filing it before CBP discovers the issue can sharply limit penalties. Assuming only the importer faces exposure — the broker can be penalized and disciplined for failing reasonable care. Discarding records before the five-year retention period runs.

## Chapter 8: Released Exams, Triage, and Broker Judgment
- **Core questions:** Which questions are fast points and which are time sinks? What is the controlling reference for this question, and can I cite it? Why was each missed question wrong — bad reference, bad concept, or bad reading?
- **Key concepts:** question **triage** (do the citable, single-reference questions first; flag the multi-step classification and valuation problems for a second pass); **reference search discipline** (go to the controlling note or Part, not the whole document); **answer elimination** when two choices share a flaw; pacing against an ~80-question, half-day clock; reading for the operative words ("most specific," "except," "primarily liable"); turning a wrong answer into a *rule* you can reuse; explaining the issue plainly to an importer (the job's real test of understanding).
- **Applied skills:** Run released CBP exams under true time pressure, citing a reference for every answer. Keep an **error log** sorted by reference, concept, and mistake type, and convert recurring errors into a one-line rule. Re-do missed questions a week later cold to confirm the fix held.
- **Common traps:** Grinding a hard classification question early and starving the easy points at the end. Picking an answer "that sounds right" without confirming the reference — on an open-book exam, an uncited answer is a guess. Logging *that* you missed a question without logging *why*, so the same mistake recurs. Studying only the topics you already like and avoiding the weak ones the timed mock exposed.

## Capstone
Prepare a complete customs-entry advisory packet for a single fictional importer and shipment — the deliverable a working broker would put in the file and a CBLE candidate should be able to defend line by line. Starting from a commercial invoice, a description of the goods, the corporate relationship between buyer and seller, the shipping route, and the importer's questions: (1) **classify** the goods through the HTSUS with a written GRI path and the controlling section/chapter note; (2) compute the **customs value** on a worksheet showing the price actually paid or payable, each statutory addition with its basis, and each permitted exclusion, and state whether the related-party price is acceptable and how you tested it; (3) determine **country of origin** and write a short origin memo, deciding whether a **USMCA** or other preference claim is supportable and what certification must exist; (4) select the **entry type**, build an entry checklist with documents and deadlines, and note the liquidation and protest timeline; (5) compute **total duties and fees** — HTSUS duty plus MPF and HMF where applicable, plus any AD/CVD or Section 301/232 layer on the correct base; (6) state the broker's **reasonable-care** obligations, the **records** to retain and for how long, and the **correction or disclosure** path if an error later surfaces; and (7) close with a short list of **unresolved questions** that require clarification from the client or a binding ruling from CBP, naming which reference you would consult or which CBP mechanism you would use. Deliverable: a packet — classification path, valuation worksheet, origin memo, entry checklist, duty/fee computation, and a reasonable-care and recordkeeping note — that another broker could pick up cold and stand behind, and that maps directly onto the reasoning the CBLE measures.

## Assessment Ideas
- HTSUS classification drills graded on the written GRI path and citation, not just the final 10-digit number.
- Valuation and duty/fee computations graded on inclusions, exclusions, the correct base for each layer, and units.
- Released-exam sets graded open-book and timed, with a required reference citation per answer.
- Capstone graded on reference use, reasonable-care judgment, deadline awareness, and importer-facing clarity.
