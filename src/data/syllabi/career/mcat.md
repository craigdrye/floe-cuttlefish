# MCAT: The Medical School Entrance Marathon
**ID**: `mcat` · **Discipline**: Medical / Premed

## Course Aim
The MCAT is the AAMC's 7-hour-30-minute, 230-question gatekeeper to U.S. and Canadian allopathic medical schools, and the dominant signal at most osteopathic programs as well. Officially it scores four sections from 118 to 132, summing to a 472-528 composite around a mean near 500, with competitive M.D. programs clustering at 510 and above and top-quartile research schools demanding 515-plus. Unofficially, it is a marathon thinking test that uses chemistry, physics, biology, biochemistry, organic chemistry, psychology, sociology, and critical reading as raw material. Roughly 80% of items hang off a short experimental passage you have never seen, and your job is to extract the underlying principle, apply it to an unfamiliar setup, and refuse to be manipulated by attractive distractors that match a memorized fact but miss the passage's logic.

This course trains the pre-med marathon thinker — the candidate who can read a dense passage on enzyme kinetics, infer the experimental variable, predict the effect of a perturbation, and defend their answer against three options written to ambush surface-level recall. Where an AP Biology or college biochemistry course rewards mastery of canonical content, the MCAT rewards integration: glycolysis read through an enzyme-defect passage, Bernoulli read through a vascular stenosis figure, attribution theory read through a sociology graph. Where CARS rewards anything science-flavored at all, it punishes outside knowledge entirely and asks for argument analysis in humanities and social-science texts most premeds would never read voluntarily.

Floe's MCAT track treats the official AAMC content outline as the map and the AAMC Official Guide as the gold standard. Voice is rigorous, kind, and passage-heavy. The track builds five durable habits: (1) a passage triage workflow that converts an 800-word stimulus into a usable mental map in 90 seconds; (2) predictive answering — generate a candidate response before reading choices, then match; (3) trap-aware elimination keyed to the AAMC's signature distractor styles (extreme language, half-right/half-wrong, out-of-scope, contradicts-passage); (4) biochem-as-default thinking for the two Bio-flavored sections; and (5) statistics literacy adequate to survive the psych/soc data figures that surprise unprepared candidates.

You will leave with reliable section-bank instincts, a calm passage workflow, a defensible CARS approach that does not collapse under a Kierkegaard passage at 1 p.m., and a study scaffold that respects the actual scoring: every section weighs equally, every section is equally winnable with discipline, and the scaled score depends as much on stamina as on content.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test AAMC-blueprint content (Chem/Phys, CARS, Bio/Biochem, Psych/Soc), MCAT-style passage reasoning, applied biochemistry, experimental-design interpretation, or the disciplined "first-pass, flag-and-move, second-pass" approach that real scored sections reward. The MCAT differs from AP Biology, AP Chemistry, AP Psychology, and most college pre-med coursework in three concrete ways:

- **Integration over isolation.** AP-level items test one concept per question. MCAT items routinely require the candidate to combine an organic mechanism with an enzyme-kinetics graph or a Bernoulli equation with a clinical vignette. Floe routes integration-heavy items here, not to AP Science Polish.
- **Passage-reading speed.** AP and college exams rarely demand 230 questions in 7.5 hours under passage conditions. The MCAT does, and CARS in particular has no discrete items at all — every CARS question lives inside a humanities or social-science passage. Floe routes timed-passage items here.
- **CARS.** No AP, MCAT, or college science course teaches it. CARS rewards argument structure, scope, tone, and inference — and explicitly punishes outside knowledge. Floe routes humanities/social-science argument questions here rather than to AP Humanities.

Voice: make a brutally long exam feel survivable without softening the science. Distractors should be plausible, not absurd. Lessons should teach a principle the candidate can transfer, not a fact to memorize.

## Exam Map and Study Rhythm
The MCAT has four scored sections totaling 230 questions across 7 hours 30 minutes of testing time. Each section is scored 118-132; the composite is 472-528 and centers near 500. Each science section is 95 minutes; CARS is 90 minutes.

| Section | Qs | Time | Passages | Discretes | Mix |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Chemical & Physical Foundations of Biological Systems | 59 | 95 min | 10 (~4 Qs each) | 15 | Gen chem 30%, gen physics 25%, biochem 25%, org chem 15%, intro bio 5% |
| Critical Analysis & Reasoning Skills (CARS) | 53 | 90 min | 9 (~5-7 Qs each) | 0 | Humanities + social sciences, no outside knowledge |
| Biological & Biochemical Foundations of Living Systems | 59 | 95 min | 10 | 15 | Biology 65%, biochem 25%, org chem 5%, gen chem 5% |
| Psychological, Social, & Biological Foundations of Behavior | 59 | 95 min | 10 | 15 | Psychology 65%, sociology 30%, biology 5% |

**Chem/Phys Foundational Concepts.** 4A translational motion, forces, work, and energy in living systems · 4B importance of fluids for circulation, gas exchange, and gas movement · 4C electrochemistry and electrical circuits, light, and sound in living systems · 4D structure, function, and reactivity of biologically relevant atoms, molecules, and chemical compounds · 4E unique nature of water and its solutions.

**Bio/Biochem Foundational Concepts.** 1A structure and function of proteins and their constituent amino acids · 1B transmission of genetic information from generation to generation · 1C transmission of heritable information from generation to generation and the processes that increase genetic diversity · 1D principles of bioenergetics and fuel molecule metabolism · 2A assemblies of molecules, cells, and groups of cells · 2B structure, growth, physiology, and genetics of prokaryotes and viruses · 2C processes of cell division, differentiation, and specialization · 3A structure and functions of the nervous and endocrine systems · 3B structure and integrative functions of the main organ systems.

**Psych/Soc Foundational Concepts.** 6A sensing the environment · 6B making sense of the environment (cognition) · 7A individual influences on behavior · 7B social processes that influence behavior · 7C attitudes and behavior change · 8A self-identity · 8B social thinking · 8C social interactions · 9A understanding social structure · 9B demographic characteristics and processes · 10A social inequality.

**CARS skills.** Foundations of Comprehension 30% · Reasoning Within the Text 30% · Reasoning Beyond the Text 40%.

**Scoring.** Each section 118-132, scaled. Composite 472-528. Mean approximately 500. Competitive U.S. M.D. programs: 510-515. Top quartile: 515-520. Top decile: 520+. Sub-500 composites typically need an upward trend or major retake plan.

**Study cadence.** Plan 3-6 months and 300-500 total hours, depending on baseline. Daily content review plus three timed AAMC-style section banks per week. In the final month, build to one or two full-length practice exams per week, using AAMC official full-lengths (FL1-FL5 plus Sample) as the gold standard — third-party FLs (Kaplan, Blueprint, Altius) are useful for stamina but score generously. Keep a written error log that tags each miss as content gap, misread, careless math, or trap phrasing, then weight review toward the dominant tag.

## Chapter 1: Chemical & Physical Foundations — General Chemistry and Physics for the Living Cell
**Core questions:** How do thermodynamics, kinetics, electrochemistry, fluids, optics, and acid-base chemistry show up in human physiology and biochemistry passages? When do you need an equation, and when does a conceptual shortcut beat the algebra?

**Key concepts**
- Thermodynamics: ΔG = ΔH − TΔS spontaneity sign analysis; ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q; ΔG° = −RT ln K; Hess's law; calorimetry q = mCΔT
- Kinetics: rate law from initial-rate data; integrated rate laws for zero, first, and second order; half-life; Arrhenius temperature dependence
- Equilibrium and acid-base: Le Chatelier, Ksp, Kw = 10⁻¹⁴; Brønsted vs. Lewis; pKa ladder for biological molecules; Henderson-Hasselbalch; pI for amino acids
- Intermolecular forces ranked: London < dipole-dipole < hydrogen bond < ion-dipole; solubility implications
- Gas laws: PV = nRT, Dalton's partial pressures, Graham's effusion, Boyle, Charles; respiratory gas exchange
- Physics: 1D and 2D kinematics including projectile range and max height; Newton's laws and free-body diagrams; circular motion a = v²/r; fluids (Bernoulli P + ½ρv² + ρgh = const, Poiseuille Q = πr⁴ΔP/8ηL, continuity, surface tension)
- Waves and sound: Doppler, intensity, beats; optics: Snell's law, lens/mirror equation 1/f = 1/d_o + 1/d_i, magnification
- Electromagnetism: Coulomb's law, E field, capacitors, RC circuits, F = qvB right-hand rule, Lenz/Faraday induction
- Modern physics: photoelectric effect, blackbody curve, radioactive decay and half-life

**Applied premed skills**
- Recognize a pKa-driven setup (buffering, drug ionization, tubular reabsorption) versus a pure equilibrium problem and choose the right equation in under 30 seconds
- Use Poiseuille and continuity to reason about arterioles, IV catheters, airway resistance, and stenosis
- Read a thin-lens diagram and choose the correct corrective lens for myopia or hyperopia without rederiving optics

**Common traps**
- Conflating spontaneity with rate: a reaction with ΔG ≪ 0 may have a high activation energy and run slowly; sign analysis is not a kinetic statement.
- Forgetting that the bicarbonate buffer is "open" (CO₂ blown off by lungs, HCO₃⁻ tuned by kidneys), making it effective at pH 7.4 despite a pKa near 6.1.
- Picking the more electronegative atom as the "better leaving group." Leaving-group ability tracks conjugate-base stability: tosylate > triflate > mesylate ≫ I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ ≫ F⁻ in SN1/SN2.
- Misapplying Bernoulli at a stenosis: velocity rises and lateral pressure falls — counterintuitive to candidates who picture "more squeezed = more pressure."
- Treating Le Chatelier as if it changed K. K is temperature-dependent only; concentration or pressure shifts re-establish the same K.

**Authoritative sources.** Kaplan MCAT Review Series (Chemistry, Physics); Princeton Review MCAT Physics and Math; ExamKrackers General Chemistry and Physics; AAMC Section Bank and Question Packs.

## Chapter 2: Chemical & Physical Foundations — Organic Chemistry, Spectroscopy, and Biomolecule Reactivity
**Core questions:** Which functional groups, mechanisms, and spectroscopy patterns does the AAMC actually test, and which ones can you drop? How do organic chemistry and biochemistry talk to each other on a passage?

**Key concepts**
- Functional groups by reactivity; nomenclature; R/S, E/Z stereochemistry; chirality and optical activity
- Substitution and elimination: SN1, SN2, E1, E2 — substrate, nucleophile/base, solvent, and leaving-group dependence
- Addition: Markovnikov vs. anti-Markovnikov (peroxide effect); electrophilic aromatic substitution directing effects
- Carbonyl chemistry: nucleophilic addition, aldol, esterification, amide hydrolysis, oxidation/reduction states
- Separation and spectroscopy: chromatography (TLC, GC, HPLC), electrophoresis, IR (key stretches O-H, N-H, C=O), ¹H NMR (chemical shift, splitting, integration), mass spec (M⁺ and fragmentation)
- Biomolecules: amino acid R-group classification (nonpolar, polar uncharged, acidic, basic), pKa values for ionizable side chains, isoelectric point, hydropathy
- Peptide bonds, primary through quaternary structure, denaturation/renaturation (Anfinsen)
- Enzyme kinetics: Km, Vmax, kcat; Lineweaver-Burk; competitive, noncompetitive, uncompetitive, mixed inhibition; allosteric regulation and cooperativity; covalent modification; feedback inhibition; cofactors (NAD⁺, FAD, CoA, B₁₂, biotin, TPP, PLP)

**Applied premed skills**
- Identify the most acidic proton in a structure by predicting conjugate-base stability (resonance, induction, hybridization, atom size)
- Predict the product of a passage reaction by tracking electron flow rather than memorizing names
- Read an IR/¹H NMR/MS table and assign peaks under 60-second time pressure
- Translate a Lineweaver-Burk plot into the type of inhibition described and the affected kinetic constant

**Common traps**
- Picking SN1 for a primary substrate. SN1 needs a stable carbocation; primary substrates with strong nucleophile in polar aprotic solvent favor SN2.
- Selecting "racemic mixture" for an SN2 product. SN2 is stereospecific — single inversion, not racemization. Racemic mixtures come from SN1 carbocations.
- Confusing competitive vs. noncompetitive inhibition on a double-reciprocal plot. Competitive shifts the x-intercept (Km changes), Vmax unchanged. Noncompetitive shifts the y-intercept (Vmax changes), Km unchanged.
- Averaging all three pKa values for an acidic amino acid's pI. Use the two pKas that flank the zwitterion — for aspartate, the α-COOH and side-chain COOH.
- Predicting a meta-directing group from a familiar functional group: −NH₂ and −OH are ortho/para directors despite their electronegative atoms because of lone-pair donation into the ring.

**Authoritative sources.** Kaplan MCAT Organic Chemistry and Biochemistry; ExamKrackers Organic Chemistry; AAMC biochemistry-heavy section bank items; Lehninger Principles of Biochemistry for deeper enzyme topics.

## Chapter 3: Biological Foundations — Cell, Molecular Biology, Genetics, and Biotech
**Core questions:** How does information flow from DNA to protein, and where do experiments, drugs, and mutations intervene? What does each organelle actually do when a passage describes a cell behaving abnormally?

**Key concepts**
- DNA structure and replication: leading vs. lagging strand, Okazaki fragments, polymerases I/II/III, telomerase
- Transcription: RNA polymerase II, general transcription factors, promoter and enhancer architecture
- mRNA processing: 5′ capping, 3′ polyadenylation, splicing by spliceosome (snRNPs), alternative splicing
- Translation: eIF complex assembly, AUG/Kozak scanning, elongation, termination; eIF2α phosphorylation as integrated stress response
- Gene regulation: operons (lac, trp), eukaryotic enhancers, chromatin remodeling, DNA methylation, histone acetylation
- Cell cycle and division: mitosis phases vs. meiosis I/II; non-disjunction in meiosis I vs. meiosis II producing distinct aneuploidy patterns
- Cytoskeleton: actin microfilaments, intermediate filaments, microtubules with associated motors kinesin (anterograde) and dynein (retrograde)
- Membrane transport: simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, primary active (Na/K ATPase), secondary active (SGLT)
- Signal transduction: GPCRs (cAMP/PKA, IP₃/DAG/PKC), RTKs (Ras/MAPK, PI3K/Akt), JAK-STAT, nuclear receptors
- Genetics: Mendelian dominant/recessive, X-linked, mitochondrial (maternal-only), penetrance vs. expressivity, Hardy-Weinberg p² + 2pq + q² = 1 and its assumptions
- Biotech: PCR (denature 95°C, anneal 50-65°C, extend 72°C), RT-PCR, qPCR, CRISPR-Cas9, Sanger vs. next-generation sequencing, Northern/Southern/Western blots, gel electrophoresis

**Applied premed skills**
- Interpret a Western blot, Southern blot, gel, or qPCR melt curve from a passage figure
- Reason about a pedigree without memorizing every inheritance pattern by name — count generations and trace carriers
- Predict the effect of a knockout, knockdown, or inhibitor on a described pathway

**Common traps**
- Confusing mitochondrial inheritance with autosomal: mtDNA passes strictly maternally; affected fathers cannot pass the trait to any children.
- Picking "transcription" when the passage describes 40S scanning. Scanning is a translation-initiation step in the cytoplasm.
- Computing Hardy-Weinberg carriers as q rather than 2pq: at q² = 1/10,000, q = 1/100 and carriers are ≈ 2q = 1/50, not 1/100.
- Forgetting that Northern detects RNA, Southern detects DNA, Western detects protein — the alphabet trick (N→R inverted... candidates flip them under stress).
- Treating CRISPR as if it edited RNA. Cas9 introduces double-strand DNA breaks; repair templates control the edit.

**Authoritative sources.** Kaplan MCAT Biology; Princeton Review MCAT Biology Review; Molecular Biology of the Cell (Alberts) for deeper reference; AAMC Section Bank Bio/Biochem.

## Chapter 4: Biological Foundations — Organ-System Physiology
**Core questions:** What is each organ system trying to homeostatically control, and what happens when it fails? Where do hormones, nerves, and feedback loops intersect?

**Key concepts**
- Nervous: resting potential, action potential ionic mechanism (Na⁺ in, K⁺ out), saltatory conduction, EPSP/IPSP summation, reflex arcs (monosynaptic stretch reflex vs. polysynaptic withdrawal), autonomic NS sympathetic vs. parasympathetic effects on each organ
- Endocrine: hypothalamic-pituitary axes (HPA, HPT, HPG, GH), insulin/glucagon, RAAS, ADH, calcium control by PTH/calcitonin/vitamin D
- Cardiovascular: cardiac cycle, ECG waveforms (P, QRS, T), Starling forces (P_c, π_c, P_if, π_if) governing capillary filtration vs. reabsorption, baroreceptor and chemoreceptor reflexes
- Respiratory: lung volumes, compliance, V/Q matching, O₂-Hb dissociation curve and the shifts (Bohr effect, 2,3-BPG, temperature, CO₂) — the CADET-face-Right mnemonic for right shift = more offload
- Renal: nephron segments and what each handles (proximal tubule bulk reabsorption, loop of Henle countercurrent multiplication, distal tubule fine tuning, collecting duct ADH/aldosterone action); acid-base compensation
- GI: sphincter physiology; gastrin, CCK, secretin, GIP; pancreatic enzymes and bile
- Reproductive: menstrual cycle hormones (LH, FSH, estradiol, progesterone) and feedback loops; spermatogenesis vs. oogenesis timing
- Immune: innate (complement, neutrophils, NK cells, macrophages) vs. adaptive (B cells producing IgM/IgG/IgA/IgE/IgD; T cells CD4 helper and CD8 cytotoxic; MHC I on all nucleated cells, MHC II on APCs)

**Applied premed skills**
- Trace one molecule (Na⁺, glucose, water, H⁺) through a body system end-to-end
- Predict a hormone change from a feedback-loop perturbation (e.g., exogenous T3 suppresses TSH and TRH)
- Read an oxyhemoglobin curve and explain the Bohr shift in exercising muscle

**Common traps**
- Picking "destroys the adrenal cortex" for chronic exogenous glucocorticoids. Mechanism is negative feedback on the hypothalamus and pituitary, suppressing CRH/ACTH; cortex atrophies from lack of stimulation, not toxicity.
- Confusing left shift (more affinity, less offload — fetal Hb, alkalosis, hypothermia) with right shift (less affinity, more offload — exercise, acidosis, fever, high 2,3-BPG).
- Treating ADH as a Na⁺ regulator. ADH increases free-water reabsorption at the collecting duct, affecting osmolality — aldosterone is the primary Na⁺ regulator.
- Missing that sympathetic stimulation dilates skeletal-muscle vessels but constricts most other vascular beds.
- Putting parietal-cell HCl secretion under direct vagal control only — gastrin (G cells) and histamine (ECL cells) are equally important triggers.

**Authoritative sources.** Kaplan MCAT Biology Review; Princeton Review MCAT Biology Review; Costanzo Physiology for deeper reference; AAMC Section Bank Bio/Biochem physiology items.

## Chapter 5: Biochemistry and Metabolism
**Core questions:** Which metabolic pathways really matter, where do they run, and how do enzymes regulate flux? What does a metabolic dysregulation passage look like under stress?

**Key concepts**
- Glycolysis: ten steps, key regulation at hexokinase, PFK-1 (rate-limiting, allosteric by F2,6BP and AMP, inhibited by ATP and citrate), and pyruvate kinase; substrate-level phosphorylation
- Hexokinase vs. glucokinase: Km, tissue distribution, product inhibition by G6P (hexokinase yes; glucokinase no)
- Gluconeogenesis: four irreversible steps and the bypass enzymes (pyruvate carboxylase, PEPCK, F1,6BPase, G6Pase)
- TCA cycle: regulation by NADH and ATP; key products per acetyl-CoA (3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 GTP, 2 CO₂)
- Electron transport chain and oxidative phosphorylation: complexes I-IV, proton motive force, ATP synthase, P/O ratios; net ATP per glucose ≈ 30-32 with glycerol-3-phosphate vs. malate-aspartate shuttle
- β-oxidation, ketogenesis (acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate, acetone), ketolysis in extrahepatic tissue
- Urea cycle (liver, partly mitochondrial, partly cytosolic); pentose phosphate pathway for NADPH and ribose-5-P
- Hormonal control: insulin (fed state, anabolic), glucagon (fasted, catabolic), epinephrine (acute stress), cortisol (chronic stress)
- Vitamins and cofactors as pathway tags: B₁ (thiamine) — PDH and α-KG dehydrogenase; B₂ (riboflavin) — FAD; B₃ (niacin) — NAD; B₅ — CoA; B₆ — transaminases; B₇ (biotin) — carboxylases; B₉/B₁₂ — one-carbon transfers

**Applied premed skills**
- Decide whether a substrate enters glycolysis, pentose phosphate, or gluconeogenesis based on cellular state
- Estimate net ATP for glucose, palmitate, or an amino acid (account for the shuttle if asked)
- Translate a Lineweaver-Burk plot or kinetic table into an inhibition type and the affected constant

**Common traps**
- Picking hexokinase as the glucose sensor in pancreatic β-cells. The high-Km, non-product-inhibited glucokinase is the glucose sensor; hexokinase saturates at low glucose and cannot signal postprandial spikes.
- Calling PFK-1 the only rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis. It is the most important committed step, but hexokinase and pyruvate kinase also regulate.
- Forgetting that ETC complexes I, III, IV pump protons; complex II does not.
- Counting acetyl-CoA → CO₂ as the only place electrons leave the TCA. Each NADH/FADH₂ produced is the actual electron carrier; CO₂ leaves carbon, not energy.
- Missing that gluconeogenesis is not simply glycolysis in reverse — four steps require different enzymes because the glycolytic steps are physiologically irreversible.

**Authoritative sources.** Kaplan MCAT Biochemistry; Lehninger Principles of Biochemistry (Nelson and Cox); AAMC Biochemistry Question Pack; Marks' Basic Medical Biochemistry for cofactor depth.

## Chapter 6: Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS)
**Core questions:** How do you read a humanities or social-science passage you do not care about, in under 10 minutes, and answer four to seven questions about argument, tone, and inference without leaking outside knowledge?

**Key concepts**
- Passage types: humanities (philosophy, ethics, art history, literature, music criticism, theology) and social sciences (anthropology, sociology, economics, political science, archaeology, cultural studies)
- Question types per AAMC: Foundations of Comprehension (30%), Reasoning Within the Text (30%), Reasoning Beyond the Text (40%)
- Argument structure: thesis, support, counterargument, qualifier, scope, rhetorical contrast
- Common trap families: extreme/absolute language ("always," "never," "must"), half-right/half-wrong (correct premise paired with wrong conclusion), out-of-scope (true in the real world but not supported by the passage), contradicts-passage, opposite

**Applied premed skills**
- Build a one-line passage map per paragraph in under 90 seconds — author's main claim, evidence type, transition
- Predict the answer before reading choices, then pick the option closest to the prediction
- Eliminate using passage support — not personal opinion, not memorized history, not science knowledge

**Common traps**
- Bringing outside knowledge into a "Reasoning Within" question. Even if the candidate knows the passage's claim about Hegel is historically debatable, the right answer cites only what the passage said.
- Picking the most "academic-sounding" or "moderate" answer reflexively. AAMC writes attractive-sounding wrong answers; the right answer matches the author's argument, not your taste.
- Treating "Reasoning Beyond" questions as a license for outside opinion. They ask you to apply the passage's logic to a new situation — still using the passage as the anchor.
- Falling for extreme language. Watch for "always," "never," "the only," "must" — they are rarely true in CARS unless the passage itself uses that scope.
- Spending more than 10 minutes on any single passage. Flag, guess, and move; the next passage is winnable.

**Authoritative sources.** AAMC Official Guide to the MCAT (CARS chapters); AAMC CARS Question Packs 1 and 2; Jack Westin daily CARS passages; ExamKrackers 101 Passages in MCAT Verbal Reasoning.

## Chapter 7: Psychological, Social, and Biological Foundations of Behavior
**Core questions:** How do biology, cognition, social context, and culture combine to produce behavior, health, and inequality? What study designs and statistics let researchers say anything reliable about it?

**Key concepts**
- Sensation and perception: Weber's law, signal detection (hits/misses/false alarms/correct rejections), Gestalt principles (proximity, similarity, closure, continuity)
- Learning: classical conditioning (Pavlov) vs. operant (Skinner); reinforcement schedules — fixed ratio, variable ratio (most resistant to extinction), fixed interval, variable interval
- Memory: Atkinson-Shiffrin model sensory → STM → LTM; episodic, semantic, procedural; encoding strategies (elaboration > rote rehearsal); proactive vs. retroactive interference
- Cognition: Piaget's four stages (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational); heuristics (representativeness, availability, anchoring); Kahneman's dual-process System 1 (fast, automatic) vs. System 2 (slow, deliberate)
- Language acquisition: Skinner (behaviorist), Chomsky (nativist universal grammar), Vygotsky (social-interactionist with zone of proximal development)
- Motivation: Maslow's hierarchy, drive-reduction, opponent-process, Yerkes-Dodson inverted-U for arousal and performance
- Emotion theories: James-Lange (physiological → emotion), Cannon-Bard (simultaneous), Schachter-Singer two-factor (arousal + cognitive label), Lazarus appraisal-first
- Stress: allostatic load; HPA-axis cortisol; sympathetic-medullary catecholamines
- Development: Erikson's eight psychosocial stages; Kohlberg's three levels and six stages of moral reasoning
- Social psychology: attribution theory (fundamental attribution error, actor-observer bias, self-serving bias); conformity (Asch); obedience (Milgram); deindividuation (Zimbardo Stanford prison); groupthink, group polarization, social loafing; prejudice, discrimination, in-group/out-group dynamics, contact hypothesis
- Sociology: Durkheim (function and anomie), Marx (conflict, class), Weber (rationalization, bureaucracy), symbolic interactionism, Cooley's looking-glass self, Goffman's dramaturgy
- Demography: transition model, migration patterns, urbanization, aging cohorts
- Health disparities and social determinants of health; U.S. healthcare insurance models (Medicare, Medicaid, employer-based, ACA marketplaces, single-payer comparisons)
- Research methods: experimental vs. observational; threats to internal and external validity; reliability vs. validity; sampling (random, stratified, convenience); statistics — mean/median/mode, SD, p-values, confidence intervals; correlation ≠ causation

**Applied premed skills**
- Distinguish association from causation in a described study and name the threat to validity
- Match a brain region or neurotransmitter to a behavior phenotype (dopamine — reward and Parkinson's; serotonin — mood; GABA — inhibition; glutamate — excitation; ACh — Alzheimer's depletion; NE — arousal)
- Apply social determinants of health to a clinical scenario without slipping into stereotype

**Common traps**
- Confusing Schachter-Singer with Cannon-Bard. Schachter-Singer requires a cognitive label of the arousal; Cannon-Bard treats arousal and emotion as simultaneous independent outputs of thalamic processing.
- Calling variable-ratio the "slowest" reinforcement schedule. It generates the highest steady response rate and the most resistance to extinction — that is why gambling works.
- Picking "personality" as the explanation for behavior when the passage describes a structural constraint (poverty, policy, neighborhood resources). The fundamental attribution error is a candidate's own trap, not just a tested concept.
- Equating dementia with delirium. Dementia is chronic, gradual, and global; delirium is acute, fluctuating, and usually reversible — the psych/soc passages exploit this routinely.
- Treating Erikson's stages as ages without identity work. The conflict at each stage (e.g., identity vs. role confusion in adolescence) is the testable content; the age range is corroborating.

**Authoritative sources.** Kaplan MCAT Behavioral Sciences Review; KhanAcademy MCAT psych/soc videos (AAMC-commissioned); 300-page AAMC psych/soc topic outline; Comer's Abnormal Psychology for DSM categories.

## Chapter 8: Capstone — Exam-Day Discipline and the Scaled Score That Ships
**Core questions:** How do you survive 7 hours 30 minutes of testing without your accuracy collapsing? What process turns a strong content base into the scaled composite that medical schools see?

**Key concepts**
- Section pacing. Chem/Phys: 59 Q / 95 min ≈ 95 seconds per question with passage time built in. CARS: 53 Q / 90 min ≈ 100 seconds per question plus 4-5 minutes of passage reading per set. Bio/Biochem: 59 Q / 95 min ≈ 95 seconds per Q. Psych/Soc: 59 Q / 95 min ≈ 95 seconds per Q.
- Section-bank pocket: in science sections, 15 discrete questions are sprinkled between passages. Discrete items run 30-60 seconds and pay for the longer passage minutes.
- Flag-and-move discipline. No item is worth burning 5 minutes. Flag, guess your best, return if time permits.
- CARS strategy. Read the passage once thoroughly — do not skim; do not pre-read questions; do not bring outside knowledge. Eliminate extreme/absolute answers. Find textual support before committing.
- Mid-day discipline. Two 10-minute breaks (one optional 30-minute lunch). Snacks, water, electrolytes; brief stretching; no review of finished sections. Phones stay in lockers; reading the previous section ruins the next one.
- Stamina training. Build to two full-lengths per week in the final month under exam timing, including the morning start time and the same caffeine/food protocol planned for test day.
- Score release timing: scores arrive about 30-35 days after the exam. AAMC offers a percentile table; competitive M.D. application cycles want 510+, top quartile 515+, top decile 520+.
- Retake calculus: most schools see all attempts. Plan a retake only if you can credibly raise the composite by 3-5 points; otherwise let the score stand.

**Applied premed skills**
- Maintain CARS accuracy after lunch — typically the highest-fatigue, highest-loss section
- Use elimination on a guess instead of magic; two-out-of-four guess is much better than four-out-of-four
- Build a final-two-weeks taper that protects sleep over content cramming

**Common traps**
- Drinking heavy caffeine the morning of the exam after a poor sleep — produces jitter, urgency, and CARS misreads.
- Reading "real" content the night before. The taper should rehearse process, not facts.
- Changing answers based on panic. AAMC's own data show first-instinct answers are right more often than rationalized rewrites.
- Skipping the optional lunch break to "save time." Cognitive fatigue costs more points than a 30-minute break ever could.

**Practice deliverables**
- Three timed AAMC full-lengths (FL3, FL4, FL5) with full review under exam conditions
- Personal error log with a "do-not-repeat" rule for each recurring miss type
- Two-page exam-day script: arrival, materials, locker policy, break behavior, snack list, mindset cues, post-exam plan

**Authoritative sources.** AAMC Official Guide to the MCAT Exam; AAMC FL Practice Exams 1-5 and Sample Exam; AAMC Section Banks; AAMC Question Packs; the published 2023 MCAT Score Scale and Percentile Tables.
