# Product Management
**ID**: `productManagement` · **Discipline**: Career · **Level**: Career

## Course Aim
Train product managers to turn ambiguity into evidence-backed decisions: identify the right problem, understand users, shape strategy, prioritize bets, define success metrics, partner with design and engineering, and communicate tradeoffs without hiding behind roadmap theater.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus had a strong practical spine: discovery, prioritization, metrics, and cross-functional decisions. This rewrite expands it into a full product operating system with product strategy, research synthesis, analytics, experimentation, roadmapping, delivery partnership, launch learning, stakeholder alignment, and a portfolio-grade capstone.

## Chapter 1: The PM Role and Product Operating System
**Key concepts**: product manager role, product trio, product vision, strategy, discovery, delivery, outcomes, stakeholders, constraints, decision rights.

**Practical questions**
- What decision is the PM actually accountable for?
- Is this a product problem, business problem, customer problem, technical problem, or organizational alignment problem?
- What would success look like if no feature had been named yet?

**Workplace skill**
- Write a product problem brief with user, business, context, evidence, constraints, and decision needed.

## Chapter 2: Customer Discovery and Problem Framing
**Key concepts**: jobs to be done, user interviews, observation, journey maps, personas, pain points, unmet needs, research bias, qualitative synthesis, opportunity solution tree.

**Practical questions**
- What are users trying to accomplish, and where does the current workflow break?
- Is this a loud customer request or a broad user problem?
- What evidence would change the roadmap?

**Workplace skill**
- Conduct a five-interview discovery sprint and synthesize findings into themes, assumptions, and opportunity areas.

## Chapter 3: Strategy, Positioning, and Product Bets
**Key concepts**: product vision, target segment, value proposition, differentiation, product strategy, business model, constraints, competitive analysis, strategic narrative.

**Practical questions**
- Who is this product for, and who is it not for?
- What advantage are we trying to build?
- Which bets support the strategy, and which are attractive distractions?

**Workplace skill**
- Create a product strategy memo linking target user, problem, insight, product bets, risks, and success metrics.

## Chapter 4: Prioritization and Roadmapping
**Key concepts**: RICE, impact/effort, MoSCoW, Kano, opportunity cost, dependency, sequencing, MVP, risk reduction, roadmap types, now-next-later, outcome roadmap.

**Practical questions**
- What should we do first, and what are we choosing not to do?
- Which risk should be reduced before scaling?
- Is the roadmap communicating learning, commitment, or theater?

**Workplace skill**
- Prioritize a messy backlog using two frameworks, then explain where the frameworks disagree and why judgment still matters.

## Chapter 5: Metrics, Analytics, and Experimentation
**Key concepts**: north-star metric, input metrics, guardrails, activation, retention, conversion, funnel, cohort, leading/lagging indicators, A/B testing, hypothesis, instrumentation.

**Practical questions**
- Which metric proves the user outcome, and which metric merely makes the dashboard look employed?
- What could improve the main metric while harming users or the business?
- What experiment would teach us fastest?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a product measurement plan with event tracking, metric definitions, expected movement, guardrails, and decision rules.

## Chapter 6: Requirements, Design, and Delivery Partnership
**Key concepts**: product requirements, user stories, acceptance criteria, design critique, technical feasibility, discovery-to-delivery handoff, backlog refinement, release slices, tradeoffs.

**Practical questions**
- What needs to be true for design and engineering to make good decisions?
- What can be cut without destroying the learning goal?
- Which technical constraint changes the product decision?

**Workplace skill**
- Write a lightweight PRD or opportunity brief with problem, users, scope, non-goals, risks, open questions, analytics, and rollout plan.

## Chapter 7: Launch, Adoption, and Learning Loops
**Key concepts**: beta, rollout, enablement, pricing, packaging, onboarding, support readiness, adoption, feedback loops, post-launch review, product operations.

**Practical questions**
- Who needs to understand, sell, support, trust, or change behavior for this launch to work?
- What will we watch in the first day, week, and month?
- What did the launch teach us that the roadmap must absorb?

**Workplace skill**
- Build a launch readiness checklist and post-launch learning memo.

## Chapter 8: Stakeholder Influence and Product Judgment
**Key concepts**: executive alignment, sales requests, customer commitments, engineering trust, design rationale, conflict, escalation, product sense, ethical product judgment.

**Practical questions**
- How do we say no without being lazy, defensive, or vague?
- Which stakeholder is reacting to risk, revenue, reputation, customer pain, or internal politics?
- When should PM escalate, decide, facilitate, or gather more evidence?

**Workplace skill**
- Present a decision memo: recommendation, rejected alternatives, evidence, tradeoffs, risks, and next learning milestone.

## Capstone: Product Bet Portfolio
Learners produce a portfolio for one product opportunity:
- problem brief
- discovery plan and synthesis
- product strategy memo
- prioritized roadmap
- metrics and experiment plan
- PRD or opportunity brief
- launch checklist
- post-launch learning plan
- stakeholder decision memo
