# Class 12 Math
**ID**: `class12Math` - **Discipline**: Maths - **Year**: University Sophomore (equivalent)

## Course Aim
Consolidate senior secondary mathematics into a calculus-centered, exam-ready, university-bridge course. Students deepen their command of functions, inverse trigonometry, matrices, determinants, continuity, differentiation, integration, differential equations, vectors, 3D geometry, linear programming, and probability while learning to present solutions with the precision expected in board exams, entrance tests, and first-year quantitative courses.

## Course Design Notes
This course should feel rigorous and purposeful: definitions come first, procedures are practiced until fluent, and applications are used to test whether students understand the mathematics rather than merely remember formulas. Route questions here when students need relations and functions, inverse trigonometric functions, matrices, determinants, continuity, differentiability, applications of derivatives, indefinite or definite integration, differential equations, area under curves, vectors, 3D lines and planes, linear programming, conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, random variables, or probability distributions. Emphasize domain and principal-value discipline, determinant and matrix structure, theorem-based calculus reasoning, exact integration setup, vector geometry, and stepwise exam presentation.

## Chapter 1: Relations, Functions, and Inverse Trigonometry
- **Key concepts**: Types of relations, reflexive, symmetric, transitive, equivalence relations, functions, one-one and onto functions, composition, invertible functions, binary operations, inverse trigonometric functions, principal value branches, domains, ranges, graphs, and standard identities involving inverse trigonometric expressions.
- **Practice questions**: Prove a relation is or is not an equivalence relation; determine whether a function is invertible; compute a composite function; find principal values of inverse trigonometric expressions; simplify an inverse trigonometric identity with domain checks.
- **Student output**: A functions and inverse-trigonometry packet with relation proofs, mapping diagrams, inverse checks, principal-value tables, and carefully stated domain restrictions.

## Chapter 2: Matrices, Determinants, and Linear Systems
- **Key concepts**: Matrix order, equality, addition, scalar multiplication, matrix multiplication, transpose, symmetric and skew-symmetric matrices, elementary operations, invertible matrices, determinants up to order 3, determinant properties, minors, cofactors, adjoint, inverse, Cramer's Rule, and matrix methods for solving systems of linear equations.
- **Practice questions**: Multiply compatible matrices and interpret dimensions; decompose a matrix into symmetric and skew-symmetric parts; evaluate a determinant efficiently; find the inverse of a 3 by 3 matrix; solve a system using matrix inverse or Cramer's Rule.
- **Student output**: A matrix-methods dossier with exact computations, determinant-property shortcuts, solved systems, and a short explanation of when a system has a unique solution.

## Chapter 3: Continuity, Differentiability, and Derivative Techniques
- **Key concepts**: Continuity at a point and on intervals, removable discontinuities, differentiability, derivative rules, chain rule, derivatives of inverse trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic functions, logarithmic differentiation, implicit differentiation, parametric differentiation, second-order derivatives, and connections between differentiability and continuity.
- **Practice questions**: Test continuity of a piecewise function; determine differentiability at a junction point; differentiate composite, implicit, parametric, logarithmic, and inverse-trigonometric expressions; compute a second derivative.
- **Student output**: A derivative-techniques problem set with domain notes, continuity checks, rule selection labels, and complete symbolic work.

## Chapter 4: Applications of Derivatives
- **Key concepts**: Rate of change, increasing and decreasing functions, critical points, local maxima and minima, absolute extrema, tangents and normals, approximation, marginal change, monotonicity tests, first derivative test, second derivative test, optimization, and curve analysis.
- **Practice questions**: Find intervals of increase and decrease; determine maxima and minima on a closed interval; write equations of tangents and normals; solve an optimization problem; interpret derivative information in a physics, economics, or geometry context.
- **Student output**: An applications-of-derivatives report with sign charts, extrema justification, tangent or normal calculations, and one contextual optimization solution.

## Chapter 5: Integral Calculus
- **Key concepts**: Antiderivatives, integration as the inverse of differentiation, standard integrals, substitution, integration by parts, partial fractions, trigonometric integrals, definite integrals, properties of definite integrals, definite integrals as limits of sums, Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, area under simple curves, and area between curves.
- **Practice questions**: Evaluate indefinite integrals using substitution, parts, or partial fractions; transform a definite integral using properties; compute a definite integral from an antiderivative; find the area between a line and a parabola; set up an integral before evaluating it.
- **Student output**: An integration portfolio with method-choice annotations, checked antiderivatives, definite-integral property work, and area diagrams.

## Chapter 6: Differential Equations and Mathematical Modeling
- **Key concepts**: Order and degree of a differential equation, general and particular solutions, formation of differential equations, initial conditions, variable separable equations, homogeneous differential equations, first-order linear differential equations, growth and decay models, and interpretation of solution constants.
- **Practice questions**: Identify order and degree; form a differential equation from a family of curves; solve a separable equation; solve a first-order linear equation; use an initial condition to find a particular solution.
- **Student output**: A differential-equation modeling brief with classification, solution steps, initial-condition work, and a short interpretation of the solution curve.

## Chapter 7: Vectors and Three-Dimensional Geometry
- **Key concepts**: Scalars and vectors, magnitude, direction, position vectors, unit vectors, vector addition, scalar multiplication, section formula in vector form, dot product, cross product, scalar triple product, direction cosines, direction ratios, equations of a line in space, skew and intersecting lines, shortest distance, equations of a plane, angles between lines, planes, and line-plane pairs, and coplanarity.
- **Practice questions**: Compute dot and cross products; find the angle between vectors; write vector and Cartesian equations of a line; determine whether two lines intersect or are skew; find the equation of a plane; calculate the shortest distance between skew lines.
- **Student output**: A vector-geometry folio with 3D sketches, line and plane equations, product interpretations, angle calculations, and shortest-distance work.

## Chapter 8: Linear Programming, Probability, and Exam Synthesis
- **Key concepts**: Linear programming formulation, objective functions, constraints, feasible region, corner-point method, bounded and unbounded regions, conditional probability, multiplication theorem, independent events, total probability, Bayes' theorem, random variables, probability distributions, mean, variance, and cumulative synthesis across calculus, algebra, vectors, and probability.
- **Practice questions**: Formulate a two-variable linear programming problem; solve an LP problem graphically; compute conditional probability; apply Bayes' theorem; find the mean and variance of a discrete random variable; solve a mixed exam problem with calculus and algebraic constraints.
- **Student output**: An exam-synthesis packet containing one linear programming model, one probability-distribution table, Bayes' theorem work, and a corrected mixed-paper section with identified concept gaps.

## Capstone
Students complete a Class 12 mathematics readiness portfolio organized around an applied or exam-style theme. It must use at least four major tools from the course, such as matrices, derivative analysis, integration, differential equations, vector geometry, linear programming, or probability. The final submission should include clean mathematical setup, exact computations, diagrams or graphs where useful, corrected timed work, and a brief oral or written defense of method choices.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written assignments graded for theorem use, notation, method choice, and exam-style presentation.
- Short quizzes on inverse trigonometric values, matrix operations, determinant properties, derivative rules, integration methods, vector products, and probability laws.
- Timed mixed-topic practice blocks modeled on NCERT, board-exam, and entrance-test problem styles.
- Error-log revisions requiring students to classify mistakes as conceptual, algebraic, notation-based, or time-management related.
- Capstone rubric covering mathematical correctness, cross-topic integration, diagrams, clarity, correction quality, and readiness for university mathematics.
