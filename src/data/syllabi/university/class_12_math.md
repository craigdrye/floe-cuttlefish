# Class 12 Math
**ID**: `class12Math` - **Discipline**: Maths - **Year**: University Sophomore (equivalent)

## Course Aim
This course consolidates senior secondary mathematics (CBSE/NCERT Class 12 and equivalent national curricula) into a single, calculus-centered, university-bridge program. The goal is not merely to pass a board exam but to leave the course able to reason like a first-year quantitative student: to state a definition precisely, choose the right tool for a problem, execute the procedure without error, and defend the answer. Calculus sits at the center — five of our chapters live inside it — because continuity, differentiation, integration, and differential equations are the language of every quantitative discipline a student will meet next, from physics and engineering to economics and machine learning.

Around that calculus core we build the algebraic and geometric machinery that makes it usable. Relations, functions, and inverse trigonometry establish the domain-and-range discipline that prevents the most common analytic errors. Matrices and determinants give students a structured way to handle linear systems and lay the groundwork for the linear algebra they will formalize later. Vectors and three-dimensional geometry turn coordinates into objects that can be added, projected, and crossed. Linear programming and probability close the loop by showing how the same precision applies to optimization under constraints and to reasoning under uncertainty — the two situations a working analyst faces most.

Throughout, the course insists on exam-grade presentation: stated assumptions, justified steps, exact (not decimal-approximated) answers where exactness is expected, and clear diagrams. A student who finishes should be able to walk into a board exam, a competitive entrance test, or a university calculus sequence and recognize the terrain.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when a student needs relations and functions, inverse trigonometric functions and their principal-value branches, matrix algebra and determinants, the matrix and Cramer methods for linear systems, continuity and differentiability, derivative techniques (chain, implicit, parametric, logarithmic), applications of derivatives (monotonicity, extrema, tangents, optimization), indefinite and definite integration, area under and between curves, differential equations and growth/decay modeling, vectors and dot/cross/triple products, three-dimensional lines and planes, linear programming by the corner-point method, conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, random variables, or probability distributions.

Definitions come first, procedures are drilled until fluent, and applications are used to test understanding rather than recall. Emphasize five habits: (1) principal-value and domain discipline for inverse functions; (2) structural thinking about matrices and determinants before brute-force computation; (3) theorem-based calculus reasoning (continuity does not imply differentiability; the converse does); (4) deliberate method selection in integration (substitution vs. parts vs. partial fractions); and (5) stepwise, exam-ready presentation. Note that some chapters intentionally cover material trimmed from the current CBSE exam syllabus (full properties of determinants, tangents and normals, variance of a random variable, coplanarity of lines) because this is a university-bridge course and those topics matter downstream; flag them as enrichment when a student is preparing strictly for the board exam. Always prefer exact forms (π/6, √3, e²) over decimals unless a numerical estimate is the point of the problem.

## Chapter 1: Relations and Functions
- **Core questions**: What makes a relation reflexive, symmetric, transitive, and therefore an equivalence relation? When is a function one-one (injective), onto (surjective), and hence invertible? How do you build and invert a composite function?
- **Key concepts**: Types of relations; equivalence relations and equivalence classes; injective, surjective, and bijective functions; composition of functions and its associativity; invertible functions and the inverse as the relation that "undoes" the original; domain, codomain, and range.
- **Applied skills**: Prove or disprove that a given relation is an equivalence relation by checking all three properties explicitly; show a function is bijective and construct its inverse; compute and simplify composite functions while tracking domains.
- **Common traps**: Asserting transitivity or symmetry from a few examples instead of a general argument; confusing codomain with range when testing for onto; forgetting that f∘g and g∘f are generally different; claiming a function is invertible without first proving it is a bijection on the stated domain.

## Chapter 2: Inverse Trigonometric Functions
- **Core questions**: Why must inverse trig functions be restricted to principal-value branches to be functions at all? What are the principal-value ranges of sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, and tan⁻¹? How do you simplify a nested inverse-trig expression without producing an out-of-range answer?
- **Key concepts**: Principal value branches — sin⁻¹ on [−π/2, π/2], cos⁻¹ on [0, π], tan⁻¹ on (−π/2, π/2); graphs and domains of the six inverse trig functions; standard identities (sin⁻¹x + cos⁻¹x = π/2; tan⁻¹x + cot⁻¹x = π/2); conditions on x for identities like tan⁻¹x + tan⁻¹y to hold.
- **Applied skills**: Evaluate exact principal values (e.g., sin⁻¹(−1/2) = −π/6, cos⁻¹(−1/2) = 2π/3); simplify expressions such as tan(2 tan⁻¹x) or cos(sin⁻¹x) using right-triangle reasoning; apply the conditional identity for tan⁻¹x + tan⁻¹y including the +π correction when xy > 1.
- **Common traps**: Giving cos⁻¹(−1/2) as −π/3 instead of 2π/3 by forgetting the [0, π] range; treating sin⁻¹(sin θ) as θ for every θ rather than only on the principal branch; dropping the +π/−π adjustment in the tan⁻¹ addition formula when the product exceeds 1.

## Chapter 3: Matrices and Determinants
- **Core questions**: When is matrix multiplication defined and why is it non-commutative? How do you decompose any square matrix into symmetric and skew-symmetric parts? Which determinant properties let you evaluate a 3×3 determinant by inspection rather than full expansion?
- **Key concepts**: Matrix order, equality, addition, scalar multiplication, and multiplication; transpose; symmetric (Aᵀ = A) and skew-symmetric (Aᵀ = −A) matrices; determinants up to order 3; properties of determinants (row/column operations, factor extraction, the effect of swapping rows); minors, cofactors, adjoint, and the inverse via A⁻¹ = adj(A)/det(A).
- **Applied skills**: Verify dimensional compatibility before multiplying and interpret the resulting order; write A = ½(A + Aᵀ) + ½(A − Aᵀ); simplify a determinant using row reduction before expanding; compute the inverse of a 3×3 matrix and check AA⁻¹ = I.
- **Common traps**: Assuming AB = BA; treating (AB)ᵀ as AᵀBᵀ rather than BᵀAᵀ; expanding a determinant the long way when a single row operation makes a row of zeros; forgetting that a matrix is invertible only when its determinant is nonzero.

## Chapter 4: Linear Systems by Matrix and Cramer Methods
- **Core questions**: How does the determinant of the coefficient matrix tell you whether a linear system has a unique solution, no solution, or infinitely many? When should you use the matrix-inverse method versus Cramer's Rule?
- **Key concepts**: Writing a system as AX = B; consistency and the role of det(A); the matrix-inverse solution X = A⁻¹B; Cramer's Rule via the determinants D, D₁, D₂, D₃; homogeneous systems and the trivial solution; geometric interpretation of three planes meeting (or not) in space.
- **Applied skills**: Solve a 3-variable system with the inverse method; apply Cramer's Rule and interpret the case D = 0; classify a system as consistent-unique, consistent-infinite, or inconsistent from the determinant evidence.
- **Common traps**: Applying Cramer's Rule when D = 0 (it gives no valid answer there — you must analyze D₁, D₂, D₃ separately); arithmetic sign errors in cofactor expansion; concluding "no solution" when the system is actually dependent (infinitely many).

## Chapter 5: Continuity and Differentiability
- **Core questions**: What three conditions must hold for a function to be continuous at a point? Why does differentiability imply continuity but not the reverse? How do you differentiate functions defined implicitly, parametrically, or through logarithms?
- **Key concepts**: Continuity at a point and on an interval; removable, jump, and infinite discontinuities; differentiability and the equality of left- and right-hand derivatives; chain rule; derivatives of inverse trig, exponential, and logarithmic functions; logarithmic, implicit, and parametric differentiation; second-order derivatives.
- **Applied skills**: Test continuity and differentiability of a piecewise function at its junction by matching limits and one-sided derivatives; differentiate xˣ by logarithmic differentiation; find dy/dx for an implicit curve and for a parametric pair (x(t), y(t)); compute d²y/dx².
- **Common traps**: Concluding a continuous function must be differentiable (|x| at 0 is the counterexample); forgetting the chain-rule factor when differentiating a composite; in parametric differentiation, computing dy/dx as (dy/dt)·(dx/dt) instead of (dy/dt)/(dx/dt); mishandling the d/dx of an inverse trig function's argument.

## Chapter 6: Applications of Derivatives
- **Core questions**: How does the sign of the first derivative classify intervals of increase and decrease? What distinguishes the first-derivative test from the second-derivative test for extrema? How do you set up and solve an optimization problem from a verbal description?
- **Key concepts**: Rate of change and related rates; increasing/decreasing functions via f′; critical points; first- and second-derivative tests; local versus absolute extrema on closed intervals; tangents and normals; linear approximation; optimization (maximizing area, minimizing cost or material).
- **Applied skills**: Build a sign chart for f′ and read off monotonic intervals; find absolute extrema on [a, b] by comparing critical values with endpoints; write tangent and normal lines at a point; translate a word problem into an objective function with a constraint, then optimize.
- **Common traps**: Reporting a local extremum as an absolute extremum without checking the endpoints; assuming every critical point is a maximum or minimum (the second derivative may vanish — a point of inflection); confusing the slopes of tangent and normal (they are negative reciprocals); forgetting to reduce the objective to a single variable using the constraint before differentiating.

## Chapter 7: Integral Calculus and Areas
- **Core questions**: How do you decide between substitution, integration by parts, and partial fractions for a given integrand? What do the properties of definite integrals (symmetry, the limits-swap rule, the a-to-2a property) let you compute that direct evaluation cannot? How does the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connect areas to antiderivatives?
- **Key concepts**: Antiderivatives and standard integrals; substitution; integration by parts (and the ILATE ordering); partial fractions; properties of definite integrals; the definite integral as a limit of a Riemann sum; the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus; area under a curve and between two curves.
- **Applied skills**: Choose and execute the right technique for ∫x·eˣ dx, ∫dx/(x²−1), or ∫sin³x dx; exploit ∫₀ᵃ f(x)dx = ∫₀ᵃ f(a−x)dx to evaluate otherwise hard definite integrals; compute the area enclosed between a line and a parabola by integrating the difference of the curves.
- **Common traps**: Omitting the constant of integration on an indefinite integral; substituting without converting the limits (or the dx) for a definite integral; choosing u and dv backwards in parts so the integral gets worse; computing area as ∫(upper − lower) but forgetting to split at intersection points where the curves swap order.

## Chapter 8: Differential Equations and Modeling
- **Core questions**: How do you read off the order and degree of a differential equation? How do you recognize and solve a separable, homogeneous, or first-order linear equation? How does a differential equation encode an exponential growth or decay process?
- **Key concepts**: Order and degree; general versus particular solutions; forming a differential equation from a family of curves by eliminating arbitrary constants; variable-separable equations; homogeneous equations solved by y = vx; first-order linear equations and the integrating factor e^∫P dx; growth and decay models.
- **Applied skills**: Classify an equation, then solve it by the matching method; use an initial condition to pin down the constant and produce a particular solution; build and solve dN/dt = kN for a population or radioactive-decay scenario and interpret the constant k.
- **Common traps**: Confusing order (highest derivative) with degree (power of that derivative after the equation is made polynomial in derivatives); forgetting that degree is undefined when derivatives appear inside transcendental functions; integrating a separable equation without the constant; computing the integrating factor with the wrong sign of P(x).

## Chapter 9: Vectors and Three-Dimensional Geometry
- **Core questions**: What do the dot and cross products measure geometrically, and when do you use each? How do you write a line and a plane in both vector and Cartesian form? How do you find the shortest distance between two skew lines?
- **Key concepts**: Position, unit, and direction vectors; direction cosines and ratios; the section formula in vector form; dot product (and projection); cross product (and area of a parallelogram); scalar triple product (and volume / coplanarity); equations of lines and planes; angles between lines, planes, and a line and a plane; shortest distance between skew lines; coplanarity of lines (enrichment beyond the trimmed CBSE syllabus).
- **Applied skills**: Compute a·b and a×b and interpret them; find the angle between two vectors via the dot product; write the vector and Cartesian equations of a line through two points; determine whether two lines intersect, are parallel, or are skew, and compute the shortest distance in the skew case.
- **Common traps**: Treating the cross product as commutative (a×b = −b×a); using |a||b|cosθ for the cross-product magnitude (it is sinθ); confusing direction ratios (any scalar multiple is fine) with direction cosines (must satisfy l² + m² + n² = 1); reading the shortest-distance formula with the wrong vector in the numerator's scalar triple product.

## Chapter 10: Linear Programming
- **Core questions**: How do you translate a resource-allocation problem into an objective function and a set of linear constraints? Why does the optimum of a linear objective over a convex feasible region always occur at a corner point? What changes when the feasible region is unbounded?
- **Key concepts**: Decision variables; objective function (maximize or minimize); constraints and non-negativity restrictions; the feasible region as a convex polygon; bounded versus unbounded regions; the corner-point (vertex) method; the case of no feasible solution.
- **Applied skills**: Formulate a two-variable LP from a word problem; graph the constraints, identify the feasible region, and find its corner points; evaluate the objective at each corner to find the optimum; for an unbounded region, test whether a maximum or minimum even exists.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting the non-negativity constraints x ≥ 0, y ≥ 0; shading the wrong side of an inequality; assuming an unbounded region always has an optimum (a maximize problem may be unbounded above); reading the optimum off an interior point instead of a vertex.

## Chapter 11: Probability and Random Variables
- **Core questions**: How does conditional probability update a belief once you learn an outcome? When are two events independent, and how does that differ from being mutually exclusive? How does Bayes' theorem reverse a conditional probability, and how do you summarize a random variable with its mean and variance?
- **Key concepts**: Conditional probability P(A|B) = P(A∩B)/P(B); the multiplication theorem; independent events (P(A∩B) = P(A)P(B)); the law of total probability; Bayes' theorem; discrete random variables and probability distributions; expectation (mean), and variance/standard deviation (variance is enrichment beyond the current CBSE exam syllabus).
- **Applied skills**: Compute conditional probabilities from a two-way table or tree; distinguish independent from mutually exclusive events with a concrete example; apply Bayes' theorem to a diagnostic-test or which-machine-made-the-defect problem; build a probability distribution table and compute E(X) = Σxᵢpᵢ and Var(X) = E(X²) − [E(X)]².
- **Common traps**: Confusing independent with mutually exclusive (mutually exclusive events with nonzero probability are actually dependent); inverting the conditional (using P(B|A) where P(A|B) is needed) — the exact error Bayes' theorem fixes; forgetting to subtract the mean squared in Var(X) = E(X²) − [E(X)]²; probabilities in a distribution that fail to sum to 1.

## Capstone
Students complete a Class 12 mathematics readiness portfolio organized around a single applied or exam-style theme of their choosing (for example: "Optimizing a small manufacturer's output," "Modeling the spread and decay of a contaminant," or "A geometry-and-probability case study"). The portfolio must integrate at least four major tools from the course — for instance matrix methods, derivative-based optimization, definite integration, a differential-equation model, vector geometry, linear programming, or a Bayes'/distribution analysis. Each section must show clean mathematical setup, exact computations, a diagram or graph where one clarifies the work, and an explicit statement of assumptions and domain restrictions. The submission also includes one block of corrected timed practice (a board- or entrance-style mixed paper) with each error classified as conceptual, algebraic, notation, or time-management, and a short written or oral defense of why each method was the right choice. The rubric rewards cross-topic integration, correctness, presentation, and demonstrated readiness for a university calculus sequence over mere completeness.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written assignments graded for theorem use, correct notation, method choice, and exam-style presentation.
- Short quizzes on inverse trig principal values, matrix and determinant operations, derivative and integration techniques, vector products, and probability laws.
- Timed mixed-topic practice blocks modeled on NCERT, board-exam, and entrance-test problem styles.
- Error-log revisions requiring students to classify each mistake as conceptual, algebraic, notation-based, or time-management related.
- Capstone rubric covering mathematical correctness, cross-topic integration, diagrams, clarity, correction quality, and readiness for university mathematics.
