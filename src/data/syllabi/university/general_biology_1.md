# General Biology I
**ID**: `genBio1` · **Discipline**: Science · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: typical first-semester majors general biology; the molecular and cellular half of a two-semester introductory sequence (e.g., OpenStax Biology 2e Units 1-3, AAAS Vision and Change core concepts)

## Course Aim
General Biology I treats life as a mechanism that can be reasoned about, not a vocabulary list to be memorized. The organizing claim is that biology runs on four coupled themes: chemistry sets the rules, structure determines function, energy and matter are conserved and tracked, and information flows from DNA to traits. A student who finishes this course should be able to start from molecules and build up — explaining why water behaves the way it does, why a membrane is selectively permeable, why a cell needs the citric acid cycle, and why a single base change can silence a protein.

The course is deliberately quantitative and evidence-driven. Students do not just state that enzymes lower activation energy; they read a Michaelis-Menten curve and decide whether an inhibitor is competitive. They do not just recite "ATP is energy currency"; they trace where the 30-32 ATP per glucose actually come from and what happens when the proton gradient leaks. Every major idea is paired with a piece of data, a diagram, or a calculation, because the skill being built is the ability to argue a mechanism from evidence and to notice when a claim outruns what the data support.

This first semester stays on molecular and cellular foundations. Organismal diversity, evolution, and ecology belong to General Biology II; here the payoff is depth on the chemistry of life, cell architecture and transport, bioenergetics, the cell cycle, and the molecular basis of inheritance, finishing with the lab and biotechnology skills (PCR, gels, sequencing, CRISPR) that let modern biologists test these ideas.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test the chemistry of life, macromolecule structure-function, cell structure and organelles, membranes and transport, thermodynamics and enzymes, cellular respiration and photosynthesis, the cell cycle and division, Mendelian and molecular genetics, or core wet-lab and biotech methods at a first-year college level for biology majors. The intended difficulty is above AP Biology breadth on mechanism and quantitation but below upper-division biochemistry or molecular genetics depth.

Keep three lines of demarcation. (1) Organismal diversity, phylogeny, natural selection mechanics, population genetics, physiology of organ systems, and ecology go to General Biology II, not here. (2) Detailed reaction mechanisms, equilibrium math, and stoichiometry beyond what a biology argument needs belong to General Chemistry — Gen Bio I uses chemistry as a tool. (3) Favor items that force a prediction, a calculation, or a data interpretation over pure recall; when recall is unavoidable, attach it to a mechanism. Distractors should encode the specific misconceptions named in each chapter's Common traps so wrong answers are diagnostic.

## Chapter 1: Biology as Evidence, Chemistry, and Scale
- **Core questions**: How do we test a biological claim, and what makes water and carbon the foundation of life chemistry?
- **Key concepts**: Hypothesis vs prediction, controls and confounds, dependent vs independent variables; atomic structure, covalent/ionic/hydrogen bonds; water's polarity, cohesion, adhesion, high specific heat, and role as solvent; pH, the logarithmic scale, buffers, and acids/bases; carbon's bonding versatility and the major functional groups (hydroxyl, carbonyl, carboxyl, amino, phosphate, sulfhydryl, methyl).
- **Applied skills**: Identify the control and the manipulated variable in an experiment; predict how a functional group changes a molecule's polarity or reactivity; estimate how a pH change shifts H+ concentration; reason about scale across the molecule-cell-organism hierarchy.
- **Common traps**: Treating correlation as causation or skipping the control; calling hydrogen bonds covalent; thinking a one-unit pH drop is a small change (it is tenfold in [H+]); confusing "polar" with "charged"; assuming a buffer eliminates rather than resists pH change.

## Chapter 2: Macromolecules and Structure-Function Reasoning
- **Core questions**: How do four classes of macromolecule get built from monomers, and how does shape dictate what they do?
- **Key concepts**: Dehydration synthesis and hydrolysis; carbohydrates (mono-, di-, polysaccharides; glycosidic bonds; starch vs glycogen vs cellulose); lipids (fats, phospholipids, steroids; saturation and fluidity; amphipathic behavior); proteins (amino acids, peptide bonds, the four levels of structure, denaturation, chaperones); nucleic acids (nucleotides, the sugar-phosphate backbone, base pairing, 5'-to-3' directionality); molecular recognition and specificity.
- **Applied skills**: Map a structural feature to a function (e.g., cellulose's beta linkages to its indigestibility); predict how a point mutation or denaturant alters protein activity; distinguish which bond type breaks during hydrolysis vs denaturation.
- **Common traps**: Saying denaturation breaks peptide bonds (it disrupts non-covalent folding, primary sequence stays intact); confusing the monomer-polymer direction of dehydration vs hydrolysis; assuming all lipids are polymers (they are not built from repeating monomers); ignoring directionality of nucleic acids; treating "saturated" as a property of the whole molecule rather than the fatty-acid tails.

## Chapter 3: Cells, Organelles, and Membranes
- **Core questions**: What distinguishes cell types, how do organelles cooperate, and how does a membrane decide what crosses?
- **Key concepts**: Prokaryotic vs eukaryotic organization; the endomembrane system (ER, Golgi, vesicles, lysosomes) and the secretory pathway; mitochondria and chloroplasts and the endosymbiotic evidence (double membranes, own DNA, ribosomes); cytoskeleton and extracellular matrix; the fluid mosaic model; passive transport (simple and facilitated diffusion, osmosis), tonicity, and active transport (the Na+/K+ pump: 3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in per ATP); endo- and exocytosis.
- **Applied skills**: Trace a secreted protein through the endomembrane system in order; predict cell volume change in hypo-, iso-, and hypertonic solutions; classify a transport event as passive or active from its energy and gradient requirements; read a micrograph for organelle identity.
- **Common traps**: Confusing hypertonic/hypotonic and predicting the wrong direction of water flow; thinking facilitated diffusion needs ATP (it does not — it is passive but protein-mediated); claiming the membrane is mostly lipid by mass (protein is ~50%); forgetting animal cells lack walls (so they lyse in hypotonic media); assuming all organelles arose by endosymbiosis (only mitochondria and plastids have the evidence).

## Chapter 4: Energy, Enzymes, and Metabolic Regulation
- **Core questions**: What does the second law require of living systems, and how do enzymes and regulation control reaction rates without changing thermodynamics?
- **Key concepts**: First and second laws, entropy, Gibbs free energy (Delta G), exergonic vs endergonic reactions, spontaneity vs rate; ATP as the coupling currency and energetic coupling; enzymes and activation energy; the induced-fit model; factors affecting rate (substrate, temperature, pH); Michaelis-Menten Km and Vmax; competitive (raises apparent Km, Vmax unchanged) vs noncompetitive (lowers Vmax, Km unchanged) inhibition; allosteric regulation and feedback inhibition.
- **Applied skills**: Decide if a reaction is spontaneous from the sign of Delta G; explain how coupling drives an endergonic step; read a rate-vs-substrate curve to extract Km and Vmax; distinguish inhibitor types from how a curve shifts; locate a feedback control point in a pathway diagram.
- **Common traps**: Equating spontaneous with fast (Delta G sets direction, enzymes set rate); claiming enzymes change Delta G or equilibrium (they only lower Ea and speed both directions equally); thinking competitive inhibition lowers Vmax (raising substrate overcomes it, so Vmax is unchanged); assuming higher temperature always speeds enzymes (denaturation reverses the trend); treating ATP as stored long-term energy rather than a turnover currency.

## Chapter 5: Cellular Respiration and Photosynthesis
- **Core questions**: How is a glucose molecule's energy harvested, and how do photosynthesis and respiration form a coupled matter-and-energy loop?
- **Key concepts**: Glycolysis (cytosol; net 2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2 pyruvate per glucose); pyruvate oxidation and the citric acid cycle (NADH, FADH2, CO2); the electron transport chain and chemiosmosis (proton-motive force, ATP synthase, ~30-32 ATP total, O2 as terminal acceptor); fermentation and NAD+ regeneration; the light reactions (photolysis of water, photosystems, NADPH and ATP); the Calvin cycle (carbon fixation by RuBisCO, reduction, regeneration); C3, C4, and CAM strategies and photorespiration.
- **Applied skills**: Track carbon, electrons, and energy through each stage; account for where each ATP and reduced carrier originates; predict the effect of blocking a complex, uncoupling the gradient, or removing O2; compare the inputs and outputs of respiration vs photosynthesis; interpret data from light-intensity, CO2, or O2-evolution experiments.
- **Common traps**: Saying glycolysis needs oxygen or occurs in the mitochondrion (it is anaerobic and cytosolic); thinking the ETC, not substrate-level steps, is where O2 binds glucose carbon (O2 only accepts electrons at the end); believing fermentation makes ATP from NADH (its job is to regenerate NAD+ so glycolysis continues); confusing the Calvin cycle with a light-independent "dark" cycle that needs no light products (it depends on ATP and NADPH from the light reactions); claiming plants only photosynthesize and do not respire.

## Chapter 6: Cell Cycle, Mitosis, and Meiosis
- **Core questions**: How is division regulated, and how do mitosis and meiosis differ in chromosome number, DNA content, and genetic outcome?
- **Key concepts**: Interphase (G1, S, G2) and M phase; cyclins, CDKs, and the G1/S, G2/M, and spindle checkpoints; cancer as loss of checkpoint control (oncogenes, tumor suppressors like p53); mitosis (one division, two identical diploid cells); meiosis I and II (two divisions, four haploid gametes); crossing over in prophase I; independent assortment in metaphase I; nondisjunction and aneuploidy.
- **Applied skills**: Compare mitosis and meiosis on chromosome number, ploidy, and variation; track DNA amount (C) and chromosome number (n) across phases; predict the consequence of nondisjunction (e.g., trisomy); identify which stage generates new allele combinations; read a cell-cycle diagram for checkpoint location.
- **Common traps**: Conflating chromosome number with DNA amount (S phase doubles DNA without doubling chromosome number; sister chromatids are one chromosome); thinking sister chromatids separate in meiosis I (homologs separate in I, sisters in II); attributing variation to mitosis (it is genetically conservative); confusing haploid/diploid; assuming crossing over happens in mitosis under normal conditions.

## Chapter 7: Mendelian Genetics and Molecular Inheritance
- **Core questions**: How do probability and Mendel's laws predict inheritance, and how does the molecular machinery turn a gene into a trait?
- **Key concepts**: Segregation and independent assortment; monohybrid (3:1) and dihybrid (9:3:3:1) crosses; the product and sum rules; test crosses and pedigrees; dominance variations (incomplete, codominance, multiple alleles, pleiotropy, epistasis); sex linkage; linkage and recombination frequency as map distance (1% = 1 map unit); DNA replication (semiconservative, leading/lagging strands, polymerase directionality); transcription and RNA processing; translation, the codon table, and the genetics of point mutations (silent, missense, nonsense, frameshift); gene regulation (the lac operon as a model).
- **Applied skills**: Compute offspring ratios with Punnett squares and the product rule; interpret a pedigree for dominant/recessive and autosomal/X-linked patterns; convert recombination frequency to map distance; translate a DNA change into its protein consequence; compare how a regulatory mutation changes expression.
- **Common traps**: Adding probabilities that should be multiplied (independent events use the product rule); assuming dominant means more common or "stronger"; reading a frameshift as a single-amino-acid change (it shifts the whole downstream reading frame); confusing transcription with replication; thinking the lac operon is "on by default"; treating linked genes as assorting independently.

## Chapter 8: Biotechnology, Lab Skills, and the Biology Studio
- **Core questions**: How do we physically manipulate and read DNA, and what makes a result trustworthy and ethical?
- **Key concepts**: Microscopy and magnification/resolution; accurate pipetting and serial dilutions; sterile technique and contamination control; PCR (primers, denaturation/annealing/extension cycles, exponential amplification); gel electrophoresis (charge, size separation, smaller fragments migrate farther); Sanger and next-generation sequencing; restriction enzymes and cloning; CRISPR-Cas9 targeting; BLAST and basic bioinformatics; controls, replicates, and reproducibility; biosafety and the ethics of genome editing.
- **Applied skills**: Choose the right method for a goal (amplify vs separate vs edit); compute a serial dilution and predict concentration; interpret a gel (band size, missing band, ladder); design appropriate positive and negative controls; read a sequencing trace for a base change; evaluate an experiment for reproducibility and ethical risk.
- **Common traps**: Saying larger fragments travel farther on a gel (smaller fragments migrate farther toward the positive electrode); thinking PCR separates DNA or that a gel copies it (PCR amplifies, the gel separates); forgetting the no-template negative control that catches contamination; assuming a single replicate is sufficient; conflating "we can edit it" with "we should" (ignoring the ethics layer).

## Capstone
Each student produces a molecular-to-cellular explanation of a chosen biological phenomenon (for example: why sickle-cell hemoglobin polymerizes, how a poison disrupts the electron transport chain, or why a specific mutation causes lactose intolerance). The deliverable must integrate at least one annotated diagram, one quantitative dataset or lab-style result (graph, gel, or cross), and one explicit genetics or metabolism mechanism, ending with a claim-evidence-reasoning paragraph and one stated limitation of the explanation. The grading rubric rewards mechanistic accuracy, correct use of data, and intellectual honesty about uncertainty over breadth of terms cited.

## Assessment Ideas
- Diagram-based items that require both labels and the mechanism connecting them.
- Data-interpretation prompts drawn from enzyme-kinetics, respiration/photosynthesis, genetics-cross, or gel/sequencing contexts.
- Calculation items: pH and [H+], dilutions, free-energy sign, ATP accounting, cross ratios, recombination-to-map-distance.
- Cumulative concept maps linking the four themes (chemistry, structure-function, energy/matter, information).
- Lab-notebook checks for method, raw data, uncertainty, controls, and conclusion.
- A written defense of a mechanism with evidence and one acknowledged limitation.
