# Contemporary Ethical Theory
**ID**: `contemporaryEthics` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Level**: University

## Course Aim
This course builds advanced ethical judgment by connecting three layers that beginning courses usually keep apart: metaethics (what moral claims mean and whether they can be true), normative theory (what makes acts right and people good), and applied ethics (how to reason through live, high-stakes cases). The goal is not to convert students to a single theory but to make them fluent across theories, fast at locating the real disagreement in a dispute, and honest about where their own confidence runs out.

A student who finishes the course should be able to do four things under time pressure. First, take a messy public argument and separate its descriptive, normative, and metaethical claims. Second, run a case through utilitarian, deontological, virtue, care, and contractualist lenses and say precisely where they converge and where they pull apart. Third, name the strongest objection to their own view and answer it without retreating into "it's all relative." Fourth, write a recommendation a non-philosopher could act on, with the uncertainty made explicit rather than hidden.

The contemporary edge is deliberate. Roughly half the course lives in metaethics and normative theory, the foundation; the other half pushes those tools against the problems that define ethics now — algorithmic systems and AI, climate and obligations to future people, global justice and migration, bioethics and moral status, and how citizens should argue when they disagree about first principles. Throughout, the course treats moral philosophy as a discipline with methods, not a set of opinions to be ranked.

## Course Design Notes
The earlier version of this syllabus had excellent contemporary topics but moved into applied cases before students had the metaethical and normative scaffolding to analyze them, and its chapters carried inconsistent structure (some listed cases, some a deliverable, one duplicated the capstone). This rewrite fixes both problems. Every chapter now follows the same four-part shape — Core questions, Key concepts, Applied skills, Common traps — so students always know what they are supposed to be able to do, not just what they should have read. The "Common traps" sections are new and intentional: most student errors in ethics are predictable (conflating relativism with tolerance, treating a theory as a calculator, smuggling values into a metric), and naming them in advance is the fastest route to better reasoning.

The chapter sequence is foundations-first. Chapters 1–4 build method, metaethics, moral psychology, and a comparative grip on the major normative theories. Chapters 5–9 apply that machinery to technology, global and intergenerational justice, bioethics and moral status, and public/democratic ethics, closing with a chapter on method-in-practice that prepares the capstone. Recurring frameworks (reflective equilibrium, a stakeholder-and-objection template, moral uncertainty) reappear across chapters so they become reflexes. Assessment rewards reconstructing opposing views charitably as much as defending one's own.

## Chapter 1: What Ethical Theory Is For
**Core questions**
- What changes when we ask "what should we do?" versus "what makes moral claims true or justified?"
- How should a serious person revise a moral belief — by adjusting the principle or by overturning the intuition?
- When is consistency a virtue, and when is it a trap that locks in an early mistake?

**Key concepts**: normative ethics, metaethics, applied ethics, descriptive vs normative claims, moral disagreement, reflective equilibrium, considered judgment, intuition, principle, case, moral uncertainty.

**Applied skills**
- Sort the claims in an argument into descriptive, normative, and metaethical buckets.
- Reconstruct an argument as premises, conclusion, the strongest objection, and a reply.
- Run reflective equilibrium on a real disagreement: identify which principle and which case judgment are in tension and decide, with reasons, which to revise.

**Common traps**
- Treating "metaethics" as harder applied ethics rather than a different question entirely.
- Assuming reflective equilibrium means "keep your intuitions and rationalize them" — it must allow either side to give way.
- Mistaking a factual disagreement (what will happen) for a moral one (what we owe), or vice versa.

## Chapter 2: Moral Truth, Relativism, and Objectivity
**Core questions**
- Are moral claims truth-apt, or do they express attitudes, plans, or commitments rather than describe facts?
- Can morality be objective without being "discovered" the way a scientific fact is?
- What exactly is wrong with "it's all just opinion," and what survives once that slogan is dropped?

**Key concepts**: moral realism, anti-realism, cognitivism vs non-cognitivism, expressivism, constructivism, error theory (Mackie), relativism, naturalism vs non-naturalism, the Euthyphro problem, Moore's open question argument, the is–ought gap.

**Applied skills**
- State a position (e.g., expressivism, constructivism, error theory) precisely enough that its defenders would accept the wording.
- Apply the open question argument to test a proposed naturalistic definition of "good."
- Distinguish descriptive relativism (cultures disagree) from metaethical relativism (truth is relative to a culture) from normative tolerance (we should not interfere).

**Common traps**
- Collapsing relativism into tolerance — relativism cannot itself ground a universal duty to tolerate.
- Treating disagreement as proof that there is no fact of the matter (people disagree about facts too).
- Assuming "constructed" means "arbitrary"; constructivists claim objectivity without mind-independent moral facts.

## Chapter 3: Reasons, Motivation, and Moral Responsibility
**Core questions**
- Must a sincere moral judgment motivate the person who makes it, at least somewhat?
- How much responsibility survives when incentives, hierarchy, or technology shape what a person does?
- When is ignorance a genuine excuse, and when is it culpable negligence?

**Key concepts**: motivational internalism vs externalism, normative reasons vs motives, moral luck (Williams, Nagel), the control principle, blame, excuse vs justification, complicity, responsibility within systems, the problem of many hands.

**Applied skills**
- Distinguish a reason that justifies an act from the motive that actually moved the agent.
- Diagnose a case of moral luck and say which kind (resultant, circumstantial, constitutive) is doing the work.
- Allocate responsibility across a chain of actors when no single decision caused the harm.

**Common traps**
- Confusing an excuse (reduces blame, admits wrong) with a justification (denies wrong).
- Letting "the system did it" dissolve all individual responsibility, or pretending systems carry none.
- Judging an agent by outcomes alone while officially endorsing the control principle.

## Chapter 4: Normative Theories Under Pressure
**Core questions**
- What does each major theory see clearly, and what does it systematically miss?
- Are moral theories decision procedures, criteria of rightness, ideals, or tools for critique — and does conflating these cause confusion?
- How demanding can morality be before it becomes unlivable, and is unlivability an objection?

**Key concepts**: act vs rule utilitarianism, deontology and the doctrine of double effect, Kantian respect for persons, virtue ethics and practical wisdom, care ethics and partiality, Scanlonian contractualism ("principles no one could reasonably reject"), rights, integrity, the demandingness objection, the separateness of persons.

**Applied skills**
- Run a single case through utilitarian, deontological, virtue, care, and contractualist analyses and chart where they agree and split.
- Separate a theory's criterion of rightness from its recommended decision procedure.
- Press the standard objections (demandingness on consequentialism, rigidity on deontology, vagueness on virtue ethics) and supply the best available reply.

**Common traps**
- Treating utilitarianism as a literal calculator and ignoring rule/indirect versions.
- Caricaturing deontology as "rules no matter the consequences" while ignoring thresholds and double effect.
- Using "virtue ethics" as a label for "be a good person" without the demand for practical wisdom and codifiable guidance.

## Chapter 5: Technology, AI, and Human Agency
**Core questions**
- Who is responsible when a system causes harm through many small, individually defensible design choices?
- What should count as meaningful consent and meaningful human oversight in data-rich, opaque systems?
- Does human enhancement threaten equality, authenticity, or dignity — or is that worry a status-quo bias?

**Key concepts**: algorithmic bias and fairness metrics, opacity and explainability, accountability gaps, automation bias, autonomy and manipulation, privacy and informed consent, surveillance, the alignment problem, the precautionary principle, dual use.

**Applied skills**
- Map an AI or data system's stakeholders, benefits, harms, and uncertainties, then locate where responsibility actually sits.
- Argue both sides of an algorithmic fairness dispute, showing that competing fairness metrics can be mathematically incompatible.
- Write a short ethical impact memo with governance options, not just a verdict.

**Common traps**
- Treating "the algorithm decided" as an accountability sink that absolves designers and deployers.
- Assuming a single fairness metric is the neutral one; metric choice is a normative choice.
- Confusing consent-as-a-clickwrap with consent that is informed, voluntary, and revocable.

## Chapter 6: Global Justice, Climate, and Future People
**Core questions**
- Do national borders change the strength of a moral obligation, or only who is positioned to discharge it?
- What do present people owe future people, and how can they be wronged if our choices change who exists?
- How should moral urgency be weighed against uncertainty, feasibility, and democratic legitimacy?

**Key concepts**: cosmopolitanism vs statism, duties of assistance vs justice, effective altruism, historical responsibility and capacity-to-pay (climate burden-sharing), intergenerational justice, the non-identity problem (Parfit), the repugnant conclusion and population ethics, social discount rate, longtermism.

**Applied skills**
- Apply the non-identity problem to a policy case and explain why "no one is harmed" does not settle the matter.
- Compare burden-sharing principles (polluter pays, ability to pay, beneficiary pays) on a concrete climate-finance dispute.
- Reason explicitly about discounting and show how the discount rate drives the conclusion.

**Common traps**
- Treating the non-identity problem as a trick rather than a real challenge to person-affecting ethics.
- Assuming a positive discount rate is value-neutral economics rather than an ethical assumption about future people.
- Sliding from "we have strong duties to future people" to "present democratic preferences don't matter."

## Chapter 7: Bodies, Life, and Moral Status
**Core questions**
- What makes a being morally considerable — sentience, rationality, relationships, potential, or species membership?
- When may autonomy yield to protection, public health, or fair allocation?
- Can metrics like the QALY make hard choices fairer, or do they launder contested value judgments?

**Key concepts**: moral status and personhood, sentience and interests, speciesism (Singer) and the argument from marginal cases, autonomy and informed consent, bodily integrity, vulnerability, disability justice, animal ethics, QALY/DALY, triage and fair-allocation principles.

**Applied skills**
- Build a defensible account of moral status and test it against hard cases (animals, embryos, patients in coma, future AI).
- Distinguish what a QALY measures from the value judgments embedded in it (e.g., disability weightings).
- Apply and critique an allocation rule (lottery, priority to worst-off, maximize life-years) in a triage scenario.

**Common traps**
- Equating "has moral status" with "has the same status as a typical adult human" — status can come in degrees and kinds.
- Treating informed consent as a signature rather than a process of disclosure, understanding, and voluntariness.
- Believing a quantitative metric is therefore value-free; the choice of metric is itself an ethical position.

## Chapter 8: Public Ethics and Democratic Disagreement
**Core questions**
- How should citizens argue and decide together when they disagree about first principles?
- When is compromise morally required, and when is it a betrayal of something non-negotiable?
- What does ethical theory contribute to public policy, beyond private conscience?

**Key concepts**: public reason (Rawls) and reasonable pluralism, legitimacy vs justice, civil disobedience and conscientious objection, the ethics of compromise, moral testimony and deference, epistemic injustice (Fricker: testimonial vs hermeneutical), institutional trust, the role of expertise in democracy.

**Applied skills**
- Frame a contested issue in terms a reasonable opponent could accept, then identify where public reason runs out.
- Distinguish testimonial injustice (deflated credibility from prejudice) from hermeneutical injustice (missing shared concepts).
- Judge whether a proposed compromise is a fair split or a capitulation, and give the criterion you used.

**Common traps**
- Confusing legitimacy (a defensible procedure produced it) with correctness (it is the right answer).
- Treating all civil disobedience as either heroic or illegitimate, instead of testing it against publicity, non-violence, and acceptance of penalty.
- Outsourcing moral judgment to experts on questions that are normative, not technical.

## Chapter 9: Method in Practice — From Theory to Recommendation
**Core questions**
- How do you reason responsibly when theories conflict and you are genuinely uncertain which is right?
- How do you give moral weight to empirical facts without letting "is" silently become "ought"?
- What does a defensible recommendation look like to someone who has to act on it?

**Key concepts**: moral uncertainty and how to act under it, the fact–value relationship in applied reasoning, stakeholder analysis, the strongest-objection test, transparency about confidence, the difference between a verdict and a justification.

**Applied skills**
- Structure a decision under moral uncertainty (e.g., weighing theories, avoiding choices that are catastrophic on any plausible view).
- Integrate empirical evidence into an ethical argument while flagging every is–ought transition.
- Produce a recommendation memo: question, facts, stakeholders, competing theories, strongest objection, justified call, residual uncertainty.

**Common traps**
- Hiding uncertainty to sound authoritative, when stating it is more honest and more useful.
- Letting the theory you happen to prefer pre-decide the case before the analysis is done.
- Confusing a confident tone for a strong argument.

## Capstone
Students write a public-facing ethics brief on a live issue of their choice (for example: a hospital's ventilator-allocation policy, a city's deployment of predictive policing, a national position on climate loss-and-damage funding, or a platform's rules on AI-generated content). The brief must (1) state the precise moral question and separate it from the empirical questions around it; (2) lay out the relevant facts and their uncertainty; (3) map the stakeholders and their interests; (4) analyze the issue through at least three normative theories, showing where they converge and diverge; (5) state and answer the single strongest objection to the author's position; and (6) deliver a justified recommendation a decision-maker could act on, with residual uncertainty made explicit. Students also submit a short reflective note explaining which of their initial intuitions changed during the analysis and why — demonstrating reflective equilibrium in action rather than merely describing it.

## Assessment Ideas
- Claim-sorting exercises (descriptive / normative / metaethical) on real op-eds
- Argument reconstruction notes with strongest-objection and reply
- Five-theory comparison table on a single case
- Algorithmic ethical-impact memo (Chapter 5)
- Triage / allocation analysis with explicit allocation rule (Chapter 7)
- Seminar leadership graded on charitable reconstruction of opposing views
- Public ethics capstone brief plus reflective note
