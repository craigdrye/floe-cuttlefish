# Contemporary Ethical Theory
**ID**: `contemporaryEthics` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Level**: University

## Course Aim
Build advanced ethical judgment by connecting metaethics, normative theory, and applied dilemmas. Students learn not only what positions philosophers defend, but how moral claims are justified, challenged, revised, and used in public decisions.

## Course Design Notes
The original syllabus had strong topics, especially AI, global justice, climate, and bioethics, but it moved too quickly into applied cases without enough scaffolding in metaethics, normative comparison, and method. This rewrite keeps the contemporary edge while adding clearer chapters, concepts, analytic skills, and repeatable decision frameworks.

## Chapter 1: What Ethical Theory Is For
**Key concepts**: normative ethics, metaethics, applied ethics, moral disagreement, reflective equilibrium, moral uncertainty, intuition, principle, case.

**Essential questions**
- What changes when we ask "what should we do?" versus "what makes moral claims true or justified?"
- How should a serious person revise a moral belief?
- When is consistency a virtue, and when is it a trap?

**Skills**
- Separate descriptive, normative, and metaethical claims.
- Reconstruct an argument with premises, conclusion, objections, and replies.
- Use reflective equilibrium to revise either a principle or a case judgment.

## Chapter 2: Moral Truth, Relativism, and Objectivity
**Key concepts**: moral realism, anti-realism, expressivism, constructivism, relativism, error theory, naturalism, non-naturalism, Euthyphro problem, open question argument.

**Essential questions**
- Are moral claims truth-apt, or do they express attitudes, commitments, or practices?
- Can morality be objective without being discovered like a scientific fact?
- What exactly is wrong with saying "it is all just opinion"?

**Applied cases**
- Cross-cultural disagreement about punishment, speech, gender, markets, and medical choice.
- Institutional value statements that sound objective but require interpretation.

## Chapter 3: Reasons, Motivation, and Responsibility
**Key concepts**: internalism, externalism, reasons for action, blame, excuse, moral luck, agency, coercion, responsibility under systems.

**Essential questions**
- Must moral judgments motivate action?
- How much responsibility remains when incentives, hierarchy, or technology shape behavior?
- When is ignorance an excuse, and when is it negligence?

**Applied cases**
- Algorithmic decision harms, corporate group decisions, bureaucratic delay, and crisis triage.

## Chapter 4: Normative Theories Under Pressure
**Key concepts**: utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, care ethics, contractualism, rights, justice, common good, demandingness, integrity, partiality.

**Essential questions**
- What does each theory see clearly, and what does it tend to miss?
- Are moral theories decision procedures, ideals, explanations, or tools for critique?
- How demanding can morality be before it becomes unlivable?

**Applied cases**
- Whistleblowing, scarce medical resources, charitable giving, family obligation, and public risk.

## Chapter 5: Technology, AI, and Human Agency
**Key concepts**: algorithmic bias, opacity, accountability gaps, autonomy, surveillance, privacy, manipulation, human enhancement, alignment, precaution.

**Essential questions**
- Who is responsible when a system causes harm through many small design choices?
- What should count as meaningful consent in data-rich systems?
- Does enhancement threaten equality, authenticity, or dignity?

**Deliverable**
- Ethical impact memo for an AI or data system, including stakeholders, benefits, harms, uncertainty, and governance options.

## Chapter 6: Global Justice, Climate, and Future People
**Key concepts**: cosmopolitanism, nationalism, duties of assistance, effective altruism, historical responsibility, capacity to pay, intergenerational justice, non-identity problem, population ethics, discounting.

**Essential questions**
- Do borders change the strength of moral obligation?
- What do current people owe future people?
- How should moral urgency be balanced against uncertainty, feasibility, and democratic legitimacy?

**Applied cases**
- Refugee policy, climate adaptation funding, geoengineering, global health prioritization, and longtermist giving.

## Chapter 7: Bodies, Life, and Moral Status
**Key concepts**: personhood, autonomy, vulnerability, bodily integrity, informed consent, disability justice, moral status, animal ethics, QALY, triage.

**Essential questions**
- What makes a being morally considerable?
- When does autonomy yield to protection, public health, or justice?
- Can metrics make hard choices fairer, or do they hide moral assumptions?

**Applied cases**
- End-of-life decisions, reproductive ethics, pandemic triage, animal research, and disability-weighted policy.

## Chapter 8: Public Ethics and Democratic Disagreement
**Key concepts**: public reason, pluralism, legitimacy, civil disobedience, compromise, moral testimony, epistemic injustice, institutional trust.

**Essential questions**
- How should citizens argue when they disagree about first principles?
- When is compromise morally required, and when is it betrayal?
- What does ethical theory contribute to policy rather than private conscience?

**Capstone**
- Students write a public-facing ethics brief on a live issue. The brief must identify the moral question, relevant facts, stakeholders, competing theories, strongest objection, and a justified recommendation.
