# Introduction to Sociology
**ID**: `introToSociology` - **Discipline**: Social Science - **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Aim
This course introduces sociology as the systematic, evidence-based study of social life: how institutions, inequality, culture, interaction, and social change shape the lives of people who are themselves agents acting back on the world. The central goal is to cultivate what C. Wright Mills called the sociological imagination, the capacity to see the link between personal biography and historical structure, between "the personal troubles of milieu" and "the public issues of social structure." A student who finishes the course should be able to take an ordinary experience, a missed rent payment, a viral protest, a friendship that forms in a dorm, and reframe it as a question about social patterns that can be investigated with data.

Sociology is not common sense dressed up in jargon, and it is not the collection of opinions about society. It is a discipline with theories that compete, methods that can be done well or badly, and ethical obligations to the people it studies. Across the course students learn to distinguish anecdote from evidence, correlation from causation, and description from explanation. They learn that the same phenomenon, school achievement, crime, gender pay differences, can be illuminated by several theories at once, and that choosing among them requires argument and evidence rather than taste. Above all they learn to treat human beings as people embedded in histories, relationships, and structures, never as mere data points or stereotypes.

By the end, students should be fluent in the founding traditions (Marx, Durkheim, Weber, Du Bois), comfortable reading a table or an ethnographic excerpt with a critical eye, and able to design a small piece of original inquiry that is theoretically informed, methodologically honest, and ethically responsible. The course is humanistic in spirit and quantitative when it counts: it wants students to care about people and to demand evidence.

## Course Design Notes
This course is built for an introductory university social science setting and assumes no prior sociology. Route questions here when students need sociological theory, research methods, culture and socialization, social interaction, groups and organizations, deviance and social control, stratification, race, class, gender, social institutions, demography, social movements, globalization, or social change. The question bank deliberately mixes recall of canonical concepts (anomie, the looking-glass self, master status) with application items that hand the student a scenario and ask them to choose the right lens, the right method, or the right inference. Many distractors are themselves real sociological terms used incorrectly, because the most common freshman error is not blankness but plausible misuse.

Three through-lines are emphasized everywhere. First, the gap between individual anecdotes and social evidence: a single story is a hypothesis, not a finding. Second, the value of theoretical pluralism: functionalist, conflict, and interactionist accounts each see something real and miss something real, and naming the trade-off is the skill. Third, the ethical responsibilities of studying communities, informed consent, confidentiality, reflexivity about the researcher's own standpoint, and humane explanation that does not reduce people to their outcomes. Facts that date (poverty rates, the gender pay gap, demographic figures) are anchored to current U.S. Census and Pew data and should be refreshed when the catalog is revisited.

## Chapter 1: The Sociological Imagination and the Founders
- **Core questions**: What does it mean to connect "personal troubles" to "public issues"? How do social structure and individual agency interact? Why did sociology emerge with modernity, and how did Comte, Marx, Durkheim, Weber, and Du Bois answer the question of social order and inequality differently?
- **Key concepts**: Sociological imagination, personal troubles vs. public issues, social structure, agency, social facts, modernity, positivism (Comte), historical materialism and class conflict (Marx), solidarity and anomie (Durkheim), rationalization and verstehen (Weber), double consciousness and the color line (Du Bois), public sociology, Harriet Martineau, Jane Addams.
- **Applied skills**: Reframe a private experience as a researchable sociological question; match a founder to a core claim (Marx-exploitation, Durkheim-integration, Weber-meaning and bureaucracy, Du Bois-race and double consciousness); identify the social structures behind an everyday choice.
- **Common traps**: Treating the sociological imagination as "blaming society" rather than linking biography and structure; collapsing all founders into "they studied society" without distinguishing their problems; assuming agency and structure are opposites rather than mutually constituting; forgetting that Du Bois and the Chicago women reformers are founders, not footnotes.

## Chapter 2: Theory and Sociological Explanation
- **Core questions**: What does each major paradigm, structural functionalism, conflict theory, symbolic interactionism, take as its central question and unit of analysis? When is a macro account more useful than a micro one? What separates a theory from an opinion?
- **Key concepts**: Structural functionalism, manifest and latent functions, dysfunction (Merton), conflict theory, ideology and power, symbolic interactionism, social construction of reality (Berger and Luckmann), feminist theory, critical race theory, intersectionality, micro/meso/macro levels, theory as a falsifiable, evidence-tested explanation.
- **Applied skills**: Analyze one institution (education, family, work) through all three classical lenses and name what each captures and misses; distinguish a manifest function from a latent one; classify a claim as macro or micro; explain how power shapes what a society treats as "normal."
- **Common traps**: Believing one paradigm is simply "correct" rather than partial; confusing latent functions with dysfunctions; treating symbolic interactionism as merely "people are different"; using "social construction" to mean "fake" rather than "produced and sustained by social processes, with real consequences."

## Chapter 3: Research Methods, Data, and Ethics
- **Core questions**: How do you turn a concept into something measurable? Which method fits which question, and what can each method legitimately claim? Why does correlation not establish causation, and what are the ethical duties owed to research participants?
- **Key concepts**: Research question and literature review, hypothesis, independent and dependent variables, operationalization, validity and reliability, surveys, in-depth interviews, ethnography and participant observation, experiments, comparative-historical analysis, content analysis, secondary data, probability vs. nonprobability sampling, sampling bias, correlation vs. causation, confounding variables, spurious relationships, reflexivity and positionality, informed consent, confidentiality, IRB review, ASA Code of Ethics.
- **Applied skills**: Choose an appropriate method for a given question and justify it; spot sampling bias in a poll or convenience sample; propose a confounder that could explain an observed correlation; write a one-line informed-consent and confidentiality plan; explain how a researcher's positionality could shape fieldwork.
- **Common traps**: Reading correlation as causation (the coffee-and-stress error); thinking a large sample fixes a biased sampling frame; conflating validity (measuring the right thing) with reliability (measuring consistently); assuming quantitative methods are "objective" and qualitative methods are "just opinion"; treating ethics as paperwork rather than ongoing obligation.

## Chapter 4: Culture, Socialization, and the Self
- **Core questions**: What are the components of culture, and how do norms differ in force? How does the self develop through interaction, and who are the agents of socialization across the life course? Where is the line between cultural explanation and stereotyping?
- **Key concepts**: Culture, material and nonmaterial culture, symbols and language (Sapir-Whorf), values, norms, folkways, mores, taboos, sanctions (positive/negative, formal/informal), ethnocentrism, cultural relativism, subculture, counterculture, cultural lag, socialization, agents of socialization (family, school, peers, media), the looking-glass self (Cooley), the development of the self and the generalized other (Mead, I and me), total institutions and resocialization (Goffman), gender and racial socialization.
- **Applied skills**: Identify the norm, the value, and the sanction in a familiar scene; classify a behavior as a folkway, more, or taboo by the strength of reaction it provokes; apply the looking-glass self to online identity; separate a cultural explanation from a stereotype.
- **Common traps**: Conflating norms (expected behaviors) with values (abstract standards); treating a folkway violation as if it were a taboo; using cultural relativism to mean "anything goes" rather than "understand before judging"; assuming socialization ends in childhood; mistaking ethnocentrism for mere preference rather than a judgment that uses one's own culture as the standard.

## Chapter 5: Social Interaction, Groups, Organizations, and Networks
- **Core questions**: How do statuses and roles structure interaction, and what happens when they collide? How does the dramaturgical model read everyday life as performance? Why do networks both create opportunity and reproduce exclusion, and what makes bureaucracy efficient and dehumanizing at once?
- **Key concepts**: Status (ascribed, achieved, master), status set, role, role set, role conflict, role strain, role exit, dramaturgy and impression management (Goffman), front stage/back stage, emotional labor (Hochschild), primary and secondary groups, reference groups, in-groups and out-groups, group size effects (dyad/triad, Simmel), groupthink, social networks, strength of weak ties (Granovetter), social capital, bureaucracy and its ideal-typical features (Weber), McDonaldization (Ritzer: efficiency, calculability, predictability, control).
- **Applied skills**: Read an interaction in dramaturgical terms (front/back stage, impression management); distinguish role conflict (between roles) from role strain (within one role); explain how weak ties find jobs; recognize the four dimensions of McDonaldization in an organization; identify a master status in a scenario.
- **Common traps**: Reversing role conflict and role strain; treating a master status as just any status rather than the one that overrides others; assuming bigger networks of strong ties are always better (weak ties carry novel information); confusing a primary group with a secondary one because of physical setting; reading emotional labor as simply "being nice."

## Chapter 6: Deviance, Social Control, and Crime
- **Core questions**: Why does what counts as deviant vary across time and place? How do the main theories, strain, differential association, control, labeling, conflict, explain rule-breaking differently? Who gets counted as a criminal, and how does that shape crime statistics?
- **Key concepts**: Deviance, conformity, social control (informal and formal), stigma (Goffman), moral entrepreneurship and moral panic, Durkheim's functions of deviance (boundary-setting, solidarity), Merton's strain theory and modes of adaptation (conformity, innovation, ritualism, retreatism, rebellion), differential association (Sutherland), control/social bond theory (Hirschi), labeling theory and primary vs. secondary deviance (Becker, Lemert), conflict perspectives on law, white-collar crime, the social construction of crime, policing, courts, incarceration, restorative justice.
- **Applied skills**: Explain why a behavior is deviant in one context and normal in another; match a scenario to the theory that best explains it; apply labeling theory's primary/secondary distinction; critique a crime statistic by asking who is counted, by whom, and how; classify a Mertonian mode of adaptation.
- **Common traps**: Treating deviance as inherently harmful or always illegal (deviance is norm-violation, not necessarily crime); confusing strain theory (blocked goals) with labeling theory (social reaction); assuming official crime rates measure crime rather than reported-and-recorded crime; missing white-collar crime because it is less policed; reading Durkheim as approving of deviance rather than analyzing its functions.

## Chapter 7: Stratification, Inequality, Race, Class, and Gender
- **Core questions**: How do societies rank people, and how stable is that ranking across generations? Is stratification a functional necessity or a product of power? How are race and gender socially constructed yet materially consequential, and why does any single category rarely tell the whole story?
- **Key concepts**: Social stratification, caste vs. class systems, income vs. wealth, life chances (Weber), social mobility (intergenerational, structural), absolute vs. relative poverty, the U.S. official poverty rate (about 11.1% in 2023) and the Supplemental Poverty Measure, Davis-Moore functionalist thesis and its critics (Tumin), Marxist conflict view of stratification, prestige, race as a social construction, ethnicity, prejudice vs. discrimination, individual vs. institutional racism, privilege, sex vs. gender, patriarchy, the gender pay gap (women earned about 85% of men's earnings in 2024, Pew), intersectionality (Crenshaw), cumulative (dis)advantage.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguish prejudice (attitude) from discrimination (action) and individual from institutional forms; compare absolute and relative poverty; read a mobility or income table without overclaiming; use intersectionality to show why one axis of inequality is incomplete; separate sex from gender.
- **Common traps**: Equating income with wealth (wealth gaps dwarf income gaps); treating "race is a social construction" as "race has no real effects"; assuming the gender pay gap is fully explained by hours or occupation; reading mobility as proof of meritocracy; using intersectionality as a slogan rather than an analytic tool that examines overlapping systems.

## Chapter 8: Institutions, Population, Social Movements, and Global Change
- **Core questions**: How do core institutions, family, education, religion, the economy, medicine, reproduce or reduce inequality? How do populations change, and what drives migration and urbanization? Why do social movements emerge when and where they do, and how does globalization reshape all of the above?
- **Key concepts**: Social institution, family forms and functions, the hidden curriculum and tracking (education), credentialism, the sick role (Parsons) and the medicalization of society, religion and Weber's Protestant ethic, capitalism vs. socialism, the gig economy and precarious work, demography, birth/death rates, total fertility rate, the demographic transition model (Stages 1-4), push/pull migration, urbanization and gentrification, collective behavior, social movements, resource mobilization theory, political opportunity structure, framing, globalization, world-systems theory (core/periphery, Wallerstein), environmental justice.
- **Applied skills**: Show how one institution reproduces or reduces inequality; place a country on the demographic transition model from its birth and death rates; distinguish collective behavior from an organized social movement; apply resource mobilization or political-opportunity framing to a movement's success; identify gentrification and its mechanisms.
- **Common traps**: Treating "the family" as one timeless natural form; confusing the hidden curriculum with tracking; assuming high birth rates mean rapid growth without checking death rates (Stage 1 vs. Stage 2); reducing social movements to grievances alone (grievances are constant; resources and opportunities vary); conflating globalization with mere trade rather than interdependence and uneven power.

## Capstone
Students complete an original sociological inquiry project on a social pattern, institution, or public issue of their choice. The project must include: (1) a clearly stated sociological question that connects biography and history; (2) a theoretical framework drawing on at least two paradigms and naming each one's strengths and limits; (3) a research design or use of a credible data source (Census, Pew, GSS, or qualitative excerpts) with explicit attention to sampling and measurement; (4) an ethical reflection on consent, confidentiality, and the researcher's own positionality; (5) an evidence summary that distinguishes correlation from causation and considers at least one alternative explanation; and (6) a public-facing product (brief, op-ed, poster, or short presentation) that explains the finding to a non-specialist audience without flattening the people behind the numbers. Assessment rewards theoretical fluency, methodological honesty, ethical awareness, and humane explanation in roughly equal measure.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short sociological imagination reflections tying a current issue or campus experience to institutions, history, and measurable patterns.
- Theory application exercises requiring functionalist, conflict, and interactionist readings of the same case, with trade-offs named.
- Methods quizzes on operationalization, sampling, validity/reliability, causal caution, and ethics.
- Data interpretation tasks using Census-style tables, GSS items, polling crosstabs, or ethnographic excerpts.
- A capstone rubric scoring theory use, evidence quality, ethical awareness, and humane explanation.
