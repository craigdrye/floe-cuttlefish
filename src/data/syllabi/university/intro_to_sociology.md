# Introduction to Sociology
**ID**: `introToSociology` · **Discipline**: Social Science · **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Philosophy
Sociology is the systematic study of human society, social structures, and social interaction. This course equips students with the "sociological imagination" to understand how broad, invisible social forces shape individual lives and behaviors.

## Module 1: The Sociological Perspective and Methods (20%)
- The Sociological Imagination (C. Wright Mills): Connecting personal troubles to public issues
- The Founders: Auguste Comte, Karl Marx, Émile Durkheim, and Max Weber
- The Three Major Theoretical Perspectives: 
  - Structural Functionalism (Durkheim, Parsons)
  - Conflict Theory (Marx, Mills)
  - Symbolic Interactionism (Mead, Goffman)
- Sociological Research Methods: Surveys, participant observation, ethnography, and secondary data analysis; distinguishing causation from correlation
- **Questions**: Analyzing a social phenomenon (like education) using all three major theoretical perspectives, explaining the concept of the sociological imagination

## Module 2: Culture, Socialization, and Interaction (20%)
- Culture: Material vs non-material culture, norms (folkways, mores, taboos), values, language (Sapir-Whorf hypothesis), ethnocentrism vs cultural relativism
- Socialization: The lifelong process of learning culture; theories of self-development (Cooley's Looking-Glass Self, Mead's "I" and "Me")
- Agents of Socialization: Family, peers, school, media
- Social Interaction in Everyday Life: Status (achieved vs ascribed), roles (role conflict, role strain), and Erving Goffman's Dramaturgical Analysis (front stage vs back stage)
- **Questions**: Identifying an instance of role strain in a real-world scenario, applying the concept of the "looking-glass self" to social media usage

## Module 3: Groups, Deviance, and Social Control (20%)
- Social Groups: Primary vs secondary groups, in-groups and out-groups, reference groups
- Formal Organizations and Bureaucracies: Max Weber's characteristics of bureaucracy, the "McDonaldization" of society
- Deviance: What is deviant? (Social construction of deviance)
- Theories of Deviance: Strain Theory (Merton), Labeling Theory, Differential Association Theory, and Conflict perspectives on crime
- Social Control and the Criminal Justice System
- **Questions**: Analyzing the "McDonaldization" of a non-food industry (e.g., healthcare), explaining how Labeling Theory can lead to a self-fulfilling prophecy

## Module 4: Social Stratification and Inequality (20%)
- Systems of Stratification: Caste vs Class systems; social mobility (intergenerational vs intragenerational)
- Theories of Stratification: The Davis-Moore thesis (Functionalist) vs Marxist perspectives
- Global Inequality: Modernization Theory vs Dependency Theory (World Systems Theory)
- Poverty in modern society: Absolute vs relative poverty, the "feminization of poverty"
- Race and Ethnicity: The social construction of race, prejudice, discrimination, systemic racism, and privilege
- Sex and Gender: Gender roles, the gender pay gap, intersectionality, and feminist theory
- **Questions**: Applying Dependency Theory to explain the economic status of a developing nation, differentiating between prejudice and systemic discrimination

## Module 5: Social Institutions and Social Change (20%)
- Family: Changing definitions of family, marriage patterns, and family as an economic unit
- Education: The hidden curriculum, tracking, and educational inequality
- Religion: Functions of religion, secularization, and fundamentalism
- The Economy and Work: Capitalism vs Socialism, the post-industrial economy, and the gig economy
- Social Change: Collective behavior, social movements (types and stages), demographic shifts, and the impact of technology/globalization
- **Questions**: Identifying the "hidden curriculum" in standard educational systems, analyzing the structural factors that give rise to specific social movements

## Stretch Zone
- Environmental Sociology: The treadmill of production and environmental justice
- Urban Sociology: Gentrification, segregation, and the spatial distribution of resources
