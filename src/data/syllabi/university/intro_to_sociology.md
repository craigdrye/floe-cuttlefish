# Introduction to Sociology
**ID**: `introToSociology` - **Discipline**: Social Science - **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Aim
Introduce students to sociology as the systematic study of social life, institutions, inequality, culture, interaction, and change. Students develop a sociological imagination: the capacity to connect personal experience with social patterns while using evidence carefully and treating people as agents embedded in histories, structures, and relationships.

## Course Design Notes
This course is built for an introductory university social science setting. Route questions here when students need sociological theory, research methods, culture, socialization, interaction, groups, deviance, stratification, race, class, gender, institutions, demography, social movements, globalization, or social change. Emphasize the difference between individual anecdotes and social evidence, the ethical responsibilities of studying communities, and the value of multiple theories for explaining the same phenomenon.

## Chapter 1: The Sociological Imagination and the Discipline
- **Key concepts**: Sociological imagination, personal troubles, public issues, social structure, agency, institutions, social facts, modernity, Auguste Comte, Karl Marx, Emile Durkheim, Max Weber, W.E.B. Du Bois, Jane Addams, Harriet Martineau, public sociology, and value-aware inquiry.
- **Practice questions**: Reframe a personal problem as a sociological question; compare two founders on inequality or social order; identify the social structures behind an everyday choice.
- **Student output**: A sociological imagination essay connecting one lived experience or current issue to institutions, history, and measurable social patterns.

## Chapter 2: Theory and Sociological Explanation
- **Key concepts**: Structural functionalism, conflict theory, symbolic interactionism, feminist theory, critical race theory, intersectionality, rationalization, bureaucracy, social construction, micro and macro analysis, manifest functions, latent functions, power, ideology, and theory-building.
- **Practice questions**: Analyze education, work, or family through three theoretical lenses; distinguish a theory from an opinion; explain how power shapes what counts as normal.
- **Student output**: A theory comparison chart that applies at least three perspectives to one social institution and names each lens's strengths and limits.

## Chapter 3: Research Methods, Data, and Ethics
- **Key concepts**: Research questions, literature review, variables, operationalization, surveys, interviews, ethnography, participant observation, experiments, comparative-historical methods, content analysis, secondary data, sampling, correlation, causation, validity, reliability, reflexivity, informed consent, confidentiality, positionality, and ASA ethical guidance.
- **Practice questions**: Choose a method for a sociological question; identify sampling bias in a poll; explain how researcher positionality can affect fieldwork.
- **Student output**: A mini research design with question, method, sample, ethics plan, possible findings, and clear limits on causal claims.

## Chapter 4: Culture, Socialization, and Identity
- **Key concepts**: Culture, material culture, nonmaterial culture, symbols, language, values, norms, folkways, mores, sanctions, ethnocentrism, cultural relativism, subculture, counterculture, socialization, agents of socialization, self, Cooley, Mead, gender socialization, racial socialization, media, and life course.
- **Practice questions**: Identify norms and sanctions in a familiar setting; apply the looking-glass self to online life; distinguish cultural explanation from stereotyping.
- **Student output**: A culture and socialization field note based on observation, with attention to norms, symbols, power, and the observer's standpoint.

## Chapter 5: Social Interaction, Groups, Organizations, and Networks
- **Key concepts**: Status, ascribed status, achieved status, master status, role, role conflict, role strain, dramaturgy, impression management, emotional labor, primary groups, secondary groups, reference groups, in-groups, out-groups, networks, social capital, organizations, bureaucracy, rationalization, McDonaldization, and institutional culture.
- **Practice questions**: Analyze an interaction using dramaturgical terms; distinguish role conflict from role strain; explain how networks can create opportunity and exclusion.
- **Student output**: An interaction or organization analysis that connects everyday behavior to roles, norms, hierarchy, and network position.

## Chapter 6: Deviance, Social Control, and Law
- **Key concepts**: Deviance, conformity, social control, stigma, moral entrepreneurship, labeling theory, strain theory, differential association, control theory, conflict perspectives on crime, policing, courts, incarceration, surveillance, restorative justice, white-collar crime, victimization, and the social construction of crime.
- **Practice questions**: Explain why deviance changes across time or place; apply labeling theory to a school or legal scenario; evaluate a crime statistic by asking who is counted and how.
- **Student output**: A deviance and social control brief that compares at least two theories and includes evidence, institutional context, and ethical concerns.

## Chapter 7: Stratification, Inequality, Race, Class, and Gender
- **Key concepts**: Stratification, social class, status, wealth, income, poverty, social mobility, life chances, caste, class systems, global inequality, race as social construction, ethnicity, racism, discrimination, prejudice, institutional racism, privilege, gender, sex, sexuality, patriarchy, gender pay gap, intersectionality, disability, and cumulative advantage.
- **Practice questions**: Distinguish prejudice from institutional discrimination; compare absolute and relative poverty; use intersectionality to explain why one category rarely tells the whole story.
- **Student output**: An inequality evidence brief using data, theory, and lived context to explain a stratification pattern without reducing people to outcomes.

## Chapter 8: Institutions, Population, Social Change, and Global Society
- **Key concepts**: Family, education, religion, work, economy, politics, health care, medicine, population, demography, migration, urbanization, environment, technology, globalization, collective behavior, social movements, resource mobilization, political opportunity, framing, backlash, reform, revolution, and environmental justice.
- **Practice questions**: Analyze how one institution reproduces or reduces inequality; interpret a demographic trend; compare theories of social movement emergence.
- **Student output**: A social change proposal or institutional analysis that identifies stakeholders, evidence, constraints, unintended consequences, and measures of success.

## Capstone
Students complete a sociological inquiry project on a social pattern, institution, or public issue. The project must include a sociological question, theory framework, research design or data source, ethical reflection, evidence summary, alternative explanations, and a public-facing product that connects biography with history without flattening individual experience.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short sociological imagination reflections tied to current issues or campus life.
- Theory application exercises that require multiple perspectives on the same case.
- Methods quizzes focused on sampling, operationalization, ethics, and causal caution.
- Data interpretation tasks using tables, graphs, census-style sources, or qualitative excerpts.
- A capstone rubric assessing theory use, evidence quality, ethical awareness, and humane explanation.
