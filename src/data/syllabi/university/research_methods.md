# Research Methods
**ID**: `researchMethods` - **Discipline**: Research - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
This course teaches students to design, evaluate, and communicate empirical research with enough rigor to avoid the failures of evidence that quietly undermine published work. The throughline is a single claim: a finding is only as trustworthy as the chain of decisions behind it. Students learn to walk that chain forward, from a vague curiosity to a sharp question, then to a defensible design, a sampling and power plan, a measurement strategy, a pre-specified analysis, an ethics review, and finally a report that states honestly what the study can and cannot support.

The course is method-agnostic by design. It treats quantitative, qualitative, and mixed-methods work as different tools for different questions rather than rival camps, and it insists that every method be judged by the same standard: does the design actually let the conclusion follow from the data? Students leave able to read a paper and locate the exact point where evidence stops and interpretation begins, to spot confounding and selection effects that a casual reader would miss, and to recognize the researcher degrees of freedom that fuel the replication crisis.

Above all, the aim is judgment, not vocabulary. Knowing the definition of internal validity is worthless if you cannot see the history threat lurking in a year-long field study. Every concept in this course is introduced as a decision a researcher must make and defend, so that by the capstone students are not reciting methods but choosing among them under real constraints of cost, ethics, and access.

## Course Design Notes
This is a seminar-lab course for students who must read research critically and produce a credible proposal or small study. Route questions here when they concern research questions, literature reviews, study design, threats to validity, sampling, statistical power, measurement, reliability and validity, choice of statistical test, qualitative analysis, research ethics and the Belmont Report, replication, preregistration, reporting guidelines such as CONSORT and STROBE, or open science. Keep the tone practical and decision-oriented: every concept should land as a move in an actual research plan, with the tradeoff made explicit. The eight chapters mirror the lifecycle of a study, and the question bank is organized along the same arc, so students can drill the exact stage where their reasoning is weakest. Distinguish carefully between correlational and causal language throughout; the most common student error is smuggling causal claims into observational designs.

## Chapter 1: Research Questions and Claims
- **Core questions**: What makes a question empirical rather than rhetorical? How do you move from a topic to a FINER-compliant question? When is exploratory work appropriate and when must you commit to a confirmatory hypothesis in advance?
- **Key concepts**: Empirical questions, theory, constructs versus variables, FINER criteria (Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, Relevant), the null and alternative hypothesis, exploratory versus confirmatory research, descriptive versus associational versus causal claims, mechanisms, scope conditions, and rival explanations.
- **Applied skills**: Turn a broad topic into a single testable question; define a construct and propose an operational definition; classify a claim as descriptive, correlational, or causal; surface the rival explanations a finding would have to defeat.
- **Common traps**: Treating a hypothesis as something to be "proven" rather than tested and potentially falsified; writing a causal question ("does X cause Y?") when the feasible design can only support association; confusing the construct (stress) with its measure (a cortisol assay); presenting an exploratory fishing expedition as if it were a preregistered confirmatory test.

## Chapter 2: Literature Review and Evidence Mapping
- **Core questions**: What has already been established, contested, or never tested? Which review type fits your goal: narrative, scoping, or systematic? How do you make screening reproducible rather than cherry-picked?
- **Key concepts**: Search strategy, Boolean search strings, databases, citation chaining (backward and forward), explicit inclusion and exclusion criteria, narrative versus scoping versus systematic reviews, PRISMA flow logic, meta-analysis, annotated bibliography, evidence gaps, and synthesis versus summary.
- **Applied skills**: Build a Boolean search string with synonyms and field tags; apply pre-stated screening rules to borderline sources; draw a PRISMA-style flow of records identified, screened, and included; identify a genuine research gap rather than a topic no one happened to study.
- **Common traps**: Confirmation bias in screening, where sources that fit the desired story get included and discordant ones quietly dropped; conflating a scoping review (map the landscape) with a systematic review (answer one narrow question definitively); citing abstracts without reading methods; mistaking "no one has studied this exact thing" for an important gap when the silence is actually a signal it does not matter.

## Chapter 3: Research Design and Validity
- **Core questions**: Which design can actually answer this question? What must be true for a causal claim to follow? What threats to internal and external validity does this design leave open?
- **Key concepts**: Experimental, quasi-experimental, observational, qualitative, and mixed-methods designs; randomization and random assignment; control and comparison groups; blinding and double-blinding; manipulation; confounding; the four big validity types (internal, external, construct, statistical conclusion); ecological validity; and the classic internal-validity threats (history, maturation, instrumentation, regression to the mean, selection, attrition).
- **Applied skills**: Match a question to the weakest design that can still answer it; diagnose which specific internal-validity threat a study leaves open; explain why random assignment supports causal inference while random sampling supports generalization; design a comparison condition that isolates the variable of interest.
- **Common traps**: Confusing random assignment (balances groups, enables causal claims) with random sampling (representative, enables generalization); reading correlation from an observational design as causation; assuming a quasi-experiment has no control group when in fact it lacks only randomization; ignoring attrition that turns a randomized trial back into a self-selected comparison; mistaking high ecological validity for high internal validity.

## Chapter 4: Sampling, Recruitment, and Power
- **Core questions**: Who is the study really about, and who will you actually reach? How many participants do you need to detect an effect worth caring about? Where will recruitment quietly distort your sample?
- **Key concepts**: Target population versus sampling frame versus achieved sample; probability sampling (simple random, stratified, cluster, systematic) versus non-probability sampling (convenience, purposive, snowball, quota); stratification; effect size; statistical power and the power equation's levers (effect size, alpha, sample size); a priori power analysis; saturation in qualitative work; recruitment and self-selection bias; attrition; and generalizability.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguish the population from the frame from the sample; choose and justify a sampling strategy under access constraints; run or read an a priori power analysis and identify which lever to move; explain how a recruitment channel biases the achieved sample; judge when qualitative saturation has plausibly been reached.
- **Common traps**: Confusing stratified sampling (sample within every subgroup) with cluster sampling (sample whole groups); believing a larger sample fixes bias when it only shrinks variance and can entrench a biased frame; running power analysis after the fact ("post hoc power") to excuse a null result; treating convenience samples as representative because they were big; conflating non-response bias with non-coverage.

## Chapter 5: Measurement and Data Collection
- **Core questions**: How do you turn an abstract construct into something observable? Is your instrument both consistent and on-target? What will you do about the data you fail to collect?
- **Key concepts**: Operationalization; the four levels of measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio); reliability (test-retest, internal consistency, inter-rater) versus the several validities (content, criterion, construct); survey and Likert-item design; interview protocols and observation; pilot testing; codebooks; missing-data mechanisms (MCAR, MAR, MNAR) and data management.
- **Applied skills**: Operationalize an abstract construct with a defensible indicator; identify the level of measurement and the statistics it permits; diagnose a measure as reliable-but-invalid or valid-but-noisy; rewrite leading, double-barreled, or loaded survey items; plan a pilot to estimate reliability before launch.
- **Common traps**: Collapsing reliability and validity, the canonical example being a scale that always reads ten pounds light (perfectly reliable, entirely invalid); treating ordinal data (class rank, Likert categories) as interval and averaging it without justification; double-barreled survey items that ask two things at once; assuming missing data are random (MCAR) when missingness is tied to the outcome (MNAR), which silently biases estimates; over-trusting a single-item measure of a rich construct.

## Chapter 6: Analysis Plans and Interpretation
- **Core questions**: Which analysis does this design and variable type call for? What does a p-value actually tell you, and what does it not? Where does the result stop supporting the conclusion?
- **Key concepts**: Descriptive statistics and measures of central tendency under skew; qualitative coding, thematic analysis, and grounded theory; t-tests, one-way ANOVA, chi-square, correlation, and regression; confidence intervals; the correct definition of a p-value; effect sizes; Type I and Type II errors; statistical power as 1 minus beta; the multiple-comparisons / family-wise error problem; robustness checks; and statistical versus practical significance.
- **Applied skills**: Select the correct test from design and variable type (e.g., three group means call for ANOVA, not three t-tests); interpret a confidence interval and an effect size rather than fixating on significance; explain a p-value precisely as the probability of data this extreme under the null; identify overclaiming in a results paragraph; choose the median over the mean under heavy skew.
- **Common traps**: Misreading a p-value as the probability the hypothesis is true, or as the effect size; running many t-tests instead of ANOVA and inflating the family-wise error rate; equating statistical significance with importance when a trivial effect reaches p < .05 in a huge sample; confusing a Type I error (false positive, reject a true null) with a Type II error (false negative); reporting "post hoc power" to interpret a null; HARKing, that is, presenting a hypothesis invented after seeing the data as if it were predicted.

## Chapter 7: Ethics, Governance, and Participant Protection
- **Core questions**: Who could this study harm, and how is that harm justified by its benefit? Has consent been genuinely informed and freely given? Are the burdens and benefits distributed fairly?
- **Key concepts**: The Belmont Report's three principles (respect for persons, beneficence, justice) and how they map to consent, risk-benefit analysis, and fair subject selection; informed consent and the right to withdraw; privacy and confidentiality versus anonymity; justified deception and debriefing; vulnerable populations; IRB review; conflicts of interest; data security and retention; and harm mitigation.
- **Applied skills**: Map an ethical concern to the correct Belmont principle; evaluate a consent form for comprehension, voluntariness, and disclosure; identify a justice violation in recruitment (e.g., risk concentrated on a vulnerable group); revise a risky protocol to reduce harm; design a debriefing that genuinely repairs deception.
- **Common traps**: Misassigning Belmont principles, especially treating fair distribution of burdens as beneficence when it is justice, or treating consent as beneficence when it is respect for persons; confusing confidentiality (you know who said it but protect it) with anonymity (you never knew); using coercive incentives and calling them compensation; assuming deception is automatically unethical rather than potentially justified with review and debriefing; collecting identifiable data with no retention or security plan.

## Chapter 8: Open Science, Reporting, and Communication
- **Core questions**: What did you decide before seeing the data, and how can a reader verify that? Which reporting guideline fits your study type? How do you communicate uncertainty without either overclaiming or false modesty?
- **Key concepts**: Preregistration and registered reports; reproducibility versus replicability; the replication crisis and its drivers (p-hacking, publication bias, the file-drawer problem, HARKing, researcher degrees of freedom); OSF and the Center for Open Science; open data and open materials; reporting guidelines (CONSORT for trials, STROBE for observational studies, PRISMA for reviews); IMRaD structure; abstracts and posters; and public communication of risk and uncertainty.
- **Applied skills**: Write a preregistration that fixes hypotheses, exclusions, and the primary analysis in advance; match a study type to its reporting guideline; decide what materials and data can ethically be shared openly; rewrite an over-strong conclusion to match the evidence; translate a technical finding into an honest public summary.
- **Common traps**: Confusing reproducibility (same data, same result) with replicability (new data, same result); treating preregistration as a guarantee of truth rather than a constraint on flexibility; calling a study a "failed replication" when it was actually underpowered; conflating publication bias (which studies get published) with p-hacking (manipulating the analysis within a study); matching the wrong guideline, e.g., applying CONSORT to an observational cohort that needs STROBE; sharing data that re-identifies participants.

## Capstone
Students produce a complete research dossier: either a publication-quality proposal or the write-up of a small empirical study they have actually run. The dossier must carry a single coherent thread from question to claim. It includes a FINER-justified research question with operationally defined constructs; an evidence map with a reproducible search and an identified gap; a design blueprint that names the validity threats it accepts and how it mitigates them; a sampling and recruitment plan with an a priori power analysis or a saturation rationale; a measurement packet with a piloted instrument and a missing-data plan; a preregistered analysis plan specifying the primary test, exclusion rules, and interpretation boundaries; an ethics review memo mapped to the Belmont principles; and an open-science and reporting package matched to the correct guideline (CONSORT, STROBE, or PRISMA). The final defense is graded above all on one thing: a clear, honest statement of what the study can and cannot claim, with the causal-versus-correlational boundary drawn explicitly.

## Assessment Ideas
- Research question workshops with peer critique against the FINER criteria.
- Literature search logs and PRISMA-style evidence maps with documented screening rules.
- Design-diagnosis exercises on flawed published or fictional studies, naming the specific validity threat.
- Measurement pilots revised on the basis of reliability, validity, and respondent burden.
- Analysis-plan defenses where students justify the chosen test, exclusions, and interpretation limits.
- Belmont-principle mapping drills on real and constructed ethics scenarios.
- Final proposal review scored for coherence across question, design, measurement, ethics, analysis, and communication.
