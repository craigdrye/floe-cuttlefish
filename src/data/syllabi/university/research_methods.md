# Research Methods
**ID**: `researchMethods` · **Discipline**: Research · **Year**: University Senior

## Course Philosophy
This course provides a rigorous foundation in empirical research design, emphasizing the scientific method, statistical validity, and ethical considerations. It bridges the gap between consuming research and producing high-quality original studies across the physical and social sciences.

## Module 1: The Foundations of Research (20%)
- The Scientific Method and Epistemology (What constitutes evidence?)
- Developing research questions: FINER criteria (Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, Relevant)
- Literature Reviews: Systematic vs scoping reviews, PRISMA guidelines, identifying gaps in existing research
- Formulating Hypotheses: Null ($H_0$) vs Alternative ($H_1$) hypotheses
- Identifying Variables: Independent, Dependent, Confounding, and Extraneous variables
- **Questions**: Defining a testable hypothesis for a vague research question, distinguishing between confounding and extraneous variables

## Module 2: Experimental and Observational Design (25%)
- Quantitative vs Qualitative vs Mixed-Methods designs
- True Experimental Design: Randomization, control groups, and manipulation (e.g., Randomized Controlled Trials - RCTs)
- Quasi-Experimental Design: Non-randomized interventions (e.g., pre-test/post-test, regression discontinuity)
- Observational Design: Cohort studies (prospective/retrospective), Case-Control studies, Cross-sectional studies
- Internal Validity: Threats (history, maturation, selection bias, attrition, instrumentation, testing effects)
- External Validity: Generalizability and ecological validity
- **Questions**: Choosing the most appropriate study design for a specific public health question, identifying the threat to internal validity in a flawed study

## Module 3: Sampling, Measurement, and Data Collection (20%)
- Sampling Methods: Probability (Simple random, stratified, cluster, systematic) vs Non-Probability (Convenience, snowball, purposive)
- Sample size determination and statistical power ($\beta$)
- Measurement Theory: Constructs, operationalization, and levels of measurement (Nominal, Ordinal, Interval, Ratio)
- Reliability (Test-retest, inter-rater, internal consistency/Cronbach's alpha) vs Validity (Construct, content, face, criterion)
- Data Collection: Surveys (Likert scales, avoiding leading questions), interviews (structured/semi-structured), and physiological measures
- **Questions**: Calculating the required sample size for an 80% powered study, distinguishing between construct validity and reliability

## Module 4: Statistical Analysis and Interpretation (20%)
- Descriptive statistics: Mean, median, standard deviation, variance, skewness
- Inferential statistics: p-values, confidence intervals, Type I ($\alpha$) and Type II ($\beta$) errors
- Choosing the right test: T-tests, ANOVA, Chi-Square, Pearson Correlation, Linear/Multiple Regression
- Interpreting effect sizes (e.g., Cohen's d) vs statistical significance
- The replication crisis, p-hacking, HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results are Known), and publication bias
- **Questions**: Selecting the appropriate statistical test for continuous vs categorical variables, explaining why a p-value of 0.04 does not prove a hypothesis is true

## Module 5: Research Ethics and Communication (15%)
- History of research ethics: The Nuremberg Code, the Declaration of Helsinki, the Belmont Report (Respect for persons, Beneficence, Justice)
- The role of the Institutional Review Board (IRB)
- Informed consent, deception in research, and debriefing
- Scientific Communication: Writing an IMRaD paper (Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion), peer-review process, open science, and pre-registration
- **Questions**: Evaluating a research proposal for Belmont Report compliance, structuring a compelling Discussion section

## Stretch Zone
- Advanced causal inference techniques (Instrumental Variables, Difference-in-Differences)
- Qualitative coding (Thematic analysis, Grounded Theory)
