# Research Methods
**ID**: `researchMethods` - **Discipline**: Research - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
Prepare students to design, evaluate, and communicate empirical research with enough rigor to avoid common failures of evidence. Students learn to move from a question to a defensible design, sampling plan, measurement strategy, analysis plan, ethics review, open-science workflow, and final report.

## Course Design Notes
This is a seminar-lab course for students who must read research critically and produce a credible proposal or small study. Route questions here when they ask about research questions, literature reviews, study design, sampling, measurement, analysis choice, research ethics, replication, preregistration, reporting guidelines, or open science. Keep the tone practical: every concept should become a decision in a research plan.

## Chapter 1: Research Questions and Claims
- **Key concepts**: Empirical questions, theory, constructs, variables, FINER criteria, hypotheses, exploratory versus confirmatory research, causal and descriptive claims, mechanisms, scope conditions, and rival explanations.
- **Practice questions**: Turn a broad topic into a testable question; distinguish a descriptive claim from a causal claim; identify assumptions behind a hypothesis.
- **Student output**: A question-framing memo with research question, construct definitions, hypothesis or exploratory aim, rival explanations, and feasibility constraints.

## Chapter 2: Literature Review and Evidence Mapping
- **Key concepts**: Search strategy, databases, keywords, citation chaining, inclusion and exclusion criteria, systematic review, scoping review, PRISMA flow logic, annotated bibliography, evidence gaps, and synthesis.
- **Practice questions**: Build a search string; decide whether a source should be included; compare narrative, scoping, and systematic review purposes.
- **Student output**: An evidence map with search terms, screening rules, source table, major findings, disagreements, and an identified research gap.

## Chapter 3: Research Design and Validity
- **Key concepts**: Experimental, quasi-experimental, observational, qualitative, and mixed-methods designs; randomization; control groups; blinding; manipulation; confounding; internal validity; external validity; ecological validity; and design tradeoffs.
- **Practice questions**: Match a question to a design; diagnose threats to internal validity; explain what a design can and cannot support.
- **Student output**: A design blueprint that justifies method choice, comparison groups, procedures, validity threats, and mitigation plans.

## Chapter 4: Sampling, Recruitment, and Power
- **Key concepts**: Populations, sampling frames, probability sampling, convenience sampling, purposive sampling, snowball sampling, stratification, sample size, statistical power, saturation, recruitment bias, attrition, and generalizability.
- **Practice questions**: Identify the target population; choose a sampling strategy; explain how recruitment can distort findings.
- **Student output**: A sampling and recruitment plan with eligibility criteria, recruitment channels, target sample size rationale, and bias risks.

## Chapter 5: Measurement and Data Collection
- **Key concepts**: Operationalization, levels of measurement, reliability, validity, survey design, Likert items, interview protocols, observation, instruments, pilot testing, inter-rater reliability, data management, and missing data planning.
- **Practice questions**: Rewrite leading survey items; distinguish reliability from validity; choose a measurement strategy for an abstract construct.
- **Student output**: A measurement packet with instrument draft, codebook or interview guide, pilot-test plan, and data quality checks.

## Chapter 6: Analysis Plans and Interpretation
- **Key concepts**: Descriptive statistics, qualitative coding, thematic analysis, t-tests, ANOVA, chi-square tests, correlation, regression, confidence intervals, p-values, effect sizes, Type I and Type II errors, multiple testing, robustness checks, and practical significance.
- **Practice questions**: Select an analysis for a design and variable type; interpret confidence intervals and effect sizes; identify overclaiming in a results paragraph.
- **Student output**: A pre-analysis plan with variables, exclusion rules, primary analysis, secondary analysis, qualitative coding approach if relevant, and interpretation boundaries.

## Chapter 7: Ethics, Governance, and Participant Protection
- **Key concepts**: Belmont Report principles, respect for persons, beneficence, justice, informed consent, privacy, confidentiality, deception, debriefing, vulnerable populations, IRB review, conflicts of interest, data security, and harm mitigation.
- **Practice questions**: Evaluate a consent form; identify justice concerns in recruitment; revise a risky protocol to reduce participant harm.
- **Student output**: An ethics review memo with consent language, risk-benefit analysis, privacy plan, data retention plan, and equity concerns.

## Chapter 8: Open Science, Reporting, and Communication
- **Key concepts**: Preregistration, registered reports, replication, reproducibility, OSF, Center for Open Science, open data, open materials, reporting guidelines, CONSORT, STROBE, transparent limitations, IMRaD structure, abstracts, posters, and public communication.
- **Practice questions**: Decide what can be shared openly; match a study type to a reporting guideline; rewrite an overstrong conclusion.
- **Student output**: A communication package with preregistration outline, reproducibility checklist, reporting guideline checklist, technical abstract, and public summary.

## Capstone
Students produce a complete research proposal or small empirical study dossier. It must include question framing, literature review plan, design justification, sampling and recruitment plan, measurement tools, analysis plan, ethics materials, open-science plan, reporting checklist, and a final presentation that states what the study can and cannot claim.

## Assessment Ideas
- Research question workshops with peer critique.
- Literature search logs and evidence maps.
- Design diagnosis exercises using flawed published or fictional studies.
- Measurement pilots with revisions based on reliability, validity, and respondent burden.
- Analysis-plan defenses where students justify tests, exclusions, and interpretation limits.
- Final proposal review scored for coherence across question, design, measurement, ethics, analysis, and communication.
