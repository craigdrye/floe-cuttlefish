# AP Environmental Science
**ID**: `apEnvironmentalScience` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: University Sophomore (equivalent)

## Course Aim
Use science to understand environmental systems, diagnose human impacts, and evaluate realistic solutions. Students should be able to connect ecosystems, Earth systems, populations, land and water use, energy, pollution, and global change through data, models, field-style reasoning, and AP-style explanations.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test AP Environmental Science ecosystems, biodiversity, populations, Earth systems, land and water use, energy resources, air and water pollution, waste, global change, environmental policy, data analysis, calculations, or free-response argumentation. Keep the course interdisciplinary: ecology, chemistry, geography, economics, politics, and ethics all show up, but claims must stay evidence-based.

## Chapter 1: Ecosystems and Energy Flow
- **Key concepts**: Ecosystem structure, biomes, food chains, food webs, trophic levels, energy pyramids, productivity, ecological efficiency, and ecosystem services.
- **Practice questions**: Trace energy through a food web; calculate trophic energy transfer; compare terrestrial and aquatic ecosystem features.
- **Student output**: An ecosystem energy-flow diagram with one disturbance and predicted effects.

## Chapter 2: Biodiversity, Cycles, and Natural Change
- **Key concepts**: Genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity; island biogeography; ecological tolerance; ecological succession; natural disruptions; carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water cycles.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret succession diagrams; trace nutrients through cycles; explain how biodiversity affects resilience.
- **Student output**: A biodiversity case brief linking evidence, ecosystem function, and conservation tradeoffs.

## Chapter 3: Populations and Human Demography
- **Key concepts**: Population growth, carrying capacity, growth rates, age-structure diagrams, survivorship curves, generalist and specialist strategies, demographic transition, fertility, mortality, and human population impacts.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret population graphs; calculate growth or doubling time; analyze demographic transition scenarios.
- **Student output**: A population data memo with graph interpretation and policy implications.

## Chapter 4: Earth Systems, Atmosphere, and Climate Foundations
- **Key concepts**: Plate tectonics, soil formation, soil properties, weathering, erosion, atmosphere layers, global wind patterns, ocean currents, ENSO, watersheds, solar radiation, seasons, rain shadows, and climate drivers.
- **Practice questions**: Explain soil or atmospheric processes; read climate and watershed diagrams; connect geography to climate patterns.
- **Student output**: An Earth-systems model showing interactions among soil, water, air, and life.

## Chapter 5: Land and Water Use
- **Key concepts**: Agriculture, irrigation, pest control, soil degradation, salinization, desertification, forestry, rangelands, mining, urbanization, overfishing, ecological footprints, tragedy of the commons, and sustainable yield.
- **Practice questions**: Evaluate land-use tradeoffs; calculate ecological footprint or yield; connect agricultural practices to pollution and biodiversity effects.
- **Student output**: A land- or water-use decision memo comparing environmental costs, benefits, and alternatives.

## Chapter 6: Energy Resources and Consumption
- **Key concepts**: Energy units, efficiency, global consumption patterns, fossil fuels, extraction impacts, nuclear fission, renewable energy sources, grid constraints, conservation, and energy policy tradeoffs.
- **Practice questions**: Calculate energy efficiency; compare fuels and renewables; analyze environmental consequences of extraction and consumption.
- **Student output**: An energy portfolio recommendation with calculations and tradeoff analysis.

## Chapter 7: Pollution, Waste, and Human Health
- **Key concepts**: Air pollutants, photochemical smog, thermal inversions, acid deposition, indoor air pollution, water pollution, eutrophication, wastewater treatment, solid waste, hazardous waste, toxicology, bioaccumulation, biomagnification, and risk.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret pollution data; explain mechanisms such as acid deposition or eutrophication; evaluate remediation options.
- **Student output**: A pollution case analysis with source, pathway, exposure, impact, and intervention.

## Chapter 8: Global Change and AP Environmental Science Studio
- **Key concepts**: Greenhouse effect, climate change evidence, ocean warming, ocean acidification, sea-level rise, stratospheric ozone depletion, invasive species, endangered species, conservation strategies, policy tools, AP calculations, and free-response argumentation.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret climate and global-change data; explain ozone depletion and greenhouse mechanisms; write AP-style solutions with evidence and calculations.
- **Student output**: A timed APES free-response portfolio plus one environmental solution proposal.

## Capstone
Students produce an environmental systems case study: define a problem, map the systems involved, analyze data, compare at least two interventions, include one calculation, and explain the tradeoffs.

## Assessment Ideas
- Data and graph interpretation checks using environmental datasets.
- Calculation drills for energy, population, pollution, and ecological metrics.
- Free-response practice scored for claim, evidence, calculation setup, and tradeoff reasoning.
- Field-style observation or local case study logs.
- A final solution memo that separates evidence from values and uncertainty.
