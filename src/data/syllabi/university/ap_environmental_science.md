# AP Environmental Science
**ID**: `apEnvironmentalScience` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: University Sophomore (equivalent)

## Course Philosophy
AP Environmental Science is an interdisciplinary course that applies scientific principles, concepts, and methodologies to understand the interrelationships of the natural world, identify and analyze environmental problems, and examine solutions for resolving/preventing them.

## Module 1: The Living World: Ecosystems and Biodiversity (20%)
- Ecosystem structure: Terrestrial and aquatic biomes
- Energy flow: Food chains, food webs, trophic levels, and the 10% rule
- Biogeochemical cycles: Carbon, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Water cycles
- Biodiversity: Genetic, species, and habitat diversity; ecosystem services
- Island biogeography and ecological tolerance
- Natural disruptions to ecosystems and ecological succession (primary vs secondary)
- **Questions**: Tracing the path of nitrogen through the cycle, evaluating the impact of an invasive species on a food web

## Module 2: Populations and Earth Systems (20%)
- Population dynamics: Generalist vs specialist species, K-selected vs r-selected species, survivorship curves
- Carrying capacity, population growth rates, and age structure diagrams
- Human population dynamics: Demographic Transition Model (DTM)
- Earth systems: Plate tectonics, soil formation/composition, and soil erosion
- Earth's atmosphere: Composition, structure, global wind patterns, and the Coriolis effect
- Watersheds, solar radiation, Earth's seasons, and geography/climate (rain shadows, El Niño/La Niña)
- **Questions**: Interpreting a DTM graph, explaining the cause of the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO)

## Module 3: Land and Water Use (20%)
- The Tragedy of the Commons
- Agriculture: Green Revolution, irrigation methods, pest control methods, meat production methods, overfishing
- Impacts of agricultural practices (salinization, desertification, runoff/eutrophication)
- Forestry (clearcutting) and Rangeland management
- Mining methods and impacts; Urbanization and ecological footprints
- Sustainability and sustainable yield
- **Questions**: Calculating an ecological footprint, evaluating the environmental tradeoffs of CAFOs (feedlots)

## Module 4: Energy Resources and Consumption (20%)
- Energy concepts and global energy consumption
- Fossil fuels: Formation, extraction (fracking, mining), and use
- Nuclear power: Fission process, radioactive waste, and major accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima)
- Renewable energy: Biomass, solar (active vs passive), hydroelectric, geothermal, hydrogen fuel cells, wind
- Energy conservation and efficiency
- **Questions**: Calculating energy efficiency, comparing the environmental impacts of coal vs natural gas

## Module 5: Pollution and Global Change (20%)
- Air pollution: Photochemical smog, thermal inversion, indoor air pollution, acid rain, noise pollution
- Water pollution: Point vs nonpoint sources, human impacts on wetlands/mangroves, eutrophication, thermal pollution, persistent organic pollutants (POPs), bioaccumulation and biomagnification
- Solid waste disposal, waste reduction methods, and sewage treatment
- Global change: Stratospheric ozone depletion (CFCs), the Greenhouse Effect, increases in greenhouse gases
- Climate change impacts: Ocean warming, ocean acidification, sea-level rise, habitat loss
- Endangered species and conservation strategies (HIPPCO, CITES, Endangered Species Act)
- **Questions**: Describing the chemical reaction that causes ozone depletion, explaining the process of biomagnification in a marine food web

## Stretch Zone
- Advanced environmental legislation and policy analysis
- Designing comprehensive mitigation strategies for urban heat islands
