# College Algebra
**ID**: `collegeAlgebra` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19, US track)
**Aligns with**: typical "MATH 110 / MATH 1314 / MAT 121" College Algebra; Khan Academy "College algebra"; CLEP College Algebra exam (approx. 60 questions in 90 minutes, weighted roughly 20% number systems, 25% equations and inequalities, 30% functions, 25% other topics)

## Course Aim
This course builds a durable, practice-forward command of algebra as the working language of functions, equations, inequalities, and quantitative models. The central goal is fluency that survives pressure: a student who finishes should be able to look at an unfamiliar expression or word problem, name the structure it belongs to, choose a method on purpose rather than by reflex, execute the algebra without arithmetic leaks, and then check the result against the original question. We treat algebra less as a bag of tricks and more as a small set of reusable moves (isolate, factor, substitute, transform, invert, model) applied across many surface forms.

The course is deliberately gateway-oriented. Roughly a quarter of incoming students arrive with fragile habits formed in earlier courses, such as distributing exponents over sums, dropping domain restrictions, or treating "cancel" as a license to delete terms. We name those habits explicitly and repair them, because they are the failure points that later sink precalculus, calculus, statistics, economics, and the lab sciences. Every chapter therefore pairs forward content with a short diagnosis of the predictable error it tends to invite.

By the end, a student should be able to move fluidly among the four representations of a function (symbolic, graphical, numerical, verbal), distinguish exact answers from approximations and know when each is appropriate, justify domain choices, and translate a real scenario into a model whose limits they can state out loud. That combination of symbolic skill plus modeling judgment is exactly what the next mathematics, science, and business courses assume on day one.

## Course Design Notes
This is a university gateway mathematics course with weekly problem sets, short timed skills quizzes, graphing-technology checks (Desmos, GeoGebra, or a graphing calculator), and explicit repair of fragile algebra habits. Route a question here when the underlying skill is real numbers, exponents, radicals, factoring, rational expressions, equations, inequalities, functions, transformations, inverse functions, polynomial functions, rational functions, exponential functions, logarithmic functions, systems, matrices, conics, sequences, or algebraic modeling. Do not route trigonometric identities, limits, or derivatives here; those belong to precalculus and calculus, though we point toward them in the final chapter.

Throughout, emphasize five habits that distinguish strong students: clean notation (an equals sign means equality, an arrow means "leads to"); explicit domain restrictions stated before simplifying, not after; the deliberate choice between exact form (such as 5√2 or ln 7) and a decimal approximation; the graph–symbol–table triangle, where any claim made in one representation is verified in another; and a final reasonableness check against the original problem, including units. Questions should reward method selection, not just computation: a recurring prompt is "name the rule before you use it, and say why a tempting alternative does not apply." Distractors in the question bank are built from the documented Common traps below, so wrong answers diagnose a specific misconception rather than acting as random noise.

## Chapter 1: Algebra Fluency and Real Numbers
- **Core questions**: What structure is this expression, and which algebraic property lets me rewrite it? Why does a particular "shortcut" produce a false statement? When is a simplification only valid on a restricted domain?
- **Key concepts**: Real-number subsets (natural, integer, rational, irrational, real), interval and set-builder notation, absolute value as distance, the field and order properties, order of operations, integer and rational exponents, radicals and the n-th root, polynomial arithmetic, special products, factoring by GCF, grouping, trinomials, difference of squares and cubes, rational expressions, and complex fractions.
- **Applied skills**: Simplify expressions with rational exponents and convert between radical and exponent form; factor a polynomial by grouping or substitution; add, subtract, multiply, and divide rational expressions over a common denominator; reduce a complex fraction; state the restricted values that the simplified form silently assumes.
- **Common traps**: Distributing an exponent over a sum, e.g. writing (a + b)^2 = a^2 + b^2; "cancelling" terms across a sum rather than factors of a product, e.g. (x + 3)/3 = x; losing the negative sign when distributing a leading minus; treating √(a^2) as a rather than |a|; and simplifying a rational expression without recording the excluded values.

## Chapter 2: Equations, Inequalities, and Applied Problem Setup
- **Core questions**: What kind of equation is this, and does the method I chose risk introducing or destroying solutions? Which solutions are real, which are extraneous, and how do I tell? How do I turn a sentence into a checkable model?
- **Key concepts**: Linear equations, literal equations and formula rearrangement, linear and compound inequalities, absolute-value equations and inequalities, quadratic equations by all methods, radical equations, rational equations, extraneous solutions, and applications involving rate, mixture, work, distance, percent change, and simple interest with explicit units.
- **Applied skills**: Solve and express a compound inequality in interval notation; isolate a chosen variable in a multivariable formula; solve a radical equation and test each candidate against the original; translate a rate, mixture, or work scenario into a single equation with labeled variables and units.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting to flip the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative; squaring both sides of a radical equation and then skipping the check, so an extraneous root sneaks in; clearing fractions in a rational equation without excluding values that zero a denominator; reading "a or b" (union) as "a and b" (intersection) for absolute-value inequalities; and mislabeling which quantity is the unknown in a word problem.

## Chapter 3: Functions, Graphs, and Transformations
- **Core questions**: Is this relation a function, and on what domain? How does a change to the formula move, stretch, or reflect the graph? When can a function be inverted, and what does the inverse undo?
- **Key concepts**: Coordinate plane, distance and midpoint formulas, equation of a circle, relations versus functions, domain and range, function notation, vertical and horizontal line tests, increasing/decreasing intervals, local and global extrema, even and odd symmetry, piecewise functions, parent functions, rigid and non-rigid transformations, the algebra of functions, composition, and inverse functions.
- **Applied skills**: Determine the domain of a rational or radical function from its restrictions; sketch a parent function after a sequence of shifts, stretches, and reflections in the correct order; compute and interpret a composition such as f(g(x)); decide whether a function is one-to-one and, if so, find and verify its inverse.
- **Common traps**: Reading the horizontal shift backward, e.g. thinking y = (x - 4)^2 moves left; applying a vertical stretch and a vertical shift in the wrong order; assuming every function has an inverse rather than checking one-to-one; confusing f^{-1}(x) with 1/f(x); and forgetting that the domain of f ∘ g is constrained by both g and the composite.

## Chapter 4: Linear and Quadratic Functions
- **Core questions**: What does the slope mean in this context, and what does the vertex tell me? Which solution method for a quadratic is fastest and safest here? When are the roots complex, and what does that imply about the graph?
- **Key concepts**: Slope as rate of change, intercepts, point-slope and slope-intercept and standard forms, parallel and perpendicular lines, linear models from data, quadratic functions in standard and vertex form, completing the square, the discriminant, the quadratic formula, complex numbers, and optimization through maxima and minima (projectile height, revenue, area).
- **Applied skills**: Build and interpret a linear model from two points or a data table, including units on the slope; convert a quadratic to vertex form by completing the square; solve a quadratic by factoring, completing the square, and the formula, and pick the most efficient route; read the vertex as the maximum or minimum in an applied optimization problem.
- **Common traps**: Using the reciprocal instead of the negative reciprocal for a perpendicular slope; sign errors in -b/(2a) for the vertex; forgetting the ± in the quadratic formula and reporting only one root; concluding "no solutions" when a negative discriminant in fact yields complex solutions; and confusing the vertex's x-value with the maximum value itself.

## Chapter 5: Polynomial Functions and Polynomial Equations
- **Core questions**: What does the degree and leading coefficient predict about end behavior? Given one zero, how do I find the rest? How does multiplicity show up in the graph and in a sign chart?
- **Key concepts**: Degree, leading coefficient, end behavior, zeros and their multiplicity, turning points, polynomial long division, synthetic division, the Remainder and Factor Theorems, the Rational Zero Theorem, Descartes' Rule of Signs, complex conjugate root pairs, the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, and polynomial inequalities.
- **Applied skills**: Predict end behavior from degree and leading sign; divide polynomials by long or synthetic division and interpret the remainder; find all zeros (real and complex) given one factor; build a sign chart and solve a polynomial inequality; sketch a graph whose intercepts, multiplicities, and end behavior all agree.
- **Common traps**: Assuming the number of real zeros always equals the degree (it counts complex zeros and multiplicity); treating a double root as two distinct x-intercepts; flipping a sign during synthetic division; forgetting that complex roots of a real polynomial come in conjugate pairs; and reading a polynomial inequality's solution off the wrong intervals of the sign chart.

## Chapter 6: Rational Functions and Rational Inequalities
- **Core questions**: Where does this function break, and is the break a hole or an asymptote? How do the degrees of numerator and denominator decide the horizontal or slant asymptote? Why can a rational inequality's solution exclude its boundary points?
- **Key concepts**: Domain of a rational function, removable discontinuities (holes), vertical asymptotes, horizontal and slant asymptotes determined by degree comparison, intercepts, sign charts, graph sketching, rational equations, rational inequalities, and direct, inverse, and joint variation models.
- **Applied skills**: Identify every discontinuity and asymptote and classify each; solve a rational equation while excluding restricted values; solve a rational inequality with test intervals, being careful at zeros of both numerator and denominator; set up and solve an inverse-variation model.
- **Common traps**: Calling every zero of the denominator a vertical asymptote when a shared factor produces a removable hole; concluding there is no horizontal asymptote when the numerator's degree merely equals the denominator's (the asymptote is the ratio of leading coefficients); multiplying a rational inequality through by a variable expression whose sign is unknown; and including denominator zeros in the solution set of an inequality.

## Chapter 7: Exponential and Logarithmic Functions
- **Core questions**: What makes growth exponential rather than linear, and how do I read its rate? How does a logarithm undo an exponential, and which log law applies? How do I solve for an exponent, and where does the domain restrict the answer?
- **Key concepts**: Exponential growth and decay, transformations of exponential graphs, the base e, compound and continuous interest, logarithms as inverses, common and natural logarithms, the product, quotient, and power laws, the change-of-base formula, exponential and logarithmic equations, and applied models including half-life, doubling time, pH, decibels, and introductory logistic growth.
- **Applied skills**: Solve an exponential equation by taking a logarithm of both sides; expand and condense logarithmic expressions using the laws; solve a logarithmic equation and reject solutions that fall outside the domain; set up and interpret a half-life, compound-interest, or continuous-growth model and identify each parameter.
- **Common traps**: Writing log(a + b) = log a + log b (the laws apply to products and quotients, never sums); confusing ln and log_10 when a problem demands a specific base; forgetting that the argument of a logarithm must be positive, so a "solution" can be extraneous; mishandling the change-of-base direction; and treating continuous growth A = Pe^{rt} as if it were the periodic-compounding formula.

## Chapter 8: Systems, Matrices, and Inequalities in Two Variables
- **Core questions**: Which solution method fits this system, and does the system have one, none, or infinitely many solutions? How do row operations encode elimination? What region satisfies a system of inequalities, and where is an objective optimized?
- **Key concepts**: Systems of linear equations in two and three variables, substitution and elimination, Gaussian elimination with augmented matrices and row operations, determinants and Cramer's Rule, nonlinear systems, systems of inequalities, feasible regions, and the vertex principle behind introductory linear programming.
- **Applied skills**: Solve a three-variable system by elimination and recognize a dependent or inconsistent outcome; reduce an augmented matrix to row-echelon form; evaluate a 2×2 or 3×3 determinant and apply Cramer's Rule; graph a system of inequalities and shade the feasible region; optimize a linear objective by testing the corner points.
- **Common traps**: Declaring a system "no solution" when row reduction actually yields infinitely many (a row of zeros equal to zero); arithmetic sign slips during row operations that silently break the solution; applying Cramer's Rule when the coefficient determinant is zero; shading the wrong side of a boundary line; and testing interior points instead of vertices when optimizing.

## Chapter 9: Conics, Sequences, Series, and Precalculus Readiness
- **Core questions**: How do I recognize a conic from its equation and put it in standard form? Is this list arithmetic or geometric, and what is its sum? Which algebra tools am I about to lean on in calculus, and are they solid?
- **Key concepts**: Conic sections (parabola, circle, ellipse, hyperbola) and their standard forms, completing the square to classify a conic, arithmetic and geometric sequences, finite and infinite geometric series, sigma notation, the Binomial Theorem and Pascal's triangle, and the explicit links from algebra to function composition, inverse functions, trigonometric setup, limits, rates of change, and optimization.
- **Applied skills**: Complete the square to identify and graph a conic; find the n-th term and the partial sum of an arithmetic or geometric sequence; evaluate a finite series in sigma notation; expand a binomial power with the Binomial Theorem; map specific course skills to the precalculus and calculus topics that will assume them.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the ellipse and hyperbola standard forms (sum versus difference of squared terms); using the arithmetic-series formula on a geometric sequence; applying the infinite-geometric-sum formula when |r| ≥ 1, where it diverges; off-by-one errors in sigma notation bounds; and miscounting terms by confusing a_1 with a_0 in sequence formulas.

## Capstone
Students complete a function-modeling project that draws on at least three of the major tool families in the course: equations and inequalities, function transformations, polynomial or rational analysis, exponential or logarithmic modeling, systems, matrices, conics, or sequences. A complete submission must (1) define every variable with units, (2) justify the domain on physical or contextual grounds, (3) solve symbolically wherever an exact answer is possible and state where approximation is necessary, (4) validate results by cross-checking a graph, a table, and the algebra, and (5) explain the model's limits, including where it stops being realistic. Strong topics include tuition or population growth, medication half-life and dosing, business break-even and pricing comparison, asset depreciation, projectile motion, and supply-and-demand or mixture planning. The deliverable is a short written report plus a defended graph; the grade rewards correct modeling judgment as heavily as correct algebra.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written problem sets graded for notation, method selection, checking, graph interpretation, and clarity of communication.
- Short timed skills quizzes on factoring, exponents, equations, inequalities, function notation, transformations, logarithm laws, and systems, designed to build speed without sacrificing accuracy.
- Technology checks using Desmos, GeoGebra, a graphing calculator, or a CAS to confirm that symbolic, tabular, and graphical answers agree.
- "Name the method" cumulative tasks where students must state which technique applies and why a tempting alternative does not, before computing anything.
- A capstone rubric covering model setup, algebraic correctness, domain and unit reasoning, multi-representation validation, interpretation, and readiness for the next mathematics course.
