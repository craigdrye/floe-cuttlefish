# Financial Accounting
**ID**: `financialAccounting` | **Discipline**: Business | **Year**: University Freshman / Sophomore

## Course Aim
Financial Accounting teaches you to read, build, and reason about the financial statements that companies publish to outsiders. The central skill is translation: turning messy real-world events — a sale on credit, a machine that wears out, a customer prepayment, a bond issued below face value — into the disciplined language of debits, credits, and accruals, and then turning those records back into a story an investor or lender can act on. Underneath every entry sits one identity, Assets = Liabilities + Equity, and one promise: that the numbers faithfully represent economic reality. This course takes that promise seriously, because financial reporting is a public trust as much as a technique.

The course moves in three arcs. First, the accounting cycle: how a transaction becomes a journal entry, a ledger balance, a trial balance, an adjusted set of accruals, and finally four linked statements. Second, the hard judgment areas where U.S. GAAP forces choices — inventory cost flow, receivables and bad-debt estimates, depreciation methods, intangibles, and long-term debt measured under the effective-interest method. Third, interpretation: ratio and cash-flow analysis that lets a user judge liquidity, solvency, profitability, and the quality of reported earnings. Throughout, modern standards (ASC 606 revenue recognition, ASC 842 leases) and the conceptual framework's qualitative characteristics anchor the mechanics in current practice.

By the end you should be able to: complete a full accounting cycle by hand or in a spreadsheet; explain how any transaction ripples across all four statements; apply accrual adjustments and closing entries; evaluate internal controls and fraud risk; compute and amortize depreciation, bad debts, and bond discounts; calculate and interpret the standard ratio set; and write a defensible decision memo that names the estimates, policies, and control risks a reader should distrust before relying on the numbers.

## Course Design Notes
- The accounting equation is the spine of the course; every new topic is reintroduced as "where does this land in Assets = Liabilities + Equity, and which statements move?"
- Mechanics and judgment alternate deliberately. Students drill journal entries and trial balances until they are automatic, then immediately confront the estimates and policy choices that make two honest accountants report different numbers.
- Use short company cases, real 10-K excerpts, footnote reading, and spreadsheet labs so students see that statements are produced under uncertainty, not handed down as fact.
- Ethics, internal control, materiality, estimation, and user decision needs are recurring threads, surfaced inside technical chapters rather than quarantined in a single ethics week.
- Standards are taught at the conceptual level appropriate to an introductory course (ASC 606's five-step model, ASC 842's right-of-use asset, the LCNRV write-down rule) without drowning students in codification detail.
- Distinguish relentlessly between cash and accrual, between an expense and a cash payment, between net income and operating cash flow — the three confusions that cause most beginner errors.

## Chapter 1: Accounting, Business, and the Reporting Environment
### Core questions
- Who actually uses financial statements, and what decision does each user make with them?
- What does the conceptual framework demand of "decision-useful" information, and where do those qualities trade off?
- When can reporting be technically correct yet still mislead?

### Key concepts
- Accounting as an information system serving investors, creditors, managers, regulators, and the public
- Forms of organization (sole proprietorship, partnership, corporation) and how ownership shapes reporting and liability
- Financial vs. managerial accounting; external vs. internal users
- The conceptual framework: relevance and faithful representation as fundamental qualities; comparability, verifiability, timeliness, understandability as enhancing qualities; materiality and going concern
- The standard-setting and oversight ecosystem: FASB (GAAP), the SEC, the PCAOB, external auditors, and management's primary responsibility for the statements
- The accounting equation, Assets = Liabilities + Equity, as the structure of the balance sheet

### Applied skills
- Build a stakeholder map for a real public company and tie three concrete decisions to specific statement lines
- Classify a list of information needs as relevant, faithfully represented, or both, and explain a relevance/reliability trade-off
- Rearrange the accounting equation to solve for any missing element

### Common traps
- Treating "faithful representation" as "the truth" — it means complete, neutral, and free from error given the estimates used, not certain
- Assuming auditors prepare the statements; management does, auditors opine on them
- Confusing GAAP (the rules, set by FASB) with the SEC (the regulator that requires public companies to follow them)
- Reading materiality as a fixed dollar threshold rather than "would it change a reasonable user's decision?"

## Chapter 2: The Four Statements and Transaction Effects
### Core questions
- How do the income statement, statement of retained earnings, balance sheet, and statement of cash flows link into one articulated system?
- Why does a single transaction often move more than one statement?
- Which items belong on the income statement and which bypass it entirely?

### Key concepts
- The four statements, their headings ("for the period" vs. "as of a date"), and how net income flows into retained earnings and retained earnings into the balance sheet
- Elements: assets, liabilities, equity, revenues, expenses, gains, losses, contributed capital, dividends, retained earnings
- Why issuing stock, borrowing, and buying assets do not touch the income statement
- Current vs. noncurrent classification and the operating cycle
- Reading the face of a 10-K's statements and the articulation between them

### Applied skills
- Trace a set of first-month events through all four statements and confirm they articulate
- Identify, for a given transaction, which statements change and in which direction
- Reconstruct one missing statement figure from the other three using articulation

### Common traps
- Recording loan proceeds or stock issuance as revenue — both are financing, not earnings
- Confusing dividends (a distribution that reduces retained earnings) with an expense (which reduces net income)
- Mixing up the period statements (income, retained earnings, cash flows) with the point-in-time balance sheet
- Forgetting that net income, not cash collected, is the link from the income statement to retained earnings

## Chapter 3: The Recording Process and Double-Entry Logic
### Core questions
- Why does double-entry bookkeeping keep the accounting equation in balance on every transaction?
- What is the normal balance of each account type, and why?
- What errors will a trial balance catch, and which will it silently miss?

### Key concepts
- Source documents, accounts, journal, ledger, posting, and the unadjusted trial balance
- Debit/credit rules: assets and expenses and dividends increase with debits; liabilities, equity, and revenue increase with credits
- Normal balances and the symmetry of the expanded accounting equation
- T-accounts for tracing transaction effects and computing ending balances
- Audit trail, documentation discipline, and the chart of accounts

### Applied skills
- Journalize and post a multi-transaction set, then prepare an unadjusted trial balance
- Use T-accounts to derive an ending balance from beginning balance plus activity
- Detect a transposition or slide error versus an error a trial balance cannot reveal

### Common traps
- Believing "debit = increase" universally; direction depends on account type
- Assuming a balanced trial balance proves correctness — it misses omitted entries, wrong accounts, and equal-but-wrong amounts
- Treating "debit" and "credit" as good/bad rather than left/right
- Posting to the wrong account (e.g., expensing an asset purchase) — balanced but wrong

## Chapter 4: Accrual Accounting, Adjusting Entries, and Closing
### Core questions
- How does accrual accounting change the story that raw cash receipts and payments tell?
- What are the four adjustment families, and how do you recognize which one a situation needs?
- Why do temporary accounts close to retained earnings while permanent accounts carry forward?

### Key concepts
- Cash basis vs. accrual basis; the revenue recognition principle and expense matching
- The four adjustments: deferred (prepaid) expenses, unearned (deferred) revenue, accrued expenses, accrued revenues
- Depreciation as systematic cost allocation, not valuation; the contra-asset Accumulated Depreciation
- Adjusted trial balance; closing temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, dividends) to retained earnings; the post-closing trial balance
- How estimates inside adjustments move reported income and the balance sheet

### Applied skills
- Prepare each of the four adjustment types and the related closing entries
- Move from unadjusted to adjusted to post-closing trial balance
- Quantify how a one-month change in a deferral or accrual shifts net income

### Common traps
- Adjusting for the balance remaining instead of the amount used or earned (e.g., supplies)
- Closing permanent accounts, or failing to close dividends, which is a temporary account but not an expense
- Treating depreciation as reducing the asset to market value rather than allocating cost
- Recording the cash side in an adjusting entry — adjustments never touch Cash

## Chapter 5: Merchandising, Inventory Costing, and Cost of Goods Sold
### Core questions
- During rising prices, how do FIFO, LIFO, and weighted average diverge in COGS, ending inventory, and reported income?
- How does a perpetual system record purchases, sales, and returns differently from a periodic one?
- When and how must inventory be written down below cost?

### Key concepts
- Merchandising flow: sales revenue, sales returns and allowances, sales discounts, COGS, gross profit
- Perpetual vs. periodic inventory systems and their journal entries
- Cost-flow assumptions: FIFO, LIFO, weighted average; the LIFO conformity and tax effects
- Inventory write-downs: lower of cost or net realizable value (LCNRV) for FIFO and weighted average; lower of cost or market (LCM) for LIFO and retail (per ASU 2015-11)
- Gross margin and inventory turnover; cut-off and obsolescence judgments

### Applied skills
- Compute COGS and ending inventory under FIFO, LIFO, and weighted average, periodic and perpetual
- Record a credit sale and its cost side under a perpetual system
- Apply LCNRV to write inventory down and record the loss

### Common traps
- Swapping the rising-price effects: FIFO gives lower COGS and higher income; LIFO gives higher COGS and lower income
- Applying weighted average as a simple price average instead of a cost-weighted average
- Using LCNRV for LIFO inventory, which still uses the older LCM rule with replacement-cost bounds
- Forgetting that a periodic system has no running COGS — it is derived only at period end

## Chapter 6: Cash, Receivables, and Internal Control
### Core questions
- How does a bank reconciliation separate genuine errors from harmless timing differences?
- Why is the allowance method more faithful than the direct write-off method?
- What control breaks down when one person both handles cash and records it?

### Key concepts
- Internal control objectives and components; segregation of duties, authorization, documentation, physical safeguards, reconciliation, independent review
- Bank reconciliation: deposits in transit, outstanding checks, bank fees, NSF checks, and which side each adjusts
- Accounts receivable; allowance for doubtful accounts; percentage-of-receivables (aging) vs. percentage-of-sales estimation; direct write-off vs. allowance method
- Sarbanes-Oxley Section 404 and management's responsibility for internal control over financial reporting
- The fraud triangle: pressure, opportunity, rationalization; ethical escalation

### Applied skills
- Complete a two-column bank reconciliation and record only the book-side adjusting entries
- Estimate the required allowance and back out the bad-debt expense given an existing allowance balance
- Diagnose a segregation-of-duties weakness and propose a specific control fix

### Common traps
- Recording journal entries for bank-side reconciling items — only book-side items (fees, NSF, errors) get entries
- Confusing the desired ending allowance balance with the period's bad-debt expense
- Believing the direct write-off method is GAAP-compliant for material amounts — it violates matching
- Adding deposits in transit on the bank side but mishandling outstanding checks (subtract them)

## Chapter 7: Long-Term Assets and Depreciation
### Core questions
- What costs get capitalized into a long-term asset versus expensed immediately?
- How do straight-line, units-of-activity, and double-declining-balance depreciation differ in their early-year expense?
- How do you record a disposal, and where does the gain or loss come from?

### Key concepts
- Capitalized cost of PP&E (purchase price plus costs to ready the asset for use) vs. period expense
- Depreciation methods: straight-line, units-of-activity, double-declining-balance (an accelerated method ignoring salvage until the floor)
- Asset disposals: gain or loss = proceeds minus book value; updating depreciation to the disposal date
- Intangibles and amortization; goodwill (not amortized, tested for impairment); R&D generally expensed as incurred under U.S. GAAP
- Impairment basics for long-lived assets

### Applied skills
- Build a multi-year depreciation schedule under each method and compare the income patterns
- Record an asset sale, deriving the gain or loss from book value
- Distinguish capital expenditures from revenue expenditures in a list of costs

### Common traps
- Subtracting salvage value in double-declining-balance before reaching the salvage floor — DDB ignores salvage in the rate
- Computing a disposal gain/loss against original cost rather than book value (cost minus accumulated depreciation)
- Capitalizing R&D, which U.S. GAAP expenses, or amortizing goodwill, which is impairment-tested instead
- Confusing depreciation expense (the period charge) with accumulated depreciation (the running total)

## Chapter 8: Liabilities, Bonds, and Financing Choices
### Core questions
- Why does a bond sell at a discount or premium, and what does that imply about the stated versus market rate?
- How does the effective-interest method compute interest expense and update carrying value each period?
- How do debt and equity financing differ in their effect on the balance sheet, risk, and cash obligations?

### Key concepts
- Current liabilities, accrued liabilities, payroll, unearned revenue, and notes payable
- Contingent liabilities: probable and estimable (accrue) vs. reasonably possible (disclose) vs. remote (ignore)
- Bonds payable: face value, stated (coupon) rate, market (effective) rate; discount when market > stated, premium when market < stated
- Effective-interest amortization: interest expense = carrying value x market rate; cash interest = face x stated rate; the difference amortizes the discount or premium
- Lease basics under ASC 842: the right-of-use asset and lease liability on the balance sheet; finance vs. operating classification
- Leverage and the cost/benefit of debt vs. equity

### Applied skills
- Determine whether a bond issues at par, a discount, or a premium from the rate comparison
- Build an effective-interest amortization line: compute interest expense, cash paid, amortization, and new carrying value
- Apply the contingency recognition rules to a set of pending lawsuits

### Common traps
- Assuming interest expense equals the cash coupon — under the effective-interest method it equals carrying value times the market rate
- Reversing the discount/premium logic (discount means the market demanded a higher yield than the coupon)
- Accruing a contingency that is only reasonably possible (it should be disclosed, not accrued)
- Forgetting that ASC 842 now puts most operating leases on the balance sheet as a right-of-use asset and liability

## Chapter 9: Revenue Recognition Under ASC 606
### Core questions
- What are the five steps of the ASC 606 model, and what does each decide?
- When is revenue recognized over time versus at a point in time?
- How do contract liabilities, discounts, and multiple performance obligations change the timing and amount of revenue?

### Key concepts
- The core principle: recognize revenue to depict the transfer of goods or services in the amount of consideration expected
- The five steps: identify the contract; identify performance obligations; determine the transaction price; allocate the price to obligations; recognize revenue as each obligation is satisfied
- Performance obligations satisfied over time vs. at a point in time; control transfer
- Variable consideration, discounts, and allocation across bundled obligations
- Contract assets and contract liabilities (deferred revenue) on the balance sheet

### Applied skills
- Walk a bundled contract (e.g., product plus service plan) through all five steps and allocate the price
- Decide whether a given obligation is satisfied over time or at a point in time
- Record deferred revenue at contract inception and recognize it as obligations are met

### Common traps
- Recognizing the full contract price up front when distinct obligations are satisfied later
- Confusing collecting cash with satisfying a performance obligation — ASC 606 keys on transfer of control, not payment
- Ignoring allocation: a discount on a bundle must be spread across obligations by relative standalone price
- Treating a contract liability (unearned revenue) as revenue before the obligation is satisfied

## Chapter 10: Statement Analysis, Cash Flows, and Decision Use
### Core questions
- What can each ratio family reveal, and what can it hide?
- How can a profitable company report weak or negative operating cash flow?
- Under the indirect method, why are some adjustments added and others subtracted?

### Key concepts
- Horizontal, vertical, and common-size analysis for trend and structure
- Liquidity (current, quick), solvency (debt-to-equity, times-interest-earned), profitability (gross margin, ROA, ROE, profit margin), efficiency (inventory turnover, receivables turnover)
- Operating, investing, and financing activities; classifying cash flows
- Indirect method: start from net income; add back non-cash expenses like depreciation; subtract increases in current operating assets, add decreases; add increases in current operating liabilities, subtract decreases
- Earnings quality: accruals, policy choices, and the gap between net income and operating cash flow; limits of ratios and the need for industry context

### Applied skills
- Compute the standard ratio set from a two-year statement set and interpret the trend
- Reconstruct operating cash flow from net income using indirect-method adjustments
- Write a lender-versus-investor decision memo emphasizing different ratios for each user

### Common traps
- Adding (instead of subtracting) an increase in accounts receivable when converting net income to operating cash flow
- Reading depreciation add-back as a cash inflow rather than the reversal of a non-cash expense
- Treating a high current ratio as unambiguously good when it can signal idle cash or stale inventory
- Comparing ratios across industries without context, or ignoring that different accounting policies make ratios non-comparable

## Capstone
Students complete a full accounting cycle and analysis project for a small business or public-company case. Deliverables: transaction analysis and journal entries; posting and an unadjusted trial balance; the four adjustment types and closing entries; an adjusted and post-closing trial balance; the four articulated financial statements; at least one judgment-area schedule (inventory cost flow, a depreciation schedule, an allowance estimate, or a bond effective-interest line); a selected ratio set with horizontal/vertical analysis; and a reconstruction of operating cash flow by the indirect method.

The project closes with a decision memo addressed to a named user — a lender, an equity investor, or a manager. The memo must state what the numbers suggest, identify the two or three estimates and policy choices that most affect the picture (and how a different honest choice would change it), flag any internal-control or fraud risks a reader should weigh, and end with a clear, defensible recommendation. The grade rewards correct mechanics and the quality of the judgment around them in equal measure.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly transaction, adjustment, and closing problem sets
- Short quizzes on debit/credit logic, statement articulation, and ratio interpretation
- A spreadsheet-based accounting-cycle lab graded on articulation and formula integrity
- An internal-control case memo built from a fraud-triangle scenario
- A judgment-area mini-project (inventory, depreciation, bad debts, or bonds) with a method-comparison write-up
- Midterm on recording, adjustments, and statement preparation
- Final capstone combining preparation, analysis, and decision use
