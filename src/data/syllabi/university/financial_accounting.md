# Financial Accounting
**ID**: `financialAccounting` | **Discipline**: Business | **Year**: University Freshman / Sophomore

## Course Aim
Financial Accounting introduces students to the logic, language, and ethical responsibilities of external financial reporting. Students learn how business transactions become financial statements, how accrual accounting supports decision-useful information, and how users evaluate performance, risk, liquidity, solvency, and stewardship. The course treats accounting as both a technical system and a public trust: students practice recording and reporting events while asking what investors, creditors, managers, employees, regulators, and communities need to know.

By the end of the course, students should be able to prepare core accounting records, explain the effects of transactions on the financial statements, apply accrual adjustments, evaluate internal controls, interpret common ratios, and use financial information to support practical business decisions.

## Course Design Notes
- Emphasize the accounting equation as the throughline from first transaction to full statement analysis.
- Use short company cases, annual report excerpts, SEC filing examples, and spreadsheet practice to connect mechanics with judgment.
- Include frequent low-stakes journal entry, T-account, trial balance, and statement-building exercises.
- Treat ethics, internal control, estimation, materiality, and user decision needs as recurring themes rather than isolated topics.
- Balance preparation and interpretation: students should know how numbers are produced and how those numbers can be used, misused, or misunderstood.

## Chapter 1: Accounting, Business, and the Reporting Environment
### Key concepts
- Accounting as an information system for investors, creditors, managers, regulators, and other stakeholders
- Forms of business organization and how ownership affects reporting
- Financial accounting versus managerial accounting
- GAAP, the conceptual framework, relevance, faithful representation, comparability, consistency, materiality, and going concern
- The role of the SEC, FASB, auditors, management responsibility, and ethical judgment in reporting
- The accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

### Practice questions
- Who uses financial accounting information, and what decisions do they make with it?
- How does the accounting equation explain the structure of the balance sheet?
- When might technically correct reporting still create an ethical problem?

### Student output
Students prepare a one-page stakeholder map for a public company and explain three decisions that depend on credible accounting information.

## Chapter 2: Financial Statements and Transaction Effects
### Key concepts
- The income statement, statement of retained earnings, balance sheet, and statement of cash flows
- Revenues, expenses, gains, losses, assets, liabilities, equity, dividends, and retained earnings
- How transactions affect multiple statements
- Current versus noncurrent classifications
- Clean statement formatting and links between statements
- Basic use of annual reports and Form 10-K financial statements

### Practice questions
- How does issuing stock affect assets and equity?
- Why does earning revenue on account increase both assets and equity?
- How are net income, dividends, and retained earnings connected?

### Student output
Students classify a set of business events and build a simple set of linked financial statements for a first month of operations.

## Chapter 3: The Recording Process
### Key concepts
- Source documents, accounts, journals, ledgers, posting, and the trial balance
- Debit and credit rules for assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, expenses, and dividends
- Normal balances and the logic of double-entry accounting
- T-accounts as a tool for tracing transaction effects
- Common errors that a trial balance can and cannot reveal
- Professional documentation and audit trail habits

### Practice questions
- What journal entry records cash paid for supplies?
- Why must every transaction preserve equality in the accounting equation?
- What kinds of errors can exist even when debits equal credits?

### Student output
Students journalize and post a two-week transaction set, prepare an unadjusted trial balance, and annotate one likely control risk in the process.

## Chapter 4: Accrual Accounting and Adjusting Entries
### Key concepts
- Cash basis versus accrual basis accounting
- Revenue recognition and expense matching
- Deferrals, accruals, prepaid expenses, unearned revenue, accrued expenses, and accrued revenue
- Depreciation as allocation rather than valuation
- Adjusted trial balances and closing entries
- How estimates affect income, assets, liabilities, and user interpretation

### Practice questions
- What adjustment records one month of expired insurance?
- Why is unearned revenue a liability before the service is performed?
- How can a small estimate change affect reported performance?

### Student output
Students prepare adjusting entries for a service business, produce an adjusted trial balance, and write a short explanation of how accrual accounting changes the story told by cash receipts and payments.

## Chapter 5: Merchandising, Inventory, and Cost of Goods Sold
### Key concepts
- Merchandising operations, sales revenue, sales returns, sales discounts, cost of goods sold, and gross profit
- Perpetual and periodic inventory systems
- FIFO, LIFO, weighted average, and the effect of inventory methods during changing prices
- Lower of cost or market and inventory write-downs
- Gross margin analysis and inventory turnover
- Ethical issues in inventory counts, cut-off, and obsolete stock estimates

### Practice questions
- How do FIFO and LIFO affect cost of goods sold when prices are rising?
- What journal entry records a credit sale under a perpetual system?
- Why does inventory valuation matter to lenders and investors?

### Student output
Students calculate ending inventory and cost of goods sold under multiple methods, then recommend which method best explains performance for a sample retailer.

## Chapter 6: Cash, Receivables, and Internal Control
### Key concepts
- Internal control objectives: safeguard assets, promote accurate records, support compliance, and encourage efficient operations
- Segregation of duties, authorization, documentation, physical controls, reconciliations, and independent review
- Bank reconciliations and cash controls
- Accounts receivable, allowance for doubtful accounts, direct write-off versus allowance method, and aging analysis
- Sarbanes-Oxley basics and management accountability for controls
- Fraud risk, pressure, opportunity, rationalization, and ethical escalation

### Practice questions
- How does a bank reconciliation separate timing differences from errors?
- Why is the allowance method more faithful than waiting until an account is written off?
- What control would reduce the risk of one employee both collecting cash and recording receipts?

### Student output
Students complete a bank reconciliation, record required entries, estimate uncollectible accounts, and propose two internal control improvements for a small business.

## Chapter 7: Long-Term Assets, Liabilities, and Financing
### Key concepts
- Cost of property, plant, and equipment
- Straight-line, units-of-activity, and double-declining balance depreciation
- Disposals, gains, losses, and asset impairment basics
- Intangible assets, amortization, goodwill, and research and development treatment
- Current liabilities, contingencies, notes payable, bonds payable, premiums, discounts, and interest expense
- How financing choices affect leverage, risk, and future cash obligations

### Practice questions
- How is annual depreciation calculated under straight-line depreciation?
- Why might a bond sell at a discount or premium?
- How do debt financing and equity financing affect the balance sheet differently?

### Student output
Students create depreciation schedules, record asset disposal entries, and compare two financing options using both accounting effects and business judgment.

## Chapter 8: Statement Analysis and Decision Use
### Key concepts
- Horizontal, vertical, and common-size analysis
- Liquidity ratios, solvency ratios, profitability ratios, and efficiency ratios
- Current ratio, quick ratio, debt-to-equity, gross margin, return on assets, return on equity, inventory turnover, and receivables turnover
- Operating, investing, and financing cash flows
- Indirect method basics for the statement of cash flows
- Limits of ratio analysis, industry context, accounting policy choices, and nonfinancial information

### Practice questions
- What does a rising current ratio suggest, and what might it hide?
- How can a company report net income but have weak operating cash flow?
- Which ratios would matter most to a lender, investor, and manager?

### Student output
Students analyze two years of financial statements for a real or simulated company, compute selected ratios, and write a decision memo for a lender or investor.

## Capstone
Students complete an accounting cycle and analysis project for a small business or public-company case. The project includes transaction analysis, journal entries, posting, trial balances, adjustments, financial statements, basic controls, selected ratio analysis, and a final decision memo. The memo should explain what the numbers suggest, what judgments or estimates matter most, and what ethical or control risks a decision maker should consider before relying on the statements.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly transaction and adjustment problem sets
- Short quizzes on debit and credit logic, statement relationships, and ratio interpretation
- Spreadsheet-based accounting cycle lab
- Internal control case memo
- Financial statement analysis brief using annual report excerpts
- Midterm focused on recording, adjustments, and statement preparation
- Final capstone combining preparation, analysis, and decision use

## Research Notes
- OpenStax, Principles of Accounting, Volume 1: Financial Accounting, for accessible coverage of the accounting cycle, statements, inventory, receivables, long-term assets, liabilities, and analysis.
- OpenStax, Introduction to Business, for business context, stakeholders, ethics, and decision-making connections.
- U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission investor resources and company filing materials for examples of public-company reporting, Form 10-K structure, and disclosure expectations.
- FASB conceptual framework summaries for reporting objectives and qualitative characteristics.
- Annual reports from public companies for applied statement analysis, notes discussion, and management discussion context.
