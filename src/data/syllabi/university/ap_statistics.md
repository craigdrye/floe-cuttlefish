# AP Statistics
**ID**: `apStatistics` · **Discipline**: Mathematics / Data Literacy · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 17-19)
**Aligns with**: College Board AP Statistics Course and Exam Description; equivalent to a one-semester non-calculus introductory college statistics course (e.g., STAT 101 / "Stats for the social and life sciences")

## Course Aim
Statistics is the discipline of reasoning honestly under uncertainty, and almost every claim a citizen meets is a statistical claim: a poll says 52% support a measure, a drug "reduces risk by 30%," a school "outperforms the district average." This course builds the literacy to interrogate those claims. By the end, you should be able to look at a study, a graph, a poll, or a block of calculator output and answer three questions out loud: *Where did these data come from, what do they actually show, and how sure can we be?*

The intellectual spine of the course is the statistical problem-solving process the American Statistical Association puts at the center of modern instruction: pose a question, collect or find data, analyze the data, and interpret results in context. We treat that loop as the thing being learned, not the formulas that decorate it. A confidence interval is not a number you compute; it is a claim about a population made from a sample, with a stated method whose long-run capture rate you can defend. A p-value is not a verdict; it is a conditional probability that assumes the null hypothesis and measures how surprising the data are under that assumption. Students who leave this course confusing those two ideas have not passed it, regardless of arithmetic.

Two commitments shape every chapter. First, *context is not optional* — on the AP exam and in real life, an answer of "reject H0" with no sentence connecting it to the actual question earns nothing and means nothing. Second, *the limits of a conclusion matter as much as the conclusion*. The single most valuable habit we build is the reflex to ask what a particular design lets you say (association? causation? generalization to whom?) and to refuse to say more. That skepticism, applied precisely rather than cynically, is the whole point.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test AP Statistics content: data displays and numerical summaries, study and experimental design, probability and random variables, sampling distributions, confidence intervals, significance tests, chi-square procedures, inference for a regression slope, reading software or calculator output, and the free-response communication skills (conditions, context, notation, conclusions) the rubrics reward.

**A revision note that matters for accuracy.** The course described here targets the *current* AP Statistics frame, whose exam runs through May 2026: 40 multiple-choice questions (90 minutes) and 6 free-response questions (90 minutes, the last being a longer Investigative Task), each section worth 50%, organized into 9 units. Beginning Fall 2026 (first exam May 2027) the course is revised: the Algebra 2 prerequisite is removed, the nine units are consolidated into five organized around the problem-solving process, the exam becomes fully digital in Bluebook with a built-in Desmos calculator alongside an approved handheld, and several topics are dropped — analyzing departures from linearity, combining random variables, the geometric distribution, the chi-square goodness-of-fit test, and the entire former Unit 9 (inference for slopes). When a prompt turns on one of those differences, name whether it targets the current or revised course. Chapters here that lean on dropped topics (chi-square goodness-of-fit and slope inference in Chapter 8) are flagged so they can be retired or reframed when the revision lands. Calculator and software output are fair game; this is a technology-active course, so reading and interpreting output is a tested skill, not a shortcut.

## Chapter 1: Exploring One-Variable Data
- **Core questions**: How do you describe a single distribution honestly? When is the mean misleading, and what should replace it? What does a z-score actually tell you, and when may you use a Normal model?
- **Key concepts**: Categorical vs. quantitative variables; bar charts, dotplots, stemplots, histograms; shape (skew, modality), center (mean vs. median), spread (range, IQR, standard deviation), and the SOCS/CUSS framing for descriptions; the 1.5×IQR outlier rule and five-number summaries; standardized scores; percentiles; the Normal distribution, the empirical (68-95-99.7) rule, and the standard Normal table / `normalcdf`.
- **Applied skills**: Write a complete distribution description that names shape, center, spread, and unusual features *in context*; compute and compare z-scores across distributions; decide whether a Normal model is appropriate and use it to find proportions and percentiles; choose mean+SD vs. median+IQR based on skew and outliers.
- **Common traps**: Reporting the mean for a strongly skewed distribution where the median is the honest summary; describing shape/center/spread with no context ("the center is 50" — of what, for whom?); confusing a z-score (how many SDs from the mean) with a percentile (proportion below); assuming any bell-shaped histogram justifies a Normal model; treating standard deviation as a count of data points rather than a typical distance from the mean.

## Chapter 2: Exploring Two-Variable Data
- **Core questions**: What does a least-squares line actually predict, and what does it not? How do you read the strength and direction of an association without overstating it? Why does "correlation is not causation" survive being a cliché?
- **Key concepts**: Two-way tables and conditional distributions; scatterplots and the description of form, direction, strength, and outliers; the correlation coefficient r (and why it is unitless and bounded by ±1); the least-squares regression line, slope and intercept *interpretation in units*; residuals and residual plots; r² as the proportion of variation in y explained by the model; high-leverage and influential points; extrapolation; transformations to achieve linearity; Simpson's paradox.
- **Applied skills**: Interpret slope, intercept, r, and r² in the context's units; use a residual plot to judge whether a linear model is appropriate; predict responsibly and flag extrapolation; explain how a lurking variable or aggregation can reverse an apparent association.
- **Common traps**: Interpreting slope as a total rather than a per-unit rate of change ("for each additional hour, score is 4.2" not "score is 4.2"); reading a high r as proof of a good linear fit when the residual plot shows curvature; confusing r with r²; calling a strong association causal from observational data; extrapolating beyond the data range and trusting the prediction; saying a model "is 84% accurate" instead of "explains 84% of the variation in the response."

## Chapter 3: Collecting Data
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between a sample that is *biased* and one that is merely *variable*? When does a study earn the right to say "causes"? To whom can these results be generalized?
- **Key concepts**: Population, sample, parameter, statistic; sources of bias (voluntary response, convenience, undercoverage, nonresponse, response/wording bias); probability sampling — simple random, stratified, cluster, systematic; observational study vs. experiment; the three principles of experimental design (control, randomization, replication); confounding; completely randomized, randomized block, and matched-pairs designs; blinding, double-blinding, placebo effect; the distinction between random *sampling* (supports generalization) and random *assignment* (supports causal claims).
- **Applied skills**: Diagnose the specific bias in a flawed sampling plan and propose a fix; design a completely randomized or block experiment and justify the choices; decide, from a study's design alone, whether it permits causal and/or generalizable conclusions.
- **Common traps**: Conflating random sampling with random assignment (they license different conclusions); claiming a larger sample fixes bias (it only shrinks variability — a biased process gives a precisely wrong answer); calling any study with treatments an experiment even when subjects were not randomly assigned; treating "blocking" as the same as "stratifying" without noting one is for experiments and one for sampling; concluding causation from an observational study because the association is strong.

## Chapter 4: Probability and Random Variables
- **Core questions**: What does independence really require, and how is it different from disjoint? How do you tell a binomial setting from a geometric one at a glance? What does expected value mean for an event that happens only once?
- **Key concepts**: Sample spaces and the basic rules (complement, addition, multiplication); mutually exclusive vs. independent events; conditional probability and the general multiplication rule; two-way tables and tree diagrams; Bayes-style "reverse" conditional reasoning; simulation as an estimation tool; discrete random variables, their probability distributions, expected value and standard deviation; binomial settings (BINS: binary, independent, fixed n, same p) and their mean np and SD √(np(1−p)); geometric settings; the behavior of sums and differences of random variables (means add; variances add for independent variables).
- **Applied skills**: Compute conditional probabilities from tables and trees; verify the binomial conditions before using a binomial model; calculate and interpret expected value and SD; design and run a simulation, then compare the estimate to a theoretical probability.
- **Common traps**: Treating disjoint (mutually exclusive) events as independent — they are nearly opposite (disjoint events with nonzero probability are *dependent*); adding standard deviations instead of variances when combining random variables; confusing P(A|B) with P(B|A); forcing a binomial model onto a setting without a fixed number of trials or with changing/ dependent probabilities; reading expected value as a guaranteed or even possible single outcome (E(X)=2.5 children).

## Chapter 5: Sampling Distributions
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between the distribution of a population, of one sample, and of a statistic over many samples? Why does the Central Limit Theorem rescue inference even when the population is skewed? What does standard error measure?
- **Key concepts**: Sampling variability and the idea of a statistic as a random variable; the sampling distribution of a sample proportion p̂ (mean p, SD √(p(1−p)/n)) and of a sample mean x̄ (mean μ, SD σ/√n); the meaning of unbiasedness vs. low variability; the Central Limit Theorem and why large n makes x̄ approximately Normal; the 10% condition for independence without replacement; the Large Counts condition (np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10) for proportions; sampling distributions for a difference of two proportions or means.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguish population, sample, and sampling-distribution quantities in a problem and label which SD/SE applies; check the relevant conditions before computing; find the probability that a sample statistic falls in a given range using the appropriate model.
- **Common traps**: Believing the CLT makes the *population* Normal (it concerns the distribution of x̄, not the data); thinking larger n changes the *center* of the sampling distribution rather than shrinking its spread; using σ/√n when only s is known without moving to the t-distribution (Chapter 7); skipping the 10% and Large Counts conditions and assuming Normality is automatic; confusing standard deviation of the population with standard error of the statistic.

## Chapter 6: Inference for Proportions
- **Core questions**: What is a confidence interval a statement *about* — and what is the one thing "95% confident" does not mean? How do you choose between a one- and two-proportion procedure? What do Type I error, Type II error, and power trade off against each other?
- **Key concepts**: Point estimate ± margin of error; the one- and two-proportion z-intervals and their conditions (Random, 10%, Large Counts); margin of error and the levers that change it (confidence level, sample size, p̂); sample-size planning for a target margin; null and alternative hypotheses for proportions; the one- and two-proportion z-tests (and the pooled proportion in the two-sample test); p-values and significance level α; Type I error (rejecting a true H0, probability α), Type II error (failing to reject a false H0, probability β), and power (1−β).
- **Applied skills**: Pick the correct procedure from the question and the number of samples; state and check conditions explicitly; interpret an interval ("we are 95% confident the true proportion is between…") and a p-value ("if H0 were true, we'd see data this extreme about __% of the time") in context; describe a Type I/II error and a power consequence in the problem's own terms.
- **Common traps**: Saying "there is a 95% probability the true proportion is in this interval" (the parameter is fixed; the *method* captures it 95% of the time); interpreting the confidence level as the chance a single interval is right; treating a p-value as P(H0 is true); concluding the null is *true* after failing to reject it; forgetting the pooled proportion in a two-proportion significance test; confusing statistical significance with practical importance.

## Chapter 7: Inference for Means
- **Core questions**: Why do we use t instead of z for means, and what changes? How do you tell a matched-pairs design from a two-independent-samples design — and why does getting it wrong invalidate the test? When is "significant" not the same as "important"?
- **Key concepts**: The t-distributions and degrees of freedom; why s (an estimate of σ) forces the heavier-tailed t; one-sample t-interval and t-test; the paired-data t (analyze the differences); two-sample t procedures and the robustness of t to non-Normality at moderate-to-large n; checking Normality / sample-size conditions; interpreting calculator/software output (t, df, p-value, interval); practical vs. statistical significance.
- **Applied skills**: Diagnose whether data are paired or independent and choose accordingly; run one-sample, paired, and two-sample t procedures and state conditions; read t-test and t-interval output and translate it into a contextual conclusion; explain how sample size affects both power and the practical meaning of a result.
- **Common traps**: Running a two-sample t-test on paired data (before/after on the *same* subjects), which ignores the pairing and usually inflates the error; using z instead of t when σ is unknown; reporting "significant" without checking whether the effect size matters in context; misreading the df or treating a one-tailed p-value as two-tailed; claiming the population is Normal rather than checking robustness conditions for the sample.

## Chapter 8: Chi-Square and Inference for Regression
- **Core questions**: When is the question about *counts in categories* rather than means or proportions, and which chi-square test answers it? What does the slope of a regression line tell you about a *population*, and how do you put a confidence interval or test on it?
- **Key concepts**: Chi-square goodness-of-fit (one categorical variable vs. a claimed distribution — *flagged for removal in the 2026-27 revision*); chi-square test of homogeneity (same variable across several populations) vs. test of independence (two variables, one population); expected counts and the (observed−expected)²/expected components; degrees of freedom and the largest contributors to the statistic; inference for the slope of a least-squares regression line, the LINER conditions (Linearity, Independence, Normality of residuals, Equal variance, Random), the t-interval and t-test for β, and reading slope inference from software output — *this entire topic (former Unit 9) is removed in the revision*.
- **Applied skills**: Select goodness-of-fit vs. homogeneity vs. independence from the data structure; compute and check expected counts; identify which cells drive a large chi-square statistic; construct and interpret a confidence interval or significance test for a regression slope from output.
- **Common traps**: Confusing homogeneity (several populations sampled separately) with independence (one sample, two variables) — same arithmetic, different conclusion and design; using chi-square when any expected count is too small (< 5) without comment; reading observed counts where the formula needs expected; interpreting a regression-slope interval as being about the sample's slope rather than the population's β; ignoring the LINER conditions, especially a residual plot that shows non-constant spread.

## Chapter 9: FRQ Studio and Communication
- **Core questions**: What separates a 4-point free-response answer from a 1-point one when the arithmetic is identical? How do you structure an inference write-up so the conditions, mechanics, and conclusion each earn their credit? How do you read and explain technology output instead of recomputing it?
- **Key concepts**: The standard inference template — state (hypotheses/parameter and procedure), plan (name the procedure and check conditions), do (mechanics, including correct notation and the test statistic/interval), conclude (a contextual sentence linking back to the question); the difference between a "describe," "explain," and "justify" verb in a prompt; the Investigative Task's habit of extending familiar tools to a novel context; calculator/Desmos and software output reading; correct statistical notation (p vs. p̂, μ vs. x̄, β vs. b).
- **Applied skills**: Write a full four-part inference solution that would earn complete credit; translate output into conclusions without rederiving everything; answer "justify" prompts with reasons rather than restated answers; budget time across a 6-question, 90-minute free-response section and the longer Investigative Task.
- **Common traps**: A conclusion with no context ("reject H0" and stop) — the most common silent point-loser; checking conditions vaguely ("it's random") instead of citing the design and computing the count thresholds; using parameter and statistic notation interchangeably; recomputing from scratch when the prompt says "use the output"; over-answering a "describe" prompt and under-answering a "justify" one; running out of time on the Investigative Task because earlier parts were over-written.

## Capstone
Students run a complete statistical investigation from question to defensible conclusion, mirroring the ASA problem-solving process the course is built on. The deliverable has five parts: (1) a clearly posed statistical question and a stated population; (2) a data plan — either an original sampling or experimental design with bias and confounding addressed, or a justified choice of an existing dataset with its provenance and limitations named; (3) exploratory analysis with appropriate graphs and numerical summaries described in context; (4) at least one inference procedure (interval or test) with conditions checked, mechanics shown, and a conclusion stated in context; and (5) an honest uncertainty-and-limitations section that states precisely what the design permits — association vs. causation, the population to which results generalize, and the residual risk of Type I/II error. Students defend the project orally, and the rubric weights design quality, correct uncertainty language, and appropriate (not overreaching) claims above computational polish. A technically clean analysis that claims more than the data support fails the capstone; a modest study that says exactly what it can and no more passes.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short interpretation prompts where context and wording carry as much weight as the calculation.
- Procedure-choice drills: present a scenario and ask only "which procedure and why" before any computation.
- Calculator/Desmos and software output-reading checks, ending in a written contextual conclusion.
- FRQ practice scored on official-style rubrics that reward conditions, context, notation, and precision.
- A final project defense focused on design quality, uncertainty, and the appropriateness of claims.
