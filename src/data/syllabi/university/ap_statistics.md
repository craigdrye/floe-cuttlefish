# AP Statistics
**ID**: `apStatistics` · **Discipline**: Mathematics / Data Literacy · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 17-19)
**Aligns with**: College Board AP Statistics Course and Exam Description; equivalent to a one-semester non-calculus introductory statistics course

## Course Aim
Build statistical literacy for real claims: how data are collected, how patterns are described, how uncertainty is quantified, and how conclusions can be made honestly. Students should leave able to read studies, polls, experiments, and regression output with healthy skepticism and precise language.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test AP Statistics data displays, study design, probability, random variables, sampling distributions, confidence intervals, significance tests, chi-square procedures, regression inference, calculator output, or FRQ communication. AP Statistics is being revised for the 2026-27 school year, so prompts should name whether they target the current course frame or the revised course when differences matter.

## Chapter 1: Exploring One-Variable Data
- **Key concepts**: Variable types, categorical and quantitative displays, shape, outliers, center, spread, five-number summaries, boxplots, standard deviation, z-scores, percentiles, Normal distributions, and transformations.
- **Practice questions**: Describe distributions with context; identify outliers; compute and interpret z-scores; use Normal models appropriately.
- **Student output**: A one-variable data report comparing two groups with graphs, summaries, and a limitations note.

## Chapter 2: Exploring Two-Variable Data
- **Key concepts**: Two-way tables, conditional distributions, association, scatterplots, correlation, least-squares regression, residuals, influential points, transformations, and correlation versus causation.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret slope, intercept, r, r-squared, and residuals; read residual plots; explain Simpson's paradox in a simple context.
- **Student output**: A regression brief with scatterplot, model, residual analysis, and careful prediction statement.

## Chapter 3: Collecting Data
- **Key concepts**: Population, sample, parameter, statistic, bias, sampling methods, observational studies, experiments, random assignment, control, replication, blocking, blinding, placebo, and generalizability versus causation.
- **Practice questions**: Identify bias; improve sampling and experimental designs; decide what conclusions a study permits.
- **Student output**: A redesigned study plan that reduces bias and states what can and cannot be concluded.

## Chapter 4: Probability and Random Variables
- **Key concepts**: Probability rules, independence, conditional probability, two-way tables, tree diagrams, simulations, discrete and continuous random variables, expected value, variance, binomial and geometric settings.
- **Practice questions**: Calculate conditional probabilities; identify binomial and geometric conditions; compute expected values and standard deviations.
- **Student output**: A simulation notebook that estimates a probability and compares it with a theoretical result.

## Chapter 5: Sampling Distributions
- **Key concepts**: Sampling variability, unbiasedness, variability, sample proportions, sample means, Central Limit Theorem, standard error, 10% condition, large counts, and difference of proportions or means.
- **Practice questions**: Check sampling-distribution conditions; compute probabilities for sample statistics; distinguish population, sample, and sampling-distribution quantities.
- **Student output**: A visual explanation of sampling variability using simulation or repeated samples.

## Chapter 6: Inference for Proportions
- **Key concepts**: Confidence intervals for one and two proportions, margin of error, sample size planning, one- and two-proportion z-tests, hypotheses, p-values, Type I and Type II errors, significance level, and power.
- **Practice questions**: Choose the right procedure; check conditions; interpret intervals and p-values correctly in context.
- **Student output**: A full inference write-up for a categorical-data question, including assumptions and conclusion.

## Chapter 7: Inference for Means
- **Key concepts**: t-distributions, one-sample t procedures, matched pairs, two-sample t procedures, robustness, degrees of freedom, confidence intervals, significance tests, and practical significance.
- **Practice questions**: Choose among one-sample, paired, and two-sample procedures; interpret calculator output; avoid p-value and confidence-level misconceptions.
- **Student output**: A quantitative-data inference memo written for a non-technical audience.

## Chapter 8: Chi-Square, Regression Inference, and FRQ Studio
- **Key concepts**: Chi-square goodness-of-fit, homogeneity, independence, expected counts, residual contributions, inference for regression slopes, LINE conditions, software output, FRQ structure, and conclusion writing.
- **Practice questions**: Select chi-square procedures; interpret residuals; build a confidence interval or test for a regression slope; write complete FRQ solutions.
- **Student output**: A timed AP Statistics FRQ portfolio with one design, one probability, one inference, and one regression or chi-square problem.

## Capstone
Students complete a statistical investigation from question to conclusion: design, data collection or dataset selection, exploratory analysis, inference where appropriate, uncertainty statement, and an honest limitations section.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short interpretation prompts where context and wording matter as much as calculation.
- Procedure-choice drills before computation.
- Calculator-output reading checks with written conclusions.
- FRQ rubrics that reward conditions, context, notation, and precision.
- A final project defense focused on design quality, uncertainty, and appropriate claims.
