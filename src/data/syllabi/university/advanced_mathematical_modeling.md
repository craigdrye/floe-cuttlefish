# Advanced Mathematical Modeling
**ID**: `mathSenior` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
Develop advanced mathematical modelers who can translate messy systems into defensible mathematics, analyze the resulting structures, compute responsibly, and communicate decisions under uncertainty. Students should understand modeling as an iterative practice: frame, simplify, solve, validate, revise, and explain.

## Course Design Notes
This is a senior-level applied mathematics course for students who have seen calculus, linear algebra, probability, and some programming. Route questions here when they involve deterministic or stochastic models, optimization, simulation, networks, sensitivity analysis, validation, or model communication. Emphasize assumptions, dimensional reasoning, proof where structure matters, computation where closed forms fail, and ethical awareness when models influence decisions.

## Chapter 1: The Modeling Cycle and Dimensional Reasoning
- **Key concepts**: Problem framing, variables, parameters, assumptions, scales, units, nondimensionalization, conservation laws, data needs, model classes, validation, sensitivity, uncertainty, and audience-aware communication.
- **Practice questions**: Turn a vague system into a tractable modeling question; check units in a proposed equation; identify assumptions that control model behavior.
- **Student output**: A model-framing dossier with system diagram, variables, assumptions, dimensional checks, data plan, and validation criteria.

## Chapter 2: Deterministic Difference and Differential Models
- **Key concepts**: Difference equations, ordinary differential equations, systems, equilibria, stability, phase lines, phase planes, linearization, logistic growth, predator-prey models, competition, SIR epidemiology, and bifurcation language.
- **Practice questions**: Find and classify equilibria; interpret phase-plane trajectories; estimate when an SIR model predicts outbreak growth; compare discrete and continuous models.
- **Student output**: A dynamical-systems analysis with equations, equilibrium work, numerical simulation, stability interpretation, and parameter sensitivity.

## Chapter 3: Optimization and Decision Models
- **Key concepts**: Objective functions, constraints, feasible regions, linear programming, simplex ideas, duality, shadow prices, integer programming, nonlinear optimization, Lagrange multipliers, KKT conditions, convexity, and tradeoff curves.
- **Practice questions**: Formulate an allocation problem as an LP; interpret a dual value; decide when integer variables are necessary; test whether a constraint is binding.
- **Student output**: An optimization memo for a resource-allocation or logistics problem, including formulation, solver output, sensitivity discussion, and decision recommendation.

## Chapter 4: Stochastic Processes and Markov Models
- **Key concepts**: Random variables, stochastic processes, transition matrices, Markov chains, absorbing states, stationary distributions, expected hitting time, Poisson processes, continuous-time Markov chains, and model calibration.
- **Practice questions**: Compute a multi-step transition probability; find a steady-state distribution; calculate expected time to absorption; choose a state space for a real process.
- **Student output**: A Markov-model notebook that defines states, estimates or justifies transitions, computes long-run behavior, and tests sensitivity to transition assumptions.

## Chapter 5: Queues, Reliability, and Service Systems
- **Key concepts**: Arrival processes, service distributions, M/M/1 and M/M/c queues, Little's Law, utilization, waiting time, bottlenecks, reliability diagrams, failure rates, redundancy, and simulation-based validation.
- **Practice questions**: Use Little's Law to check a queue model; compare one fast server with several slower servers; estimate the effect of utilization on wait time.
- **Student output**: A service-system analysis for a clinic, call center, computer cluster, or transit process, with formulas, simulation checks, and operational recommendations.

## Chapter 6: Networks and Graph Models
- **Key concepts**: Graphs, adjacency matrices, paths, connectivity, centrality, PageRank, bipartite graphs, random graphs, small-world networks, scale-free networks, flow, diffusion, contagion, robustness, and community structure.
- **Practice questions**: Compute centrality in a small network; compare contagion on two network topologies; interpret an adjacency matrix; identify a vulnerable cut or bottleneck.
- **Student output**: A network-analysis report with graph construction, centrality or flow results, visualization, algorithmic explanation, and limits of interpretation.

## Chapter 7: Simulation, Monte Carlo, and Agent-Based Models
- **Key concepts**: Monte Carlo estimation, pseudo-randomness, sampling distributions, variance reduction, numerical experiments, cellular automata, agent-based models, local rules, emergence, verification, validation, reproducibility, and computational experiments.
- **Practice questions**: Design a Monte Carlo estimate with uncertainty; explain how local agent rules create global behavior; distinguish verification from validation.
- **Student output**: A reproducible simulation study with code, experiment design, random seed policy, convergence or sensitivity checks, and a written interpretation.

## Chapter 8: Model Validation, Ethics, and Communication Studio
- **Key concepts**: Residuals, goodness of fit, cross-validation at a conceptual level, parameter uncertainty, structural uncertainty, scenario analysis, robustness, interpretability, bias, fairness, misuse, stakeholder communication, and technical writing.
- **Practice questions**: Diagnose a model that fits history but fails a stress test; write an uncertainty statement for a decision maker; identify ethical risks in a predictive or allocation model.
- **Student output**: A model audit that evaluates assumptions, data quality, uncertainty, ethical stakes, and communication choices for an existing or student-built model.

## Capstone
Students complete a full modeling project for a real, simulated, or instructor-approved system. The capstone must move through the modeling cycle: problem framing, assumptions, mathematical formulation, analysis or proof, computation, validation, sensitivity or uncertainty analysis, ethical reflection, and final communication. Projects may draw from epidemiology, ecology, transportation, networks, energy systems, finance, public policy, or operations.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short modeling briefs graded on assumptions, variables, units, and the match between question and model type.
- Analytical problem sets on equilibria, optimization structure, Markov behavior, and network measures.
- Computational labs checked for reproducibility, parameter experiments, and interpretation of output.
- Peer review of model formulations before computation begins.
- Final capstone defense using a rubric for mathematical correctness, validation, uncertainty, ethics, and communication.
