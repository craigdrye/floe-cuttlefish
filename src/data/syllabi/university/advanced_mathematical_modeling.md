# Advanced Mathematical Modeling
**ID**: `mathSenior` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
This course trains students to do the thing that distinguishes a mathematician from a calculator: turn a messy, under-specified, real-world system into mathematics that is honest, tractable, and defensible. Modeling is not the act of remembering a formula and plugging in numbers. It is a disciplined cycle — frame the question, state assumptions, choose a model class, simplify, solve or simulate, validate against evidence, quantify uncertainty, revise, and communicate to people who will act on the result. By the end of the course, a student should be able to look at a system they have never seen before (an outbreak, a queue, a logistics network, a market, a power grid) and produce a model whose every assumption can be named, whose units check out, and whose conclusions come with error bars.

The intellectual core of the course is the distinction between a model and reality. A model is a deliberate, useful lie: it throws away most of the world to keep the part that matters for one decision. "All models are wrong, but some are useful" is the working motto, but the course pushes harder than the slogan — it asks *which* wrongness matters, *for whom*, and *at what cost*. We therefore treat assumptions, dimensional consistency, sensitivity, and validation as first-class mathematical objects, not afterthoughts. A model that fits historical data perfectly but collapses under a stress test, or that hides a regressive policy inside an "objective" optimization, has failed even if every equation is correct.

The course is computational and ethical as much as it is analytical. Where closed forms exist, students derive and analyze them (equilibria, stability, stationary distributions, dual values). Where they do not, students simulate responsibly — fixing seeds, reporting Monte Carlo standard error, separating verification from validation. And because mathematical models increasingly decide who gets a loan, a transplant, or bail, students learn to interrogate the social stakes of a model with the same rigor they bring to its algebra.

## Course Design Notes
This is a senior-level applied mathematics course. Students are assumed to have completed single- and multivariable calculus, linear algebra, and an introductory probability course, and to be comfortable writing short programs (Python/R/MATLAB) for simulation and numerical work. Route questions here when they involve deterministic or stochastic dynamics, optimization, simulation, networks, sensitivity analysis, model validation, or the communication and ethics of decision models. Questions that are purely about computing a derivative, solving a single linear system, or evaluating a probability belong in the prerequisite courses; questions here should require a *modeling judgment* — choosing a model class, defending an assumption, interpreting a dual value, or diagnosing why a fitted model fails out of sample.

Each chapter is built around four lenses. **Core questions** name the central things a student must be able to answer. **Key concepts** lists the vocabulary and machinery. **Applied skills** is what the student should be able to *do* with their hands. **Common traps** captures the recurring errors that separate a B from an A — usually a confusion of model with reality, a units error, a stability/optimum misclassification, or an overclaim from limited evidence. The Capstone integrates all eight chapters on one real system.

## Chapter 1: The Modeling Cycle and Dimensional Reasoning
- **Core questions**: What decision is this model for, and who acts on it? Which assumptions actually control the behavior? Do the units balance, and what does nondimensionalization reveal about the governing scales?
- **Key concepts**: Problem framing, state variables vs. parameters, assumptions, scales, units, dimensional homogeneity, the Buckingham Pi theorem, nondimensionalization, conservation laws, model classes (deterministic/stochastic, discrete/continuous, mechanistic/empirical), validation criteria.
- **Applied skills**: Convert a vague prompt into a precise modeling question with explicit inputs, outputs, and a decision; perform a dimensional-consistency check and catch a hidden units error; nondimensionalize a simple equation to expose the dominant balance; write a one-page assumption ledger that a reviewer could attack.
- **Common traps**: Modeling the phenomenon "in general" instead of for a specific decision (no decision means no way to judge the model); confusing a parameter with a variable; assuming a quantity is constant because it is hard to measure; writing an equation that is dimensionally inconsistent (e.g., adding a rate to a quantity) and not noticing.
- **Student output**: A model-framing dossier with system diagram, variables, assumptions, dimensional checks, data plan, and validation criteria.

## Chapter 2: Deterministic Difference and Differential Models
- **Core questions**: What are the equilibria of this system and are they stable? What does the phase portrait tell you about long-run behavior? When does a discrete model diverge qualitatively from its continuous analog, and why?
- **Key concepts**: Difference equations, ODEs and systems of ODEs, equilibria/fixed points, linearization and the Jacobian, eigenvalue stability criteria, phase lines and phase planes, logistic growth, Lotka-Volterra predator-prey, competition models, the SIR model and the basic reproduction number R0 = beta/gamma, bifurcations.
- **Applied skills**: Find and classify equilibria via linearization; sketch and read a phase plane; determine from R0 whether an SIR outbreak grows; simulate a system numerically and connect the trajectory back to the analytic stability result.
- **Common traps**: Treating a non-zero equilibrium of a predator-prey system as a global attractor when it is actually a center (neutral cycles); concluding stability from the sign of f rather than from the sign of f' (the derivative) at the equilibrium; forgetting that a discrete logistic map can be chaotic where the continuous logistic ODE is monotone; misreading R0 > 1 as "everyone gets infected" rather than "initial cases grow."
- **Student output**: A dynamical-systems analysis with equations, equilibrium work, numerical simulation, stability interpretation, and parameter sensitivity.

## Chapter 3: Optimization and Decision Models
- **Core questions**: How do I encode this resource decision as an objective plus constraints? At an optimum, which constraints bind, and what does the shadow price of each tell the decision maker? When do I genuinely need integer variables, and what does that cost?
- **Key concepts**: Objective functions, feasible regions, linear programming, the simplex method, LP duality and weak/strong duality, shadow prices, reduced costs, sensitivity ranges, integer and mixed-integer programming, convexity, nonlinear optimization, Lagrange multipliers, the KKT conditions, Pareto frontiers and tradeoff curves.
- **Applied skills**: Formulate an allocation or logistics problem as an LP; read solver output to identify binding constraints and interpret a dual value as the marginal worth of a resource; decide when relaxing integrality is safe; check convexity to know whether a local optimum is global.
- **Common traps**: Interpreting a shadow price outside its valid sensitivity range; assuming the LP relaxation of an integer program gives the integer optimum (it only bounds it); treating a local optimum as global for a non-convex problem; confusing a non-binding constraint (slack, zero shadow price) with a removable one; sign errors in Lagrange/KKT multipliers.
- **Student output**: An optimization memo for a resource-allocation or logistics problem, including formulation, solver output, sensitivity discussion, and decision recommendation.

## Chapter 4: Stochastic Processes and Markov Models
- **Core questions**: Does this process satisfy the Markov property at my chosen state granularity? What is its long-run distribution, and does one exist? How long until the process is absorbed, and how sensitive is that to the transition estimates?
- **Key concepts**: Random variables, stochastic processes, the Markov property, transition matrices, n-step transitions via matrix powers, irreducibility and aperiodicity, stationary and limiting distributions, absorbing states and fundamental matrix N = (I - Q)^{-1}, expected hitting/absorption times, Poisson processes, continuous-time Markov chains and generator matrices.
- **Applied skills**: Compute multi-step transition probabilities by powering the transition matrix; solve pi P = pi (with probabilities summing to 1) for a stationary distribution; use the fundamental matrix to get expected time to absorption; choose a state space that actually makes the process Markovian.
- **Common traps**: Assuming a limiting distribution exists for a periodic or reducible chain; reading rows and columns of the transition matrix backwards (P_ij is i to j); forgetting the normalization constraint when solving for the stationary distribution; choosing a state that omits the history needed for the Markov property to hold; confusing a stationary distribution with the time spent before absorption.
- **Student output**: A Markov-model notebook that defines states, estimates or justifies transitions, computes long-run behavior, and tests sensitivity to transition assumptions.

## Chapter 5: Queues, Reliability, and Service Systems
- **Core questions**: What is the bottleneck and how close to capacity is it? How does waiting time blow up as utilization approaches 1? Is one fast server better than several slower ones, and what does redundancy buy in a reliability diagram?
- **Key concepts**: Arrival processes, service-time distributions, Kendall notation, M/M/1 and M/M/c queues, utilization rho = lambda/mu, average number in system L = rho/(1-rho), Little's Law L = lambda*W, waiting-time formulas, bottleneck analysis, series vs. parallel reliability, failure rates, redundancy, simulation-based validation.
- **Applied skills**: Apply Little's Law to sanity-check a model or a measured system; compute M/M/1 performance measures and show how W explodes as rho approaches 1; compare pooled vs. parallel server configurations; build a reliability block diagram and compute system reliability.
- **Common traps**: Believing a system with rho < 1 has small waits (waiting time grows nonlinearly and diverges as rho approaches 1); applying steady-state formulas to a system that never reaches steady state; assuming Little's Law needs Poisson arrivals (it holds for any stable system); adding the reliabilities of series components instead of multiplying them.
- **Student output**: A service-system analysis for a clinic, call center, computer cluster, or transit process, with formulas, simulation checks, and operational recommendations.

## Chapter 6: Networks and Graph Models
- **Core questions**: Which nodes matter, and by which definition of "matter"? How does the network's topology change the dynamics of contagion, flow, or failure? Where is the structure fragile?
- **Key concepts**: Graphs and adjacency matrices, degree, paths and connectivity, centrality measures (degree, betweenness, closeness, eigenvector), PageRank, bipartite graphs, Erdos-Renyi random graphs, small-world and scale-free networks, degree distributions and power laws, max-flow/min-cut, diffusion and contagion, percolation and robustness, community detection.
- **Applied skills**: Build a graph and its adjacency matrix from data; compute and contrast centrality measures, explaining when each is the right one; reason about how a scale-free degree distribution changes epidemic thresholds and targeted-attack vulnerability; find a min cut / bottleneck.
- **Common traps**: Assuming degree centrality and betweenness rank nodes the same way (they routinely disagree); reading a high average degree as "robust" when a scale-free hub structure is fragile to targeted attack; confusing min-cut with a single weakest edge; treating PageRank as popularity rather than a stationary distribution of a random walk; ignoring that contagion threshold depends on degree variance, not just the mean.
- **Student output**: A network-analysis report with graph construction, centrality or flow results, visualization, algorithmic explanation, and limits of interpretation.

## Chapter 7: Simulation, Monte Carlo, and Agent-Based Models
- **Core questions**: How many samples do I need for a target precision, and how do I report that precision? Did my simulation reproduce the math I intended (verification), and does that math reproduce the world (validation)? How do local agent rules generate global pattern?
- **Key concepts**: Monte Carlo estimation, the law of large numbers, the 1/sqrt(N) standard-error scaling, confidence intervals for estimates, pseudo-random number generators and seeds, variance reduction (antithetic variates, control variates, importance sampling), cellular automata, agent-based models, local rules and emergence, verification vs. validation, reproducibility.
- **Applied skills**: Design a Monte Carlo estimate and report it with a standard error and confidence interval; quadruple sample size to halve the error and explain why; apply a variance-reduction technique and justify it; specify a seed policy so a study is reproducible; explain an emergent ABM result in terms of its local rules.
- **Common traps**: Reporting a Monte Carlo point estimate with no uncertainty; expecting error to halve when you double N (it only drops by sqrt(2)); confusing verification (code matches model) with validation (model matches reality); not fixing or recording the random seed; over-interpreting an emergent pattern as a real-world prediction without validation.
- **Student output**: A reproducible simulation study with code, experiment design, random seed policy, convergence or sensitivity checks, and a written interpretation.

## Chapter 8: Model Validation, Uncertainty, Ethics, and Communication Studio
- **Core questions**: Does the model generalize beyond the data it was fit to? How much of the output uncertainty comes from parameters vs. from the model's structure? Who is harmed if this model is wrong or misused, and how would the decision maker know?
- **Key concepts**: Residual analysis, goodness of fit vs. predictive accuracy, overfitting, out-of-sample and cross-validation reasoning, parameter uncertainty vs. structural uncertainty, sensitivity analysis (local vs. global, e.g. Sobol indices conceptually), scenario and stress testing, robustness, interpretability, bias and fairness, feedback loops, model misuse, stakeholder communication, technical writing.
- **Applied skills**: Diagnose a model that fits history but fails a stress test; separate parameter from structural uncertainty and say which dominates; run a one-at-a-time and a global sensitivity analysis and explain the difference; write an honest uncertainty statement for a non-technical decision maker; identify the fairness and feedback-loop risks in an allocation or prediction model.
- **Common traps**: Equating in-sample fit (high R-squared) with predictive validity; reporting only a local sensitivity sweep when interactions matter; presenting a single point forecast as if it were certain; assuming a model that "uses no protected attributes" is therefore fair (proxies and feedback loops); treating ethics as a disclaimer paragraph rather than a design constraint.
- **Student output**: A model audit that evaluates assumptions, data quality, uncertainty, ethical stakes, and communication choices for an existing or student-built model.

## Capstone
Students complete a full modeling project on a real, simulated, or instructor-approved system and defend it. The project must traverse the entire modeling cycle: (1) frame a specific decision and stakeholder; (2) state and justify assumptions with a dimensional check; (3) choose and formulate a model class (dynamical, optimization, stochastic, network, or hybrid); (4) analyze or prove what can be proven (equilibria/stability, optimum structure, stationary behavior); (5) compute or simulate responsibly with reported uncertainty; (6) validate against evidence and stress-test; (7) perform a sensitivity/uncertainty analysis distinguishing parameter from structural uncertainty; (8) reflect on ethical stakes and misuse; and (9) communicate to the intended decision maker in writing and orally. The defense is graded against a rubric covering mathematical correctness, validation rigor, uncertainty honesty, ethical awareness, and clarity. Project domains may include epidemiology, ecology, transportation, telecommunications, energy systems, finance, public policy, or operations.

## Assessment Ideas
- Short modeling briefs graded on assumptions, variables, units, and the match between the question and the chosen model class.
- Analytical problem sets on equilibria and stability, LP duality and shadow prices, Markov long-run behavior, queueing formulas, and network measures.
- Computational labs checked for reproducibility (seed policy), reported Monte Carlo error, parameter experiments, and interpretation of output.
- Peer review of model formulations *before* computation begins, focused on assumptions and dimensional consistency.
- Final capstone defense using a rubric for mathematical correctness, validation, uncertainty, ethics, and communication.
