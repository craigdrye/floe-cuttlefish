# Advanced Mathematical Modeling
**ID**: `mathSenior` · **Discipline**: Mathematics · **Year**: University Senior

## Course Philosophy
This course applies high-level mathematics to real-world, complex systems. It spans deterministic and stochastic models, optimization, and network theory, emphasizing the translation of physical, biological, or economic phenomena into mathematical language.

## Module 1: Deterministic Continuous Models (20%)
- Review of Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) and systems of ODEs
- Modeling population dynamics: Predator-Prey systems (Lotka-Volterra equations), competition models
- Epidemiology: The SIR model (Susceptible, Infected, Recovered) and calculating $R_0$
- Phase plane analysis, stability of equilibria, and bifurcations
- **Questions**: Finding the equilibrium points of a predator-prey system, determining the conditions under which an epidemic will occur in an SIR model

## Module 2: Optimization and Linear Programming (20%)
- Formulating optimization problems: Objective functions and constraints
- Linear Programming (LP): The Simplex Method and Duality Theory
- Integer Programming and Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP)
- Non-linear optimization: Gradient descent, Lagrange multipliers, and Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions
- Applications to supply chain logistics and resource allocation
- **Questions**: Formulating an LP problem for a manufacturing plant, interpreting the shadow price (dual value) of a constraint

## Module 3: Stochastic Models and Markov Processes (20%)
- Introduction to stochastic processes
- Discrete-time Markov Chains: Transition matrices, absorbing states, steady-state distributions
- Continuous-time Markov Chains and Poisson processes
- Queuing Theory (M/M/1 and M/M/c queues): Little's Law, wait times, and system utilization
- Applications to telecommunications and customer service systems
- **Questions**: Calculating the expected time to absorption in a Markov Chain, applying Little's Law to optimize a server cluster

## Module 4: Network Theory and Graph Models (20%)
- Graph theory fundamentals: Nodes, edges, degrees, adjacency matrices
- Centrality measures: Degree, Betweenness, Closeness, and Eigenvector centrality (PageRank algorithm)
- Network topologies: Random networks (Erdős-Rényi), Small-World networks (Watts-Strogatz), Scale-Free networks (Barabási-Albert)
- Dynamics on networks: Spread of information/disease across different network topologies
- **Questions**: Calculating the PageRank of a small web graph, simulating a contagion process on a scale-free network vs a random network

## Module 5: Simulation and Agent-Based Modeling (20%)
- Monte Carlo simulations: Generating random variables, numerical integration, and estimating probabilities
- Cellular Automata (e.g., Conway's Game of Life)
- Agent-Based Modeling (ABM): Simulating emergent behavior from simple local rules (e.g., flocking, traffic jams, segregation models like Schelling's)
- Validating and verifying simulation models against empirical data
- **Questions**: Designing a Monte Carlo simulation to estimate $\pi$, interpreting the emergent phenomena in a Schelling segregation model

## Stretch Zone
- Chaos Theory and fractals (The Lorenz attractor)
- Game Theory and evolutionary dynamics (Evolutionarily Stable Strategies)
