# English Composition 101
**ID**: `englishComposition101` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: University Freshman
**Aligns with**: the WPA Outcomes Statement for First-Year Composition and the ACRL Framework for Information Literacy

## Course Aim
This course moves students from the formulaic five-paragraph essay of secondary school toward flexible, thesis-driven academic writing. The shift is not about adding rules; it is about replacing a template with judgment. A student who has internalized "intro, three body paragraphs, conclusion" can pass a timed exam but cannot enter a scholarly conversation, decide how much to concede to an opponent, or organize a ten-page argument that no formula anticipated. The aim is to make writing a way of thinking: every chapter treats drafting, research, and revision as acts of inquiry rather than as a one-shot performance graded only on its surface.

The intellectual core is rhetorical: writing always answers a situation. Before a sentence is worth polishing, a writer must know who the reader is, what the writing is supposed to accomplish, what the genre and context permit, and what exigence makes the message worth producing at all. From that foundation the course builds outward — reading sources as arguments to be answered (the "they say / I say" move), staking contestable theses, structuring claims with evidence and warrants, evaluating and integrating sources ethically, synthesizing a body of research, and revising for meaning before polishing for style. Citation and academic integrity are taught not as bureaucratic compliance but as the visible trace of a writer's reasoning and respect for other voices.

By the end, a student can take an unfamiliar topic and turn it into a researchable question, find and judge sources, build an argument that survives a skeptical reader, document it accurately in MLA or APA, and revise it into prose a stranger can follow. Just as important, the student can articulate *why* a given choice works — naming the rhetorical situation, the structural logic, or the revision decision — so that the skill transfers to lab reports, application essays, and workplace writing long after the course ends.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve college-level writing: rhetorical situation, active reading and summary, thesis development, paragraph structure, argument and the Toulmin model, logical fallacies, research questions, information literacy and source evaluation, library and database search, source integration, quoting versus paraphrasing, MLA and APA citation, plagiarism and patchwriting, synthesis, the research essay, revision versus editing, peer review, academic style and concision, multimodal composition, and the first-year writing portfolio. Treat writing as a recursive process of thinking, not as the production of a fixed artifact.

The chapter order is dependency-driven. The rhetorical situation comes first (Chapter 1) because every later choice — what to read, what to claim, how to cite, what to cut — is downstream of audience, purpose, and context. Active reading (Chapter 2) precedes thesis work because a writer cannot enter a conversation they have not understood. Thesis and architecture (Chapter 3) and argument structure (Chapter 4) are taught back to back so students learn to distinguish a topic from a claim and a claim from its supporting logic. Research and information literacy (Chapter 5) then feed source integration and integrity (Chapter 6), which feed synthesis and the longer research essay (Chapter 7). Revision and peer review (Chapter 8) come before style and the portfolio (Chapter 9) because revision is structural and must precede sentence-level polish; multimodal adaptation and reflective transfer close the course (Chapter 10). Questions should reward writers who can name the rhetorical situation, distinguish revision from editing, integrate a source with context and analysis rather than dropping a quotation, and explain a choice rather than merely apply a rule.

## Chapter 1: The Writing Process and the Rhetorical Situation
- **Core questions**: What problem is a piece of writing trying to solve, and for whom? How do audience, purpose, context, and genre change every decision a writer makes? Why is writing recursive rather than linear, and what does "drafting to discover" mean?
- **Key concepts**: writer, audience, purpose, context, message, and exigence; genre and discourse community; the recursive process of invention, drafting, revision, and editing; low-stakes versus high-stakes writing; rhetorical analysis; reflection and transfer.
- **Applied skills**: identify the audience, purpose, and exigence of a published text; compare how two genres (an op-ed and a research article) treat the same topic; turn a broad subject into a writing problem; choose a drafting strategy that fits a real deadline; write a rhetorical analysis memo.
- **Common traps**: starting to draft before knowing the audience or purpose; treating genre conventions as universal rules rather than situation-specific choices; assuming a confident first draft needs no revision; confusing the recursive process with a one-way pipeline; mistaking a topic ("dorm food") for a purpose ("persuade administrators to extend dining hours").

## Chapter 2: Active Reading and the Academic Conversation
- **Core questions**: How do you read a source as an argument you can answer rather than a fact to absorb? What separates an accurate summary from a distortion? How does a writer enter an ongoing scholarly conversation instead of starting from scratch?
- **Key concepts**: annotation; accurate summary versus paraphrase; identifying a source's main claim, supporting points, assumptions, and implications; the "they say / I say" framework; intellectual generosity and the principle of charity; agreeing, disagreeing, and complicating.
- **Applied skills**: summarize an argument in a few sentences without inserting your own view; name the unstated assumption a source relies on; distinguish a main claim from a supporting point; write a response that agrees with a difference, disagrees with reasons, or complicates a claim.
- **Common traps**: summarizing your reaction instead of the source's actual argument; cherry-picking a quotation that misrepresents the author's position (the straw man of reading); confusing summary with paraphrase, or either with patchwriting; treating a source you disagree with uncharitably so the rebuttal looks easy; mining a source for quotable lines without grasping its overall claim.

## Chapter 3: Thesis, Claims, and Essay Architecture
- **Core questions**: What turns a topic into an arguable thesis? Why should an essay be organized by claims rather than by topics? How does an introduction set up stakes, and how does a conclusion synthesize rather than repeat?
- **Key concepts**: topic versus research question versus thesis; the arguable, specific, and significant claim; scope and stakes ("so what?"); claim-based outlining; paragraph unity and the topic sentence; the MEAL paragraph (Main idea, Evidence, Analysis, Link); transitions and old-to-new information flow; framing introductions and synthesizing conclusions.
- **Applied skills**: revise a vague or factual thesis into a contestable one; outline an essay by claims instead of subtopics; diagnose and fix weak paragraph order; build a MEAL paragraph that includes analysis, not just evidence; write a conclusion that states what the argument reveals and why it matters.
- **Common traps**: writing a thesis that merely announces a topic ("This essay discusses X") or states an uncontestable fact; confusing a strong opinion ("I hate X") with an arguable academic claim; dropping evidence into a paragraph without the analysis that interprets it; organizing by source rather than by idea; ending with a conclusion that repeats the introduction word for word or introduces brand-new evidence.

## Chapter 4: Argument, the Toulmin Model, and Logical Reasoning
- **Core questions**: What are the parts of an argument, and which one is usually left unstated? How do you appeal to a reader's reason, character, and emotion without manipulating them? How do you respond to a counterargument fairly instead of attacking the person who holds it?
- **Key concepts**: ethos, pathos, and logos; the Toulmin model — claim, grounds (evidence), warrant, backing, qualifier, and rebuttal; the warrant as the unstated assumption linking evidence to claim; counterclaim, concession, and rebuttal; qualifiers that scope a claim; common informal fallacies (slippery slope, hasty generalization, straw man, false dilemma, ad hominem, circular reasoning, appeal to authority, non sequitur).
- **Applied skills**: map an op-ed onto the Toulmin model and identify its warrant; add a qualifier to an overbroad claim; acknowledge a counterclaim and answer it with reasons rather than insults; diagnose a fallacy by describing its faulty inference structure (not just labeling it); repair a fallacious argument by reconstructing a valid version.
- **Common traps**: omitting the warrant and assuming evidence "speaks for itself"; treating a fallacy label as a knockout instead of explaining why the inference fails; rebutting a counterargument with ad hominem ("anyone who disagrees doesn't care about students"); circular reasoning that restates the claim as its own support; mistaking a strong emotional appeal for an evidence-based one; generalizing from a single anecdote (the group-chat sample) to a policy claim.

## Chapter 5: Research Questions and Information Literacy
- **Core questions**: How does a topic become a researchable question? What makes a source credible, and credible *for what purpose*? How do scholarly, trade, and popular sources differ, and when is each appropriate?
- **Key concepts**: research question versus topic; the ACRL idea that authority is constructed and contextual; keywords, controlled vocabulary, and Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT) plus quotation-mark phrasing; library databases versus open-web search; peer review; primary, secondary, and tertiary sources; scholarly, trade, and popular sources; evaluating currency, relevance, authority, accuracy, and purpose; lateral reading to verify a source.
- **Applied skills**: refine a broad topic into a focused, researchable question; build a Boolean search that narrows precisely; compare database results with open-web results on the same question; evaluate a source's credibility for a specific use; read laterally to check who is behind a website.
- **Common traps**: confusing "peer-reviewed" with "edited" or "popular but reputable"; using OR when you mean AND (broadening a search you meant to narrow); trusting authority in the abstract rather than for the specific claim and context; treating a high search ranking as evidence of credibility; settling a research question before reading enough to know whether it is answerable.

## Chapter 6: Source Integration, Citation, and Academic Integrity
- **Core questions**: When should you quote, when paraphrase, and when summarize? How do you integrate a source so it advances your argument instead of interrupting it? What is the line between paraphrase and plagiarism, and why does citation matter beyond avoiding punishment?
- **Key concepts**: quoting, paraphrasing, and summarizing; the quotation sandwich (signal phrase, quotation, analysis); signal phrases and rhetorical verbs; MLA 9 and its nine core elements (author, title of source, title of container, other contributors, version, number, publisher, publication date, location); APA 7 author-date citations, including narrative versus parenthetical form and "et al." for three or more authors; in-text citation versus the works-cited or references list; plagiarism, patchwriting, and common knowledge.
- **Applied skills**: integrate a quotation with a signal phrase and follow-up analysis; turn a quotation into an accurate paraphrase in your own structure; choose when a quotation's exact wording matters versus when to summarize; build a basic MLA works-cited entry from the core-element template and a basic APA reference; identify patchwriting and repair it into a true paraphrase with a citation.
- **Common traps**: dropping a quotation into a paragraph with no introduction or analysis (the "drive-by" quotation); patchwriting — swapping a few synonyms while keeping the source's sentence structure — and believing it is paraphrase; thinking length determines summary versus paraphrase; omitting a citation because the idea "felt like common knowledge"; assuming an annotated bibliography or a works-cited list replaces in-text citation; confusing MLA and APA conventions (page numbers vs. publication year, "Works Cited" vs. "References").

## Chapter 7: Synthesis and the Research Essay
- **Core questions**: How do you put multiple sources into conversation instead of summarizing them one after another? How do you organize a research essay of seven to ten pages around an argument rather than a list of sources? What do you do when new evidence complicates your working thesis?
- **Key concepts**: synthesis versus summary; mapping sources by position, method, or finding; identifying agreement, tension, and gaps; using one source to extend, qualify, or complicate another; the research essay's working thesis, evidence plan, and counterargument; revising a claim in light of new evidence; the difference between a report (here is what sources say) and an argument (here is what I conclude and why).
- **Applied skills**: group sources into a literature map by stance or method; write a synthesis paragraph that makes a new claim from two or more sources; use a second source to complicate a first; structure a multi-page essay by claims with integrated evidence; write a revision memo explaining how new evidence reshaped the thesis.
- **Common traps**: "source dumping" — stacking summaries side by side and calling it synthesis; organizing the essay source-by-source instead of idea-by-idea; cherry-picking sources that agree and ignoring the ones that complicate the claim; dismissing a source on personal preference rather than evidence; clinging to the original thesis after the evidence has undermined it.

## Chapter 8: Revision, Peer Review, and Feedback
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between revision and editing, and why does order matter? How do you give feedback that helps a writer think rather than just marking errors? How do you receive criticism on your own draft without either ignoring it or surrendering your argument?
- **Key concepts**: revision (re-seeing meaning, structure, and argument) versus editing and proofreading (sentence-level correctness); global versus local concerns; reverse outlining to test structure; descriptive, evaluative, and directive peer feedback; higher-order concerns before lower-order concerns; the writer's authority over which feedback to take.
- **Applied skills**: diagnose whether a draft's problem is structural or cosmetic and fix the structural one first; reverse-outline a draft to expose a thesis-evidence mismatch; give a peer specific, actionable feedback rather than "good job"; prioritize global revisions over comma fixes; write a revision plan from a set of peer comments.
- **Common traps**: proofreading commas before fixing a thesis that contradicts the body; treating revision as recopying the draft more neatly; giving vague praise ("I liked it") that the writer cannot act on; fixing every reader comment uncritically and losing the argument's coherence; confusing more academic-sounding vocabulary with a stronger argument.

## Chapter 9: Academic Style, Clarity, and Concision
- **Core questions**: What makes academic prose clear without making it dull? How do you cut filler without flattening your voice? How do conventions of style differ across disciplines and registers?
- **Key concepts**: clarity, concision, and sentence rhythm; active versus passive voice and when each is appropriate; the old-to-new flow and topic-string cohesion; nominalizations and empty filler ("it is important to note that"); concrete subjects and strong verbs; tone and register; discipline-specific conventions; sentence variety.
- **Applied skills**: revise a wordy, passive sentence into a concrete active one; cut filler phrases without losing meaning; replace a nominalization with a verb; vary sentence length for rhythm; adjust register to fit an academic versus a public audience.
- **Common traps**: inflating vocabulary to sound scholarly rather than to be precise; assuming passive voice is always wrong (it is correct when the actor is unknown or irrelevant); adding intensifiers ("very," "really," "in a significant manner") instead of specifics; mistaking longer sentences for more sophisticated ones; confusing a stylistic preference with a grammatical rule.

## Chapter 10: Multimodal Composition, Reflection, and the Portfolio
- **Core questions**: How does an argument change when it moves from a printed essay to a podcast, slide deck, poster, or public op-ed? What does reflection reveal about a writer's growth, and why does it support transfer? How do you assemble and frame a portfolio that demonstrates development, not just polish?
- **Key concepts**: multimodal composition (linguistic, visual, aural, spatial, gestural modes); adapting argument and evidence for a new medium and audience; design and accessibility basics; reflective writing and metacognition; transfer of learning to new contexts; the writing portfolio and its reflective introduction.
- **Applied skills**: adapt an academic argument into a podcast script, poster, or slide deck for a public audience; explain how a modal change alters evidence and emphasis; write a reflective portfolio introduction that names specific growth across the term; curate revised work as evidence of process, not just product.
- **Common traps**: treating multimodal adaptation as decoration rather than a rhetorical re-design for a new audience; preserving an essay's structure unchanged in a medium that demands a different one; writing reflection as vague self-praise instead of evidence-based analysis of choices; submitting only polished final drafts and hiding the revision that demonstrates growth; ignoring accessibility (alt text, captions, contrast) in visual and aural work.

## Capstone
Students assemble and defend a first-year writing portfolio that demonstrates growth across the entire writing process, not just a stack of finished essays. The portfolio must include: (1) a rhetorical analysis that explains how audience, purpose, context, and genre shape one published text; (2) a short argument essay built on the Toulmin model with a clear claim, evidence, warrant, counterargument, and rebuttal; (3) an annotated bibliography that summarizes and evaluates sources for a developing research question; (4) a seven-to-ten-page research essay that synthesizes sources around an argument, integrates quotations with analysis, and documents them accurately in MLA or APA; (5) a revision memo tracing at least one substantive global revision and the reasoning behind it; and (6) a reflective introduction that names specific growth in invention, research, source use, revision, and style and argues for how those skills will transfer. Strong portfolios show a writer who can name *why* their choices work — connecting a structural decision, a source integration, or a revision to the rhetorical situation it serves.

## Assessment Ideas
- Low-stakes reading responses and "they say / I say" summaries to build the conversation habit before high-stakes drafts.
- Thesis workshops and claim-based outlining exercises that separate topics from arguable claims.
- Toulmin argument maps and fallacy-diagnosis drills that require explaining the faulty inference, not just labeling it.
- Annotated bibliographies and source-integration exercises (the same source used as quotation, paraphrase, and synthesized evidence).
- Peer-review sessions and draft conferences graded on the usefulness of feedback, not just on receiving it.
- Revision memos that document a global revision and the reasoning behind it.
- Portfolio assessment that rewards development over time, with a reflective introduction, rather than a single polished product.
