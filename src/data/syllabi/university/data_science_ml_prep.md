# Data Science / ML Interview Prep
**ID**: `ml` - **Discipline**: Technology - **Year**: University Senior / Career Hopper

## Course Aim
This course prepares graduating students and career changers to pass data science and machine learning interviews by building genuine fluency across the full analytical workflow rather than memorizing trivia. Interviews in 2026 rarely reward people who can recite the definition of precision but cannot say why precision is the wrong metric for a rare-disease screen. They reward people who can frame an ambiguous business question, write correct SQL against a messy schema, reason about uncertainty out loud, train a defensible baseline, and explain a tradeoff in two sentences a product manager understands. We train all of those skills in parallel.

The organizing idea is that interview preparation and portfolio preparation are the same activity. Every technical concept in the course becomes a small, reproducible artifact: a cleaning notebook, a query set, a statistics memo, an experiment brief, an evaluation report. Students arrive at the loop with concrete work to point at, which converts vague "tell me about a project" prompts into specific, confident answers. The artifacts also force the habits interviewers probe for indirectly: stating assumptions, sanity-checking row counts, knowing the grain of a table, and admitting what the data cannot tell you.

The course is deliberately breadth-first and depth-on-demand. It assumes a working knowledge of programming and college statistics but re-derives the load-bearing ideas (bias-variance, leakage, the central limit theorem, base rates) because those are exactly the places candidates get caught. We optimize for the interview surface area that actually appears in screens and on-sites at product companies, analytics teams, and applied-ML groups, and we are explicit about what we are deprioritizing (deep learning internals, distributed-systems engineering, research-paper reproduction) so students spend their limited prep time well.

## Course Design Notes
This is a career-prep studio built from short concept briefings, timed coding drills, case discussions, and project sprints, with weekly mock interviews. Route questions here when learners need data science or ML interview prep, ML fundamentals, statistics and probability review, Pandas practice, SQL analysis, A/B testing and product-sense cases, model evaluation, metric selection, take-home project coaching, or responsible-AI discussion. Emphasize clear reasoning, reproducible notebooks, defensible assumptions, base-rate awareness, and concise communication over memorized API surface. Whenever a question can be answered two ways — a fast, interpretable baseline versus a heavier model — push students to articulate the tradeoff rather than reflexively reaching for the complex option, because that judgment is what distinguishes a hire from a no-hire.

## Chapter 1: Data Workflow and Problem Framing
- **Core questions**: How do you turn "engagement is down, find out why" into a concrete analysis with a measurable target? What is the unit of analysis in this dataset, and why does it determine everything downstream? Why is a baseline the first thing you should produce, not the last?
- **Key concepts**: The data science lifecycle, stakeholder/clarifying questions, units of analysis, target variable definition, features vs. labels, baselines, data provenance and lineage, reproducibility, scoping, and structured technical storytelling (situation -> approach -> finding -> recommendation).
- **Applied skills**: Decompose a vague product question into a hypothesis, a success metric, and a data inventory; identify the observation unit and grain of a table on sight; state assumptions and risks before touching code; write a one-paragraph "what would change my recommendation" caveat.
- **Common traps**: Jumping to modeling before defining the target; reporting an accuracy number without naming the baseline it beats (95% accuracy is worthless if the positive class is 4%); confusing the unit of analysis (user vs. session vs. event) and silently double-counting; treating a metric as if it were the goal rather than a proxy for it.

## Chapter 2: Python, Notebooks, and Pandas Fluency
- **Core questions**: Why did this merge change the row count, and is that a bug or expected? How do you write preprocessing so a reviewer can rerun it and get the same numbers? What is the difference between a chained `.loc` assignment that works and one that triggers SettingWithCopyWarning?
- **Key concepts**: Jupyter workflow and execution order, Python data types and functions, NumPy arrays and vectorization, Pandas Series/DataFrame, filtering and boolean masks, `groupby`/`agg`, `merge` join types, `NaN` handling, datetime and string methods, `apply` vs. vectorized ops, and readable notebook structure.
- **Applied skills**: Clean a messy column and document each transformation; write a reusable, tested preprocessing function; diagnose a row-count change after a merge using a key-uniqueness check; restart-and-run-all to prove reproducibility; profile a slow loop and vectorize it.
- **Common traps**: One-to-many joins quietly fanning out rows (and then aggregating the inflated result); relying on hidden notebook state so results break on restart; using `inplace=True` and `apply(axis=1)` as defaults without understanding cost; treating `NaN == NaN` as `True`; mutating a slice and being surprised the original did not change.

## Chapter 3: SQL and Relational Data for Analysts
- **Core questions**: What is the grain of the result of this query, and does it match what the stakeholder asked for? When a join duplicates rows, how do you find the offending key? Why compute this in SQL before pulling into Pandas?
- **Key concepts**: Tables, primary/foreign keys, logical query order (FROM -> WHERE -> GROUP BY -> HAVING -> SELECT -> ORDER BY -> LIMIT), `JOIN` types, aggregation, `HAVING` vs. `WHERE`, CTEs, window functions (`ROW_NUMBER`, `RANK`, `LAG`, running aggregates), `NULL` semantics, three-valued logic, and metric numerators/denominators.
- **Applied skills**: Write top-N-per-group with a window function; compute a 7-day rolling average; deduplicate with `ROW_NUMBER()` in a CTE; build a funnel/retention query; reason about which side of a `LEFT JOIN` introduces NULLs.
- **Common traps**: Filtering an outer-joined table in `WHERE` (silently turning a LEFT JOIN into an INNER JOIN — the filter belongs in the `ON` clause); `COUNT(column)` skipping NULLs while `COUNT(*)` does not; `NULL` comparisons returning unknown rather than false; assuming `SELECT` aliases are usable in `WHERE`; mixing grains so an aggregate is computed over fanned-out rows.

## Chapter 4: Probability and Statistical Reasoning
- **Core questions**: Given a test that is 99% accurate for a disease with 0.1% prevalence, what is the probability a positive patient is actually sick — and why is the answer surprisingly low? What exactly does a 95% confidence interval mean, and what does it not mean? Why does the central limit theorem let you use a normal approximation even when the data are skewed?
- **Key concepts**: Random variables, conditional probability, Bayes' theorem and base rates, common distributions, expectation/variance/covariance, correlation vs. causation, sampling and standard error, the central limit theorem, confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, p-values, effect size, statistical power, and Type I vs. Type II error.
- **Applied skills**: Compute a posterior probability from sensitivity, specificity, and prevalence; choose an appropriate test for two groups (t-test, proportion test, Mann-Whitney) and justify it; construct and correctly interpret a confidence interval; explain the CLT to a nontechnical teammate in plain language.
- **Common traps**: Interpreting a p-value as the probability the null is true; reading "95% CI" as "95% chance the parameter is in this interval"; ignoring base rates so a positive test feels far more conclusive than it is; equating statistical significance with practical importance; confusing standard deviation with standard error.

## Chapter 5: Exploratory Analysis and Product Experiments
- **Core questions**: Daily active users dropped 8% overnight — what is your diagnostic decision tree? How do you size and run an A/B test for a ranking change, and how do you pick guardrail metrics? When can an overall metric move one way while every segment moves the other (Simpson's paradox), and how do you catch it?
- **Key concepts**: EDA and chart choice, segmentation and cohorts, outliers, funnels, A/B testing, randomization and the unit of randomization, primary vs. guardrail metrics, minimum detectable effect, power and required sample size, test duration, novelty and primacy effects, network interference, multiple comparisons, and Simpson's paradox.
- **Applied skills**: Build a structured root-cause diagnosis (instrumentation bug vs. seasonality vs. real change vs. mix shift); design an experiment with hypothesis, randomization unit, primary metric, guardrails, MDE, duration, and a decision rule; pick a chart that does not mislead; segment a top-line move to find what actually changed.
- **Common traps**: Peeking at results and stopping early when significance appears (inflating false positives); randomizing at the wrong unit so treatment leaks across users; reading correlation in an EDA plot as causation; ignoring multiple comparisons when slicing into dozens of segments; declaring a win on a novelty bump that fades.

## Chapter 6: Machine Learning Foundations
- **Core questions**: For a 5,000-row tabular dataset, why is logistic regression or gradient boosting usually the right first move and not a neural network? Where is the leakage in this feature list? What does the bias-variance tradeoff predict will happen as you add features without more data?
- **Key concepts**: Supervised vs. unsupervised learning, train/validation/test splits, k-fold and time-series cross-validation, data leakage, overfitting/underfitting, the bias-variance tradeoff, regularization (L1/L2), linear and logistic regression, decision trees, random forests, gradient boosting (XGBoost/LightGBM), k-means, and PCA.
- **Applied skills**: Choose and justify a model for a small tabular problem; spot leakage from target-derived or future-information features; set up a leak-free cross-validation pipeline that fits scaling/encoding inside the fold; explain L1 vs. L2 and when each helps; compare an interpretable baseline against a stronger model honestly.
- **Common traps**: Fitting the scaler or imputer on the full dataset before splitting (leakage); using k-fold on time-ordered data and leaking the future; chasing accuracy on imbalanced data; treating tree feature importances as causal effects; reaching for deep learning on small tabular data where boosting wins on accuracy, speed, and explainability.

## Chapter 7: Evaluation, Error Analysis, and Communication
- **Core questions**: For fraud detection where positives are 0.5% of traffic, which metric do you optimize and why is accuracy useless here? Your model is 90% accurate but the business is unhappy — how do you do error analysis to find out why? How do you choose the classification threshold for a model that informs a costly intervention?
- **Key concepts**: The confusion matrix, accuracy, precision, recall, F1, ROC AUC vs. PR AUC, log loss, RMSE/MAE, calibration, threshold selection, class imbalance, the precision-recall tradeoff, subgroup/fairness performance, residual analysis, and model cards.
- **Applied skills**: Map a business cost structure (cost of a false positive vs. a false negative) to a metric and a threshold; build and read a confusion matrix; choose PR AUC over ROC AUC under heavy imbalance and explain why; do slice-based error analysis to find a failing subgroup; write a concise model card a stakeholder can act on.
- **Common traps**: Defaulting to accuracy on imbalanced data; quoting ROC AUC when the positive class is rare (PR AUC is more honest); leaving the threshold at 0.5 without tying it to costs; reporting only aggregate metrics that hide a subgroup the model fails; confusing a well-discriminating model (high AUC) with a well-calibrated one.

## Chapter 8: Ethics, Deployment Basics, and Interview Readiness
- **Core questions**: What are the realistic ethical and privacy risks in this dataset, and how would you mitigate them? After a model ships, how do you know it is still working — what do you monitor, and what is the difference between data drift and concept drift? How do you structure a take-home so a reviewer can grade it in fifteen minutes?
- **Key concepts**: Privacy, consent, and data retention; PII handling; bias and fairness; explainability; model monitoring; data drift vs. concept drift; batch vs. online scoring; feature stores and model registries; take-home structure; portfolio polish; and behavioral/STAR interview framing.
- **Applied skills**: Audit a dataset for ethical and privacy risk and propose mitigations; design a basic monitoring plan (input distribution checks, performance tracking, alert thresholds); write a clear take-home README with setup, approach, results, and next steps; answer a tradeoff question with evidence; tell a project story in STAR form without rambling.
- **Common traps**: Treating fairness as a single post-hoc metric instead of a definition-dependent design choice; assuming static performance and never planning for drift; over-engineering a take-home with unnecessary deep learning instead of a clean baseline and good writeup; reciting responsibilities in behavioral answers instead of owning a specific result; submitting a notebook that does not run end-to-end on a fresh clone.

## Capstone
Students complete an end-to-end data science or ML interview project on a real or instructor-approved dataset and defend it in a 45-minute mock on-site. Required components: (1) a problem brief with target definition, success metric, and stated assumptions; (2) a reproducible cleaning notebook that runs top-to-bottom on a fresh clone; (3) a SQL or relational analysis with at least one window function and explicit notes on grain; (4) an EDA section and either a designed experiment or a clearly framed observational analysis with stated limitations; (5) a statistics memo quantifying uncertainty; (6) at least one ML model with a documented, leak-free baseline-and-candidate comparison; (7) an evaluation report with the right metric for the problem, a confusion matrix or residual analysis, threshold/subgroup checks, and a model card; (8) an ethics-and-monitoring note. The defense is graded like a real loop: candidates must justify every methodological choice, name the tradeoffs they rejected, and field "what would you do with one more week" and "where is this most likely to fail in production" follow-ups.

## Assessment Ideas
- Timed coding drills in Python, Pandas, and SQL with short written explanations of approach.
- Notebook reviews scored for reproducibility, explicit assumptions, clean code, and meaningful interpretation.
- Mock interviews rotating through probability/base-rate problems, ML concepts, product-sense and A/B cases, and metric/threshold selection.
- Take-home project checkpoints for scoping, cleaning, modeling, and communication, plus an ethics review.
- Final portfolio review and live defense graded with a rubric covering technical correctness, evidence quality, communication, and readiness for follow-up probing.
