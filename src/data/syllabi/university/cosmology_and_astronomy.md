# Cosmology and Astronomy
**ID**: `cosmologyAndAstronomy` - **Discipline**: Science - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
This course studies the universe across every accessible scale — from the geometry of the night sky and the orbits of nearby bodies, through the births and deaths of stars, out to galaxies, black holes, and the expanding cosmos as a whole. The organizing idea is that astronomy is an *observational* science: we cannot run experiments on stars, so every claim must be reconstructed from light, gravity, and a handful of physical laws applied with discipline. Students learn to move fluently between a physical model (Newtonian gravity, blackbody radiation, general relativity, the Friedmann equations) and the evidence that constrains it (spectra, light curves, rotation curves, the cosmic microwave background, gravitational-wave strain).

A second, equally important aim is calibrated humility. Modern cosmology is built on extraordinary claims — that 95% of the energy content of the universe is dark matter and dark energy, that spacetime began expanding 13.8 billion years ago, that information about distant galaxies arrives compressed into a few photons per square meter. The course trains students to ask of every result: what was actually measured, what was assumed, and how big is the uncertainty? Students should leave able to distinguish a 5-sigma detection from a suggestive hint, a model-dependent inference from a direct observation, and an established consensus from an active frontier where the experts themselves disagree.

The mathematical level assumes comfort with calculus, vectors, and basic differential equations, and willingness to carry units and orders of magnitude through every estimate. But equations here are never decorative: each one earns its place by connecting to something a telescope or detector can register. By the end, students should be able to read a research-level figure — a Hubble diagram, an H-R diagram, a power spectrum, a chirp waveform — and explain both what it shows and what would falsify the model it supports.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve observational astronomy, telescopes and detectors, spectroscopy and the Doppler effect, celestial mechanics and Kepler's laws, stellar structure and evolution, the H-R diagram, supernovae and compact objects, galaxies and large-scale structure, the cosmic distance ladder, general relativity, black holes and gravitational waves, Big Bang cosmology, the CMB, big-bang nucleosynthesis, dark matter, dark energy, inflation, and the ultimate fate of the universe.

Keep evidence visible throughout. A well-posed item should let students practice the discipline of asking "how do we know this?" rather than reciting a fact. Numerical items should reward order-of-magnitude reasoning and correct unit handling over plug-and-chug. Where the field is genuinely unsettled in 2026 — the Hubble tension, DESI's hints of evolving dark energy, JWST's unexpectedly mature early galaxies — present the controversy honestly rather than pretending to a false consensus. Avoid items that turn on naming Roman gods or memorizing orbital periods; those belong to a lower tier. The standard of difficulty is a strong senior undergraduate who could go on to a graduate program.

## Chapter 1: Light, Telescopes, and the Tools of Observation
**Core questions**: How does every property of a distant object — temperature, composition, motion, distance — get encoded in the light we receive? What sets the resolution and sensitivity limits of a telescope, and why do we build instruments across the entire electromagnetic spectrum?
**Key concepts**: The electromagnetic spectrum and atmospheric windows; blackbody radiation, Wien's law, and the Stefan-Boltzmann law; emission, absorption, and continuous spectra (Kirchhoff's laws); the Doppler shift and radial velocity; angular resolution and the diffraction limit (~lambda/D); collecting area and limiting magnitude; the apparent/absolute magnitude system and the distance modulus; why space telescopes, adaptive optics, and interferometry exist.
**Applied skills**: Estimate a star's surface temperature from its peak wavelength or color; read an absorption spectrum to identify elements and a radial velocity; convert between apparent magnitude, absolute magnitude, and distance; compare the trade-offs of optical, radio, infrared, X-ray, and gravitational-wave observatories.
**Common traps**: Treating the magnitude scale as linear or "bigger = brighter" (it is logarithmic and inverted); confusing redshift due to motion with cosmological redshift; assuming a bigger telescope mainly means more magnification (it means more light and finer resolution); forgetting that Earth's atmosphere blocks most of the spectrum, which is *why* we orbit telescopes.

## Chapter 2: Celestial Mechanics and Gravitational Weighing
**Core questions**: How do Kepler's empirical laws follow from Newtonian gravity, and how does that let us "weigh" objects we can never touch? Why is orbital motion the single most powerful tool astronomers have for measuring mass?
**Key concepts**: Kepler's three laws and Newton's generalization (P^2 = 4*pi^2*a^3 / [G(M+m)]); the gravitational two-body problem, conic-section orbits, and orbital energy; escape velocity and the vis-viva equation; the virial theorem (2K + U = 0) for bound systems; tidal forces and the Roche limit; center of mass and the radial-velocity method for exoplanets and binary stars.
**Applied skills**: Use Kepler's third law to derive the mass of the Sun, a planet with a moon, or a galaxy from its rotation; apply the virial theorem to estimate a cluster's mass from velocity dispersion; compute escape velocity and orbital speed; explain how a wobble in a star's spectrum reveals an unseen planet.
**Common traps**: Forgetting that Kepler's third law in its simple form assumes the central mass dominates; conflating orbital period with rotation period; thinking objects in orbit are "weightless" because gravity is absent (they are in free fall, and gravity is what holds the orbit); using the virial theorem on systems that are not gravitationally bound or relaxed.

## Chapter 3: Stellar Structure, Fusion, and the Lives of Stars
**Core questions**: What holds a star up against its own gravity for billions of years, and what determines whether it ends as a white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole? Why does a single number — mass — set almost everything about a star's fate?
**Key concepts**: Hydrostatic equilibrium and the virial relation between gravity and pressure; the proton-proton chain and CNO cycle; the mass-luminosity relation (L ~ M^3.5 on the main sequence); the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram and its physical axes; star formation, the main sequence, red giants, and the asymptotic giant branch; electron and neutron degeneracy pressure; the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 solar masses); core-collapse vs. Type Ia supernovae; nucleosynthesis and where the elements come from.
**Applied skills**: Place a star on the H-R diagram from its luminosity and temperature and infer its evolutionary stage; estimate main-sequence lifetime from mass (lifetime ~ M/L ~ M^-2.5); contrast the end states of a 0.5, 8, and 25 solar-mass star; trace the origin of carbon, iron, and gold to specific stellar processes.
**Common traps**: Believing more massive stars live longer (they have more fuel but burn it far faster, so they live *shorter*); confusing the two supernova types and their progenitors; thinking the Sun will explode (it will not — it lacks the mass and will become a white dwarf); treating the H-R diagram as a track a single star follows in place rather than a snapshot of populations.

## Chapter 4: Galaxies, Rotation Curves, and the Distance Ladder
**Core questions**: How do we measure distances to objects far beyond parallax range, and how do those distances reveal that most of a galaxy's mass is invisible? How is the universe organized on the largest scales?
**Key concepts**: Milky Way structure and stellar populations; Hubble's tuning-fork galaxy classification; flat galaxy rotation curves and the dark-matter halo inference; the cosmic distance ladder (parallax → main-sequence fitting → Cepheid period-luminosity relation → Type Ia supernovae); active galactic nuclei and quasars powered by supermassive black holes; galaxy clusters, the intracluster medium, and the cosmic web of filaments and voids.
**Applied skills**: Use a Cepheid's period to get its luminosity and hence its galaxy's distance; interpret a flat rotation curve as evidence for unseen mass; classify galaxies from morphology; explain why standard candles must be *standardizable* to be useful.
**Common traps**: Thinking flat rotation curves can be explained by visible matter alone (the enclosed luminous mass falls off, yet orbital speed stays constant); confusing the rungs of the distance ladder or skipping the calibration that links them; assuming quasars are nearby stars (they are extremely luminous, extremely distant AGN); treating "standard candle" as meaning identical rather than calibratable.

## Chapter 5: General Relativity, Black Holes, and Gravitational Waves
**Core questions**: When does Newtonian gravity break down, and what does it mean for mass to curve spacetime? What can we actually observe about a black hole, given that nothing escapes its horizon?
**Key concepts**: The equivalence principle and spacetime curvature; gravitational lensing and time dilation; the Schwarzschild radius (r_s = 2GM/c^2) and the event horizon; rotating (Kerr) black holes, accretion disks, and relativistic jets; the no-hair theorem; Hawking radiation and the information paradox; the Event Horizon Telescope images of M87* and Sgr A*; gravitational waves, the LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA network, chirp signals, and multi-messenger astronomy (GW170817).
**Applied skills**: Compute a Schwarzschild radius for any mass and compare it to the object's physical size; explain how a lensing arc or Einstein ring constrains foreground mass; read a gravitational-wave chirp to infer that two compact objects merged and roughly how massive they were; distinguish what the EHT "image" of a black hole actually shows (the shadow and surrounding emission, not the horizon itself).
**Common traps**: Imagining black holes as cosmic vacuum cleaners that suck in everything (orbits far from the horizon are ordinary); thinking the event horizon is a physical surface; believing we have "seen" a singularity or the horizon directly; confusing gravitational lensing with the gravitational redshift of the lensed light.

## Chapter 6: The Expanding Universe and the Hot Big Bang
**Core questions**: What is the evidence that the universe is expanding and was once hot and dense — and what does "expansion" actually mean for space, light, and distance? How do we reconstruct conditions a fraction of a second after the beginning?
**Key concepts**: Hubble-Lemaitre law (v = H0*d) and the scale factor a(t); cosmological redshift as stretching of space, not Doppler motion; the Hubble time as a rough age estimate; big-bang nucleosynthesis and the predicted ~75% H / ~25% He abundance; recombination and the release of the CMB at redshift ~1100; the near-perfect 2.725 K blackbody spectrum of the CMB and its tiny anisotropies; the horizon and flatness problems and the inflationary solution.
**Applied skills**: Estimate the age of the universe from H0; convert a redshift to a scale factor (1+z = 1/a); explain why the CMB blackbody spectrum and the light-element abundances are independent confirmations of a hot early universe; articulate what problems inflation was invented to solve and what evidence supports it.
**Common traps**: Treating cosmological redshift as ordinary Doppler shift (space itself expands, so distant galaxies can recede faster than light without violating relativity); thinking the Big Bang happened "at a point" in pre-existing space; assuming the CMB is light from the stars or the explosion itself (it is the relic glow of the hot plasma at recombination); confusing inflation with the general expansion.

## Chapter 7: Dark Matter, Dark Energy, and the Fate of the Cosmos
**Core questions**: What independent lines of evidence force us to conclude that ~27% of the universe is dark matter and ~68% is dark energy? How do the density parameters set the geometry and ultimate fate of the universe — and is the standard model starting to crack?
**Key concepts**: Converging dark-matter evidence (galaxy rotation curves, cluster velocity dispersions, gravitational lensing, the Bullet Cluster, CMB acoustic peaks); the cosmological constant and accelerating expansion from Type Ia supernovae (1998); the Friedmann equations and the critical density; density parameters Omega_m, Omega_Lambda, Omega_k and the flat/open/closed trichotomy; the Lambda-CDM concordance model; possible fates (heat death/Big Freeze, Big Crunch, Big Rip); 2026 frontier tensions — the ~5-sigma Hubble tension (SH0ES ~73 vs. Planck/CMB ~67.4 km/s/Mpc) and DESI DR2's multi-sigma hint of *evolving* dark energy (w0-wa) rather than a pure cosmological constant.
**Applied skills**: Lay out why no single observation establishes dark matter but the *convergence* of independent probes does; interpret a supernova Hubble diagram as evidence for acceleration; reason from Omega values to spatial geometry and long-term fate; state precisely what the Hubble tension is (a disagreement between early- and late-universe measurements of the same number) and why it might signal new physics.
**Common traps**: Conflating dark matter (clumps, gravitates, explains structure) with dark energy (smooth, drives acceleration); thinking dark matter is just dim ordinary matter (the CMB and nucleosynthesis cap the baryon budget far below the total); assuming the Hubble tension is a measurement error (JWST has largely ruled out the leading systematic explanations); treating Lambda-CDM as final rather than an excellent but possibly incomplete fit.

## Chapter 8: Frontiers, Uncertainty, and Communicating Cosmology
**Core questions**: How do astronomers quantify what they don't know, and how do you tell a robust result from a tantalizing fluctuation? Where is the field genuinely open in 2026, and how should those open questions be communicated honestly to the public?
**Key concepts**: Statistical significance, the sigma scale, and the "look-elsewhere" effect; systematic vs. statistical uncertainty; parameter estimation and confidence contours; the role of large surveys and simulations (DESI, Euclid, Rubin/LSST, JWST); the falsifiability boundary around inflationary and multiverse claims; current live debates — the Hubble tension, evolving dark energy, JWST's surprisingly massive and mature high-redshift galaxies and "little red dots," and early supermassive black holes.
**Applied skills**: Read a confidence-contour or posterior plot and state what it does and does not constrain; judge whether a "tension" is a discovery, a hint, or noise; critique a popular-science headline against the underlying result; explain a frontier controversy to a general audience without overclaiming or false balance.
**Common traps**: Reporting a 2-sigma hint as a discovery; treating "we don't know yet" as a failure rather than an honest, quantified state of knowledge; presenting speculative, currently-untestable ideas (multiverse, some string-landscape claims) as if they had the same evidential status as the CMB; assuming a striking simulation image is itself data.

## Capstone
Students produce a research-style cosmology or astronomy report on a single well-defined question — for example: "Does the flat rotation curve of galaxy NGC ____ require dark matter, or can MOND account for it?", "What does the GW chirp from a specific LIGO event tell us about the merging compact objects?", "How does the CMB acoustic-peak structure pin down the curvature and matter content of the universe?", or "Is DESI's evidence for evolving dark energy strong enough to abandon the cosmological constant?" The report must (1) state the physical model and its key equations, (2) present the actual observational evidence with a real figure or dataset, (3) carry units and uncertainties through every estimate, (4) explicitly separate what is measured from what is assumed, and (5) close with a clear, accurate explanation pitched to an educated non-specialist. Strong capstones name what observation would *falsify* the favored model.

## Assessment Ideas
Use quantitative problem sets (Kepler weighing, Schwarzschild radii, distance moduli, Hubble-time estimates), spectrum and light-curve interpretation, H-R diagram and rotation-curve reading, figure critiques of real papers, a model-comparison memo on dark matter or dark energy, and the capstone presentation. Reward students who connect every mathematical claim to a specific observation and who report uncertainties rather than bare numbers.
