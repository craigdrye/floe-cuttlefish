# Multivariable Calculus
**ID**: `multivariableCalculus` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
Develop a rigorous and application-aware command of calculus for functions of several variables. Students learn the geometry of vectors and surfaces, partial derivatives, gradients, optimization, multiple integration, vector fields, line and surface integrals, and the major integral theorems of vector calculus so they can model change, accumulation, flow, flux, and conservation laws in mathematics, physics, engineering, data science, and economics.

## Course Design Notes
This is a university multivariable calculus course with computational fluency, geometric reasoning, and theorem-level interpretation. Route questions here when students need vectors in two or three dimensions, space curves, surfaces, partial derivatives, chain rules, gradients, tangent planes, optimization, Lagrange multipliers, double and triple integrals, coordinate changes, vector fields, line integrals, surface integrals, Green's Theorem, Stokes' Theorem, the Divergence Theorem, or modeling with fields and flux. Emphasize diagrams, domains, units, orientation, hypotheses of theorems, and the connection between local rates and global accumulation.

## Chapter 1: Vectors, Curves, and Geometry in Space
- **Key concepts**: Coordinate systems, vectors in R^2 and R^3, dot products, cross products, projections, lines, planes, distances, quadric surfaces, cylindrical and spherical coordinates, vector-valued functions, velocity, acceleration, arc length, curvature, and motion in space.
- **Practice questions**: Find equations for lines and planes; compute angles and projections; identify a quadric surface; describe a space curve parametrically; compute velocity, speed, and curvature.
- **Student output**: A geometry packet with sketches, vector computations, parametrized curves, and written explanations connecting algebraic equations to spatial objects.

## Chapter 2: Functions of Several Variables and Partial Derivatives
- **Key concepts**: Scalar fields, domains, graphs, level curves, level surfaces, limits, continuity, partial derivatives, higher partial derivatives, Clairaut's Theorem, tangent planes, linear approximation, differentials, local linearity, and error estimates.
- **Practice questions**: Determine the domain of a two-variable function; show a limit fails by path testing; compute first and second partial derivatives; find a tangent plane; use linear approximation to estimate change.
- **Student output**: A partial-derivatives problem set with domain diagrams, limit arguments, derivative computations, and a short explanation of local linearization.

## Chapter 3: Chain Rules, Gradients, and Directional Change
- **Key concepts**: Multivariable chain rules, implicit differentiation, directional derivatives, gradients, steepest ascent, normal vectors, level-set geometry, Jacobian matrices, differentiability, and sensitivity analysis.
- **Practice questions**: Apply the chain rule to nested dependencies; compute a directional derivative; use the gradient to find a normal line or tangent plane; interpret a Jacobian in a model.
- **Student output**: A gradient and chain-rule lab tracing dependencies in a physical, economic, or data model and explaining the meaning of each derivative.

## Chapter 4: Optimization and Constrained Problems
- **Key concepts**: Critical points, local extrema, saddle points, Hessian matrices, second derivative test, absolute extrema on compact regions, boundary checking, constrained optimization, Lagrange multipliers, multiple constraints, and applied optimization.
- **Practice questions**: Classify critical points; optimize on a closed bounded region; solve a Lagrange multiplier problem; compare unconstrained and constrained solutions; interpret a multiplier as sensitivity.
- **Student output**: An optimization memo with solved examples, boundary analysis, second-derivative reasoning, and one modeled constrained problem with assumptions stated clearly.

## Chapter 5: Double Integrals and Planar Accumulation
- **Key concepts**: Double integrals over rectangles and general regions, Fubini's Theorem, Type I and Type II regions, changing order of integration, polar coordinates, area, volume, average value, mass, center of mass, moments, and density.
- **Practice questions**: Set up and evaluate iterated integrals; change the order of integration; convert a planar region to polar coordinates; compute mass or centroid from a density function.
- **Student output**: A double-integral problem set with region sketches, multiple setups for the same region, and applied accumulation calculations with units.

## Chapter 6: Triple Integrals and Change of Variables
- **Key concepts**: Triple integrals, rectangular coordinates, cylindrical coordinates, spherical coordinates, volume, mass, moments of inertia, coordinate transformations, Jacobians, change-of-variables formula, and choosing coordinates for symmetry.
- **Practice questions**: Describe a solid with inequalities; evaluate a triple integral in the most natural coordinate system; compute a Jacobian; transform a region under a change of variables.
- **Student output**: A coordinate-systems lab comparing rectangular, cylindrical, spherical, and custom transformations for volume and mass problems.

## Chapter 7: Vector Fields and Line Integrals
- **Key concepts**: Vector fields, flow diagrams, conservative fields, potential functions, work, circulation, line integrals of scalar and vector fields, path dependence, Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals, Green's Theorem, curl in the plane, and planar circulation and flux.
- **Practice questions**: Evaluate a line integral along a parametrized curve; test whether a vector field is conservative; find a potential function; use Green's Theorem to convert between line and double integrals.
- **Student output**: A vector-field portfolio with field sketches, work calculations, conservative-field tests, and a Green's Theorem example with orientation explained.

## Chapter 8: Surface Integrals and Integral Theorems
- **Key concepts**: Parametric surfaces, tangent vectors, surface area, oriented surfaces, flux integrals, curl, divergence, Stokes' Theorem, Divergence Theorem, conservation laws, source and circulation interpretations, and links to electromagnetism and fluid flow.
- **Practice questions**: Parametrize a surface; compute surface area or flux; choose between direct integration, Stokes' Theorem, and the Divergence Theorem; interpret divergence or curl in a model.
- **Student output**: A vector-calculus theorem packet with surface parametrizations, orientation checks, flux and circulation computations, and explanations of theorem hypotheses.

## Capstone
Students complete a multivariable calculus modeling project centered on change, accumulation, flow, flux, or optimization. The project must define variables and domains, use at least three course tools such as gradients, tangent planes, constrained optimization, multiple integrals, coordinate transformations, line integrals, surface integrals, Green's Theorem, Stokes' Theorem, or the Divergence Theorem, and present both mathematical setup and applied interpretation. Suitable topics include heat or concentration fields, terrain and gradient flow, constrained design optimization, mass and moment calculations, fluid flow through a surface, circulation around a boundary, electromagnetic field examples, or economic production models.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written problem sets graded for setup, computation, diagrams, theorem use, and interpretation.
- Short quizzes on vector geometry, partial derivatives, gradients, coordinate systems, Jacobians, line integrals, curl, divergence, and orientation.
- Modeling assignments that require students to define domains, units, constraints, and assumptions before calculating.
- Computational labs using graphing tools, Python, Julia, MATLAB, or a CAS to visualize surfaces, fields, and regions of integration.
- Capstone rubric covering mathematical setup, correct theorem selection, geometric reasoning, computation, modeling assumptions, and communication.
