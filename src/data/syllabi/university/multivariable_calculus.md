# Multivariable Calculus
**ID**: `multivariableCalculus` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
Multivariable calculus is the calculus of functions that depend on, and produce, more than one number at a time. It extends the single-variable ideas of slope, accumulation, and the Fundamental Theorem into a setting where derivatives become gradients and Jacobians, integrals become double and triple integrals over regions and solids, and the Fundamental Theorem reappears in four increasingly powerful disguises — the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals, Green's Theorem, Stokes' Theorem, and the Divergence Theorem. This course builds a rigorous, geometry-first, application-aware command of that machinery so students can model change, accumulation, flow, flux, and conservation in physics, engineering, data science, economics, and pure mathematics.

The deeper aim is to make the geometry inescapable. A student who finishes this course should not see "compute the partial derivative" or "evaluate the triple integral" as symbol-pushing rituals, but should reflexively draw the domain, orient the surface, picture the gradient pointing uphill perpendicular to a level set, and ask whether a field is conservative before grinding through a line integral. The single most important habit the course instills is choosing the right coordinate system and the right theorem before computing: a flux problem that is brutal as a direct surface integral may collapse to a one-line triple integral under the Divergence Theorem, and a region that is hopeless in Cartesian coordinates may be trivial in polar, cylindrical, or spherical form.

Throughout, the course holds two standards at once. Computations must be disciplined — correct limits of integration, honest Jacobians, tracked signs and orientations, attached units. And every major theorem must be understood as a precise statement with hypotheses, not a magic formula: students should know that Green's Theorem needs a positively oriented simple closed curve and a region with no holes (or the corrected multiply-connected version), that the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals needs a conservative field, and that the Divergence Theorem needs a closed outward-oriented surface bounding a solid. Knowing when a theorem does *not* apply is treated as part of knowing the theorem.

## Course Design Notes
This is a university multivariable calculus course (the standard third-semester "Calculus III" / MIT 18.02-level course) that fuses computational fluency, geometric reasoning, and theorem-level interpretation. Route questions here when students need vectors and geometry in two or three dimensions, the dot and cross product, lines, planes, and quadric surfaces, space curves and curvature, functions of several variables, limits and continuity, partial derivatives, the chain rule, directional derivatives and the gradient, tangent planes and linear approximation, unconstrained and constrained optimization (including Lagrange multipliers), double and triple integrals, polar/cylindrical/spherical coordinates and the change-of-variables Jacobian, vector fields, line integrals, conservative fields and potential functions, surface integrals and flux, and the four great integral theorems (Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals, Green's, Stokes', Divergence).

The course is sequenced so geometry leads and computation follows. Vectors and the geometry of space (Chapter 1) supply the language; differential multivariable calculus (Chapters 2–4) develops local behavior — partial derivatives, the gradient, optimization; integral multivariable calculus (Chapters 5–6) develops accumulation over regions and solids and the all-important coordinate changes; and vector calculus (Chapters 7–8) unifies the two halves through fields, line and surface integrals, and the integral theorems that generalize the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. Two threads run the whole way through: the gradient as the bridge between algebra and geometry (it points in the direction of steepest ascent and is normal to level sets), and the integral theorems as one idea — "the integral of a derivative over a region equals something evaluated only on the boundary." Emphasis is placed on drawing domains and regions of integration, attaching units, checking orientation, stating theorem hypotheses, and connecting local rates to global accumulation. Computational labs use Python (NumPy/SymPy/Matplotlib), Julia, MATLAB, or a CAS to visualize surfaces, fields, and regions and to confirm hand calculations — never to replace them.

## Chapter 1: Vectors, Curves, and the Geometry of Space
- **Core questions**: How do the dot product and cross product encode angle, projection, area, volume, and perpendicularity, and how do you turn a geometric description ("the plane through these three points," "the line where two planes meet") into an equation? How does a parametrization let calculus describe motion along a curve, and what do velocity, speed, and curvature measure?
- **Key concepts**: Vectors in R^2 and R^3, magnitude and unit vectors, the dot product and the angle/projection formulas, the cross product and its magnitude as area, the scalar triple product as signed volume, lines and planes (vector, parametric, and scalar forms), distance from a point to a line or plane, quadric surfaces, cylindrical and spherical coordinate descriptions, vector-valued functions r(t), velocity and acceleration, arc length, the unit tangent, and curvature.
- **Applied skills**: Compute angles, scalar and vector projections, and the area of a parallelogram or triangle from cross products; write the equation of a plane from a point and a normal or from three points; find the line of intersection of two planes and the distance from a point to a plane; identify a quadric surface (ellipsoid, paraboloid, hyperboloid, cone) from its equation by examining traces; parametrize a curve and compute velocity, speed, arc length, and curvature.
- **Common traps**: Treating the cross product as commutative (a × b = −b × a) or trying to cross two scalars or a scalar with a vector; confusing the dot product (a scalar, measuring alignment) with the cross product (a vector, measuring perpendicular area); forgetting that the cross product is defined only in R^3; reading off a plane's normal as the variable vector ⟨x, y, z⟩ instead of the coefficient vector ⟨a, b, c⟩; conflating speed |r'(t)| with the magnitude of acceleration; assuming the parameter t is arc length when it is not.

## Chapter 2: Functions of Several Variables, Limits, and Partial Derivatives
- **Core questions**: What does the graph and the family of level curves/surfaces of a function of several variables look like, and when does a limit at a point actually exist? What does a partial derivative measure, and what does local linearity — the tangent plane — let you predict?
- **Key concepts**: Scalar fields f(x, y) and f(x, y, z), domains and ranges, graphs, level curves and level surfaces, limits along paths, continuity, partial derivatives as rates holding other variables fixed, higher-order partials, Clairaut's (Schwarz's) Theorem on equality of mixed partials, the tangent plane, linear approximation, total differentials, and error/sensitivity estimates.
- **Applied skills**: Determine and sketch the domain of a multivariable function; show a limit fails by finding two paths to the point with different limiting values; compute first and second partial derivatives, including mixed partials, and verify Clairaut's Theorem; find the tangent plane to z = f(x, y) at a point and use the linear approximation (differential) to estimate a nearby value or propagate measurement error.
- **Common traps**: Assuming a two-variable limit exists because the limit along the x- and y-axes agree (they must agree along *every* path, so equality on two paths only suggests, never proves, existence); proving non-existence requires only two disagreeing paths, but proving existence requires a genuine bound (e.g., polar/squeeze); differentiating with respect to x while accidentally treating y as a constant *multiple* of x; assuming mixed partials are always equal even when the second partials are not continuous; confusing the tangent-plane approximation (linear) with the function itself far from the base point.

## Chapter 3: The Chain Rule, Gradients, and Directional Change
- **Core questions**: When variables depend on other variables in a web of relationships, how does the multivariable chain rule track every path of dependence? What is the gradient, why does it point in the direction of steepest ascent, and why is it perpendicular to level sets?
- **Key concepts**: The multivariable chain rule (tree diagrams, intermediate variables), implicit differentiation via the chain rule, directional derivatives D_u f = ∇f · u (with u a *unit* vector), the gradient ∇f, the gradient as the direction and rate of steepest ascent, the gradient as the normal to level curves/surfaces, tangent planes to level surfaces F(x, y, z) = k, the Jacobian matrix for vector-valued maps, differentiability, and sensitivity analysis.
- **Applied skills**: Apply the chain rule to nested and implicit dependencies and organize the computation with a dependency tree; compute a directional derivative in a given direction (after normalizing) and find the direction of maximum increase and its rate; use ∇F as a normal to find the tangent plane and normal line to a level surface; interpret a Jacobian as the local linear map of a transformation or model.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting to normalize the direction vector before computing a directional derivative (D_u f uses a *unit* vector — otherwise the answer is scaled by |u|); thinking the maximum rate of increase is ∇f itself rather than its magnitude |∇f|; using the gradient of z − f(x, y) versus f(x, y) and getting the tangent plane sign wrong; dropping a term in the chain rule by missing one of the paths a variable influences; confusing the gradient (lives in the input space) with the graph's slope.

## Chapter 4: Optimization and Lagrange Multipliers
- **Core questions**: How do you find and classify the peaks, valleys, and passes of a surface, and how do you find absolute extrema over a closed bounded region including its boundary? When a problem comes with a constraint, why does the gradient of the objective have to be parallel to the gradient of the constraint at an optimum?
- **Key concepts**: Critical points (where ∇f = 0 or is undefined), local maxima, minima, and saddle points, the second-derivative test via the discriminant D = f_xx f_yy − (f_xy)^2 (the Hessian determinant), absolute extrema on compact regions via interior critical points plus boundary analysis, constrained optimization, the method of Lagrange multipliers ∇f = λ∇g, multiple constraints, and the multiplier λ as a sensitivity (shadow price).
- **Applied skills**: Find and classify all critical points using the second-derivative test, including recognizing the inconclusive case D = 0; find absolute extrema on a closed bounded region by checking interior critical points and parametrizing/analyzing each boundary piece; solve constrained optimization with Lagrange multipliers, including problems with two constraints; interpret λ as the rate of change of the optimum with respect to the constraint level.
- **Common traps**: Using the single-variable second-derivative test (just f_xx) instead of the discriminant D, and forgetting that D > 0 with f_xx > 0 is a min, D > 0 with f_xx < 0 is a max, D < 0 is a saddle, and D = 0 is inconclusive; finding interior critical points but forgetting to check the boundary when seeking absolute extrema; in Lagrange problems, dividing by a variable that could be zero and silently dropping solutions; ignoring the constraint equation itself as one of the equations to solve; assuming every critical point is an extremum.

## Chapter 5: Double Integrals and Planar Accumulation
- **Core questions**: How does a double integral accumulate a density over a two-dimensional region, and how do you turn that region into correct limits of integration in the right order — or in polar coordinates? When is changing the order of integration the key move?
- **Key concepts**: Double integrals as limits of Riemann sums, Fubini's Theorem, iterated integrals over rectangles and over general regions, Type I (vertically simple) and Type II (horizontally simple) regions, reversing the order of integration, double integrals in polar coordinates with the area element dA = r dr dθ, area, volume under a surface, average value, and mass, center of mass, and moments from a density.
- **Applied skills**: Set up and evaluate iterated integrals over general regions with correct, sometimes variable, limits; sketch a region and rewrite a given iterated integral with the order of integration reversed (essential when the inner antiderivative is impossible in the given order); convert a region with circular symmetry to polar coordinates, remembering the r factor; compute volume, mass, centroid, and moments from a density function with attached units.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting the Jacobian factor r when switching to polar coordinates (dA = r dr dθ, not dr dθ); writing constant outer limits that depend on the inner variable, or vice versa; reversing the order of integration without redrawing the region, producing wrong limits; mixing up Type I and Type II descriptions; integrating a density times the wrong moment arm when computing center of mass; losing units by treating a mass integral as a pure area.

## Chapter 6: Triple Integrals and Change of Variables
- **Core questions**: How do you describe a solid with inequalities and integrate over it, and how do you choose among rectangular, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates to exploit symmetry? What does the Jacobian of a general change of variables do, and why does it appear?
- **Key concepts**: Triple integrals over solids, iterated integration and projection onto a coordinate plane, cylindrical coordinates (dV = r dz dr dθ), spherical coordinates (dV = ρ^2 sin φ dρ dφ dθ), volume, mass, center of mass, and moments of inertia, the general change-of-variables theorem, the Jacobian determinant ∂(x, y)/∂(u, v), and choosing or constructing a transformation to simplify a region or integrand.
- **Applied skills**: Describe a solid as a set of inequalities and set up a triple integral with correct ordered limits; choose the natural coordinate system from the symmetry of the solid and integrand and write the correct volume element; compute the Jacobian of a transformation and use it to convert an integral over an awkward region into one over a simple region (e.g., a rectangle or disk); compute volumes, masses, and moments of inertia.
- **Common traps**: Using dV = dρ dφ dθ instead of ρ^2 sin φ dρ dφ dθ in spherical coordinates, or dropping the r in cylindrical; mixing up the spherical angles φ (from the positive z-axis) and θ (around the z-axis), and confusing conventions that swap their names; forgetting the absolute value on the Jacobian; using the Jacobian of the wrong direction of the transformation (the area/volume factor is |∂(x,y)/∂(u,v)|); writing spherical limits for φ outside [0, π].

## Chapter 7: Vector Fields, Line Integrals, and Green's Theorem
- **Core questions**: How do you integrate a scalar or a vector field along a curve, and what do these line integrals measure — arc-length mass, work, circulation, flux? When is a field conservative, so that work depends only on endpoints, and how does Green's Theorem convert a boundary line integral into a region double integral?
- **Key concepts**: Vector fields and flow/field diagrams, line integrals of scalar fields (∫ f ds) and of vector fields (∫ F · dr), work and circulation, conservative (path-independent) fields, potential functions, the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals, the test for conservativeness on a simply connected domain (∂P/∂y = ∂Q/∂x in the plane; curl F = 0 in space), Green's Theorem in circulation and flux form, the scalar curl in the plane, and orientation of boundaries.
- **Applied skills**: Evaluate a line integral by parametrizing the curve and substituting; test whether a field is conservative and, if it is, find a potential function and evaluate work as Δf between endpoints; apply Green's Theorem to convert a closed-curve line integral into a double integral (or vice versa) and use it to compute area; explain the positive (counterclockwise, region-on-the-left) orientation of a boundary.
- **Common traps**: Declaring a field conservative from ∂P/∂y = ∂Q/∂x without checking that the domain is simply connected (the classic punctured-plane field ⟨−y, x⟩/(x²+y²) satisfies the test but is not conservative); forgetting that line integrals of vector fields depend on orientation and flip sign when the path reverses, while scalar line integrals ∫ f ds do not; applying Green's Theorem to a non-closed curve or with the wrong orientation; mismatching the parametrization's direction with the stated orientation; confusing circulation form with flux form of Green's Theorem.

## Chapter 8: Surface Integrals and the Integral Theorems of Vector Calculus
- **Core questions**: How do you parametrize a surface, compute its area, and integrate flux through it, and how do Stokes' Theorem and the Divergence Theorem generalize Green's Theorem and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus? Given a problem, how do you choose among direct integration, Stokes', and the Divergence Theorem?
- **Key concepts**: Parametric surfaces r(u, v), tangent vectors r_u and r_v, the surface area element |r_u × r_v| dA, oriented surfaces and the unit normal, flux integrals ∬ F · dS, the divergence and curl of a vector field, Stokes' Theorem (∮ F · dr = ∬ curl F · dS), the Divergence (Gauss) Theorem (∯ F · dS = ∭ div F dV), the physical meaning of divergence (source/sink density) and curl (local rotation/circulation density), and links to fluid flow and electromagnetism.
- **Applied skills**: Parametrize a surface and compute surface area or the flux of a field through it with correct orientation; recognize when Stokes' Theorem turns a hard surface integral of curl F into an easy boundary line integral (or vice versa); recognize when the Divergence Theorem turns a hard closed-surface flux integral into an easy triple integral of divergence; interpret div F and curl F in a physical model and state the hypotheses each theorem requires.
- **Common traps**: Using the Divergence Theorem on a surface that is not closed (it requires a closed surface bounding a solid, with outward orientation) — or forgetting to close it with an extra cap and subtract that cap's contribution; getting the orientation/normal direction wrong and producing a flux with the wrong sign; confusing div F (a scalar field) with curl F (a vector field), or applying curl to a scalar; forgetting that Stokes' Theorem requires the curve to be the *boundary* of the oriented surface with compatible (right-hand-rule) orientation; treating |r_u × r_v| as r_u × r_v (area element is the magnitude).

## Capstone
Students complete a multivariable calculus modeling project that moves from a concrete question about change, accumulation, flow, flux, or optimization to a defended quantitative answer. The project must (1) define the variables, domain, and any constraints, with units stated; (2) justify the choice of coordinate system, parametrization, or theorem before computing; (3) use at least three major course tools — for example gradients and directional derivatives, tangent planes/linear approximation, constrained optimization via Lagrange multipliers, double or triple integration, a change of variables with its Jacobian, line integrals and conservative-field tests, surface integrals and flux, Green's Theorem, Stokes' Theorem, or the Divergence Theorem; and (4) present both the mathematical setup and an honest applied interpretation, including where the model's assumptions could break down. Suitable topics include heat or concentration fields and their gradient flow, terrain modeling and steepest-descent paths, constrained engineering or economic design optimization with shadow-price interpretation of the multiplier, mass/centroid/moment-of-inertia calculations for a designed object, fluid flow through a surface analyzed by the Divergence Theorem, circulation around a boundary via Stokes' or Green's Theorem, and electromagnetic field examples (Gauss's or Ampère's law). Strong projects make explicit *why* the chosen theorem applies (checking its hypotheses) and validate at least one result by an independent method (e.g., a direct computation confirming a theorem-based shortcut, or a numerical/CAS check).

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written problem sets graded for setup, correct limits and Jacobians, diagrams, theorem selection and hypothesis-checking, sign/orientation discipline, and interpretation.
- Short quizzes targeting recurring traps: dot vs. cross product, the directional-derivative unit-vector requirement, the discriminant second-derivative test, the polar/cylindrical/spherical volume elements, conservative-field tests and simple connectivity, and orientation in Green's/Stokes'/Divergence Theorems.
- Modeling assignments that require students to define domains, units, constraints, and assumptions and to justify the coordinate system or theorem *before* computing.
- Computational labs in Python (SymPy/Matplotlib), Julia, MATLAB, or a CAS to visualize surfaces, vector fields, and regions of integration and to confirm hand computations.
- Capstone rubric covering model formulation, correct theorem selection and hypothesis-checking, geometric reasoning, computational correctness, modeling assumptions, independent validation, and communication.
