# LSAT Prep
**ID**: `lsat` · **Discipline**: Exam Prep · **Year**: University Senior

## Course Philosophy
The Law School Admission Test (LSAT) measures advanced reading comprehension, logical reasoning, and analytical reasoning skills. This syllabus focuses on formal logic, argument deconstruction, and intense time-management strategies required to score in the top percentiles. (Note: The LSAT removed the "Logic Games" section in August 2024, shifting focus entirely to Logical Reasoning and Reading Comprehension).

## Module 1: Formal Logic Fundamentals (20%)
- Conditional Statements: Sufficient vs Necessary conditions (If/Then)
- Translating English into formal logic notation ($A \rightarrow B$)
- The Contrapositive ($\neg B \rightarrow \neg A$)
- Logical Fallacies: Mistaken Reversal ($B \rightarrow A$) and Mistaken Negation ($\neg A \rightarrow \neg B$)
- Compound conditionals (And/Or) and De Morgan's Laws
- "Unless," "Except," "Until," and "Without" formulas
- Quantifiers: "All," "Most," "Some," and "None" logic
- **Questions**: Translating a complex legal statute into a chain of conditional statements, identifying the contrapositive of a quantified statement

## Module 2: Logical Reasoning - Argument Structure & Flaws (25%)
- Anatomy of an Argument: Identifying the Conclusion, Premises, and Sub-conclusions
- Argumentative strategies: Counter-premises, context, and the role of specific claims
- Flaw Questions: Identifying common logical fallacies (Ad Hominem, Correlation vs Causation, Part-to-Whole/Composition, Equivocation, False Dilemma, Circular Reasoning)
- Role of a Statement Questions
- Method of Reasoning Questions
- **Questions**: Deconstructing a dense paragraph to isolate the main conclusion, accurately naming the logical flaw in a flawed argument

## Module 3: Logical Reasoning - Assumptions, Strengthening, & Weakening (25%)
- The concept of the "Gap" between premises and the conclusion
- Necessary Assumption Questions: The Negation Test (if the assumption is false, the argument collapses)
- Sufficient Assumption Questions: Finding the missing premise that mathematically proves the conclusion
- Strengthen and Weaken Questions: Evaluating how new information impacts the validity of the argument (especially in causal arguments)
- Evaluate the Argument Questions
- Resolve the Paradox Questions
- **Questions**: Applying the Negation Test to distinguish between a necessary and a sufficient assumption, identifying the answer choice that most weakens a causal claim

## Module 4: Reading Comprehension - Active Reading & Structure (15%)
- Reading for Structure vs Reading for Detail (VIEWSTAMP: Viewpoints, Structure, Tone, Arguments, Main Point)
- Identifying the author's voice and recognizing competing viewpoints within a passage
- Passage Types: Law, Science, Humanities, Social Sciences
- Comparative Reading passages: Analyzing the relationship between Passage A and Passage B (agreement, disagreement, overlapping topics)
- Annotation and highlighting strategies for speed and retention
- **Questions**: Determining the primary purpose of a dense science passage, identifying where the author's tone shifts from objective to critical

## Module 5: Reading Comprehension - Question Types & Strategy (15%)
- Main Point and Primary Purpose questions
- Detail and "According to the Passage" questions (proving answers with text)
- Inference questions (what must be true based on the passage)
- Author's Attitude and Tone questions
- Vocabulary in Context questions
- Parallel Reasoning questions within Reading Comprehension
- Time management strategies: Triage (knowing when to skip and guess)
- **Questions**: Inferring the author's likely response to a new hypothetical scenario based on the passage, distinguishing between a valid inference and a likely assumption

## Stretch Zone
- Parallel Reasoning and Parallel Flaw questions in Logical Reasoning (highly time-consuming question types)
- Principle and Principle-Application questions
