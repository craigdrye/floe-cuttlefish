# LSAT Prep
**ID**: `lsat` - **Discipline**: Exam Prep - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
The LSAT does not test what you know; it tests how you reason under time pressure with incomplete information. This course builds the three things a strong score actually requires: a precise model of argument structure, fluent recognition of the test's recurring question tasks, and the test-day judgment to spend finite minutes where they pay off. Since the August 2024 redesign, the scored exam is two Logical Reasoning sections and one Reading Comprehension section (each 35 minutes), drawn from a four-section sitting in which one section is an unscored experimental that looks identical to the rest. Analytical Reasoning (the old "logic games") was retired in 2024; this course treats it only as optional formal-logic practice for students using legacy materials, and is explicit that games no longer appear on the test.

The course's spine is a deliberate-practice loop rather than a tips-and-tricks tour. Students learn to read a stimulus once, prephrase an answer before touching the choices, and eliminate wrong answers by naming the specific defect in each — too strong, reversed, out of scope, or unsupported. Equal weight goes to the review half of practice: blind review (re-solving missed items untimed before checking answers), error logging by cause rather than by topic, and converting recurring misses into targeted drills. The aim is repeatable process that survives the stress and fatigue of a real 2.5-hour administration.

Beyond the score, the course connects LSAT skills to the first year of law school. The discipline of finding a stimulus's conclusion, distinguishing holding from dicta, and reading a dense passage for structure rather than memorizing detail is the same discipline that case briefing and cold calls demand. Students finish with a calibrated study plan, a documented error history, a defensible pacing strategy, and a realistic sense of their score range from a full official PrepTest taken under test conditions.

## Course Design Notes
This is an exam-prep course organized around official-style practice, cause-based error review, and sustainable confidence rather than gimmicks. Route questions here when students need LSAT study planning, conditional and quantified logic, Logical Reasoning question-type recognition, assumption/flaw/strengthen/weaken work, Reading Comprehension structure and question strategy, timed pacing and skip decisions, wrong-answer review, score diagnostics, the Argumentative Writing task, or law-school readiness. The recurring move is the same across every chapter: state why the credited answer is supported, name precisely why each trap fails, and feed the miss back into a review loop until the pattern is visible. Facts about test structure, scoring (120-180 scaled), timing, and the writing task are current as of 2026; flag to students that many free or older resources still teach logic games, which no longer count. Keep difficulty calibrated to genuine LSAT register: the credited answer is rarely the most dramatic, and the hardest part is usually distinguishing the right answer from a single well-engineered trap.

## Chapter 1: LSAT Orientation, Scoring, and a Study System That Holds
- **Core questions**: What is on the current scored LSAT, and what changed in 2024? How do raw, scaled (120-180), and percentile scores relate? How do you read a diagnostic and turn it into a weekly plan tied to a real test date?
- **Key concepts**: Two scored LR sections plus one scored RC section; the unscored experimental that mimics a real section; 35-minute sections and the 10-minute break; raw-to-scaled conversion and curve variation; percentile interpretation; diagnostic testing; target-score setting; official PrepTests via LawHub; blind review; error logs; the difference between timed accuracy and untimed understanding.
- **Applied skills**: Read a diagnostic score report and identify whether misses are concept, process, timing, or attention errors; build a dated weekly plan with section mix, full-test cadence, and rest days; choose official over unofficial practice and explain why.
- **Common traps**: Believing logic games are still tested; chasing a target score with no baseline; grinding timed sections without untimed review; treating a single PrepTest score as a fixed verdict rather than a range.

## Chapter 2: Argument Structure and Conditional Logic
- **Core questions**: What is the conclusion, and what is offered to support it? How do you translate "unless," "only if," "no X without Y," and "all/some/most" into precise logical form? When does a contrapositive license a valid inference?
- **Key concepts**: Conclusion vs. premise vs. sub-conclusion vs. background; counter-premises and concessions; conditional statements (sufficient -> necessary); the contrapositive; "unless" / "only if" / "no...without" translations; quantifiers (all, some, most, none) and what they license; causal vs. correlational claims; indicator words and their limits.
- **Applied skills**: Translate dense conditional claims into clean if-then form and write their contrapositives; locate the main conclusion in a multi-claim stimulus; separate the author's view from views the author reports or rejects.
- **Common traps**: Mistaken reversal (treating necessary as sufficient); mistaken negation (denying the antecedent); reading "some" as "most" or "all"; assuming the first or last sentence is automatically the conclusion; trusting indicator words over actual logical role.

## Chapter 3: Logical Reasoning Foundations — Reading, Tasks, and Prephrasing
- **Core questions**: How do you classify a question from its stem before reading answers? What does prephrasing buy you, and how do you do it quickly? What standard must a credited answer meet for Must Be True, Main Point, Role, and Method questions?
- **Key concepts**: Question-stem families and task recognition; prephrasing the answer; the support standard for inference vs. argument-based questions; Must Be True / Most Strongly Supported; Main Point; Role-of-a-statement; Method (description) of reasoning; efficient one-pass stimulus reading; the four wrong-answer families (too strong, reversed, out of scope, unsupported).
- **Applied skills**: Match a stem to its task in seconds; predict the answer before reading choices; eliminate by naming the specific defect rather than by feel; cite the line that proves a Must-Be-True answer.
- **Common traps**: Reading the stem after the stimulus and re-reading needlessly; picking the "true and tempting but unresponsive" answer; over-relying on outside knowledge for inference questions; rejecting the credited answer because it is understated.

## Chapter 4: Assumptions, Flaws, Strengthen, and Weaken
- **Core questions**: How do you tell a necessary assumption from a sufficient one, and how does the negation test confirm a necessary assumption? What is the precise flaw in a given argument? Which fact most strengthens or weakens a stated conclusion without attacking a claim the argument never made?
- **Key concepts**: The argument's gap; necessary assumption (the negation test) vs. sufficient assumption (justifies the conclusion); named flaws — correlation-causation, sampling/unrepresentative survey, equivocation, part-to-whole and whole-to-part, circular reasoning, ad hominem, false dilemma, appeal to authority, percentage-vs-number; strengthen, weaken, and evaluate questions; resolve-the-paradox.
- **Applied skills**: Negate a candidate assumption and check whether the argument collapses; identify and name the flaw type rather than paraphrasing it; choose the answer that affects the exact conclusion, including overlooked alternative causes.
- **Common traps**: Confusing necessary with sufficient assumptions; weakening a premise instead of the conclusion's support; mistaking "the argument could be stronger" for a flaw; picking a strengthener that restates a premise; falling for an out-of-scope but emotionally satisfying weakener.

## Chapter 5: Advanced Logical Reasoning and Section Timing
- **Core questions**: How do you handle parallel-reasoning and parallel-flaw questions efficiently? How do principle questions move between abstract rules and concrete cases? When should you skip a time-heavy question, and how should you guess? How do you pace two 35-minute LR sections without burning out?
- **Key concepts**: Parallel reasoning and parallel flaw (matching abstract structure, not content); principle (identify and apply); formal-logic chains across multiple conditionals; EXCEPT questions; comparative ("most" / "best") answer standards; pacing checkpoints; deliberate skip decisions; letter-of-the-day guessing for unanswered items; section endurance across back-to-back LR.
- **Applied skills**: Abstract an argument to its skeleton to find its structural twin; map a principle to the case it governs; decide in real time whether a question is worth its minutes; complete a timed mini-section and review every decision point, not just the misses.
- **Common traps**: Matching surface topic instead of logical structure on parallel questions; reversing the principle's direction; flipping an answer's truth value on EXCEPT questions; refusing to skip and running out of time; leaving questions blank instead of guessing (the LSAT has no wrong-answer penalty).

## Chapter 6: Reading Comprehension — Structure and Passage Control
- **Core questions**: What is the passage's main point in one sentence, and what is each paragraph doing? Whose viewpoints are present, and where does the author shift from reporting to evaluating? In a comparative pair, what is the precise relationship between Passage A and Passage B?
- **Key concepts**: Main point and primary purpose; author viewpoint vs. cited viewpoints; paragraph function and structure; tone and scope; thesis and supporting evidence; scope and viewpoint shifts; the four passage genres (law, science, humanities, social science); comparative (dual) passages; structural annotation; memory vs. location ("know where it is, not what it says").
- **Applied skills**: State a one-sentence main point and label each paragraph's job; pinpoint where the author turns from neutral exposition to judgment; characterize the A-B relationship (agree, dispute, generalize/specify, debate) precisely.
- **Common traps**: Over-annotating and absorbing detail instead of mapping structure; conflating a cited view with the author's view; reading both comparative passages as if they fully agree or fully oppose; chasing tone words without anchoring them to text.

## Chapter 7: Reading Comprehension — Question Types and Timed Strategy
- **Core questions**: How do you prove a detail answer from the text? How do you tell a valid inference from a merely plausible assumption? How do you allocate roughly 8-9 minutes per passage and decide passage order in a 35-minute section?
- **Key concepts**: Main point, primary purpose, detail/explicit, inference, function, author attitude, analogy/application, vocabulary-in-context, and comparative cross-passage questions; the text-support standard; return-to-passage strategy; choosing passage order by difficulty and topic comfort; time allocation across four passages.
- **Applied skills**: Defend a detail answer with a specific line; distinguish a supported inference from an over-reach; choose which passage to attempt first under time; budget and recover time when one passage runs long.
- **Common traps**: Picking an inference that the passage merely permits rather than requires; choosing an answer that is true in the world but unsupported by the text; sinking time into the hardest passage first; answering attitude questions from one charged word.

## Chapter 8: Review Loops, Argumentative Writing, Test-Day Readiness, and Law-School Skills
- **Core questions**: How do you run blind review and an error log that actually changes behavior? How do you approach the 50-minute Argumentative Writing task (15 minutes prewriting, 35 minutes drafting)? What is a complete test-day routine, and how do LSAT habits transfer to first-year law school?
- **Key concepts**: Blind review (re-solve untimed before checking the key); error logs categorized by cause (concept, process, timing, attention); pattern diagnosis; endurance building with full PrepTests; the Argumentative Writing task — a debatable issue with three or four perspectives, taken online up to eight days early, unscored but required on file; the digital testing interface and accommodations; score variance and superscoring/best-score policies; case briefing; holding vs. dicta; professional self-management and application timeline.
- **Applied skills**: Classify each miss by cause and decide drill-vs-retest; write a timed argumentative essay that engages specific perspectives and supplies your own reasons and evidence; build a concrete test-day checklist; connect LSAT reading and argument habits to a sample case brief.
- **Common traps**: Reviewing only what you got wrong and ignoring lucky guesses; logging misses by topic instead of by cause; treating the writing task as throwaway because it is unscored (schools still see it and it must be on file); cramming a full test the day before instead of resting.

## Capstone
Students complete a full LSAT readiness cycle. They sit one official full-length PrepTest under realistic conditions (correct sections, strict 35-minute timing, a single 10-minute break, no pausing), then blind-review every flagged item before scoring. After scoring, they produce a section-by-section diagnosis, corrected explanations for a representative set of misses that name the trap in each rejected choice, and an error-log analysis grouping misses by cause. From that analysis they design a targeted drill set, run it, and take a follow-up timed section to measure change. The deliverable is a portfolio containing: the scored report with an honest score range, the cause-grouped error log, the corrected explanations, a defensible pacing and skip strategy for each section type, a test-day routine and checklist, a timed Argumentative Writing sample, and a one-page law-school readiness memo connecting specific LSAT skills (finding the conclusion, structural reading, distinguishing necessary from sufficient conditions) to case briefing and first-year legal analysis.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly Logical Reasoning drills graded on the quality of elimination explanations, not raw accuracy.
- Reading Comprehension passage maps checked for one-sentence main point, paragraph function, and viewpoint attribution.
- Timed mini-sections followed by blind review and written correction notes that name each trap.
- Error-log conferences focused on recurring causes of misses and the next-step drill they imply.
- A timed Argumentative Writing sample assessed for engagement with the given perspectives, clarity of thesis, and supporting reasons.
- Full PrepTest simulations assessed for pacing, stamina, review depth, and the realism of the resulting score range.
