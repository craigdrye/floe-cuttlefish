# LSAT Prep
**ID**: `lsat` - **Discipline**: Exam Prep - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
Build the habits, concepts, and test-day judgment students need for a strong LSAT performance and a smoother transition into law-school reading and reasoning. The course emphasizes argument structure, Logical Reasoning, Reading Comprehension, timed strategy, careful review loops, and law-school readiness. It also gives legacy Analytical Reasoning and logic games enough attention for students using older materials or needing formal-logic practice, while noting that the current LSAT focuses on Logical Reasoning and Reading Comprehension.

## Course Design Notes
This is an exam-prep course built around official-style practice, error review, and sustainable confidence. Route questions here when students need LSAT study planning, argument analysis, formal logic, Logical Reasoning question types, Reading Comprehension strategy, timed sections, wrong-answer review, score diagnostics, or law-school preparation. Emphasize repeatable process over tricks: students should learn why an answer is supported, why tempting answers fail, and how to review misses until patterns become visible.

## Chapter 1: LSAT Orientation, Baseline, and Study System
- **Key concepts**: Current LSAT structure, scored sections, experimental section awareness, raw and scaled scores, diagnostic testing, score goals, study timelines, official PrepTests, blind review, error logs, confidence tracking, and test registration basics.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret a diagnostic score report; design a weekly study plan for a target test date; explain the difference between timed performance and untimed understanding.
- **Student output**: A personalized LSAT study plan with baseline data, target score, weekly section mix, review routine, rest days, and official practice resources.

## Chapter 2: Argument Structure and Formal Logic
- **Key concepts**: Conclusions, premises, sub-conclusions, background, counter-premises, conditionals, sufficient and necessary conditions, contrapositives, unless statements, quantifiers, causal claims, comparison claims, and common indicator words.
- **Practice questions**: Translate conditional claims into plain if-then structure; identify the main conclusion in a dense stimulus; separate evidence from author opinion.
- **Student output**: An argument map packet with annotated stimuli, conditional translations, contrapositive practice, and notes on recurring structure mistakes.

## Chapter 3: Logical Reasoning Foundations
- **Key concepts**: Question stems, task recognition, prephrasing, conclusion support, answer choice standards, wrong-answer families, must-be-true reasoning, main point questions, role questions, method of reasoning, and efficient stimulus reading.
- **Practice questions**: Match question stems to tasks; predict the answer before reading choices; eliminate answers that are too strong, irrelevant, reversed, or unsupported.
- **Student output**: A Logical Reasoning process checklist plus a set of annotated problems showing task, conclusion, evidence, prediction, and elimination rationale.

## Chapter 4: Assumptions, Flaws, Strengthen, and Weaken
- **Key concepts**: Argument gaps, necessary assumptions, sufficient assumptions, the negation test, flaw types, causation, correlation, sampling, equivocation, part-to-whole reasoning, circular reasoning, strengthen questions, weaken questions, evaluate questions, and resolve-the-paradox questions.
- **Practice questions**: Distinguish necessary from sufficient assumptions; identify the flaw in a causal argument; choose the answer that most weakens a conclusion without attacking the wrong claim.
- **Student output**: An assumption and flaw notebook with categorized misses, corrected explanations, and one self-written example for each major flaw pattern.

## Chapter 5: Advanced Logical Reasoning and Timing
- **Key concepts**: Parallel reasoning, parallel flaw, principle questions, principle application, formal logic chains, except questions, comparative answer choices, pacing checkpoints, skip decisions, guessing strategy, and section endurance.
- **Practice questions**: Decide when to skip a time-heavy parallel question; match an abstract principle to a concrete case; complete a timed mini-section and review every decision point.
- **Student output**: A timed Logical Reasoning report with question-type data, pacing notes, skipped-question analysis, and a plan for the next practice set.

## Chapter 6: Reading Comprehension Structure and Passage Control
- **Key concepts**: Passage purpose, author viewpoint, competing viewpoints, structure, tone, thesis, evidence, scope shifts, comparative reading, law, science, humanities, social science passages, annotation discipline, and memory versus location strategy.
- **Practice questions**: State the main point in one sentence; identify where the author changes from reporting to evaluating; compare the relationship between Passage A and Passage B.
- **Student output**: A Reading Comprehension passage map set with viewpoint notes, paragraph functions, main point statements, and line-reference habits.

## Chapter 7: Reading Comprehension Questions and Timed Strategy
- **Key concepts**: Main point, primary purpose, detail, inference, function, author attitude, analogy, application, vocabulary in context, comparative questions, answer support, passage return strategy, passage order, and time allocation.
- **Practice questions**: Prove a detail answer with text; distinguish a valid inference from a plausible assumption; choose which passage to do first in a timed section.
- **Student output**: A timed Reading Comprehension review packet with passage maps, missed-question explanations, evidence citations, and pacing adjustments.

## Chapter 8: Review Loops, Test-Day Readiness, and Law-School Skills
- **Key concepts**: Blind review, wrong-answer journals, pattern diagnosis, endurance building, full PrepTests, accommodation planning, digital testing interface, stress routines, score variance, application timeline awareness, legal reading habits, case briefing, and professional self-management.
- **Practice questions**: Classify a miss as concept, process, timing, or attention; decide whether to drill or take another full test; connect LSAT reading habits to law-school case analysis.
- **Student output**: A final readiness portfolio with score trend, error patterns, refined timing plan, test-day checklist, and a first-semester law-school skills reflection.

## Capstone
Students complete a full LSAT readiness cycle: one official full-length PrepTest under realistic conditions, blind review, scored review, error-log analysis, targeted drills, and a follow-up timed section. The capstone deliverable includes a score report, section-by-section diagnosis, corrected explanations for representative misses, pacing plan, test-day routine, and a short law-school readiness memo connecting LSAT skills to legal reading and argument analysis.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly Logical Reasoning drills graded on explanation quality, not just accuracy.
- Reading Comprehension passage maps checked for structure, viewpoint, and evidence support.
- Timed mini-sections followed by blind review and written correction notes.
- Error-log conferences focused on recurring causes of misses and next-step drills.
- Full PrepTest simulations assessed for pacing, stamina, review depth, and readiness planning.
