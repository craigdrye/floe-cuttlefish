# General Chemistry I
**ID**: `genChem1` · **Discipline**: Science · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: typical first-semester university general chemistry with lab

## Course Aim
Build the quantitative and particle-level language of chemistry: atoms, moles, bonding, reactions, gases, energy, solutions, and laboratory evidence. Students should leave able to set up problems cleanly, reason from particles to observations, and use units as a guardrail rather than decoration.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test first-semester college chemistry: measurement, atomic structure, periodic trends, bonding, Lewis structures, molecular geometry, stoichiometry, reactions, solutions, gases, thermochemistry, colligative properties, lab technique, and dimensional analysis. Keep every topic connected to three views: particle model, equation or calculation, and lab or real-world evidence.

## Chapter 1: Matter, Measurement, and the Mole
- **Key concepts**: Matter classification, physical and chemical change, SI units, dimensional analysis, significant figures, accuracy, precision, density, atomic structure, isotopes, average atomic mass, Avogadro's number, molar mass, and empirical formulas.
- **Practice questions**: Convert among mass, moles, particles, and volume; track significant figures; compute empirical formulas from percent composition.
- **Student output**: A unit-roadmap problem set with annotations explaining every conversion factor.

## Chapter 2: Atomic Structure and Periodic Trends
- **Key concepts**: Electromagnetic radiation, photons, atomic spectra, Bohr model, quantum numbers, orbitals, electron configurations, periodic table organization, effective nuclear charge, shielding, atomic radius, ionization energy, electron affinity, and electronegativity.
- **Practice questions**: Write electron configurations; rank atoms or ions by trends; explain trends using shielding and nuclear charge.
- **Student output**: A periodic trends explainer that uses electron structure rather than memorized arrows.

## Chapter 3: Bonding, Lewis Structures, and Molecular Shape
- **Key concepts**: Ionic and covalent bonding, lattice energy intuition, bond polarity, Lewis structures, formal charge, resonance, octet exceptions, VSEPR shapes, molecular polarity, sigma and pi bonds, and hybridization at an introductory level.
- **Practice questions**: Draw Lewis structures; assign geometry and polarity; compare bond lengths, bond energies, and lattice energies.
- **Student output**: A structure-property dossier for a molecule or ionic compound.

## Chapter 4: Intermolecular Forces and Properties
- **Key concepts**: London dispersion, dipole-dipole forces, hydrogen bonding, ion-dipole forces, boiling point, melting point, viscosity, surface tension, solubility, and the distinction between bonds and intermolecular forces.
- **Practice questions**: Rank boiling points; predict solubility; explain macroscopic properties from molecular interactions.
- **Student output**: A property prediction memo connecting IMF evidence to observed behavior.

## Chapter 5: Stoichiometry and Aqueous Reactions
- **Key concepts**: Balanced equations, limiting reactants, theoretical and percent yield, complete ionic and net ionic equations, precipitation, acid-base reactions, redox reactions, oxidation states, molarity, dilution, electrolytes, and titration.
- **Practice questions**: Balance equations; solve limiting-reactant and yield problems; predict products; calculate titration concentrations.
- **Student output**: A reaction analysis packet with equation, particle view, calculation, and expected observation.

## Chapter 6: Gases and Gas Stoichiometry
- **Key concepts**: Pressure units, Boyle's law, Charles's law, Avogadro's law, ideal gas law, gas density, molar mass from gas data, gas stoichiometry, partial pressures, collecting gas over water, kinetic molecular theory, diffusion, effusion, and real-gas deviations.
- **Practice questions**: Apply PV=nRT; correct for water vapor; calculate molar mass from gas data; predict ideal-gas deviations.
- **Student output**: A gas-law lab analysis with graph, equation, assumptions, and error discussion.

## Chapter 7: Thermochemistry
- **Key concepts**: System and surroundings, heat, work, internal energy, enthalpy, endothermic and exothermic processes, calorimetry, specific heat, phase changes, Hess's law, standard enthalpies of formation, and bond-energy estimates.
- **Practice questions**: Solve calorimetry problems; combine reactions using Hess's law; compute reaction enthalpy from formation data.
- **Student output**: A thermochemistry calculation brief comparing calorimetry, Hess's law, and tabulated data.

## Chapter 8: Solutions, Colligative Properties, and Lab Studio
- **Key concepts**: Dissolution, concentration units, molarity, molality, mass percent, mole fraction, solubility, Henry's law, vapor pressure lowering, boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, osmotic pressure, van't Hoff factor, lab glassware, SDS, PPE, titration technique, spectrophotometry, and notebooks.
- **Practice questions**: Convert concentration units; solve colligative-property problems; identify lab procedure errors; interpret calibration curves.
- **Student output**: A final lab portfolio entry with procedure, raw data, calculation trail, uncertainty, and chemical explanation.

## Capstone
Students complete a general chemistry case study that connects a balanced equation, mole calculation, particle-level explanation, energy or gas/solution data, and lab evidence.

## Assessment Ideas
- Unit-roadmap quizzes where setup earns as much attention as arithmetic.
- Particle-diagram prompts for bonding, reactions, gases, and solutions.
- Lab practicals on glassware choice, safety, titration, and data quality.
- Mixed stoichiometry problems that require equations, units, limiting reactants, and interpretation.
- A final cumulative problem defense where students explain why the answer is chemically reasonable.
