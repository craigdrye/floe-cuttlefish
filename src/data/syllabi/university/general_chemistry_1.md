# General Chemistry I
**ID**: `genChem1` · **Discipline**: Science · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18–19)
**Aligns with**: typical first-semester university gen-chem (CHEM 101 / 1A / 1110 / 1411); textbook anchors include Zumdahl, Tro, OpenStax *Chemistry*, McMurry & Fay; lab pairs with Gen Chem I lab. (AP Chemistry — placed in our sophomore bucket — covers a similar but somewhat extended scope; treat Gen Chem I as the standard 14-week version that any freshman science/pre-med/engineering student takes.)

## Course Philosophy
General Chemistry is the **language layer** of the natural sciences. Pre-meds, engineers, biologists, materials scientists, environmental scientists, and chemists all share a vocabulary anchored in atoms, moles, bonding, stoichiometry, energy, and reactivity. The most common student failure is treating chemistry as memorisation; the right framing is that chemistry is a small set of conservation principles (mass, energy, charge, electrons) applied to a sea of particular cases. Students who internalise *the mole* as a counting unit and *stoichiometry* as accounting find the rest of the year tractable. Internally, every concept must connect to (a) the particle picture (atoms/electrons/ions in motion), (b) the equation/quantitative form, and (c) a real laboratory or industrial scenario. The course should leave students fluent in unit analysis, comfortable converting between mass/moles/molecules/volume, and able to write a balanced equation for any common reaction class.

## Module 1: Matter, Measurement, and the Mole (12%)
- **Classifying matter**: pure substances (elements, compounds) vs mixtures (homogeneous, heterogeneous); physical vs chemical change
- **Properties**: extensive (mass, volume) vs intensive (density, melting point); physical vs chemical properties
- **States of matter** at the particulate level: solid, liquid, gas, plasma; phase changes and energy
- **SI units and dimensional analysis**: base and derived units; prefixes (nano, micro, milli, centi, kilo, mega); the unit-cancellation method
- **Significant figures**: counting, addition/subtraction (decimal places), multiplication/division (sig figs); rounding; exact vs measured; the danger of trusting calculator digits
- **Accuracy vs precision**; systematic vs random error; reporting uncertainty
- **Density** and its applications; specific gravity
- **Atomic theory**: Dalton's postulates and what later got revised; the discovery of the electron (Thomson), the nucleus (Rutherford gold-foil), the proton, the neutron
- **Atomic structure**: protons, neutrons, electrons; Z, A, isotopes; isotope notation; mass spectrometry intuition; **average atomic mass** as a weighted average over isotopes
- **The mole** as a counting unit: Avogadro's number 6.022 × 10²³; molar mass; converting between mass, moles, and number of particles
- **Empirical and molecular formulas**: from percent composition to empirical formula to molecular formula given molar mass; combustion analysis
- **Questions**: convert between mass / moles / particles / volume of a gas at STP; compute the empirical formula from elemental analysis; identify isotopes from mass spec peaks; carry sig figs through a multi-step calculation

## Module 2: Atomic Structure, Quantum Numbers, and Periodic Trends (13%)
- **Light and the electromagnetic spectrum**: c = λν; E = hν; the photoelectric effect (Einstein) and the photon picture; spectroscopy as evidence
- **Atomic spectra**: line spectra; the Bohr model — successes (H spectrum, Rydberg formula) and failures (multi-electron atoms)
- **De Broglie wavelength**, the wave-particle duality, the Heisenberg uncertainty principle (qualitative)
- **Quantum mechanical model**: orbitals as probability distributions; the Schrödinger equation conceptually
- **Quantum numbers**: n (principal), ℓ (angular momentum, s/p/d/f), mₗ (magnetic), mₛ (spin); allowed combinations
- Shapes of s, p, d orbitals; nodes; degeneracy; effective nuclear charge (Zeff) and shielding
- **Electron configuration**: aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion, Hund's rule; condensed/noble-gas notation; exceptions (Cr, Cu and friends — and the *why* in d-block half-/full-shell stability)
- **Periodic table organisation**: groups, periods, blocks; main-group vs transition vs lanthanide/actinide
- **Periodic trends and their explanations** (always trace back to Zeff and shielding):
  - Atomic radius (decrease across, increase down)
  - Ionic radius (cation < parent atom < anion)
  - Ionisation energy (general increase across, decrease down; small dips at ns² → np¹ and np³ → np⁴)
  - Electron affinity
  - Electronegativity (Pauling scale)
  - Metallic character
- **Questions**: write electron configurations including ions and exception elements; rank by atomic/ionic radius and IE; explain "why" using Zeff/shielding; compute the wavelength of a transition with the Rydberg formula

## Module 3: Bonding I — Ionic, Covalent, and Lewis Structures (13%)
- **The octet rule** and its limits; ionic vs covalent vs metallic bonding as a spectrum
- **Ionic bonding**: lattice energy and the Born–Haber cycle (intuition); Coulomb's law dependence on charge and distance; trends in lattice energy
- **Covalent bonding**: electron sharing; bond length, bond energy; single/double/triple bond trends
- **Polarity**: electronegativity differences; polar covalent vs nonpolar; dipole moments
- **Lewis structures**: counting valence electrons; central atom selection; lone pairs; expanded octets (period 3+); incomplete octets (B, Be); odd-electron species (NO, NO₂)
- **Formal charge** and its role in choosing the best Lewis structure; minimising formal charge magnitude; placing negative formal charge on more electronegative atoms
- **Resonance**: when one Lewis structure is insufficient (NO₃⁻, CO₃²⁻, O₃, benzene); equivalent vs non-equivalent resonance contributors
- **VSEPR theory**: electron-pair geometries vs molecular geometries; standard shapes (linear, bent, trigonal planar, trigonal pyramidal, tetrahedral, trigonal bipyramidal, see-saw, T-shaped, octahedral, square pyramidal, square planar)
- Bond angles and the effect of lone pairs (compression of bonded angles)
- **Molecular polarity**: vector sum of bond dipoles; symmetric arrangements that cancel
- **Questions**: draw Lewis structures with formal charges; assign VSEPR geometry and bond angles; predict molecular polarity; rank lattice energies of ionic compounds

## Module 4: Bonding II — Hybridisation, Molecular Orbital Intro, and Intermolecular Forces (8%)
- **Valence Bond Theory and hybridisation**: sp, sp², sp³, sp³d, sp³d²; relating hybridisation to electron-pair geometry
- **Sigma vs pi bonds**; rotation about single vs double bonds; multiple bonds as 1σ + (n−1)π
- **Molecular Orbital theory** (intro): bonding vs antibonding; bond order = ½(N_bonding − N_antibonding); the diatomics of period 2 (H₂, He₂, Li₂, B₂, C₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Ne₂); MO explanation of O₂ paramagnetism
- **Intermolecular forces (IMFs)**: dispersion (London), dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonding (only when H is bound to N, O, or F), ion-dipole; relative strengths
- **Property consequences of IMFs**: boiling/melting points, viscosity, surface tension, solubility ("like dissolves like")
- **Questions**: assign hybridisation to each atom in a molecule; predict relative boiling points from IMF analysis; explain why H₂O has a higher boiling point than H₂S; build the MO diagram for N₂ and O₂ and predict bond order and magnetism

## Module 5: Stoichiometry and Reactions in Aqueous Solution (15%)
- **Balancing chemical equations**; writing complete, ionic, and net ionic equations
- **Reaction stoichiometry**: mole–mole, mass–mass, mass–particle problems; **limiting reagent** and excess reagent; **theoretical, actual, and percent yield**
- **Reaction classification**:
  - Combination, decomposition, single replacement, double replacement, combustion
  - **Precipitation reactions**: solubility rules (always-soluble groups, always-insoluble groups, exception lists)
  - **Acid–base reactions** (introductory): Arrhenius vs Brønsted–Lowry; strong acids (HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄), strong bases (Group 1 hydroxides, Ca/Sr/Ba hydroxides); neutralisation; conjugate pairs
  - **Oxidation–reduction (redox)**: oxidation states, the OIL RIG mnemonic, half-reactions, balancing redox in acidic and basic solutions; activity series for metals; common oxidising and reducing agents
- **Solutions and concentration**:
  - Molarity (M = mol/L); preparing solutions; serial dilution; M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
  - Molality, mole fraction, mass percent, parts per million/billion (intro)
  - Electrolytes: strong, weak, non-electrolytes; conductivity intuition
- **Solution stoichiometry and titrations**: equivalence point, indicators, primary standards; quantitative analysis with titrations
- **Questions**: balance redox equations in acid and base; identify the limiting reagent; compute percent yield; predict products of precipitation, acid-base, and redox reactions; titration calculations

## Module 6: Gases and Gas Stoichiometry (8%)
- **Pressure**: SI unit Pa, common units (atm, mmHg/torr, bar, psi); manometers and barometers
- **The gas laws** as historical limits and their unification:
  - Boyle (P-V), Charles (V-T), Gay-Lussac (P-T), Avogadro (V-n)
  - **Combined gas law** and the **ideal gas law** PV = nRT
- **Standard temperature and pressure (STP)**: modern IUPAC (0 °C, 100 kPa) vs traditional (0 °C, 1 atm); molar volume 22.4 L/mol at the traditional STP
- **Gas density** ρ = PM/RT; molar mass from gas density measurements
- **Stoichiometry of gas reactions**; volume-volume relationships at constant T and P; using PV = nRT to bridge gas measurements and moles
- **Dalton's law of partial pressures**; collecting gases over water and the vapour-pressure correction
- **Kinetic Molecular Theory** of gases: postulates, the connection to PV = nRT; relationship between rms speed and temperature (uᵣₘₛ = √(3RT/M))
- **Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution** of speeds; how the curve shifts with T
- **Effusion and diffusion**: Graham's law (rate ∝ 1/√M)
- **Real gases**: deviations from ideality; van der Waals equation (intuition); when ideal-gas approximation fails (high P, low T)
- **Questions**: PV = nRT problems including non-STP conditions; collect-over-water partial pressure problems; compute gas density and molar mass; predict deviation from ideal behaviour for given conditions

## Module 7: Thermochemistry (12%)
- **Energy** types: kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical; the law of conservation of energy
- **System vs surroundings**; **endothermic vs exothermic**; sign conventions for q and w
- **First law of thermodynamics**: ΔU = q + w (chemistry sign convention); pressure-volume work w = −PΔV
- **Heat capacity** and specific heat capacity; q = m·c·ΔT; phase-change energies (ΔH_fus, ΔH_vap) and the "flat spots" on a heating curve
- **Calorimetry**: constant-pressure (coffee-cup) and constant-volume (bomb); calculating ΔH and ΔU from calorimetry data; q_lost = −q_gained for ideal calorimetry
- **Enthalpy** H and ΔH; standard states and standard enthalpies; ΔH_rxn vs ΔH_combustion; **enthalpy is a state function**
- **Hess's Law**: combining reactions to find ΔH for a target reaction; the value of cycle-thinking
- **Standard enthalpies of formation** ΔH_f°; computing ΔH_rxn = Σ ΔH_f°(products) − Σ ΔH_f°(reactants)
- **Bond energies**: estimating ΔH_rxn from average bond energies (and the limitations of the estimate)
- **Foods and fuels**: caloric content, the energy of common fuels per gram and per mole, the carbon footprint connection
- **Questions**: calorimetry problems mixing solids/liquids/phase changes; Hess's law cycles; ΔH_f°-table problems; bond-energy estimates and comparison with experimental ΔH

## Module 8: Solutions and Colligative Properties (5%)
- **The solution process**: solute, solvent, dissolution at the particulate level; energetics of dissolution (ΔH_solution from lattice energy and hydration)
- **"Like dissolves like"** as a polarity rule; aqueous vs nonpolar solvents
- **Saturation**: unsaturated, saturated, supersaturated; solubility curves with temperature; gas solubility decreasing with T
- **Henry's law** for gas solubility; pressure dependence
- **Concentration units revisited**: molarity, molality, mass %, mole fraction; converting between them given solution density
- **Colligative properties** (depend on number of particles, not identity):
  - Vapour pressure lowering (Raoult's law)
  - **Boiling point elevation** ΔT_b = K_b · m · i
  - **Freezing point depression** ΔT_f = K_f · m · i (the icy-roads salt example)
  - **Osmotic pressure** Π = MRT · i (and biological relevance)
- The van 't Hoff factor i for electrolytes (NaCl ≈ 2, CaCl₂ ≈ 3 in dilute solution)
- **Questions**: convert concentration units given density; predict relative boiling points/freezing points from electrolyte vs non-electrolyte; osmotic pressure problems for biological scenarios

## Module 9: Lab and Skills Layer (cross-cutting, 8%)
- Laboratory glassware and uses: beaker, Erlenmeyer, graduated cylinder, volumetric flask, burette, pipette (volumetric, Mohr), funnel, watch glass, crucible, test tube
- **Reading volumes correctly** (meniscus); zeroing balances; significant figures from each instrument
- **Lab safety**: PPE (safety glasses always, lab coat, gloves), the eyewash and safety shower, the fume hood, the difference between burning, splashes, and inhalation incidents
- **SDS literacy**: the GHS pictograms (corrosive, flammable, toxic, oxidiser, etc.); reading hazard statements
- **Common Gen Chem I lab techniques**: filtration (gravity vs vacuum), titration (proper burette technique, indicator selection), recrystallisation (intro), distillation (intro), spectrophotometry (Beer-Lambert A = εbc), gravimetric analysis
- **Lab notebooks**: chronological, dated, ink, signed; raw observations vs interpretation; if it's not in the notebook, it didn't happen
- **Calculating with measured values**: significant figures through a multistep calculation; uncertainty propagation (intuition only at Gen Chem I — full propagation is in physical chemistry)
- **Common Gen Chem I labs**: density determination, gas laws (Boyle's), calorimetry of acid-base or dissolution, percent water in a hydrate, stoichiometry of a precipitate, titration of an unknown acid, beer's-law calibration of a coloured complex
- **Questions**: design a calorimetry experiment to determine the specific heat of an unknown metal; identify a procedural error in a written lab procedure; calculate concentration from a Beer-Lambert measurement

## Module 10: Skill Connectors — Quantitative Problem-Solving Patterns (cross-cutting, 6%)
- **The mole as the universal converter**: mass ↔ moles ↔ particles ↔ volume of solution ↔ volume of gas; once a value is in moles, *anything* can be derived from it
- **The "PV = nRT then stoichiometry then % yield" chain**: a single multi-step problem combining gas law, limiting reagent, and yield
- **Setting up a problem before plugging in numbers**: writing the balanced equation, listing knowns and unknowns, sketching a unit roadmap, and only then computing
- **Estimation and sanity-checking**: order-of-magnitude expectations, rough mass conservation, checking that an answer's units make sense
- **Common student traps**:
  - Forgetting to balance the equation before stoichiometry
  - Using mass directly instead of converting to moles
  - Mixing up molarity and molality
  - Using K instead of °C (or vice versa) in gas law and thermochemistry problems
  - Forgetting the van 't Hoff factor for electrolyte colligative properties
  - Reporting more sig figs than the data justifies
  - Confusing intramolecular bonds and intermolecular forces (the *both* are why ice melts AND why H₂O is a liquid at all)
- **Questions**: integrative multistep problems combining multiple modules

## Stretch Zone (push beyond Gen Chem I; previewing Gen Chem II / AP Chem)
- **Kinetics**: rate laws, integrated rate laws (zero, first, second order), half-life, Arrhenius equation k = A·exp(−Ea/RT), reaction mechanisms, rate-determining step, catalysts; collision theory and transition-state theory
- **Equilibrium**: K_c, K_p, K_eq, Q vs K, Le Châtelier's principle, ICE tables, RICE tables; the "x is small" approximation and when it fails
- **Acid–base equilibria** in depth: K_a/K_b, weak acid pH, polyprotic acids, buffers, Henderson–Hasselbalch, titration curves, indicators, salts of weak acids/bases
- **Solubility equilibria** (K_sp); the common ion effect; conditional solubility from pH and complexation
- **Thermodynamics II**: entropy (S), Gibbs free energy ΔG = ΔH − TΔS; spontaneity criteria; the link between ΔG° and K
- **Electrochemistry**: galvanic and electrolytic cells, standard reduction potentials, cell potential E_cell, Nernst equation, batteries, corrosion, electrolysis, Faraday's laws
- **Coordination chemistry**: ligands, denticity, isomerism, crystal-field theory and colour
- **Nuclear chemistry**: alpha/beta/gamma decay, half-life, nuclear stability, fission and fusion, radiocarbon dating
- **Modern computational chemistry intuition**: density functional theory (DFT), what computational labs deliver to experimentalists, AlphaFold-style ML for chemistry (e.g., MIT/Berkeley's MACE-type interatomic potentials, generative chemistry models)
- **Common conceptual jumps to push for**:
  - Why electronegativity decreases down a group despite increasing nuclear charge (shielding wins)
  - Why O₂ is paramagnetic from MO theory but predicted diamagnetic from Lewis
  - Why ice is less dense than liquid water (hydrogen-bonding lattice geometry)
  - Why CO₂ is nonpolar but H₂O is polar despite both being triatomic with polar bonds
- **The single transferable skill**: dimensional analysis. A student who never loses track of units almost never makes a fatal error.
