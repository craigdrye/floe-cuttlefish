# General Chemistry I
**ID**: `genChem1` · **Discipline**: Science · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: typical first-semester university general chemistry with lab (ACS-aligned, OpenStax Chemistry 2e Ch. 1-9 scope)

## Course Aim
General Chemistry I teaches you to think in three registers at once: the particle world you cannot see, the equation or calculation that models it, and the macroscopic observation you can measure in lab. The course's central claim is that almost every quantitative result in introductory chemistry is a disciplined unit conversion away from a count of atoms — and that the mole is the bridge between the gram you weigh and the 10^23 particles that actually react. Students should leave able to set up a problem cleanly before touching a calculator, carry units as a built-in error check, and explain *why* an answer is chemically reasonable, not just what the number is.

The deeper aim is fluency in the explanatory chain that runs through the whole semester: electron structure drives periodic trends, periodic trends drive bonding, bonding drives molecular shape and intermolecular forces, and those forces drive the bulk properties — boiling points, solubilities, gas behavior, reaction energetics — that we measure. A student who has internalized this course does not memorize that water boils high; they predict it from hydrogen bonding, and they can defend the prediction against a plausible-sounding wrong answer.

Finally, the course is laboratory-anchored. Every calculation should connect to evidence: a titration curve, a calorimeter trace, a gas collected over water, a calibration line. We treat significant figures, uncertainty, and procedure choice as chemistry, not bookkeeping, because the quality of a measurement is part of the chemical claim being made.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test first-semester college chemistry: measurement and dimensional analysis, atomic structure and the quantum model, periodic trends, ionic and covalent bonding, Lewis structures and VSEPR, intermolecular forces, stoichiometry and limiting reactants, aqueous and redox reactions, solution concentration, gas laws and kinetic molecular theory, thermochemistry and calorimetry, and colligative properties. This is the "before equilibrium and kinetics" half of the two-semester sequence; deep treatments of chemical equilibrium, acid-base equilibria, reaction rates, electrochemistry, and nuclear chemistry belong to General Chemistry II.

Three design rules keep the bank rigorous. First, prefer problems where a wrong *setup* — inverting a ratio, forgetting to convert mL to L, reading molar volume as the answer — produces a specific, tempting wrong number; the best distractors are the results of real, nameable mistakes. Second, keep every topic tethered to the particle/equation/evidence triad so that conceptual and computational questions reinforce each other. Third, demand unit literacy: a numerically correct answer with the wrong or missing units is treated as incomplete. Distractors should teach, naming the misconception that generated them rather than being arbitrary near-misses.

## Chapter 1: Matter, Measurement, and Dimensional Analysis
- **Core questions**: What distinguishes elements, compounds, and homogeneous vs. heterogeneous mixtures? How do significant figures, accuracy, and precision shape what a measurement can claim? Why is dimensional analysis the safest way to get from one quantity to another?
- **Key concepts**: matter classification; physical vs. chemical change; SI base and derived units; significant-figure rules for multiplication, addition, and exact numbers; accuracy vs. precision; density as mass/volume; unit conversion via labeled factors.
- **Applied skills**: convert between metric units using chained conversion factors; track significant figures through multi-step calculations; compute and rearrange density; classify a sample and a change correctly from a description.
- **Common traps**: inverting density (volume/mass instead of mass/volume); applying the multiplication sig-fig rule to addition (or vice versa); treating exact/counted numbers as if they limit sig figs; confusing accuracy (closeness to true value) with precision (reproducibility).

## Chapter 2: Atoms, Isotopes, and the Mole
- **Core questions**: How do protons, neutrons, and electrons define identity, mass, and charge? Why is the mole the central counting unit of chemistry, and how does it connect grams to particles? How is average atomic mass an isotope-weighted average?
- **Key concepts**: subatomic particles and isotope notation; atomic number vs. mass number; average atomic mass from isotopic abundances; Avogadro's number (6.022 x 10^23); molar mass; interconversion of mass, moles, and number of particles; empirical vs. molecular formulas.
- **Applied skills**: convert among grams, moles, and particles in either direction; compute average atomic mass from abundance data; derive an empirical formula from percent composition and a molecular formula from empirical formula plus molar mass.
- **Common traps**: multiplying grams by Avogadro's number without first dividing by molar mass; reporting an empirical formula when a molecular formula is asked (or vice versa); confusing mass number with average atomic mass; forgetting that moles of ions differ from moles of formula units.

## Chapter 3: The Quantum Model and Electron Configuration
- **Core questions**: How did atomic spectra force a quantized model of the atom? What do the four quantum numbers permit and forbid? How does electron configuration encode an atom's chemistry?
- **Key concepts**: electromagnetic spectrum, E = h*nu, c = lambda*nu; Bohr model and line spectra; quantum numbers (n, l, ml, ms) and their allowed ranges; orbital shapes; Aufbau principle, Hund's rule, Pauli exclusion; ground-state configurations and noble-gas shorthand; configurations of ions.
- **Applied skills**: write ground-state electron configurations for atoms and ions; identify valid vs. invalid quantum-number sets; relate spectral lines to energy-level transitions; connect configuration to position on the periodic table.
- **Common traps**: assigning l >= n or ml outside the range -l to +l; removing electrons from the wrong subshell when forming cations (remove highest-n first, not last-filled); confusing the n=1 ground state with excited states; mixing up wavelength and frequency in the inverse relationship.

## Chapter 4: Periodic Trends
- **Core questions**: Why do atomic radius, ionization energy, electron affinity, and electronegativity vary across and down the table? How do effective nuclear charge and shielding explain the trends — and the exceptions?
- **Key concepts**: effective nuclear charge (Zeff) and shielding; atomic and ionic radius; isoelectronic series; first and successive ionization energies; electron affinity; electronegativity; the trend exceptions at the Be/B and N/O steps.
- **Applied skills**: rank atoms or ions by a given trend; justify a ranking using Zeff and shielding rather than memorized arrows; predict ion size relative to the parent atom; explain why ionization energy dips from Be to B and from N to O.
- **Common traps**: assuming trends are perfectly monotonic and missing the Be->B (s vs. p subshell) and N->O (paired-electron repulsion in half-filled p) exceptions; predicting that a cation is larger than its neutral atom; treating "more protons" as automatically meaning "stronger pull" without accounting for shielding; conflating ionization energy with electron affinity.

## Chapter 5: Bonding, Lewis Structures, and Molecular Shape
- **Core questions**: When does bonding go ionic vs. covalent, and why? How do you build a valid Lewis structure, choose among resonance forms with formal charge, and translate it into a 3-D shape and an overall polarity?
- **Key concepts**: ionic vs. covalent bonding and lattice-energy intuition; bond polarity and the electronegativity difference; counting valence electrons (including ionic charge); octet rule and its exceptions; formal charge and resonance; VSEPR electron-domain and molecular geometries; bond angles; molecular polarity from shape plus bond dipoles; sigma/pi bonds and introductory hybridization.
- **Applied skills**: count valence electrons correctly for neutral species and ions; draw Lewis structures and pick the best resonance structure by formal charge; assign electron-domain and molecular geometry; decide whether a molecule is polar by checking whether bond dipoles cancel.
- **Common traps**: forgetting to add electrons for a negative ion or subtract for a positive ion (e.g., NH4+ has 8 valence electrons, not 9); calling a molecule polar because its bonds are polar even when symmetry cancels the dipoles (CO2 is linear and nonpolar); confusing electron-domain geometry with molecular geometry when lone pairs are present; assuming every atom must obey the octet.

## Chapter 6: Intermolecular Forces and Bulk Properties
- **Core questions**: What is the difference between a chemical bond and an intermolecular force? How do dispersion, dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonding, and ion-dipole interactions set boiling point, viscosity, surface tension, and solubility?
- **Key concepts**: London dispersion forces and polarizability; dipole-dipole forces; hydrogen bonding (N-H, O-H, F-H); ion-dipole forces; "like dissolves like"; relationship of IMF strength to boiling/melting point, viscosity, surface tension, and vapor pressure.
- **Applied skills**: identify the dominant intermolecular force in a substance; rank boiling points or vapor pressures from structure; predict miscibility/solubility; explain a macroscopic property mechanistically from molecular interactions.
- **Common traps**: treating breaking a hydrogen bond as breaking a covalent bond (IMFs are far weaker and intermolecular, not intramolecular); ignoring dispersion forces in large nonpolar molecules and so mis-ranking boiling points; assuming any molecule containing H can hydrogen-bond (it needs H bonded to N, O, or F); confusing higher vapor pressure with higher boiling point (they move oppositely).

## Chapter 7: Stoichiometry, Aqueous Reactions, and Solutions
- **Core questions**: How does a balanced equation turn into a quantitative prediction? How do you find the limiting reactant and percent yield? How do net ionic equations, solubility rules, and molarity describe what actually happens in solution?
- **Key concepts**: balancing equations; mole ratios; limiting reactant and theoretical/percent yield; molecular, complete ionic, and net ionic equations; solubility rules and precipitation; acid-base neutralization; oxidation states and redox identification; molarity, dilution (M1V1 = M2V2), and titration stoichiometry.
- **Applied skills**: solve mass-to-mass and limiting-reactant problems with correct mole ratios; write net ionic equations by removing spectator ions; assign oxidation states and identify what is oxidized/reduced; calculate concentration from titration data and perform dilution calculations.
- **Common traps**: using gram ratios instead of mole ratios; assuming the reactant present in smaller mass (rather than smaller stoichiometric supply) is limiting; leaving spectator ions in a net ionic equation; using volume in mL instead of L in molarity calculations; reporting percent yield above 100% without flagging an error.

## Chapter 8: Gases and Gas Stoichiometry
- **Core questions**: What do the simple gas laws and the ideal gas law let you predict, and when do real gases deviate? How do partial pressures and collection over water work? What does kinetic molecular theory explain about diffusion and effusion?
- **Key concepts**: pressure units (atm, mmHg, kPa, torr); Boyle's, Charles's, and Avogadro's laws; ideal gas law PV = nRT with R = 0.08206 L*atm/(mol*K); molar volume 22.4 L/mol at STP; gas density and molar mass from gas data; Dalton's law of partial pressures and vapor-pressure correction; kinetic molecular theory; Graham's law of effusion; causes of real-gas deviation.
- **Applied skills**: apply PV = nRT and the combined gas law with consistent units and absolute temperature; compute molar mass or density from gas measurements; correct collected-gas pressure for water vapor; compare effusion rates via the inverse-square-root-of-molar-mass relationship; predict the direction of deviation from ideality.
- **Common traps**: using Celsius instead of kelvin in gas-law calculations; reporting 22.4 L as the answer when moles differ from 1; forgetting to subtract water vapor pressure when a gas is collected over water; inverting Graham's law so the heavier gas appears to effuse faster; mismatching R's units with the pressure/volume units used.

## Chapter 9: Thermochemistry and Calorimetry
- **Core questions**: How are heat, work, internal energy, and enthalpy related? How do you read the sign of q for endo- vs. exothermic processes? How do calorimetry, Hess's law, and standard enthalpies of formation each get you to a reaction enthalpy?
- **Key concepts**: system vs. surroundings; first law and the sign conventions for q and w; enthalpy (H) and delta-H; endothermic vs. exothermic; specific heat and q = m*c*delta-T; heats of fusion (334 J/g) and vaporization (2260 J/g) for water; heating curves; Hess's law; standard enthalpies of formation and delta-H_rxn = sum(products) - sum(reactants).
- **Applied skills**: solve calorimetry problems including phase changes; combine reactions with Hess's law, reversing and scaling delta-H correctly; compute reaction enthalpy from formation data; assign the correct sign to q for the system given what the surroundings do.
- **Common traps**: getting the sign of q backward (surroundings warming means the system released heat, q_system < 0); forgetting that delta-H_f of an element in its standard state is zero; failing to reverse the sign of delta-H when a reaction is reversed in Hess's law; omitting the phase-change term when a substance crosses its melting or boiling point; using delta-T in Fahrenheit or with the wrong sign.

## Chapter 10: Colligative Properties and the Lab Studio
- **Core questions**: Why do solute particles — not their identity — control freezing-point depression, boiling-point elevation, vapor-pressure lowering, and osmotic pressure? How do concentration units, the van't Hoff factor, and good lab technique tie measurement to chemical meaning?
- **Key concepts**: concentration units (molarity, molality, mass percent, mole fraction) and when each is appropriate; colligative properties; van't Hoff factor i for electrolytes; delta-T_f = i*K_f*m and delta-T_b = i*K_b*m; Raoult's and Henry's laws; glassware selection and uncertainty; titration technique; spectrophotometry and Beer's law calibration; lab notebook and safety (SDS, PPE).
- **Applied skills**: convert among concentration units; predict and compute colligative-property shifts using the correct van't Hoff factor; rank solutions by effect on freezing/boiling point; read a calibration curve and apply Beer's law; identify procedural errors and propagate uncertainty.
- **Common traps**: using i = 1 for an electrolyte (CaCl2 dissociates into 3 ions, so i ~ 3); mixing molarity and molality (colligative formulas use molality); assuming a more concentrated solution always boils higher without accounting for particle count; reading boiling-point elevation and freezing-point depression with the wrong sign; over-reporting precision beyond what the glassware supports.

## Capstone
Students complete an integrated general-chemistry case study built around a single real reaction (for example, the combustion of a hydrocarbon fuel, the neutralization in an antacid, or the precipitation that hardens water). The deliverable must connect, in one coherent narrative: (1) a balanced equation with a net ionic form where relevant; (2) a mole/limiting-reactant calculation predicting product quantities; (3) a particle-level explanation of bonding, geometry, or intermolecular forces involved; (4) a quantitative energy, gas, or solution result (calorimetry, PV = nRT, or a colligative property) with correct units and significant figures; and (5) supporting lab evidence — a titration result, calorimeter trace, gas measurement, or calibration line — with an uncertainty estimate. Students defend why each number is chemically reasonable, demonstrating the particle/equation/evidence fluency that is the goal of the course.

## Assessment Ideas
- Setup-graded problem sets where a clean dimensional-analysis layout earns as much credit as the final number.
- Particle-diagram prompts for bonding, reactions, gas behavior, and dissolution.
- Lab practicals on glassware choice, safety, titration technique, and data quality.
- Mixed stoichiometry problems requiring a balanced equation, unit conversions, limiting-reactant logic, and interpretation.
- A cumulative problem defense where students must explain why an answer is chemically reasonable, not merely arithmetically correct.
