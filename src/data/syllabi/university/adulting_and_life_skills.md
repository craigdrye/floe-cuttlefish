# Adulting & Life Skills (University)
**ID**: `lifeSkillsUni` - **Discipline**: Life Skills - **Year**: University (All)

## Course Aim
Most of the expensive mistakes young adults make are not failures of character — they are failures of information arriving too late. A lease is signed before anyone explains "joint and several liability." A first paycheck is smaller than expected because nobody decoded the W-4. A credit card balance quietly compounds because "minimum payment" sounded like a plan. This course front-loads that information so students meet adult systems with a map instead of a surprise.

The aim is practical competence under realistic constraints: limited money, limited time, imperfect information, and decisions that have to be made anyway. Students learn to model money over time, read a contract for the clauses that actually bite, compare offers and insurance plans on total cost rather than sticker numbers, navigate taxes and healthcare paperwork, run a functioning household, and protect their time and relationships from burnout. Every chapter ends in something reusable — a template, a checklist, a script — so the course output survives past the final week.

The tone is direct and kind. Adulthood is treated as a set of learnable systems, not a moral test, and "I don't know how this works yet" is the normal starting point rather than a confession. The course rewards students who can explain *why* a choice is right and who can say honestly what they would do next when a real-world system is confusing — because that judgment, not memorized trivia, is what keeps adult life from becoming a series of avoidable emergencies.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve budgeting, cash flow, credit scores, debt payoff, student loans, saving and investing basics, renting and leases, roommates, utilities, job search and interviewing, offers and salary negotiation, workplace rights, insurance, taxes, healthcare paperwork, meal planning, food safety, household maintenance, transport, time management, boundaries, conflict, or burnout. The unifying lens is the *total-cost, over-time* view: sticker price is rarely the real number, and most adult decisions are tradeoffs across money, time, and risk.

Because tax brackets, contribution limits, and benefit thresholds change yearly, questions that depend on specific dollar figures should either pin the tax year explicitly or test the durable structure (how a deductible interacts with an out-of-pocket maximum) rather than a number that will drift. Distractors should mirror the real misconceptions students arrive with — that minimum payments retire debt, that the cheapest rent is the cheapest apartment, that a bigger refund means you "won" at taxes — so that getting the question right means dislodging a belief, not just recalling a fact. Favor scenarios with concrete numbers students can reason through, and reward the habit of asking "compared to what, and over what time horizon?"

## Chapter 1: The Money Map — Cash Flow and Budgeting on Real Income
**Core questions**: Where does money actually go each month, and how do you build a budget that survives contact with real life? How do you plan for expenses that do not arrive monthly?
**Key concepts**: Net (take-home) vs gross pay, fixed vs variable costs, irregular/lumpy expenses, the 50/30/20 framework, zero-based budgeting, sinking funds, the emergency fund (commonly 3–6 months of essential expenses), cash-flow timing, automation, and the difference between a need, a want, and a risk.
**Applied skills**: Build a monthly budget from take-home pay; size and stage an emergency fund; create a sinking fund for annual costs (insurance, registration, gifts); adjust a budget after a rent increase; decide which payments to automate and which to keep manual.
**Common traps**: Budgeting off gross pay instead of take-home; treating annual or quarterly bills as surprises instead of dividing them into monthly sinking funds; assuming "no debt" equals "financially safe" while holding zero buffer; confusing a tight month (cash-flow timing) with actually being broke.

## Chapter 2: Credit and Debt — Scores, Cards, and Getting Out of the Hole
**Core questions**: What actually moves a credit score, and how does revolving debt turn small balances into large ones? When is debt a tool and when is it a trap?
**Key concepts**: FICO factor weights (payment history ~35%, amounts owed/utilization ~30%, length of history ~15%, new credit ~10%, credit mix ~10%), credit utilization (keep it well under 30%, ideally under 10%), APR vs interest rate, minimum payment, the avalanche (highest-APR-first) vs snowball (smallest-balance-first) payoff methods, secured vs unsecured cards, and the cost of carrying a balance.
**Applied skills**: Estimate how long a balance takes to clear paying only the minimum; compare avalanche vs snowball on a real set of debts; lower a utilization ratio without closing old accounts; read why a single missed payment outweighs almost anything else.
**Common traps**: Believing the minimum payment is a payoff plan; closing your oldest card to "simplify" (which shortens history and raises utilization); thinking carrying a small balance "builds credit" (it does not — paying on time does); confusing APR with the dollar cost of interest.

## Chapter 3: Saving and Investing — Compounding, Accounts, and Fees
**Core questions**: How does compounding work for you (saving) and against you (debt and fees)? Which account belongs around which goal?
**Key concepts**: Compound growth, time horizon, the difference between saving and investing, emergency cash vs invested money, index funds and diversification, expense ratios, employer 401(k) match (free money), Roth vs traditional tax treatment, IRA basics, and risk tolerance.
**Applied skills**: Capture a full employer match before anything else; compare Roth vs traditional given an expected future tax bracket; project the multi-decade cost of a 1% fee gap; match a goal's time horizon to cash, bonds, or stocks.
**Common traps**: Leaving an employer match on the table; treating the emergency fund as investable; ignoring expense ratios because "1% sounds small"; assuming Roth is always better without comparing tax rates now vs later; mistaking a brokerage *account* for an *investment* (an empty account earns nothing).

## Chapter 4: Housing — Leases, Roommates, and Utilities
**Core questions**: What does it really cost to move in and live somewhere, and which lease clauses can hurt you most? How do you share a home without the friendship or the finances collapsing?
**Key concepts**: Rent, security deposit, application fee, guarantor/cosigner, lease term, joint and several liability, subletting and assignment, the implied warranty of habitability, renters insurance (covers your stuff and liability) vs the landlord's policy (covers the building), utilities and setup costs, and roommate agreements.
**Applied skills**: Compare two apartments on total move-in and monthly cost (not just rent); spot risky or unenforceable lease clauses; draft a roommate agreement and a fair bill split; document a unit's condition before move-in; write a clear, dated maintenance request.
**Common traps**: Picking the lowest rent without counting deposit, utilities, and commute; not realizing joint and several liability means you can owe the *entire* rent if a roommate bails; assuming the landlord's insurance covers your belongings; skipping move-in photos and losing the deposit fight later; believing a lease can waive the warranty of habitability (generally it cannot).

## Chapter 5: Career Launch — Materials, Search, and Interviewing
**Core questions**: How do you get a real human to read your application, and how do you tell a credible story about your value in an interview?
**Key concepts**: Resume tailoring, applicant tracking systems (ATS) and keywords, cover letters, portfolios, informational interviews, networking, references, the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result), behavioral vs technical questions, and the follow-up/thank-you note.
**Applied skills**: Tailor a resume to a specific job description; write a short, specific outreach email; structure a behavioral answer with STAR and quantified results; prepare thoughtful questions to ask interviewers; send an effective follow-up.
**Common traps**: One generic resume blasted everywhere; listing duties instead of results ("responsible for X" vs "increased X by 20%"); rambling answers with no result/payoff; treating networking as asking for a job rather than asking for information; ghosting the thank-you note.

## Chapter 6: Offers, Negotiation, and Workplace Rights
**Core questions**: How do you compare two job offers on what they are actually worth, and how do you negotiate without blowing up the relationship? What protections do you have at work?
**Key concepts**: Total compensation (base, bonus, equity, 401(k) match, PTO, health premiums), market-rate research, anchoring and the negotiation range, BATNA/walk-away, benefits open enrollment, at-will employment, exempt vs non-exempt and overtime, harassment/discrimination policy, and documentation.
**Applied skills**: Convert two offers into comparable total-comp figures; research a realistic salary range; draft a respectful negotiation email with a target and rationale; identify which benefits materially change take-home value; document a workplace problem and route it correctly.
**Common traps**: Comparing offers on base salary alone and ignoring a $6,000 match or higher premiums; accepting the first number because negotiating "feels rude" (the first offer is usually a starting point); negotiating with no market data; confusing PTO accrual rules; not documenting incidents contemporaneously.

## Chapter 7: Healthcare and Taxes — The Paperwork That Costs Money
**Core questions**: How do you choose a health plan for *your* likely usage, and how do you read the tax forms that decide your take-home pay and your April outcome?
**Key concepts**: Premium, deductible, copay, coinsurance, out-of-pocket maximum, in-network vs out-of-network, HMO vs PPO, HDHP + HSA (triple tax advantage) and FSA (use-it-or-lose-it), the modern W-4 (allowances are gone), W-2 vs 1099, FICA (7.65% employee / 15.3% self-employment), withholding, standard deduction, and estimated quarterly taxes.
**Applied skills**: Compute total expected annual cost of a health plan for a given usage scenario (premium + cost-sharing up to the out-of-pocket max); decide between a low-premium/high-deductible and high-premium/low-deductible plan; reason about HSA eligibility; adjust a W-4 to avoid a surprise bill or a giant refund; sort and store the tax documents an employee or freelancer needs.
**Common traps**: Choosing the lowest premium without checking the deductible and out-of-pocket max; thinking a copay is the same as coinsurance; believing a big refund means you "won" (it means you over-lent the government interest-free); assuming a 1099 contractor pays the same FICA as a W-2 employee (they pay both halves, ~15.3%, plus owe estimated quarterly payments); forgetting the danger of under-withholding.

## Chapter 8: Domestic Systems — Food, Transport, and Maintenance
**Core questions**: How do you run a household — fed, clean, mobile, and maintained — on limited money and time? When do you repair, replace, or call a professional?
**Key concepts**: Meal planning and batch cooking, grocery budgeting, basic nutrition, food safety and the 40°F–140°F "danger zone" (the 2-hour rule, refrigerate at/below 40°F, reheat to 165°F), cleaning rotations, laundry basics, the repair-vs-replace decision, routine car/transport maintenance and the true cost of owning vs commuting, and household inventory.
**Applied skills**: Plan a week of meals to a grocery budget; build a realistic cleaning and laundry rotation; apply the danger-zone rule to leftovers and meal prep; decide whether to repair or replace an appliance using cost and remaining life; schedule preventive maintenance before something breaks.
**Common traps**: Leaving cooked food in the danger zone "just for a bit"; cooling a big pot of food slowly in one deep container; deferred maintenance that turns a cheap fix into an expensive failure; sinking repair money into something past its useful life; underestimating the all-in cost of car ownership (insurance, fuel, maintenance, depreciation) vs transit.

## Chapter 9: Time, Boundaries, and Burnout
**Core questions**: How do you protect attention and energy across a busy life, and how do you have the hard conversations — with a manager, a landlord, a roommate — without damage?
**Key concepts**: Calendar/task systems, prioritization (urgent vs important), batching and deep-work blocks, sustainable sleep, the warning signs of burnout, active listening, "I-statements" and boundary-setting, conflict de-escalation, knowing which support service fits which problem, and the skill of asking for help early.
**Applied skills**: Rebuild an overloaded week around true priorities; write a clear, non-hostile boundary message; plan and rehearse a difficult conversation; name early burnout signals and design a reset; pick the right resource (HR, a clinic, a tenant hotline, a financial counselor) for a given problem.
**Common traps**: Treating "busy" as the same as "productive"; saying yes to everything and resenting it later; raising a problem only after it has become a crisis; framing conflict as winning rather than solving; ignoring sleep as if it were optional; not knowing that free or low-cost support services exist.

## Capstone — The Independent-Adult Starter Kit
Students assemble a single, genuinely reusable portfolio they could hand to their slightly-younger self: (1) a personal budget with an emergency-fund target and sinking funds; (2) a debt-and-savings plan that compares at least two strategies over time with stated assumptions; (3) a housing packet — apartment total-cost comparison, lease red-flag checklist, move-in photo plan, and roommate agreement; (4) a career folder — tailored resume, outreach script, STAR answer bank, and an offer/total-comp comparison with a negotiation script; (5) a healthcare-and-tax kit — a plan comparison run on their own usage scenario, a W-4 sanity check, and a document checklist; (6) a weekly operating plan for food, chores, transport, and maintenance; and (7) boundary and difficult-conversation scripts with a list of real support resources.

The defense element matters as much as the documents: students must walk through three decisions in the kit and explain *why* — the assumptions, the tradeoffs, and what they would do if a real-world system turned out to work differently than expected. The pass bar is simple and demanding: the kit has to be good enough that the student would actually use it.

## Assessment Ideas
Use scenario-based checks ("which plan is cheaper for this person's year?"), document drafts, spreadsheet tasks, comparison memos, role-plays for negotiation and boundary conversations, and peer feedback, culminating in the portfolio and a short oral defense. Assess clarity, realism, total-cost reasoning, risk awareness, ethical judgment, and whether a student can articulate a sensible next step when a real-world system is confusing.
