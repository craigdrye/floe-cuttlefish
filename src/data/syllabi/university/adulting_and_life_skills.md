# Adulting & Life Skills (University)
**ID**: `lifeSkillsUni` - **Discipline**: Life Skills - **Year**: University (All)

## Course Aim
Help university students move into independent adulthood with fewer expensive surprises and more confidence. Students learn how to manage money, housing, healthcare, work, taxes, food, time, conflict, and basic systems of adult life while building reusable documents and decision tools.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve budgeting, credit, student loans, renting, roommates, utilities, job search, interviewing, salary negotiation, insurance, taxes, healthcare, meal planning, household maintenance, time management, boundaries, burnout, or practical independent-living decisions. Tone should be direct and kind: the course treats adulthood as a set of learnable systems, not a moral test.

## Chapter 1: Money Map and Budgeting on Real Income
**Key concepts**: Income, fixed costs, variable costs, irregular income, emergency fund, cash flow, needs and wants, 50/30/20 budgeting, automation, sinking funds, and financial tradeoffs.
**Practice questions**: Build a monthly budget from a starting salary or student income; plan for annual expenses; adjust a budget after rent changes; decide what to automate; identify which expenses are urgent, important, optional, or risky.
**Student output**: A personal budget template with a one-month plan, emergency-fund target, and a short reflection on likely pressure points.

## Chapter 2: Credit, Debt, Student Loans, and Saving
**Key concepts**: Credit score, payment history, utilization, interest, APR, compounding, minimum payment, credit cards, student loans, grace periods, repayment plans, retirement accounts, index funds, and risk.
**Practice questions**: Compare credit-card payoff timelines; calculate the cost of delaying loan repayment; explain subsidized and unsubsidized loans; compare Roth and traditional accounts; decide whether a reward card is worth it.
**Student output**: A debt-and-savings scenario analysis that compares at least two choices over time and names the assumptions behind the calculation.

## Chapter 3: Housing, Leases, Roommates, and Utilities
**Key concepts**: Rent, deposit, guarantor, application fee, lease term, joint and several liability, subletting, habitability, repairs, renters insurance, utilities, shared expenses, and roommate agreement.
**Practice questions**: Identify risky lease clauses; compare two apartments by total move-in cost; draft a roommate bill split; decide what to document before moving in; write a maintenance request.
**Student output**: A housing packet with apartment comparison, lease checklist, move-in photo log plan, and roommate agreement draft.

## Chapter 4: Career Launch and Professional Communication
**Key concepts**: Resume, cover letter, applicant tracking system, portfolio, informational interview, networking, references, STAR method, interview preparation, follow-up email, and workplace norms.
**Practice questions**: Tailor a resume to a job description; write an outreach email; answer a behavioral interview prompt; compare two job postings; ask thoughtful questions at the end of an interview.
**Student output**: A career launch folder containing a tailored resume, outreach script, interview answer bank, and professional email templates.

## Chapter 5: Offers, Negotiation, and Workplace Rights
**Key concepts**: Salary, benefits, PTO, health insurance, retirement match, equity, relocation, market research, negotiation, HR, performance review, harassment policy, documentation, and workplace boundaries.
**Practice questions**: Compare two offers; research salary ranges; draft a negotiation email; identify benefits that change total compensation; respond professionally to a workplace problem.
**Student output**: An offer-comparison and negotiation plan that includes target, walk-away concerns, questions for HR, and a respectful script.

## Chapter 6: Healthcare, Insurance, and Taxes
**Key concepts**: Premium, deductible, copay, coinsurance, out-of-pocket maximum, network, HMO, PPO, HSA, FSA, W-4, W-2, 1099, withholding, estimated taxes, standard deduction, and tax documents.
**Practice questions**: Compare health plans for a medical scenario; find the lowest expected total cost; fill out a sample W-4 decision; sort tax forms; estimate freelance tax obligations.
**Student output**: A healthcare-and-tax checklist with plan comparison, appointment script, tax document folder, and annual calendar reminders.

## Chapter 7: Domestic Systems, Food, Transport, and Maintenance
**Key concepts**: Meal planning, grocery budget, nutrition basics, food safety, cleaning schedule, laundry, basic repairs, car maintenance, public transport, appointment scheduling, and household inventory.
**Practice questions**: Plan meals under a budget; create a cleaning rotation; decide when to repair or replace an item; schedule routine maintenance; prepare for a week with limited time.
**Student output**: A weekly operating plan for food, chores, appointments, transport, and one maintenance task.

## Chapter 8: Time, Conflict, Boundaries, and Burnout
**Key concepts**: Calendar system, task batching, prioritization, deep work, burnout, sleep, mental health access, active listening, conflict resolution, boundaries, support networks, and asking for help.
**Practice questions**: Rebuild an overloaded week; write a boundary-setting email; plan a difficult roommate or manager conversation; identify early signs of burnout; choose which support service fits a problem.
**Student output**: A personal operating manual with calendar rules, boundary scripts, support contacts, and a reset plan for stressful weeks.

## Capstone
Students assemble an independent-adult starter kit: budget, debt/savings plan, housing checklist, career materials, healthcare/tax folder, weekly operating plan, and conflict/boundary scripts. The capstone should be useful enough that students can actually reuse it after the course.

## Assessment Ideas
Use scenario-based checks, document drafts, role-plays, spreadsheet tasks, comparison memos, peer feedback, and a final portfolio. Assess clarity, realism, risk awareness, ethical judgment, and whether a student can explain what they would do next when a real-world system is confusing.
