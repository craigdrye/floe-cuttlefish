# University Foundations in Science
**ID**: `uniScience` · **Discipline**: Science · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: introductory "How to Do Science", lab-skills, scientific methods, and research literacy courses

## Course Aim
Turn first-year students from consumers of scientific conclusions into careful users of scientific evidence. By the end, students should be able to read a paper outside their strongest field, identify the question, design, controls, data, statistics, limitations, ethics, and strongest critique.

## Course Design Notes
This course should be rigorous but not bloated. Route questions here when they ask about scientific method, experimental design, measurement, uncertainty, reproducibility, literature critique, lab safety, research ethics, open science, or scientific communication across disciplines.

## Chapter 1: What Science Is and How Models Work
- **Key concepts**: Hypotheses, predictions, theories, evidence, falsifiability, exploratory versus confirmatory work, models, mechanisms, causation, and pseudoscience warning signs.
- **Practice questions**: Classify claims by testability; identify ad hoc explanations; explain why a useful model can still be incomplete.
- **Student output**: A demarcation memo that turns a broad public claim into testable scientific subclaims.

## Chapter 2: Experimental and Observational Design
- **Key concepts**: Variables, operational definitions, controls, randomization, blinding, confounding, replication, sample size, field versus lab tradeoffs, and natural experiments.
- **Practice questions**: Design a study for a hypothesis; diagnose confounds; select controls; compare experimental and observational conclusions.
- **Student output**: A study-design blueprint with question, method, variables, controls, sampling plan, and limitation notes.

## Chapter 3: Measurement, Data, and Statistical Thinking
- **Key concepts**: Accuracy, precision, bias, random error, calibration, significant figures, uncertainty propagation, descriptive statistics, visualization, p-values, confidence intervals, effect sizes, and practical significance.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret error bars; identify misleading graphs; explain what a p-value or confidence interval does and does not mean.
- **Student output**: A data-analysis memo using one dataset, at least one visualization, and an uncertainty statement.

## Chapter 4: Reading Scientific Literature
- **Key concepts**: IMRaD structure, abstracts, figures, methods, primary literature, reviews, preprints, peer review, citation trails, conflicts of interest, and critical reading checklists.
- **Practice questions**: Extract a paper's central claim; identify missing methods detail; compare a paper with a press release.
- **Student output**: A three-pass paper critique with hypothesis, method, evidence, limitations, and follow-up question.

## Chapter 5: Lab Practice, Safety, and Reproducibility
- **Key concepts**: Lab notebooks, standard operating procedures, PPE, safety data sheets, chemical and biological hazards, data provenance, version control, workflow documentation, open data, and FAIR principles.
- **Practice questions**: Improve a lab notebook entry; choose safety precautions; identify why a result would be hard to reproduce.
- **Student output**: A reproducibility packet for a small lab or analysis: protocol, raw data plan, file structure, and revision log.

## Chapter 6: Research Ethics and Scientific Integrity
- **Key concepts**: Human-subjects ethics, informed consent, beneficence, justice, privacy, research misconduct, plagiarism, authorship, conflicts of interest, dual use, community impact, and institutional review.
- **Practice questions**: Classify misconduct scenarios; revise a consent process; allocate authorship roles with justification.
- **Student output**: An ethics review memo for a proposed study or data project.

## Chapter 7: Scientific Communication and Public Trust
- **Key concepts**: Scientific writing, figures and tables, captions, talks, posters, lay summaries, uncertainty communication, risk communication, overclaiming, media translation, and trust.
- **Practice questions**: Rewrite an overstrong claim; improve a figure caption; translate a technical abstract for a general audience.
- **Student output**: A two-audience communication package: one technical summary and one public-facing explanation.

## Chapter 8: Methods Across Scientific Fields
- **Key concepts**: Physics experiments and models, chemical synthesis and characterization, biological assays, clinical trials, Earth and climate models, computational studies, social-science methods, and mixed-methods research.
- **Practice questions**: Match research questions to methods; identify when a method does not generalize; compare evidence standards across fields.
- **Student output**: A methods comparison matrix for three fields, including what each method can prove, estimate, or only suggest.

## Capstone
Students choose a published paper, technical report, or preprint and produce a full scientific critique: research question, design, controls, data, statistical reasoning, reproducibility, ethical considerations, communication quality, and next study.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly paper-reading drills using short excerpts.
- Study-design critiques focused on controls and confounds.
- Data interpretation prompts that reward uncertainty and effect size language.
- A final oral paper defense where students must answer skeptical but fair questions.
