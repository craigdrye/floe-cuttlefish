# University Foundations in Science
**ID**: `uniScience` · **Discipline**: Science · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18–19)
**Aligns with**: introductory "How to Do Science" / "Science Foundations" / "Methods in Science" courses; mirrors freshman lab-skills modules and the AAAS *Vision and Change* recommendations; complements but doesn't replace content courses (Bio/Chem/Physics)

## Course Philosophy
Most students arrive at university having "done science" only as a series of memorised conclusions. The freshman foundations course exists to flip that — to teach science as a *practice* and a *culture*: a system of questions, methods, evidence, peer review, error correction, and ethical norms. Mastering this course is what separates a student who passively consumes textbooks from one who can read a paper, design an experiment, run a clean analysis, communicate uncertainty honestly, and navigate the research community. The big internal goal: by the end of this course, a student should be able to take a real published paper in any field they don't yet know technically, identify the hypothesis, the design, the controls, the statistics, the limitations, and the strongest critique. This is the **trans-disciplinary skill** that separates a modern scientist from a modern technician.

## Module 1: What Science Is (and Isn't) — Philosophy of Method (12%)
- **The scientific method** — and why "the" is misleading: hypothesis-driven, exploratory, observational, theoretical, computational; different fields, different rhythms
- **Hypotheses**: testable, falsifiable, specific; vs predictions; vs theories (theory in science = "well-supported explanatory framework", *not* "guess")
- Popper's **falsifiability** criterion and its limits; Kuhn on paradigms and "normal science" vs revolutionary science; Lakatos on research programs
- **Theory–data relationship**: theory-laden observation; the underdetermination problem; abductive inference (inference to the best explanation)
- **Pseudoscience markers**: unfalsifiability, ad hoc rescues, lack of progressive predictions, cherry-picking, conspiracy framings
- **Demarcation cases**: astrology vs astronomy, homeopathy vs pharmacology, intelligent design vs evolutionary biology, parapsychology
- **Models**: physical, mathematical, computational; "all models are wrong, some are useful" (Box); the difference between a model that fits and a model that explains
- **Questions**: classify a claim as falsifiable or not, and write a falsification protocol; identify ad-hoc rescues in quasi-scientific arguments; explain why "evolution is just a theory" misuses the word "theory"

## Module 2: Experimental Design (18%)
- **Variables**: independent (manipulated), dependent (measured), controlled (held constant), confounding (uncontrolled and correlated with both IV and DV)
- **Operationalisation**: turning a fuzzy concept ("stress", "happiness", "ecosystem health") into a measurable proxy; construct validity
- **Controls**: positive control, negative control, sham/placebo, vehicle control, internal control; why "no control" makes most experiments useless
- **Randomization** (assignment) and **blinding** (single, double, triple); the placebo effect and its bidirectional pitfalls
- **Replication** vs **repetition** (technical replicates vs biological replicates); the "n = 1" problem
- **Sample size and power**: why underpowered studies routinely produce false positives that fail to replicate
- **Within-subjects vs between-subjects designs**; matched-pairs; crossover designs; counterbalancing; carryover effects
- **Factorial designs**: 2 × 2 and beyond; main effects vs interactions; why interaction effects are usually the interesting finding
- **Quasi-experiments and natural experiments**: when randomization is impossible; difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, instrumental variables (intuition only)
- **Field vs lab tradeoffs**: ecological validity vs experimental control
- **Pre-registration** of hypotheses and analyses; registered reports; their role in fighting p-hacking and HARKing (hypothesising after results known)
- **Questions**: design a clean experiment for a given hypothesis identifying IV/DV/controls; spot the confound in a sloppy study; pick the right control for a drug, a teaching intervention, an ecological manipulation; decide between within- and between-subjects design with reasoning

## Module 3: Measurement, Data, and Statistics for Science (15%)
- **Levels of measurement**: nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio; what statistics are appropriate for each
- **Accuracy vs precision**; bias (systematic error) vs noise (random error)
- **Significant figures** and propagation of uncertainty (sum, product, exponentiation rules)
- **Calibration** of instruments; reference standards; traceability
- **Descriptive statistics**: mean, median, mode, range, variance, standard deviation; when each is misleading (skew, outliers)
- **Visualising data**: histograms, scatterplots, box plots, error bars; when bar charts of means hide everything (use jittered scatter or violin plots)
- **Inferential statistics** primer (deeper coverage in AP Statistics): null hypothesis, p-value, confidence interval, effect size; what a p-value is and isn't
- **The ASA's six principles on p-values** (2016) and the move toward effect sizes and confidence intervals
- **Multiple comparisons** and the family-wise error rate; Bonferroni, FDR (Benjamini–Hochberg)
- **Frequentist vs Bayesian** framings (intuition); priors and updating
- **Causal inference**: correlation ≠ causation; the directed acyclic graph (DAG) framework (intuition); confounders, mediators, colliders; Hill's criteria for causation
- **Data hygiene**: outliers (genuine vs error vs interesting), missing data, exploratory data analysis vs hypothesis-confirmatory analysis
- **Questions**: read an error bar correctly (SE vs SD vs CI); compute propagated uncertainty for a derived quantity; identify p-hacking patterns in a methods section; classify a confounder/mediator/collider in a causal diagram

## Module 4: Reading and Critiquing the Scientific Literature (15%)
- **Anatomy of a paper**: Abstract → Introduction → Methods → Results → Discussion → References; supplementary materials; the "IMRaD" structure
- **How to read a paper efficiently**: title → abstract → figures → conclusions → methods (in that order, not start to finish); the "three-pass" approach
- **Primary vs secondary vs tertiary sources**; the role of textbooks, reviews, meta-analyses, and pre-prints
- **Pre-print servers** (arXiv, bioRxiv, medRxiv, SSRN); the trade-off between speed and peer review; lessons from COVID-19
- **Peer review**: single-blind, double-blind, open peer review, post-publication review; review report structure; the limits and abuses of peer review
- **Predatory journals** and how to spot them (Beall's list legacy, the Cabells indices, look for editorial transparency, publication fees, indexing)
- **Citation literacy**: tracking forward and backward citations (Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, Connected Papers); citation graphs and how ideas spread; **citation cartels**, salami slicing, gift authorship
- **Critical reading checklist**: hypothesis stated? Sample described? Controls present? Pre-registered? Statistics appropriate? Effect sizes reported? Limitations acknowledged? Conflicts of interest disclosed?
- **Replication crisis**: psychology's Reproducibility Project (2015), the cancer-biology replications, the social-science replications; *why* it happened (pressure to publish, p-hacking, file-drawer effect, low-power norms); current reform momentum
- **Questions**: read a short methods section and identify three reasons the conclusion may not generalize; flag a likely predatory journal; propose follow-up experiments after reading a single-paper finding

## Module 5: The Practical Lab — Notebooks, Safety, and Reproducibility (12%)
- **The lab notebook**: chronological, dated, signed; raw data + interpretation; "would a stranger reading this in 5 years be able to reproduce the experiment?"; why bound paper notebooks are still standard in many fields, and the move toward electronic lab notebooks (ELNs)
- **Standard operating procedures (SOPs)** and protocols; SOPs as institutional memory
- **Lab safety**:
  - General: PPE (safety glasses, lab coat, gloves), eyewash and shower locations, fire extinguishers, fume hoods, biosafety cabinets
  - Chemical: SDS (Safety Data Sheets) reading; hazard symbols (GHS); chemical hygiene plan; segregation of incompatibles (oxidisers vs flammables, acids vs bases, water-reactives); proper waste disposal
  - Biological: BSL-1 through BSL-4 levels; sharps; autoclave; spill protocols
  - Radiation: ionising vs non-ionising; ALARA; dosimetry
  - Electrical, mechanical, cryogenic safety
- **Reproducibility infrastructure**: version control (git), code-and-data sharing (GitHub, Zenodo, OSF, Figshare), containerisation (Docker, Singularity), workflow tools (Snakemake, Nextflow, Make)
- **Open science practices**: open data, open source, open access; CC licenses; FAIR data principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable)
- **Statistical software literacy**: spreadsheet (Excel/Google Sheets) vs scripting (R, Python with pandas/numpy/scipy/statsmodels, Julia); strengths and pitfalls of each; why analyses in spreadsheets are hard to audit
- **Questions**: critique a sample lab-notebook entry; pick the right PPE for a given procedure; identify why a published study fails to be reproducible; propose an open-science workflow for a small project

## Module 6: Scientific Communication (10%)
- **Writing for the field**: structure of a scientific paper; voice (active vs passive — modern guidance favours active where appropriate); precision over flourish
- **Figures and tables**: choosing the right chart; axes labelled with units; error bars defined in the caption; colour-blind-safe palettes; figure-as-argument (each figure should make one point)
- **Tufte principles**: data-ink ratio, chartjunk, small multiples, integration of figure and text
- **Posters and talks**: the elevator pitch, the 3-minute thesis, the conference talk, the seminar talk; how to handle questions; how to pace
- **Public communication**: writing for a general audience without dumbing down; the "ladder of abstraction"; analogy and metaphor; the dangers of overclaiming; navigating media interviews
- **Visualization for the public**: information graphics, interactive visualizations, dashboards; the difference between persuasion and accuracy
- **Communicating uncertainty**: probability vs confidence; weather forecasts as a model; the IPCC's likelihood scale; the COVID-era debates over framing risk
- **Questions**: redesign a bad chart; write a 250-word lay summary of a technical paper; rewrite an overclaiming press release more honestly

## Module 7: Research Ethics and the Sociology of Science (10%)
- **Foundational ethical principles** (Belmont Report): respect for persons, beneficence, justice
- **Human subjects research**: Tuskegee, Henrietta Lacks/HeLa cells, Milgram, Stanford Prison; the IRB process today
- **Animal research ethics**: 3Rs (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement); IACUC; species-appropriate considerations
- **Research misconduct**: fabrication, falsification, plagiarism (FFP); landmark cases (Hwang Woo-suk's stem cells, Diederik Stapel's psychology fabrications, Andrew Wakefield's MMR-autism study); the Office of Research Integrity
- **Authorship**: ICMJE criteria; gift, ghost, and guest authorship; honorary first-authorship; CRediT taxonomy
- **Conflicts of interest**: financial, ideological, careerist; required disclosures
- **Bias in science**: who chooses research questions? whose populations get studied? historical exclusions in clinical trials (women, non-white populations); the WEIRD-sample critique in psychology (Henrich et al., 2010)
- **Equity, diversity, and inclusion in research**: pipeline issues, mentorship gaps, citation bias; recent efforts and their critiques
- **Dual-use research**: gain-of-function, nuclear, AI; the Asilomar conference legacy; modern AI-safety analogues
- **Science and the public**: vaccine hesitancy, climate communication, science denialism; trust as a finite resource
- **The economics of science**: grant funding (NIH, NSF, ERC, JSPS), the "publish or perish" treadmill, citation metrics (h-index, JIF and its critiques — DORA), the rise of pre-print culture
- **Questions**: identify FFP categories in a hypothetical scenario; write an authorship-allocation memo for a complex collaboration; design a community-engaged research consent process

## Module 8: Domain Tour — Methods Across Fields (8%)
- **Physics**: theory + experiment + computation; the role of large collaborations (LHC, LIGO); systematic vs statistical errors; "5-sigma" thresholds and why
- **Chemistry**: synthesis + characterisation (NMR, mass spec, crystallography); reaction yields; mechanism diagrams as evidence
- **Biology**: from molecules to ecosystems; model organisms (E. coli, yeast, Arabidopsis, C. elegans, Drosophila, zebrafish, mouse); the rise of -omics; high-throughput screening
- **Medicine and clinical research**: phase I–IV trials; double-blind RCTs; meta-analyses and the Cochrane reviews; evidence hierarchies
- **Earth and climate science**: long time-series, models with internal variability, attribution science (how do we say climate change "caused" a heatwave?); IPCC process
- **Computer science / data science**: benchmarks, ablations, fair comparisons; the special problem of leakage; reproducibility in ML (random seeds, hardware variability, dataset versioning)
- **Social sciences**: surveys, observational data, natural experiments, large-scale field experiments; ethnography; mixed-methods research
- **Humanities-adjacent science**: linguistics (corpora, judgement studies), archaeology (radiocarbon, stratigraphy, isotopes), forensic science (and the National Academy of Sciences 2009 critique)
- **Questions**: pair a research question with the right methodological tradition; identify why a method appropriate in one field is misleading in another (e.g., in-vitro generalising to in-vivo; lab to field; WEIRD undergrads to "humans")

## Stretch Zone (push beyond first-year foundations)
- **Bayesian inference** as a coherent framework: Bayes factors, posterior predictive checks; the Bernstein–von Mises theorem (intuition)
- **Causal inference**: do-calculus, counterfactuals, propensity scores, structural causal models (Pearl)
- **Replication and metascience**: COS Reproducibility Project for Psychology; many-analysts studies (Silberzahn et al.); the *garden of forking paths*; specification curves
- **Open science 2.0**: registered reports, badges, sharing standards; the politics of data sharing in industry-funded research
- **AI in science (2024–2026)**: large-language-model-assisted hypothesis generation, AlphaFold and structural biology, ML for protein design (Baker lab, RFdiffusion), foundation models for materials science; the LLM-paper-mill explosion and how journals are responding (PubPeer, image forensics tools)
- **The reproducibility tools stack**: Quarto, Jupyter, Pluto, executable papers; computational notebooks as paper-of-record
- **Information theory** as a unifying framework (entropy, mutual information) for measurement and modelling
- **Philosophy of statistics** in depth: severity testing (Mayo), error-statistical methodology, the fundamental problem of causal inference (Rubin)
- **Common student traps**:
  - Treating "statistically significant" as "important"
  - Confusing precision with accuracy (a tightly clustered set of measurements that are systematically wrong)
  - Reading a p < 0.05 result as "the effect is real" when power is low and prior probability of the hypothesis is small (Ioannidis 2005)
  - Treating peer review as a stamp of correctness rather than a filter
  - Believing that models are either right or wrong rather than useful for some purposes and not others
  - Drawing causal arrows from observational data without explicit assumptions
  - Forgetting that *absence of evidence is not evidence of absence* — but neither is "more research is needed" a free lunch
- **The skill the modern scientist must master in 2026**: working *with* AI tools (literature search, code, drafting) while being able to verify, critique, and own every claim. Outsourcing thinking is the new failure mode; outsourcing toil is the new advantage.
