# International Relations
**ID**: `internationalRelations` · **Discipline**: Social Science · **Year**: University (Sophomore–Junior)

## Course Aim
International Relations studies how states, international organizations, firms, courts, armed groups, movements, and individuals pursue interests, make claims, cooperate, and fight under conditions of anarchy and deep uncertainty. The course treats world politics as something to be analyzed rather than cheered or condemned: students learn to take any international event — an alliance forming, a war beginning, a treaty stalling, a sanction biting — and explain it with disciplined attention to actors, interests, incentives, institutions, evidence, and what remains genuinely unknown. The recurring intellectual move is separating description (what happened) from explanation (why), prediction (what next), and prescription (what should be done), and refusing to let one collapse into another.

The course is theory-plural by design. Realism, liberal institutionalism, and constructivism are taught as competing lenses, each with characteristic insights and blind spots, alongside Marxist and world-systems analysis, feminist IR, postcolonial approaches, and critical security studies. No single theory is treated as the truth; the skill is knowing which lens a given puzzle rewards and why a rival lens would object. Theory is paired throughout with method: bargaining models of war, levels-of-analysis reasoning, careful case selection, and source triangulation are taught as tools for turning headlines into evidence rather than into opinion. Quantitative and legal claims — capability ratios, treaty text, court jurisdiction, sanctions data — are tied to specific cases rather than asserted as universals.

By the end of the course, students should be able to read a fast-moving crisis and write a non-partisan briefing that maps the actors, applies at least two theories, identifies the institutional and legal terrain, weighs economic and human stakes, audits its sources, and labels its own uncertainty. The standard is analytical fluency under pressure: explaining why bargaining failed, why a powerful state complied with a weak institution, or why rivalry and cooperation coexist on the same day — without sliding into advocacy slogans or single-cause stories.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when students need to analyze interstate and civil conflict, alliances and deterrence, international law and organizations (UN, ICJ, ICC, WTO, NATO, EU, regional bodies), foreign-policy decision-making, international political economy (trade, finance, sanctions, development), human rights and migration, and transnational problems (climate, pandemics, cyber, AI governance, critical minerals). Keep answers operational and theory-aware: name the actors and their interests, identify the relevant level of analysis, surface the institutional and legal constraints, and state what the evidence can and cannot show. Always distinguish what actors *say* (public justification) from what the situation *rewards* (strategic incentive), and flag uncertainty explicitly rather than projecting false confidence about live events. Prefer concrete cases — recent or historical — over abstractions, and when a student reaches for a single cause, push for the competing explanation and the level of analysis it implies. Treat the post-2019 paralysis of the WTO Appellate Body, the post-2022 UN veto-initiative debates, and the gap between R2P's promise and its practice as standing reminders that institutions are sites of politics, not neutral machinery.

## Chapter 1: What Is International Relations? Levels and Lenses
**Core questions**: What makes an issue "international" rather than merely domestic? How can the same event look different at the individual, state, and system levels? What facts would you need before making a responsible claim about a crisis still unfolding?
**Key concepts**: The actors of world politics (states, IGOs, NGOs, firms, courts, networks, armed groups, individuals); sovereignty, anarchy, power, legitimacy, interests, norms, institutions, security, interdependence, global governance; Waltz's three images / levels of analysis (individual, domestic/state, international system); the difference between description, explanation, prediction, prescription, and evaluation; reading news as evidence (actors, claims, timeline, incentives, uncertainty, source comparison).
**Applied skills**: Take one current event and re-explain it three times — once as a story about a leader, once as a story about domestic politics, once as a story about the system — and show what each level highlights and hides. Build a one-page brief that separates established facts from contested claims and labels the missing information.
**Common traps**: Treating "anarchy" as chaos rather than as the absence of a central authority above states; assuming the state is always the right unit of analysis; mistaking a vivid anecdote for a system-level pattern; smuggling a prescription ("they should…") into what claims to be an explanation; over-updating on the first report of a breaking crisis.

## Chapter 2: Theories of World Politics
**Core questions**: How would a realist, a liberal, and a constructivist each explain the same alliance, war, or treaty? What does each lens reveal about nuclear proliferation, and what does it miss? When does economic interdependence dampen conflict, and when does it create exploitable vulnerability?
**Key concepts**: Realism and neorealism (anarchy, survival, self-help, relative gains, balance of power, polarity, the security dilemma, offensive vs. defensive realism); liberalism and neoliberal institutionalism (absolute gains, complex interdependence, democratic-peace arguments, institutions reducing transaction costs and uncertainty); constructivism (identity, norms, socialization, the social construction of interests and sovereignty); critical approaches (Marxist and world-systems analysis, dependency, feminist IR, postcolonial theory, race and empire, critical security studies); theory as a disciplined lens, not a complete account.
**Applied skills**: Write a comparative theory memo applying three lenses to one issue, then defend which is most persuasive *for that case* and name the evidence that would change your mind. Distinguish a theory's core assumptions from its empirical predictions so you can say when it has actually been tested.
**Common traps**: Treating realism as "pessimism" and liberalism as "optimism" rather than as assumptions about structure and cooperation; reading the democratic-peace claim as "democracies never fight" instead of "democracies rarely fight *each other*"; assuming constructivism denies material power (it explains how material facts acquire meaning); using "it depends on your theory" as an excuse to avoid evidence.

## Chapter 3: States, Foreign Policy, and Diplomacy
**Core questions**: How can two governments with the same information reach different decisions? What makes a diplomatic signal credible rather than cheap talk? Why do leaders sometimes choose costly confrontation when a compromise is visibly available?
**Key concepts**: The modern state system, sovereignty, recognition, borders, nationalism, state capacity; foreign-policy decision-making (rational-actor model, bureaucratic politics, organizational process, leader psychology and misperception, public opinion, interest groups, regime type); Putnam's two-level game; diplomacy and coercion (negotiation, signaling, credibility, costly signals, mediation, sanctions, summitry, back channels, public diplomacy); hard, soft, and smart power and their limits; intelligence, secrecy, and escalation risk.
**Applied skills**: Reconstruct a real foreign-policy decision as a decision tree — choices, constraints, information gaps, and plausible unintended consequences — and identify which decision-making model best fits the evidence. Classify a given signal (a troop movement, a summit, a sanction) as credible or cheap and explain why.
**Common traps**: Defaulting to the "unitary rational actor" when bureaucratic bargaining or domestic ratification better explains the outcome; assuming a costly policy was irrational rather than asking what audience or commitment problem made it rational; treating soft power as a substitute for, rather than a complement to, hard power; reading every leader's statement as their true private belief.

## Chapter 4: International Institutions and Law
**Core questions**: Why do states comply with international law when central enforcement is weak? Which institutional design choices make cooperation easier or harder? How should an analyst judge UN action when the permanent members disagree?
**Key concepts**: The UN system (General Assembly, Security Council, Secretariat, agencies, peacekeeping, the P5 veto and the post-2022 veto-initiative requiring an automatic GA debate after any veto); sources of international law (treaties, customary law via state practice plus *opinio juris*, general principles); jurisdiction, compliance, and the gap between legal authority and practical capacity; courts and accountability (ICJ for state disputes, ICC for individual criminal responsibility, ad hoc tribunals, universal jurisdiction, debates over selectivity); regional bodies and alliances (NATO and Article 5, the EU, African Union, OAS, ASEAN); NGOs, transnational advocacy networks, and private standard-setters.
**Applied skills**: Produce an institutional-design analysis of one organization — mandate, membership, decision rules, achievements, constraints, and a defensible reform proposal. Locate a specific dispute in the correct forum (ICJ vs. ICC vs. regional court vs. arbitration) and state what remedy that body could actually grant.
**Common traps**: Confusing the ICJ (states) with the ICC (individuals), and either with the (non-UN) European Court of Human Rights; treating a Security Council veto as illegal rather than as a designed feature; assuming a treaty signature equals a binding obligation (ratification, reservations, and self-executing vs. non-self-executing status matter); inferring that weak enforcement means law has no effect, when reputation, reciprocity, and domestic incorporation often drive compliance.

## Chapter 5: War, Peace, and Security
**Core questions**: If war is costly for everyone, why does bargaining ever fail? What separates deterrence from compellence, and why is one usually harder? When does peacekeeping reduce violence, and when does it become contested or ineffective?
**Key concepts**: Forms of conflict (interstate, civil, proxy, insurgency, terrorism, cyber, hybrid and gray-zone competition); the bargaining model of war and Fearon's three rationalist explanations — private information with incentives to misrepresent, commitment problems (including power transitions and first-strike advantage), and issue indivisibility; deterrence, compellence, the stability of a secure second strike, arms control, proliferation and nonproliferation regimes (NPT); preventive vs. preemptive war; peacekeeping (consent, impartiality, limited force), peacebuilding, ceasefires, DDR, and transitional justice; human security and the widening of the security agenda.
**Applied skills**: Build a conflict-analysis map (parties, grievances, resources, external backers, escalation risks, peace efforts, plausible next steps) and locate the bargaining-failure mechanism most consistent with the evidence. Distinguish a deterrent threat from a compellent demand in a real crisis and assess each for credibility.
**Common traps**: Explaining war by "aggression" or "irrationality" instead of identifying the specific bargaining failure; conflating preventive war (attacking a rising power now) with preemptive war (striking an imminent attack); assuming nuclear weapons make a state safe in every dimension rather than stabilizing one specific deterrent relationship; treating all peace operations as the robust, enforcement-style mission when most rest on consent.

## Chapter 6: International Political Economy
**Core questions**: Who gains and who loses from a trade agreement, and over what time horizon? How can sanctions signal resolve while imposing humanitarian and diplomatic costs that blunt their effect? Why do states disagree so sharply about the fairness of the same global economic rules?
**Key concepts**: Trade, finance, development, debt, sanctions, global supply chains, industrial policy, and economic statecraft; competing IPE lenses (mercantilist/economic nationalism, liberal, Marxist, dependency, institutionalist); the Bretton Woods order (IMF for balance-of-payments and macro surveillance, World Bank for development finance, GATT→WTO for trade rules) and the post-2019 paralysis of the WTO Appellate Body and "appeals into the void"; comparative vs. absolute advantage and its distributional politics; globalization and backlash (inequality, capital mobility, tariffs, investment screening, friend-shoring, resilience); development debates (aid, debt relief, conditionality, institutions, extractive vs. inclusive growth).
**Applied skills**: Write a policy-neutral economic brief on one trade, debt, sanctions, or development question that names the winners and losers, the time horizon, and at least two readings of the evidence. Explain a specific sanctions regime in terms of its goal (coerce, constrain, signal), its mechanism, and its observed and unintended effects.
**Common traps**: Assuming "free trade is good for everyone" when the gains are aggregate and the losses are concentrated; treating sanctions as automatically effective rather than asking against whom and through what channel; confusing the IMF (short-term stabilization) with the World Bank (long-term development); narrating development as a single ladder all countries climb identically, ignoring structural position in the world economy.

## Chapter 7: Rights, People, and Global Society
**Core questions**: How can human-rights claims both challenge and depend on state sovereignty? What actually distinguishes a migrant, a refugee, an asylum seeker, and an internally displaced person? How should an analyst weigh moral urgency, legal authority, and operational feasibility in a humanitarian crisis?
**Key concepts**: The human-rights regime (UDHR; the civil-political and economic-social-cultural covenants; the contested status of collective and "third-generation" rights); refugee and humanitarian law (the 1951 Convention and non-refoulement, the laws of armed conflict / IHL); the Responsibility to Protect and its three pillars from the 2005 World Summit (state responsibility, international assistance, timely collective response when a state "manifestly fails") and the gap between doctrine and practice; migration, asylum, diaspora politics, remittances, border regimes, and trafficking; global civil society, social movements, digital activism, misinformation; the unequal distribution of insecurity and representation by gender, race, ethnicity, religion, and indigeneity.
**Applied skills**: Build a source-based case profile on a rights or migration issue that separates legal categories from empirical claims and from stakeholder arguments. Apply the correct legal label (refugee vs. economic migrant vs. IDP) to a real population flow and explain the protections that follow from each.
**Common traps**: Using "refugee" as a catch-all for everyone on the move (the legal definition is specific and consequential); reading R2P as a license for intervention (it is primarily a state's duty to its own people, and Pillar III runs through the Security Council); assuming naming a right guarantees its enforcement; treating sovereignty and human rights as simple opposites when rights regimes are enforced largely *through* states.

## Chapter 8: Planetary Problems and Current-Events Analysis
**Core questions**: Why are climate negotiations so hard even when the physical risk is well documented? When do transnational problems demand formal treaties, and when does informal coordination work better? How can rivals cooperate on one issue while competing fiercely on another?
**Key concepts**: Climate as a global commons / collective-action problem (free riding, burden-sharing, mitigation vs. adaptation, climate finance, loss and damage); other planetary problems (pandemics and global health governance, food and water insecurity, the energy transition and critical minerals, AI and cyber governance, space politics); global public goods and why they are under-provided; the coexistence of great-power competition with issue-specific cooperation; methods for current-events analysis (triangulating sources, tracking incentives, avoiding single-cause explanations, labeling and updating uncertainty).
**Applied skills**: Diagnose a transnational problem as a collective-action structure — who benefits, who pays, who can defect — and judge whether a treaty, club, or informal regime fits best. Produce a short portfolio of current-events briefs that each apply theory, institutions, evidence, and explicit uncertainty labels.
**Common traps**: Treating climate inaction as ignorance rather than as a collective-action and distributional problem; assuming a global problem requires a single global treaty when smaller "climate clubs" or standards may move faster; reading cooperation and rivalry as mutually exclusive; mistaking the loudest source for the most reliable one and failing to revise judgments as evidence changes.

## Capstone
Students produce a non-partisan international crisis briefing on a live or recent case. The brief must (1) establish a factual background timeline and an actor map with each actor's interests and capabilities; (2) apply at least two IR theories and explain what each reveals and obscures; (3) identify the institutional and legal terrain — the relevant organizations, the correct legal forum, and what authority or remedy actually exists; (4) where conflict is involved, name the specific bargaining-failure mechanism (private information, commitment problem, or indivisibility) most consistent with the evidence; (5) weigh the economic stakes and the human impacts using legal categories precisely; (6) audit its sources for date, author, evidence type, scope, and what each source cannot prove; and (7) close with an explicit uncertainty assessment and a short options section that states assumptions and lays out the tradeoffs and evidentiary burdens of each path — without advocacy slogans. Students defend the brief orally, fielding challenges on causal claims, source quality, level-of-analysis choices, and unacknowledged uncertainty.

## Assessment Ideas
- **Weekly global news log (10%)**: Track one issue across multiple sources, recording actors, evidence, incentives, source bias, and uncertainty notes.
- **Comparative theory memo (15%)**: Apply at least three IR lenses to one issue and defend the most persuasive for that case.
- **Institutional design analysis (15%)**: Dissect one organization or treaty regime — mandate, membership, decision rules, achievements, constraints, and a reform proposal.
- **Conflict map / Model Diplomacy role brief (15%)**: Map a conflict or argue a state's position in a structured simulation, then reflect on bargaining, information, and constraints.
- **Midterm concept exam (15%)**: Precise definitions and case application across theory, law, security, and IPE.
- **Crisis-briefing capstone (30%)**: The full non-partisan briefing and oral defense described above.

Assess for accurate institutional and legal detail, correct use of theory, clear identification of actors and incentives, disciplined separation of public justification from strategic incentive and of description from prescription, and honest labeling of uncertainty.

## Research Notes
- Council on Foreign Relations backgrounders and Model Diplomacy cases support structured, non-partisan scenario analysis; the International Studies Association teaching resources help calibrate terminology and core debates.
- OpenStax political science chapters and LibreTexts social science materials support introductory concepts, especially institutions, comparative-politics links, and political-economy background.
- Students should compare public-facing sources with primary documents whenever possible: treaty text, UN resolutions and the 2005 World Summit Outcome (paras 138–139), ICJ and ICC opinions, government statements, NGO reports, and IO datasets.
- For accuracy on live issues, anchor claims to primary or institutional sources and record publication date, author or institution, evidence type, geographic scope, and what the source cannot prove — then state the residual uncertainty rather than hiding it.
