# International Relations
**ID**: `internationalRelations` | **Discipline**: Social Science | **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
This course introduces students to the major theories, institutions, conflicts, and global problems that shape international relations. Students learn to explain how states and non-state actors pursue interests, make claims, cooperate, compete, and respond to crises under conditions of uncertainty. The course is rigorous but introductory: it emphasizes conceptual clarity, evidence-based comparison, and nonpartisan current-events analysis rather than policy advocacy.

By the end of the course, students should be able to analyze an international event through multiple theoretical lenses, distinguish description from interpretation, evaluate institutional design and legal claims, and write a concise briefing that connects current developments to historical patterns and scholarly concepts.

## Course Design Notes
- Use a case-driven rhythm: each chapter should pair core concepts with at least one historical case and one current issue.
- Keep theory plural: realism, liberalism, constructivism, Marxist and world-systems approaches, feminist IR, postcolonial theory, and critical security studies should be presented as tools with strengths and limits.
- Build a current-events habit: students should maintain a weekly global news log that tracks source, actors, interests, evidence, uncertainty, and possible bias.
- Avoid partisan framing: controversial issues should be assessed through competing arguments, institutional constraints, empirical evidence, and clearly stated assumptions.
- Useful open and public-facing anchors include Council on Foreign Relations backgrounders and Model Diplomacy cases, International Studies Association resources, OpenStax political science materials, LibreTexts social science chapters, and introductory IR syllabi from universities.

## Chapter 1: What Is International Relations?
### Key Concepts
- International relations as the study of interaction among states, international organizations, firms, movements, armed groups, courts, networks, and individuals.
- Sovereignty, anarchy, power, legitimacy, interests, norms, institutions, security, interdependence, and global governance.
- Levels of analysis: individual decision makers, domestic politics, state behavior, regional systems, and the international system.
- The difference between description, explanation, prediction, prescription, and evaluation.
- How to read global news: actors, claims, evidence, timeline, incentives, uncertainty, and source comparison.

### Practice Questions
- What makes an issue "international" rather than merely domestic?
- How would the same event look different at the individual, state, and system levels?
- What facts would you need before making a responsible claim about a fast-moving crisis?

### Student Output
Students produce a one-page current-events brief that identifies the actors, timeline, stakes, missing information, and at least two possible interpretations of one recent international event.

## Chapter 2: Theories of World Politics
### Key Concepts
- Realism and neorealism: anarchy, survival, relative gains, balance of power, deterrence, polarity, and the security dilemma.
- Liberalism and neoliberal institutionalism: cooperation, absolute gains, democratic peace arguments, international law, institutions, and economic interdependence.
- Constructivism: identity, norms, socialization, legitimacy, and the idea that interests are partly made through interaction.
- Critical approaches: Marxist and world-systems analysis, dependency, feminism, postcolonial theory, race and empire, and critical security studies.
- Theory as a disciplined lens rather than a complete explanation of every case.

### Practice Questions
- How would a realist, liberal, and constructivist explain alliance formation?
- What does each theory help reveal about nuclear proliferation, and what might it miss?
- When does economic interdependence reduce conflict, and when might it create vulnerability?

### Student Output
Students write a comparative theory memo applying three IR theories to the same issue, with a final paragraph explaining which lens is most persuasive and why.

## Chapter 3: States, Foreign Policy, and Diplomacy
### Key Concepts
- The modern state system, sovereignty, recognition, borders, nationalism, and state capacity.
- Foreign policy decision making: rational actor models, bureaucratic politics, organizational process, leader psychology, public opinion, interest groups, and regime type.
- Diplomacy, negotiation, signaling, credibility, coercion, mediation, sanctions, summitry, back channels, and public diplomacy.
- Hard power, soft power, smart power, and the limits of each.
- Intelligence, secrecy, misperception, and the risks of escalation.

### Practice Questions
- How can bureaucratic politics change the outcome of a foreign policy decision?
- What makes a diplomatic signal credible?
- Why do leaders sometimes choose costly policies even when compromise is available?

### Student Output
Students reconstruct a major foreign policy decision as a decision tree, identifying choices, constraints, likely information gaps, and possible unintended consequences.

## Chapter 4: International Institutions and Law
### Key Concepts
- The United Nations system: General Assembly, Security Council, Secretariat, agencies, peacekeeping, and the politics of veto power.
- International law: treaties, customary law, jurisdiction, compliance, enforcement, and the difference between legal authority and practical capacity.
- Courts and accountability: International Court of Justice, International Criminal Court, tribunals, universal jurisdiction, and debates over selectivity.
- Regional organizations and alliances, including NATO, the European Union, African Union, Organization of American States, ASEAN, and others.
- Non-governmental organizations, transnational advocacy networks, expert communities, and private standard-setting bodies.

### Practice Questions
- Why do states comply with international law when enforcement is weak?
- What institutional design choices make cooperation easier or harder?
- How should analysts evaluate UN action when powerful states disagree?

### Student Output
Students complete an institutional design analysis of one international organization, explaining its mandate, membership, decision rules, achievements, constraints, and reform proposals.

## Chapter 5: War, Peace, and Security
### Key Concepts
- Interstate war, civil war, proxy war, insurgency, terrorism, cyber conflict, hybrid war, and gray-zone competition.
- Causes of war: security dilemma, commitment problems, incomplete information, preventive war, diversionary incentives, nationalism, and failed bargaining.
- Deterrence, compellence, arms control, nuclear strategy, mutually assured destruction, proliferation, and nonproliferation regimes.
- Peacebuilding, peacekeeping, mediation, ceasefires, transitional justice, disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration.
- Human security and the widening of security studies beyond military threats alone.

### Practice Questions
- Why can bargaining fail even when war is costly for all sides?
- What is the difference between deterrence and compellence?
- When does peacekeeping reduce violence, and when can it become ineffective or contested?

### Student Output
Students prepare a conflict analysis map that identifies parties, grievances, resources, external actors, escalation risks, peace efforts, and plausible next steps.

## Chapter 6: International Political Economy
### Key Concepts
- Trade, finance, development, debt, sanctions, global supply chains, industrial policy, and economic statecraft.
- Mercantilist, liberal, Marxist, dependency, and institutional approaches to the global economy.
- Bretton Woods institutions: International Monetary Fund, World Bank, General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, World Trade Organization, and debates over reform.
- Globalization and backlash: inequality, labor, capital mobility, technology, outsourcing, tariffs, investment screening, and resilience.
- Development debates: aid, debt relief, conditionality, corruption, capacity, extractive institutions, and sustainable growth.

### Practice Questions
- Who gains and who loses from a trade agreement, and over what time horizon?
- How can sanctions signal resolve while also creating humanitarian or diplomatic costs?
- Why do countries disagree about the fairness of global economic rules?

### Student Output
Students write a policy-neutral economic brief on one trade, debt, sanctions, or development issue, presenting stakeholder interests and at least two competing interpretations of the evidence.

## Chapter 7: Rights, People, and Global Society
### Key Concepts
- Human rights: civil, political, economic, social, cultural, collective, and contested rights claims.
- Universal Declaration of Human Rights, refugee law, humanitarian law, Responsibility to Protect, and debates over sovereignty and intervention.
- Migration, refugees, asylum, diaspora politics, remittances, border regimes, trafficking, and citizenship.
- Global civil society, social movements, media, digital activism, misinformation, and transnational networks.
- Gender, race, ethnicity, religion, indigeneity, and the unequal distribution of insecurity and representation in world politics.

### Practice Questions
- How can human rights claims both challenge and depend on state sovereignty?
- What is the difference between a migrant, refugee, asylum seeker, and internally displaced person?
- How should analysts weigh moral urgency, legal authority, and operational feasibility in humanitarian crises?

### Student Output
Students create a source-based case profile on a human rights or migration issue, distinguishing legal categories, empirical claims, stakeholder arguments, and unresolved questions.

## Chapter 8: Planetary Problems and Current-Events Analysis
### Key Concepts
- Climate change as an environmental, economic, security, justice, and governance problem.
- Global public goods, collective action, free riding, burden sharing, adaptation, mitigation, climate finance, and loss and damage.
- Pandemics, food insecurity, water scarcity, energy transition, critical minerals, artificial intelligence, cyber governance, and space politics.
- Great-power competition and global cooperation: why rivalry can coexist with issue-specific coordination.
- Methods for current-events analysis: triangulating sources, identifying uncertainty, tracking incentives, avoiding single-cause explanations, and updating judgments as evidence changes.

### Practice Questions
- Why are climate negotiations difficult even when the physical risk is widely documented?
- When do transnational problems require formal treaties, and when might informal coordination work better?
- How can students evaluate a breaking-news claim without overreacting to incomplete information?

### Student Output
Students produce a current-events analysis portfolio with three short briefs, each using theory, institutions, evidence, and uncertainty labels to assess a major global issue.

## Capstone
Students complete a nonpartisan international crisis briefing. The project should include a background timeline, actor map, theory comparison, institutional options, legal or normative claims, economic stakes, human impacts, source audit, uncertainty assessment, and a short recommendation section that states assumptions clearly. Strong projects avoid advocacy slogans and instead show how different policy choices carry different risks, tradeoffs, and evidentiary burdens.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly news log with source comparison and uncertainty notes.
- Theory application memo using at least three IR lenses.
- Institutional design analysis of an international organization or treaty regime.
- Conflict map or Model Diplomacy-style role brief.
- In-class simulation with reflection on bargaining, information, and constraints.
- Midterm concept exam emphasizing precise definitions and case application.
- Final crisis briefing with annotated sources and a short oral presentation.

## Research Notes
- Council on Foreign Relations and Model Diplomacy cases are useful for structured, nonpartisan scenario analysis.
- International Studies Association teaching resources and university IR introductions can help calibrate terminology and core debates.
- OpenStax political science chapters and LibreTexts social science materials can support introductory concepts, especially institutions, comparative politics links, and political economy background.
- Students should compare public-facing sources with primary documents when possible: treaties, UN resolutions, court opinions, government statements, NGO reports, and international organization datasets.
- Research habits matter: record publication date, author or institution, evidence type, geographic scope, and what the source cannot prove.
