# International Relations
**ID**: `internationalRelations` · **Discipline**: Social Science · **Year**: University Junior

## Course Philosophy
This course examines the theoretical frameworks and empirical realities of how sovereign states interact in the global arena. It analyzes power dynamics, international institutions, conflict, and the political economy from a systemic perspective.

## Module 1: Grand Theories of International Relations (25%)
- What is International Relations? Levels of analysis (Individual, State, International System)
- Realism (Classical vs Structural/Neorealism): Anarchy, the security dilemma, balance of power, polarity (unipolar, bipolar, multipolar), and the pursuit of relative gains (Morgenthau, Waltz, Mearsheimer)
- Liberalism (Neoliberal Institutionalism): Democratic peace theory, economic interdependence, absolute gains, and the role of international institutions in mitigating anarchy (Kant, Keohane)
- Constructivism: The social construction of reality, "Anarchy is what states make of it" (Wendt), norms, identity, and ideas
- Critical Theories: Marxism/World Systems Theory (core vs periphery), Feminism in IR, Post-colonialism
- **Questions**: Analyzing a current conflict using Neorealist vs Constructivist lenses, explaining the security dilemma

## Module 2: The State, Foreign Policy, and Diplomacy (20%)
- The concept of Sovereignty (Treaty of Westphalia, 1648) and the Nation-State
- Foreign Policy Analysis: How states make decisions (Rational Actor Model vs Bureaucratic Politics Model)
- Diplomacy: Hard power (military/economic coercion) vs Soft power (cultural/ideological appeal) vs Smart power
- Deterrence theory, Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD), and nuclear proliferation
- **Questions**: Evaluating a historical foreign policy decision using the Bureaucratic Politics model, comparing hard and soft power strategies

## Module 3: International Law and Organizations (20%)
- The role and structure of the United Nations (General Assembly, Security Council, Secretariat)
- International Law: Sources (treaties, customary law), enforcement mechanisms, and state compliance
- Human Rights: The Universal Declaration of Human Rights, humanitarian intervention, and the "Responsibility to Protect" (R2P) doctrine
- The International Criminal Court (ICC) and the International Court of Justice (ICJ)
- Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs) and transnational advocacy networks
- **Questions**: Analyzing the structural limitations of the UN Security Council, evaluating the legal and moral arguments for R2P

## Module 4: International Political Economy (IPE) (20%)
- Mercantilism vs Economic Liberalism
- The Bretton Woods System: The IMF, the World Bank, and the WTO (formerly GATT)
- Globalization: Drivers, impacts on state sovereignty, and backlash
- International trade, tariffs, protectionism, and free trade agreements (e.g., NAFTA/USMCA, EU)
- The North-South divide: Theories of economic development, foreign aid, and debt crises
- **Questions**: Comparing Mercantilist and Liberal approaches to trade policy, evaluating the role of the IMF in developing economies

## Module 5: Contemporary Global Security Challenges (15%)
- The changing nature of war: Interstate vs intrastate conflict, asymmetrical warfare, terrorism
- Transnational threats: Climate change, pandemics, cyber warfare, and resource scarcity
- The rise of non-state actors (multinational corporations, terrorist organizations)
- Shifting global hegemony: The rise of China and the future of the liberal international order
- **Questions**: Analyzing climate change as a global security threat, predicting the impact of a multipolar system on global stability

## Stretch Zone
- Game Theory in IR (Prisoner's Dilemma, Chicken)
- Deep analysis of specific regional conflicts (e.g., the Middle East, the South China Sea)
