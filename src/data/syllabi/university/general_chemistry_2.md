# General Chemistry 2
**ID**: `generalChemistry2` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Philosophy
Continuing from General Chemistry 1, this course transitions from the static properties of atoms and molecules to the dynamic study of chemical systems. It heavily emphasizes the mathematics of reaction rates, equilibrium, thermodynamics, and electrochemistry.

## Module 1: Chemical Kinetics (20%)
- Reaction Rates: Average vs instantaneous rates, measuring rates
- Rate Laws: Determining the rate law from experimental data (method of initial rates), the rate constant ($k$), and reaction order (zero, first, second)
- Integrated Rate Laws: Calculating concentration over time; half-life formulas ($t_{1/2}$)
- Collision Theory and Activation Energy ($E_a$)
- The Arrhenius Equation: The effect of temperature on the rate constant
- Reaction Mechanisms: Elementary steps, molecularity, rate-determining steps, and the role of catalysts
- **Questions**: Determining the rate law from a table of initial rates, using the Arrhenius equation to calculate activation energy

## Module 2: Chemical Equilibrium (20%)
- The concept of dynamic equilibrium and the equilibrium constant ($K_c$, $K_p$)
- The Reaction Quotient ($Q$): Predicting the direction of a reaction ($Q$ vs $K$)
- Calculating equilibrium concentrations using ICE (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) tables
- Le Chatelier's Principle: Predicting shifts due to changes in concentration, pressure, volume, and temperature
- The relationship between $\Delta G^\circ$ and $K$
- **Questions**: Setting up an ICE table to solve for equilibrium concentrations using the quadratic formula, predicting the shift of an endothermic reaction when temperature is increased

## Module 3: Acid-Base Equilibria (25%)
- Brønsted-Lowry definition of acids and bases; conjugate acid-base pairs
- The autoionization of water ($K_w$) and the pH/pOH scales
- Strong vs Weak acids and bases: Acid dissociation constant ($K_a$) and base dissociation constant ($K_b$)
- Calculating the pH of weak acid and weak base solutions
- Polyprotic acids
- Acid-base properties of salts (hydrolysis)
- Buffers: The common ion effect, buffer capacity, and the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation
- Acid-Base Titrations: Interpreting titration curves, finding the equivalence point, and choosing indicators
- **Questions**: Calculating the pH of a buffer solution after the addition of a strong acid, sketching the titration curve for a weak acid/strong base titration

## Module 4: Aqueous Equilibria (Solubility and Complex Ions) (15%)
- Solubility Product Constant ($K_{sp}$): Calculating molar solubility
- Predicting precipitation: The ion product ($Q_{sp}$) vs $K_{sp}$
- The Common Ion Effect on solubility
- The effect of pH on solubility
- Complex ion equilibria ($K_f$, formation constants)
- **Questions**: Calculating the solubility of $AgCl$ in pure water vs in a $0.1M$ $NaCl$ solution, determining if a precipitate will form when two solutions are mixed

## Module 5: Thermodynamics and Electrochemistry (20%)
- Thermodynamics Review: Enthalpy ($\Delta H$), Entropy ($\Delta S$), and the Second/Third Laws of Thermodynamics
- Gibbs Free Energy ($\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$) and spontaneity
- Calculating $\Delta G^\circ$ from standard formation values, and $\Delta G$ under non-standard conditions ($\Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT \ln Q$)
- Electrochemistry: Balancing complex redox reactions in acidic/basic solutions using the half-reaction method
- Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells: Anode, cathode, salt bridge, standard cell potential ($E^\circ_{cell}$)
- The Nernst Equation: Calculating cell potential under non-standard conditions
- Electrolysis and Faraday's Law
- **Questions**: Calculating the standard free energy change of a reaction, using the Nernst equation to find the voltage of a concentration cell

## Stretch Zone
- Nuclear Chemistry: Kinetics of radioactive decay, mass defect, binding energy
- Introduction to Coordination Chemistry
