# General Chemistry II
**ID**: `generalChemistry2` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Aim
Move from first-semester chemistry foundations into dynamic chemical systems: how fast reactions happen, how far they go, how acids and bases behave, how thermodynamics predicts spontaneity, and how electrons can do useful work.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test chemical kinetics, equilibrium, acid-base chemistry, solubility, thermodynamics, electrochemistry, nuclear chemistry, coordination chemistry, laboratory reasoning, or integrated quantitative problem solving. Keep problem setup explicit: balanced equation, equilibrium or rate expression, assumptions, units, and chemical interpretation.

## Chapter 1: Kinetics and Rate Laws
- **Key concepts**: Average and instantaneous rates, rate laws, reaction order, method of initial rates, rate constants, units of k, integrated rate laws, half-life, and concentration-time data.
- **Practice questions**: Determine rate laws from data; use integrated rate laws; interpret half-life patterns.
- **Student output**: A kinetics data analysis with graph, rate law, order, and physical interpretation.

## Chapter 2: Mechanisms, Catalysis, and Temperature Effects
- **Key concepts**: Elementary steps, molecularity, rate-determining step, reaction mechanisms, intermediates, catalysts, activation energy, collision theory, transition state, Arrhenius equation, and reaction-coordinate diagrams.
- **Practice questions**: Validate mechanisms; identify intermediates and catalysts; calculate activation energy from Arrhenius data.
- **Student output**: A mechanism memo connecting proposed steps to the observed rate law.

## Chapter 3: Chemical Equilibrium
- **Key concepts**: Dynamic equilibrium, equilibrium constants, Kc, Kp, reaction quotient Q, ICE tables, Le Chatelier's principle, pressure and temperature effects, and equilibrium approximations.
- **Practice questions**: Predict reaction direction; solve equilibrium concentrations; justify shifts after disturbances.
- **Student output**: An equilibrium problem set with assumptions checked and chemical meaning stated.

## Chapter 4: Acids, Bases, and Buffers
- **Key concepts**: Bronsted-Lowry acids and bases, conjugate pairs, Kw, pH, pOH, strong and weak acids and bases, Ka, Kb, percent ionization, polyprotic acids, salt hydrolysis, common ion effect, buffers, buffer capacity, and Henderson-Hasselbalch equation.
- **Practice questions**: Calculate pH for weak acids, weak bases, salts, and buffers; compare acid strength; explain buffer behavior after addition.
- **Student output**: A buffer design brief with target pH, component choice, calculation, and limitation.

## Chapter 5: Titrations, Solubility, and Complex Ions
- **Key concepts**: Acid-base titration curves, equivalence point, half-equivalence point, indicators, Ksp, molar solubility, Qsp, selective precipitation, common ion effect, pH effects on solubility, complex ion formation, and Kf.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret titration curves; calculate solubility under changing conditions; predict precipitation and complex-ion effects.
- **Student output**: A titration or solubility lab analysis with graph, calculation, and procedural critique.

## Chapter 6: Thermodynamics and Free Energy
- **Key concepts**: Entropy, second and third laws, standard molar entropy, Gibbs free energy, spontaneity, standard free energy, nonstandard conditions, relationship between delta G, Q, K, enthalpy, and entropy.
- **Practice questions**: Calculate delta S, delta G, and temperature thresholds; connect equilibrium constants to free energy; interpret spontaneity carefully.
- **Student output**: A thermodynamics decision table comparing enthalpy, entropy, temperature, and equilibrium.

## Chapter 7: Electrochemistry
- **Key concepts**: Redox balancing, galvanic cells, electrolytic cells, anode, cathode, salt bridge, standard reduction potentials, cell potential, Nernst equation, concentration cells, electrolysis, Faraday's law, batteries, corrosion, and free energy links.
- **Practice questions**: Balance redox equations; calculate cell potentials; apply Nernst equation; solve electrolysis mass or time problems.
- **Student output**: An electrochemical cell diagram with half-reactions, electron flow, voltage calculation, and real-world use.

## Chapter 8: Nuclear, Coordination, and Chemistry II Studio
- **Key concepts**: Radioactive decay, half-life, nuclear equations, mass defect, binding energy, fission, fusion, coordination complexes, ligands, oxidation states, isomerism, crystal-field intuition, integrated lab skills, and cumulative problem solving.
- **Practice questions**: Solve decay problems; balance nuclear equations; name or interpret coordination complexes; combine equilibrium, thermodynamics, and electrochemistry.
- **Student output**: A final integrated chemistry case study with data, calculations, and explanatory narrative.

## Capstone
Students complete a Chemistry II systems case: choose a reaction or process, analyze rate, equilibrium, thermodynamic favorability, and electron transfer or acid-base behavior, then present a lab- or industry-relevant recommendation.

## Assessment Ideas
- Mixed calculation sets that require setup, assumptions, units, and interpretation.
- Data-based kinetics and equilibrium labs.
- Titration, solubility, and electrochemistry practical writeups.
- Short conceptual checks on Q versus K, delta G versus spontaneity, and rate versus extent.
- A cumulative oral or written defense of an integrated chemical system.
