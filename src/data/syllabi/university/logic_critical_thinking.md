# Logic & Critical Thinking
**ID**: `logicCriticalThinking` - **Discipline**: Humanities / Philosophy - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: typical PHIL 100 / PHIL 110 / Logic / Critical Reasoning courses

## Course Aim
This course trains the single most transferable skill in any degree: taking a messy claim and figuring out, precisely, whether you should believe it. Students learn to reconstruct an argument in standard form, name the inference that is supposed to carry it, and then stress-test that inference with the tools logicians and scientists actually use — truth tables, quantifier rules, probability, controlled comparison, and source verification. The goal is not to win debates but to track the truth: to say exactly what is being claimed, what evidence would count against it, and what the strongest opposing case looks like.

We treat reasoning as a craft with two halves that constantly check each other. The formal half (propositional and predicate logic) gives crisp standards for when a conclusion *must* follow. The informal half (fallacies, bias, induction, causation, evidence) handles the looser, real-world arguments where conclusions are only made *more probable* — and where most actual mistakes happen. A student who can do both can move fluidly between a proof, a Bayesian update on a medical test result, a critique of a shaky study, and a paragraph of honest argumentative prose.

By the end, students should reason better under the conditions that matter now: scrolling, arguing, and deciding in a feed full of confident strangers, viral statistics, and AI-generated text. The course deliberately ends in public life — misinformation, expertise, synthetic media, and the discipline of changing one's own mind — because logic that never leaves the worksheet is logic that was never really learned.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve arguments, premises and conclusions, validity vs. soundness, deductive vs. inductive vs. abductive reasoning, propositional or predicate logic, truth tables, natural deduction, quantifiers, informal fallacies, cognitive bias, probability and Bayes, correlation vs. causation, scientific method and study design, source evaluation, misinformation, argument mapping, or the structure of reasoned prose. The recurring discipline is: **identify the exact claim, state the evidence that would count against it, and articulate the strongest objection before replying.**

Chapters move from the anatomy of a single argument (Ch. 1) through formal logic (Ch. 2-3), into the messier real-world reasoning where most errors live (Ch. 4-6), and out into public/digital judgment and writing (Ch. 7-8). Earlier formal chapters are deliberately rule-precise so later chapters can lean on them ("this is just affirming the consequent dressed up as a headline"). Questions should reward students who distinguish *what follows* from *what is true*, and who can repair a bad argument rather than merely label it.

## Chapter 1: Arguments, Charity, and Standard Form
**Core questions**: What turns a set of sentences into an argument? How do you separate premises from conclusion, surface a hidden premise, and rebuild a weak argument in its strongest form before judging it?
**Key concepts**: Statement vs. command/question, premise, conclusion, inference, premise/conclusion indicators ("since," "therefore"), standard form, implicit (suppressed) premise, deductive vs. inductive vs. abductive argument, validity, soundness, inductive strength, cogency, the principle of charity, and steelmanning.
**Applied skills**: Convert prose into numbered standard form; classify an argument as deductive, inductive, or abductive; supply the missing premise that makes an enthymeme valid; distinguish a *valid* argument from a *true* conclusion; rewrite a flimsy argument into its most defensible version before attacking it.
**Common traps**: Treating "valid" as a synonym for "true" or "good" (validity is only about form — premises true → conclusion true); calling an argument unsound just because you dislike the conclusion; confusing soundness (valid + actually-true premises) with validity; ignoring an unstated premise and then refuting a claim no one made.

## Chapter 2: Propositional Logic
**Core questions**: When does a conclusion follow with logical necessity from compound statements? How do truth tables and a few inference rules let you certify or refute an argument mechanically?
**Key concepts**: Negation, conjunction, disjunction (inclusive vs. exclusive), conditional, biconditional, truth table, tautology, contradiction, contingency, logical equivalence, De Morgan's laws, modus ponens, modus tollens, hypothetical syllogism, disjunctive syllogism, conditional proof, and reductio ad absurdum.
**Applied skills**: Translate English (including "unless," "only if," "neither…nor") into symbols; build a full truth table; test an argument's validity by truth table; run short natural-deduction proofs; apply De Morgan's and contraposition correctly.
**Common traps**: Affirming the consequent ("If P then Q; Q; so P") and denying the antecedent ("If P then Q; not-P; so not-Q") — both invalid, unlike modus ponens and modus tollens; reading "only if" as "if"; treating the conditional P→Q as equivalent to its converse Q→P rather than its contrapositive ¬Q→¬P; assuming "or" must be exclusive.

## Chapter 3: Predicate Logic and Quantifiers
**Core questions**: Why isn't "all" the same as "some," and how does the *order* of quantifiers change what a sentence means? How do you build a concrete countermodel to show an argument is invalid?
**Key concepts**: Predicate, name (constant), variable, universal quantifier (∀), existential quantifier (∃), quantifier negation, scope, "only" vs. "all," mixed quantifiers and quantifier order, identity, uniqueness, interpretation/model, and counterexample/countermodel.
**Applied skills**: Translate "all," "some," "no," and "only" statements into quantified form; negate a quantified statement by pushing the negation inward (∀ ↔ ∃ flips at each step); explain why "everyone loves someone" ≠ "someone is loved by everyone"; construct a small model that makes the premises true and the conclusion false.
**Common traps**: Negating "All S are P" as "No S are P" instead of the correct "Some S is not P"; swapping the order of mixed quantifiers and assuming the meaning is unchanged; translating "Only members may vote" as "All voters are members reversed" or otherwise getting subject/predicate backwards; thinking one supporting example proves a universal claim.

## Chapter 4: Informal Fallacies and Cognitive Bias
**Core questions**: How do you diagnose *why* a real-world argument is bad — and distinguish genuinely defective reasoning from a conclusion you simply find unpopular? Which of your own biases are most likely to be operating right now?
**Key concepts**: Ad hominem, straw man, red herring, equivocation, composition/division, begging the question (circular reasoning), false dichotomy, slippery slope, hasty generalization, appeal to authority vs. legitimate expertise, sunk-cost fallacy, gambler's fallacy, confirmation bias, availability heuristic, anchoring, framing, and motivated reasoning.
**Applied skills**: Diagnose the specific fallacy in a public claim and explain the flaw; tell a fallacy apart from a merely weak-but-relevant argument; steelman an opponent before responding; name a likely bias in your own first reaction to evidence.
**Common traps**: "Fallacy-hunting" — slapping a Latin label on any argument you dislike (e.g., calling all expert citation "appeal to authority," when citing relevant experts is reasonable); confusing a circumstantial point about a person with a genuine ad hominem; mistaking a slippery-slope *warning* for a fallacy when the causal chain is actually supported; assuming naming a bias in others immunizes you from it.

## Chapter 5: Induction, Probability, and Causation
**Core questions**: When does evidence make a conclusion *more probable* without guaranteeing it? Why does a positive result on an accurate test for a rare condition often still mean you probably don't have it?
**Key concepts**: Generalization, sample, representativeness, sample size, analogical argument, correlation vs. causation, confounder, reverse causation, conditional probability, base rate, Bayes' theorem, prior vs. posterior, expected value, and inference to the best explanation (abduction).
**Applied skills**: Judge whether a sample supports a generalization; identify a plausible confounder behind a correlation; distinguish P(A|B) from P(B|A); update a base rate after evidence (e.g., compute a positive predictive value); rank competing explanations by fit, simplicity, and scope.
**Common traps**: The base-rate fallacy — ignoring prevalence and reading a 95%-accurate test as "95% chance I'm sick" (with 1% prevalence and a 10% false-positive rate, a positive result implies only ~9% chance of disease); confusing correlation with causation; the gambler's fallacy (independent events have no "memory"); transposing the conditional, i.e., assuming P(A|B) = P(B|A).

## Chapter 6: Scientific Reasoning and Evidence
**Core questions**: What makes a claim testable rather than merely persuasive? When a prediction fails, how do you tell whether the *theory* is wrong or just some auxiliary assumption — and why does that make falsification messier than it sounds?
**Key concepts**: Hypothesis, prediction, falsifiability (Popper), auxiliary hypothesis, the Quine-Duhem (underdetermination) problem, control group, randomization, blinding, replication, peer review, p-value and p-hacking, publication bias, the demarcation problem, and pseudoscience markers (unfalsifiability, special pleading).
**Applied skills**: Extract a study's testable prediction; critique a study design (control, sample, confounds, blinding); decide whether a failed prediction refutes the core theory or an auxiliary assumption; explain why replication and pre-registration matter; identify what makes a claim pseudoscientific.
**Common traps**: Treating "not yet disproven" as "proven," or any single study as settled science; reading a non-significant p-value as proof of no effect (absence of evidence vs. evidence of absence); assuming a low p-value means a large or important effect; thinking unfalsifiable equals certainly false rather than scientifically empty.

## Chapter 7: Public Discourse, Digital Claims, and AI
**Core questions**: How do professional fact-checkers verify a claim faster and more accurately than smart people who read carefully? How should you weigh expertise, and how do you reason about AI output that is fluent but possibly fabricated?
**Key concepts**: Misinformation vs. disinformation, source reliability, lateral reading vs. vertical reading (Wineburg/SHEG), legitimate expertise vs. credentialism, rhetoric and the ethics of persuasion, conspiracy reasoning and unfalsifiability, deepfakes and synthetic media, AI "hallucination" / confabulation, and verification/triangulation.
**Applied skills**: Verify a claim by *leaving the page* and reading laterally about the source; weigh expert against non-expert evidence on a technical question; spot the unfalsifiable, ever-expanding structure typical of conspiracy reasoning; critique an AI-generated chain of reasoning and check its citations; separate bias, honest error, and deliberate fraud.
**Common traps**: Vertical reading — judging a site's credibility by its polish, logo, or domain instead of by what independent sources say about it; treating a confident AI answer as a source rather than a claim to verify; dismissing all expertise as "elitism" while accepting any contrarian who agrees with you; assuming a viral statistic is real because it's specific.

## Chapter 8: Argument Mapping and Reasoned Prose
**Core questions**: How do you make the hidden structure of a real argument visible — and then write prose that states a thesis, anticipates the strongest objection, and replies honestly? What would actually change your mind?
**Key concepts**: The Toulmin model (claim, grounds/data, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal), argument map (claims, reasons, objections), thesis, objection and reply, qualification/hedging, fair quotation, citation, intellectual honesty, and revision.
**Applied skills**: Map a complex argument into claim → grounds → warrant, adding qualifier and rebuttal; identify the *warrant* (the unstated assumption linking grounds to claim); write a paragraph that raises and answers the strongest objection; quote an opponent fairly; revise vague prose into a precise, qualified claim.
**Common traps**: Confusing the *grounds* (evidence) with the *warrant* (the assumption connecting evidence to claim); writing a "strongest objection" that is actually a straw man; treating a qualifier ("usually," "in most cases") as weakness rather than precision; mistaking confident tone for argued support.

## Capstone
Each student chooses one genuinely contested public claim or controversy (a health statistic, a policy argument, a viral study, an AI-generated explanation, a conspiracy narrative) and produces an analysis that runs the full pipeline: (1) reconstruct the main argument(s) in standard form and identify the key inference; (2) apply the right tool — a validity check, a quantifier translation, a Bayesian update, or a study critique — to test it; (3) verify the underlying evidence by reading laterally and naming source reliability; (4) build a Toulmin map exposing claim, grounds, warrant, and qualifier; (5) state the strongest objection and a fair reply; and (6) deliver a clear final essay or presentation that ends with an explicit "what would change my mind" statement and a calibrated confidence level. Graded on accuracy of reconstruction, correct tool use, evidential honesty, and the quality of the objection-and-reply — not on which side they take.

## Assessment Ideas
Argument reconstructions; truth-table and translation quizzes; short natural-deduction proof sets; quantifier-negation and countermodel exercises; fallacy-and-bias field guides with original examples and repairs; Bayesian/base-rate problem cases; study critiques separating claim/method/evidence/limits; lateral-reading verification reports with stated confidence; Toulmin argument maps; and the final reasoned essay with objection, reply, and a revision note.
