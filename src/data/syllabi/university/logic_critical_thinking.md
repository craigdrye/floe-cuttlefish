# Logic & Critical Thinking
**ID**: `logicCriticalThinking` · **Discipline**: Humanities / Philosophy · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18–19)
**Aligns with**: typical PHIL 100 / PHIL 110 / "Logic" / "Critical Reasoning" / "Reasoning and Argumentation"; textbook anchors include Hurley's *A Concise Introduction to Logic*, Copi & Cohen, Bergmann/Moor/Nelson *The Logic Book*, Sinnott-Armstrong & Fogelin *Understanding Arguments*

## Course Philosophy
*PLACEHOLDER — to be expanded.* Logic & Critical Thinking is the **operating system** under every other discipline. Students who take it well never write a sloppy argument again — in philosophy, law, science, journalism, code review, or a Slack thread. The internal goal is dual: (1) **formal** logic — propositional and basic predicate calculus, valid forms, proof technique — so students can prove and refute with rigour; (2) **informal** logic and critical thinking — fallacy taxonomy, evaluating evidence, charitable interpretation, statistical and causal reasoning — so students survive the modern information environment. The course is the natural deepening of Module 1 of Intro to Philosophy.

## Module 1: What Is an Argument? (10%)
- Statements vs questions vs commands; truth-aptness; declarative form
- Premises and conclusions; argument indicators ("therefore", "since", "because", "so")
- Standard form: numbered premises, line, conclusion
- Inductive vs deductive vs abductive arguments
- Validity (deductive: if premises true, conclusion *must* be true) vs **soundness** (valid + actually true premises)
- Strength and cogency (for inductive arguments)
- Implicit premises; the principle of charity in reconstruction
- **Questions**: convert prose into standard form; identify implicit premises; classify arguments as deductive/inductive

## Module 2: Propositional Logic (18%)
- Connectives: ¬ (not), ∧ (and), ∨ (inclusive or), → (conditional), ↔ (biconditional); truth-functional definitions
- Translating English into symbols; the gap between "if-then" in English and the material conditional; "unless", "only if", "necessary/sufficient" translations
- Truth tables: building them, reading them; tautology, contradiction, contingent
- Logical equivalence: De Morgan's laws, double negation, contraposition, distribution, implication-as-disjunction (P→Q ≡ ¬P ∨ Q)
- Tautological consequence and validity by truth table
- **Natural deduction** rules: modus ponens, modus tollens, hypothetical syllogism, disjunctive syllogism, simplification, conjunction, addition, constructive dilemma; conditional proof; reductio ad absurdum
- Proof construction; common student mistakes (affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, illicit conversion)

## Module 3: Predicate (Quantifier) Logic (12%)
- Beyond propositions: predicates, names, variables
- Universal quantifier ∀ and existential quantifier ∃; reading "for all", "there exists"
- Translating English: "all S are P", "no S are P", "some S are P", "some S are not P"; multi-place predicates ("loves", "between")
- Negation of quantifiers: ¬∀x P(x) ≡ ∃x ¬P(x); ¬∃x P(x) ≡ ∀x ¬P(x)
- Mixed quantifiers and order matters: "every man loves some woman" ≠ "there is some woman every man loves"
- Identity (=) and uniqueness; "the F" (definite descriptions, Russell)
- Natural deduction with quantifier rules: UI, EI, UG, EG (introduction and elimination)
- Counterexamples by model construction

## Module 4: Informal Reasoning and Fallacies (15%)
- **Fallacies of relevance**: ad hominem (abusive, circumstantial, tu quoque), appeal to authority/popularity/ignorance/pity/force, straw man, red herring
- **Fallacies of ambiguity**: equivocation, amphiboly, accent, composition, division
- **Fallacies of presumption**: begging the question (circular), complex question, false dichotomy, hasty generalisation, slippery slope, post hoc ergo propter hoc, sunk cost, gambler's fallacy
- **Fallacies in statistical reasoning**: cherry-picking, base-rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, anecdotal evidence, regression to the mean misread, Simpson's paradox surprises
- **Cognitive biases** (Kahneman, Tversky, Stanovich): confirmation bias, availability, anchoring, framing, hindsight, motivated reasoning, dunning-kruger, in-group bias
- The **principle of charity**: reconstructing the strongest possible version of an opponent's argument before responding
- The **steelman** vs the strawman; intellectual humility

## Module 5: Inductive and Probabilistic Reasoning (12%)
- Generalisation: sample size, representativeness, biased sampling
- Analogical reasoning: relevant similarities, disanalogies
- Causal reasoning: correlation vs causation; Mill's methods (agreement, difference, concomitant variation, residues); Hill's criteria for causation
- Confounders, lurking variables, reverse causation
- Probability fundamentals: conjunction, disjunction, conditional probability; Bayes' theorem and its informal use ("update on evidence")
- Common probabilistic errors: ignoring base rates, treating P(A|B) as P(B|A) (the prosecutor's fallacy), misunderstanding "1-in-a-million" claims
- Inference to the best explanation (abduction): generating alternatives, ranking by simplicity, fit with background, predictive power
- Decision theory intuition: expected value; risk; Pascal's wager pattern

## Module 6: Scientific Reasoning, Evidence, and Demarcation (10%)
- Hypothesis testing as a logical structure (modus tollens of predictions)
- Falsifiability (Popper) and its limits; ad hoc rescues; auxiliary hypotheses; Quine-Duhem
- Theory choice criteria: fit, simplicity, consilience, predictive novelty
- Observation as theory-laden; the underdetermination problem
- Pseudoscience markers; the demarcation problem; canonical case studies (astrology, homeopathy, parapsychology, intelligent design)
- Reading studies critically: sample size, controls, blinding, replication, peer review, conflicts of interest, p-hacking, publication bias

## Module 7: Argumentation in Context — Public Discourse and the Web (10%)
- The structure of an op-ed, a tweet thread, a Reddit reply, a podcast claim
- Misinformation vs disinformation; the difference between bias, error, and fraud
- Source evaluation: primary vs secondary; expertise vs credentialism; the appropriate appeal to authority
- Lateral reading (Wineburg) — opening tabs to verify rather than reading deeply within a single dubious source
- AI and reasoning in 2026: LLM hallucinations as a category; deepfakes; synthetic media; "liar's dividend"; how to verify with reverse image search, original-source tracing, archive.org
- Conspiracy theories: structural features (unfalsifiability, selection bias, elite explanation), why they're appealing, how to engage charitably
- Rhetoric vs argument: what each does well; persuasion ethics

## Module 8: Writing Reasoned Prose (8%)
- The thesis-antithesis-synthesis essay; the "Smith argues X… The strongest objection is… The best response is… Therefore" template
- Steelmanning before responding
- Distinguishing your view from the views you're considering
- Citation and intellectual honesty; quoting in context vs quote-mining
- The 5-paragraph essay's strengths and limits; longer-form structure
- Editing for clarity: cutting hedges, replacing nominalisations, active voice, parallel structure (Strunk & White, Pinker *Sense of Style*)

## Module 9: Skill Connectors and Practice (5%)
- Reading a contract / privacy policy / scientific abstract for hidden premises
- Argument mapping (Toulmin: claim, ground, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal); software (Rationale, Argunet) for visualising
- Debate practice: switch sides, defend the position you disagree with
- Spotting your *own* fallacies — the hardest skill

## Stretch Zone (push beyond intro logic/critical thinking)
- **Modal logic**: necessity, possibility, possible-worlds semantics; Kripke; applications in philosophy of mind, religion, ethics
- **Many-valued and fuzzy logics**; intuitionistic logic and constructive proof; relevance logic
- **Set theory and metalogic**: the difference between syntax and semantics; soundness and completeness theorems (Gödel); Gödel's incompleteness theorems and what they actually say (vs popular misreadings)
- **Bayesian epistemology**: priors, likelihoods, posteriors; the problem of priors; Dutch book arguments; Cox's theorem
- **Formal epistemology**: belief revision, AGM postulates, lottery and preface paradoxes, knowability paradox
- **Game theory and rational choice**: minimax, Nash, repeated games, Newcomb's problem, the prisoners' dilemma in social science
- **Computational logic**: SAT solvers, automated theorem provers (Lean, Coq, Isabelle); the rise of formal verification
- **Philosophy of language and reasoning**: Frege, Russell, Wittgenstein on meaning; Grice's maxims (cooperative principle); presupposition vs entailment
- **Common student traps to drill**:
  - Confusing validity with truth (a valid argument can have a false conclusion if a premise is false)
  - Treating "I can't think of a counterexample" as proof
  - Affirming the consequent in everyday reasoning
  - Slipping between universal and existential quantifiers
  - Relying on emotional resonance as evidence
  - Dismissing an argument because of who made it (genetic fallacy)
  - Treating statistical correlations as causal in casual conversation
- The single most leveraged habit: when reading any claim, ask three questions — *what exactly is the claim, what would count as evidence against it, and what is the best objection to it?* A student who internalises this for every important claim is functionally above 99% of public-discourse participants by their second year.
