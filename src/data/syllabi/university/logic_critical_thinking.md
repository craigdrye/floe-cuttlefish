# Logic & Critical Thinking
**ID**: `logicCriticalThinking` - **Discipline**: Humanities / Philosophy - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: typical PHIL 100 / PHIL 110 / Logic / Critical Reasoning courses

## Course Aim
Teach students to reconstruct, test, and improve arguments in formal and everyday settings. Students learn formal logic, informal fallacies, probabilistic reasoning, source evaluation, and clear argumentative writing so they can reason better in philosophy, law, science, journalism, coding, and public life.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve arguments, validity, soundness, fallacies, propositional logic, predicate logic, truth tables, proof, induction, probability, causation, evidence, scientific reasoning, misinformation, argument mapping, or reasoned prose. The core habit is: identify the exact claim, the evidence that would count against it, and the strongest objection.

## Chapter 1: Arguments, Charity, and Standard Form
**Key concepts**: Statement, premise, conclusion, inference, argument indicator, standard form, implicit premise, deductive argument, inductive argument, abductive argument, validity, soundness, strength, cogency, and principle of charity.
**Practice questions**: Convert prose into standard form; classify argument types; identify hidden premises; distinguish validity from truth; improve a weak reconstruction.
**Student output**: An argument reconstruction portfolio using examples from essays, news, science, law, and everyday claims.

## Chapter 2: Propositional Logic
**Key concepts**: Negation, conjunction, disjunction, conditional, biconditional, truth table, tautology, contradiction, contingency, equivalence, De Morgan's laws, modus ponens, modus tollens, conditional proof, and reductio.
**Practice questions**: Translate English into symbols; build truth tables; test validity; prove simple arguments; avoid affirming the consequent and denying the antecedent.
**Student output**: A formal logic problem set with translations, truth tables, and natural-deduction proofs.

## Chapter 3: Predicate Logic and Quantifiers
**Key concepts**: Predicate, name, variable, universal quantifier, existential quantifier, negated quantifier, identity, uniqueness, scope, mixed quantifiers, model, counterexample, and quantifier rules.
**Practice questions**: Translate "all," "some," "none," and "only" claims; negate quantified statements; explain why quantifier order matters; construct countermodels.
**Student output**: A translation and model-building notebook showing how natural-language claims become formal structure.

## Chapter 4: Informal Fallacies and Cognitive Bias
**Key concepts**: Ad hominem, straw man, red herring, equivocation, composition, division, begging the question, false dichotomy, slippery slope, hasty generalization, sunk cost, gambler's fallacy, confirmation bias, availability, anchoring, framing, and motivated reasoning.
**Practice questions**: Diagnose fallacies in public claims; distinguish bad reasoning from merely unpopular conclusions; steelman before responding; identify one's own likely biases.
**Student output**: A fallacy-and-bias field guide with original examples, repairs, and fairer versions of flawed arguments.

## Chapter 5: Induction, Probability, and Causation
**Key concepts**: Generalization, sample, representativeness, analogy, causal inference, correlation, confounder, reverse causation, conditional probability, Bayes' theorem, base rate, expected value, and inference to the best explanation.
**Practice questions**: Evaluate sample quality; compare analogies; identify confounders; update beliefs using base rates; distinguish P(A|B) from P(B|A); rank explanations.
**Student output**: A probabilistic reasoning memo analyzing one statistical or causal claim from public life.

## Chapter 6: Scientific Reasoning and Evidence
**Key concepts**: Hypothesis, prediction, falsifiability, auxiliary hypothesis, Quine-Duhem problem, theory choice, replication, peer review, control, blinding, p-hacking, publication bias, pseudoscience, and demarcation.
**Practice questions**: Identify testable predictions; critique study design; distinguish failed prediction from failed auxiliary assumption; evaluate pseudoscience claims; explain replication and bias.
**Student output**: A study critique that separates claim, method, evidence, limitations, and appropriate conclusion.

## Chapter 7: Public Discourse, Digital Claims, and AI
**Key concepts**: Misinformation, disinformation, source reliability, lateral reading, expertise, credentialism, rhetoric, persuasion ethics, conspiracy reasoning, deepfakes, synthetic media, hallucination, and verification.
**Practice questions**: Verify a claim by tracing sources; compare expert and non-expert evidence; identify unfalsifiable patterns; critique AI-generated reasoning; separate bias, error, and fraud.
**Student output**: A digital verification report documenting how a claim was checked and what confidence level remains.

## Chapter 8: Argument Mapping and Reasoned Prose
**Key concepts**: Toulmin model, claim, ground, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal, argument map, thesis, objection, reply, citation, intellectual honesty, clarity, and revision.
**Practice questions**: Map a complex argument; write an objection and reply; quote fairly; revise vague prose; switch sides in debate; explain what would change one's mind.
**Student output**: A final reasoned essay with argument map, strongest objection, reply, and revision note.

## Capstone
Students analyze one important claim or controversy by reconstructing arguments, checking evidence, applying formal or probabilistic tools where relevant, identifying objections, and producing a clear final essay or presentation.

## Assessment Ideas
Use argument reconstructions, truth-table quizzes, proof sets, fallacy repairs, probability cases, source-verification reports, study critiques, argument maps, and a final reasoned essay.
