# Comparative Practical Politics

## Course Description
Comparative Practical Politics studies how democratic institutions convert votes, parties, money, law, administration, media, and public pressure into binding decisions. The course is organized around the United States, the United Kingdom, and the European Union because they make similar democratic promises through sharply different institutional machinery: presidential federalism, parliamentary and devolved government, and supranational policy-making among member states.

The course treats institutions as practical operating systems. Students learn how to read a political event by asking where authority sits, which actors can veto or delay action, how parties and elections shape incentives, and why the same policy proposal can move quickly in one system and stall in another.

## Course Aim
Students will learn to compare political systems without assuming that one country's rules, party labels, or policy pathways are universal. By the end of the course, students should be able to explain how formal institutions and informal power interact in real policy disputes across the U.S., U.K., and EU.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when students need comparative institutional analysis of the U.S., U.K., and EU: elections, parties, legislatures, executives, courts, bureaucracy, lobbying, media, policy implementation, or democratic accountability. Keep answers operational rather than ideological: who has authority, where are the veto points, what incentives matter, and how would the same issue move differently across systems?

## Learning Outcomes
By the end of the course, students will be able to:

- Map authority across presidential, parliamentary, federal, unitary, devolved, and supranational systems.
- Explain how electoral rules shape party systems, coalition incentives, representation, turnout, polarization, and accountability.
- Compare legislative and executive power in practice, including agenda control, committees, delegated authority, courts, bureaucracy, and crisis powers.
- Analyze how interest groups, donors, unions, civil society, media, expertise, and public opinion influence decisions before and after formal votes.
- Translate ideological labels across political contexts and identify when the same label carries different meanings in the U.S., U.K., and EU.
- Build evidence-based explanations for why a policy was adopted, amended, delayed, judicially challenged, administratively weakened, or abandoned.

## Core Comparative Questions
- Who has legal authority, who has agenda control, and who has practical leverage?
- Where are the veto points, and are they partisan, territorial, judicial, bureaucratic, financial, or procedural?
- How do electoral systems convert votes into seats, and how does that affect campaign strategy and governing coalitions?
- How do party discipline, primaries, manifestos, whips, caucuses, and coalition agreements shape legislative behavior?
- How do constitutional courts, ordinary courts, administrative agencies, and supranational courts change policy after elected officials act?
- How do lobbying, campaign finance, expert consultation, protest, media framing, and social movements affect policy without appearing in final roll-call votes?
- How should analysts compare concepts such as liberal, conservative, socialist, nationalist, populist, green, federal, sovereign, and democratic deficit across countries?

## Course Structure

### Unit 1: Comparing Political Systems
**Central problem:** Political comparison requires more than listing institutions; it requires understanding how institutions allocate authority and incentives.

**Key concepts**
- Comparative method
- Formal and informal institutions
- State capacity
- Constitutional rules and constitutional conventions
- Presidentialism, parliamentarism, and semi-parliamentary dynamics
- Federalism, devolution, unitary government, and supranationalism
- Veto players and agenda setters
- Path dependence and institutional change

**U.S./U.K./EU coverage**
- U.S. constitutional separation of powers, federalism, and judicial review
- U.K. parliamentary sovereignty, cabinet government, devolution, and constitutional conventions
- EU competences, treaties, member-state sovereignty, supranational institutions, and the democratic deficit debate

**Applied comparative questions**
- Why does a U.S. president often struggle to pass legislation even after winning a national election?
- Why can a U.K. government with a Commons majority often legislate quickly, and when does that power break down?
- Is the EU best understood as an international organization, a federal system, a regulatory state, or something else?

### Unit 2: Elections, Representation, and Party Systems
**Central problem:** Electoral rules do not simply count preferences; they structure the choices voters, parties, donors, activists, and candidates make before ballots are cast.

**Key concepts**
- Single-member districts
- First-past-the-post
- Proportional representation
- Mixed electoral systems
- Electoral thresholds
- Primaries and candidate selection
- Party identification and partisan sorting
- Coalition formation
- Manifestos and mandates
- Turnout, malapportionment, and districting

**U.S./U.K./EU coverage**
- U.S. presidential, congressional, primary, and state-level elections
- U.K. Westminster elections, devolved elections, local elections, and referendum politics
- European Parliament elections, national party delegations, Europarties, coalition groupings, and second-order election dynamics

**Applied comparative questions**
- Why do the U.S. and U.K. both use plurality elections but produce different party discipline and executive-legislative relations?
- How does proportional representation change the bargaining power of smaller parties?
- What does a European Parliament election tell us about EU politics, national politics, and public attitudes toward integration?

### Unit 3: Legislatures and Lawmaking
**Central problem:** Legislatures are not just voting chambers. They are agenda-setting, bargaining, scrutiny, and accountability institutions.

**Key concepts**
- Bicameralism and unicameralism
- Committees and hearings
- Agenda control
- Whips and party discipline
- Amendments and riders
- Filibuster, cloture, and obstruction
- Confidence votes
- Delegated legislation and secondary legislation
- Ordinary legislative procedure
- Trilogues, consultation, and consent
- Budget authorization and appropriation

**U.S./U.K./EU coverage**
- U.S. House and Senate rules, committees, leadership, budget politics, and divided government
- U.K. House of Commons, House of Lords, select committees, whips, statutory instruments, and confidence
- European Commission proposal power, European Parliament committees, Council formations, ordinary legislative procedure, and trilogue bargaining

**Applied comparative questions**
- Why can a policy with majority public support fail in the U.S. Congress?
- How meaningful is parliamentary scrutiny when the government controls the Commons timetable?
- Where does compromise happen in the EU legislative process, and why is it often difficult for citizens to see?

### Unit 4: Executives, Bureaucracy, and Implementation
**Central problem:** Passing a law is not the same as governing. Executive offices, departments, regulators, civil servants, and courts shape what policy becomes in practice.

**Key concepts**
- Executive orders and executive discretion
- Cabinet government
- Ministerial responsibility
- Civil service neutrality
- Administrative capacity
- Regulatory agencies
- Delegation and rulemaking
- Implementation gaps
- Emergency powers
- Judicial review of administration

**U.S./U.K./EU coverage**
- U.S. presidency, federal agencies, rulemaking, executive orders, and state implementation
- U.K. prime minister, cabinet, departments, civil service, regulators, and ministerial accountability
- European Commission, comitology, EU agencies, national transposition, infringement proceedings, and Court of Justice enforcement

**Applied comparative questions**
- When does executive action compensate for legislative gridlock, and when does it create instability?
- How can a U.K. minister be politically responsible for decisions made deep inside a department or agency?
- Why does EU implementation depend so heavily on national administrations?

### Unit 5: Courts, Constitutions, Rights, and Legal Constraints
**Central problem:** Courts can protect rights, police institutional boundaries, and reshape policy, but their authority varies across systems.

**Key concepts**
- Constitutional review
- Parliamentary sovereignty
- Rights adjudication
- Common law and civil law traditions
- Supreme courts and constitutional courts
- Judicial independence
- Proportionality
- Supremacy and direct effect of EU law
- Compliance and backlash

**U.S./U.K./EU coverage**
- U.S. Supreme Court, federal courts, constitutional rights, and federal-state disputes
- U.K. Supreme Court, Human Rights Act, devolution cases, and judicial review of executive action
- Court of Justice of the European Union, General Court, national courts, supremacy of EU law, and post-Brexit legal conflict

**Applied comparative questions**
- Why is the U.S. Supreme Court more visibly central to national politics than many courts in parliamentary systems?
- How can U.K. courts review government action in a system committed to parliamentary sovereignty?
- How did EU legal integration make national courts part of the EU political system?

### Unit 6: Ideology, Cleavages, and Political Conflict
**Central problem:** Political labels travel badly. The same word can signal different histories, coalitions, and policy commitments across systems.

**Key concepts**
- Liberalism, conservatism, socialism, social democracy, nationalism, populism, environmentalism, and Christian democracy
- Class, region, religion, race, ethnicity, age, education, urban-rural divides, and identity
- Left-right competition
- Cultural backlash and authoritarian-libertarian divides
- Euroscepticism and sovereignty
- Realignment and dealignment
- Polarization and affective polarization

**U.S./U.K./EU coverage**
- U.S. liberal-conservative polarization, race, religion, region, and party sorting
- U.K. class, nation, Brexit, unionism, devolution, and party realignment
- EU integration cleavage, national party families, populism, green politics, radical right parties, and democratic backsliding

**Applied comparative questions**
- Why does "liberal" mean different things in U.S., U.K., and European party politics?
- How did Brexit reorganize political conflict in the U.K.?
- Why do some EU conflicts divide left from right while others divide integrationists from sovereigntists?

### Unit 7: Money, Interests, Media, and Public Pressure
**Central problem:** Democratic power operates through elections, but also through organized access, expertise, public campaigns, protest, and media attention.

**Key concepts**
- Interest groups and lobbying
- Campaign finance
- Political action committees and donors
- Unions, business associations, and civil society
- Think tanks and expert networks
- Consultation and impact assessment
- Protest and social movements
- Media systems
- Agenda setting and framing
- Transparency, corruption risk, and public trust

**U.S./U.K./EU coverage**
- U.S. campaign finance, lobbying disclosure, PACs, Super PACs, advocacy groups, cable news, and digital campaigning
- U.K. party finance, lobbying rules, public consultation, unions, newspapers, broadcasters, and watchdog institutions
- EU Transparency Register, consultation processes, Brussels lobbying, expert groups, European Citizens' Initiative, and transnational advocacy networks

**Applied comparative questions**
- Why is money more visible in U.S. electoral politics than in many European systems?
- How do groups influence U.K. policy when party discipline limits visible legislative rebellion?
- Does the EU's reliance on consultation improve expertise, widen access, or privilege organized interests?

### Unit 8: Policy Case Workshops
**Central problem:** Comparative politics becomes most useful when students can apply institutional analysis to real policy problems.

**Workshop cases**
- Climate and energy policy
- Immigration and asylum
- Health care and public health
- Budgets, taxation, and fiscal rules
- Digital regulation and platform governance
- Trade and industrial policy
- Criminal justice and civil liberties
- Devolution, secession, and territorial conflict

**Key concepts**
- Policy feedback
- Venue shopping
- Multi-level governance
- Regulatory capacity
- Crisis politics
- Policy diffusion
- Blame avoidance
- Accountability chains

**Applied comparative questions**
- Which system can adopt a climate target fastest, and which is more likely to implement it consistently?
- Why do immigration politics often move across local, national, and supranational venues?
- How do fiscal rules constrain elected governments differently in the U.S., U.K., and EU?

## Assignments and Assessment Ideas
- **Institutional map briefing, 15%:** Students diagram the formal and informal decision pathway for one policy area in the U.S., U.K., or EU.
- **Comparative election memo, 15%:** Students compare how electoral rules shaped one recent election campaign, party strategy, and governing mandate in two systems.
- **Legislative process simulation, 15%:** Students represent executive, legislative, party, committee, interest-group, media, court, and administrative actors in a policy negotiation.
- **Ideology translation paper, 15%:** Students analyze one political label or cleavage across at least two systems and explain where direct comparison misleads.
- **Policy-tracing project, 30%:** Students follow one policy proposal from agenda-setting through adoption, blockage, court challenge, or implementation. The project must identify veto points, coalition incentives, evidence used by advocates, and accountability mechanisms.
- **Participation and reading notes, 10%:** Students prepare short analytical notes that connect readings to current political events.

## Capstone
Students complete a comparative policy-tracing project on one issue across at least two systems. The final brief must identify decision venues, veto players, party incentives, administrative pathways, court or legal constraints, public pressure, and why the policy moved differently in each system.

## Assessment Ideas
Use institutional maps, election memos, process simulations, ideology translation papers, policy-tracing briefs, current-event reading notes, and comparative oral defenses. Assess students for accurate institutional detail, careful comparison, and avoidance of false equivalence.

## Sample Final Project Prompts
- Why did a specific climate, immigration, health, budget, or digital regulation proposal move faster in one system than another?
- How did party discipline, coalition politics, or divided government affect the fate of a recent bill?
- How did courts or regulators change the meaning of a policy after elected officials acted?
- How did media framing and organized interests affect the policy agenda before a formal vote occurred?
- What would happen if one system imported another system's electoral rule, committee structure, executive power, or judicial review model?

## Practical Skills Developed
- Reading political news institutionally rather than reactively
- Identifying the relevant decision venue
- Distinguishing public justification from institutional incentive
- Comparing cases without false equivalence
- Writing policy memos and analytical briefings
- Using official legislative, electoral, judicial, budgetary, and regulatory sources
- Explaining complex political processes clearly to non-specialist audiences
