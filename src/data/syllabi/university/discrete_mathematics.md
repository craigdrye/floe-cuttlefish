# Discrete Mathematics
**ID**: `discreteMath` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Stage**: University Freshman/Sophomore
**Aligns with**: first university discrete math courses for CS, math, data science, and logic students (MIT 6.042J, Berkeley CS70, Stanford CS103)

## Course Aim
Discrete mathematics is where students stop computing answers and start proving claims. The aim of this course is to give learners fluent command of the proof language and the finite structures that underpin all of computer science and much of modern mathematics: logic and quantifiers, rigorous proof, sets and functions, counting, induction and recursion, relations and orders, number theory and modular arithmetic, graphs and trees, and their direct computational uses. By the end, a student should be able to read a definition and immediately know what would count as a proof, a disproof, or a counterexample.

The course is deliberately proof-first. Calculation matters, but the deeper skill is justification: stating exactly what is assumed, what is to be shown, and why each step follows. Students learn to negate a nested quantified statement without guessing, to choose between direct proof, contrapositive, contradiction, cases, and induction on principled grounds, and to model a real computational system - a hash table, a dependency graph, a scheduling constraint, a cryptographic toy - as a precise discrete object they can reason about. These habits are what later courses in algorithms, theory of computation, databases, and cryptography silently assume you already have.

The third aim is mathematical maturity through volume and variety. Discrete math rewards seeing many small structures and noticing the shared skeleton: that the pigeonhole principle, the handshaking lemma, and a counting bijection are the same idea wearing different clothes. Students build that pattern library here so that an unfamiliar problem in a future course feels like a variation rather than a wall.

## Course Design Notes
This is a rigorous first course with frequent proof writing and structured problem sets, pitched at the level of MIT 6.042J, Berkeley CS70, and Stanford CS103. Route a question here when a student needs propositional or predicate logic, proof methods, set and function notation, counting and combinatorics, induction and recurrences, relations and partial orders, modular arithmetic and elementary number theory, graphs and trees, or the discrete structures behind algorithms, data structures, databases, automata, and cryptography.

Pedagogically, emphasize definitions over slogans, quantifier discipline, the search for counterexamples before committing to a proof, small concrete cases before the general argument, and clean written solutions rather than answer-only calculation. The most common student failures are not arithmetic errors but reasoning errors: confusing a conditional with its converse, asserting "for all" from a single example, dividing in modular arithmetic, off-by-one errors in induction base cases, and overcounting in combinatorics. Questions should target those errors directly. Where a topic has a CS payoff - induction as loop-invariant reasoning, recurrences as runtime analysis, modular arithmetic as hashing and RSA, graphs as dependency and network models - make that connection explicit so students see discrete math as infrastructure, not trivia.

## Chapter 1: Logic, Language, and Quantifiers
- **Core questions**: What does an implication actually claim, and when is it vacuously true? How do you correctly negate a statement with nested quantifiers? Why are a conditional, its converse, and its inverse generally not equivalent, while a conditional and its contrapositive always are?
- **Key concepts**: Propositions and predicates; logical connectives; truth tables; implication, converse, inverse, contrapositive; logical equivalence; tautology and contradiction; De Morgan's laws; universal and existential quantifiers; nested quantifiers and their order; negation of quantified statements; translating English and program specifications into formal logic.
- **Applied skills**: Build and read truth tables; prove two formulas equivalent symbolically; flip the order of nested quantifiers and explain when the meaning changes; translate a database or program precondition into a quantified statement; identify the precise flaw in an invalid inference.
- **Common traps**: Treating "if P then Q" as "if Q then P" (affirming the converse); negating "for all x, P(x)" as "for all x, not P(x)" instead of "there exists x, not P(x)"; swapping the order of unlike quantifiers and assuming the meaning is preserved; thinking a false hypothesis makes an implication false rather than vacuously true.

## Chapter 2: Proof Methods and Mathematical Writing
- **Core questions**: Given a claim, how do you decide between direct proof, contrapositive, contradiction, and cases? What is the difference between proving a statement and merely checking examples? How do you disprove a universal claim, and what does a single counterexample establish?
- **Key concepts**: Definitions and theorem structure; direct proof; proof by contrapositive; proof by contradiction; proof by cases; biconditional (if-and-only-if) proofs; existence and uniqueness proofs; counterexamples; the role and the danger of diagrams and examples; proof writing and rigor.
- **Applied skills**: Select an appropriate strategy from the structure of a statement; prove parity and divisibility claims; disprove a false universal with a minimal counterexample; rewrite an informal argument into a valid proof that separates assumptions from conclusion; recognize when an "obvious" step needs justification.
- **Common traps**: Proving the converse by accident in a biconditional and stopping halfway; "proof by example" - verifying instances and calling a universal claim proven; assuming what is to be shown (begging the question); using a picture as the proof rather than as a guide; forgetting the uniqueness half of an existence-and-uniqueness claim.

## Chapter 3: Sets, Functions, and Cardinality
- **Core questions**: What does it mean for two sets to be equal, and how does extensionality drive set proofs? When is a function injective, surjective, or bijective, and why do the chosen domain and codomain decide the answer? How do bijections let us compare the sizes of infinite sets?
- **Key concepts**: Sets, subsets, power sets, set-builder notation; union, intersection, difference, complement; Cartesian products and ordered pairs; functions, domain, codomain, image, preimage; injection, surjection, bijection; inverse functions and composition; |P(A)| = 2^n for a finite set of size n; finite cardinality and a first look at countable versus uncountable.
- **Applied skills**: Prove a set identity by element-chasing (mutual subset inclusion); classify a function by checking the definitions of injective and surjective; construct an explicit bijection to count or compare sizes; compute power-set and Cartesian-product sizes; explain how changing the codomain can turn a non-surjection into a surjection.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the subset relation with element membership (A subset of B versus A in B); proving only one direction of a set equality; assuming every function has an inverse rather than only bijections; conflating image with codomain; forgetting that the empty set is a subset of every set and an element of every power set.

## Chapter 4: Counting and Combinatorics
- **Core questions**: When does order matter (permutations) versus not (combinations)? How do you count without overcounting or double-subtracting? When is the pigeonhole principle the right tool, and how do you choose the pigeons and holes?
- **Key concepts**: Sum rule, product rule, complement rule; permutations and combinations; binomial coefficients, Pascal's identity, the binomial theorem; inclusion-exclusion; the pigeonhole principle (basic and generalized); arrangements with repetition; combinatorial (bijective) proof.
- **Applied skills**: Decide whether order matters and pick the right formula; count strings or passwords under constraints; apply inclusion-exclusion to overlapping categories; prove a binomial identity combinatorially (count one set two ways); use pigeonhole to prove existence without construction.
- **Common traps**: Multiplying when choices are not independent, or adding when cases overlap; using permutations where combinations are correct (counting the same selection in every order); forgetting to add back the triple-overlap in inclusion-exclusion; treating C(n,k) and the ordered count P(n,k) as interchangeable; in generalized pigeonhole, using floor where the ceiling is required.

## Chapter 5: Induction, Recursion, and Recurrences
- **Core questions**: Why does proving a base case plus an inductive step justify a claim for all n? When is strong induction necessary rather than weak? How does a recurrence describe an algorithm's running time, and how do you solve the common forms?
- **Key concepts**: Weak induction; strong induction; structural induction (preview); recursive definitions; loop and recursive invariants; arithmetic and geometric sums; linear recurrences; recursion trees; divide-and-conquer recurrences; the Master Theorem (e.g. T(n)=2T(n/2)+n is Theta(n log n)); connection to algorithm analysis.
- **Applied skills**: Prove a summation or divisibility formula by induction with an explicit base case and inductive hypothesis; use strong induction for tiling, factorization, or recursive-structure claims; set up and solve a recurrence by unrolling or recursion tree; read a loop invariant as an induction proof; map a recursive algorithm to its recurrence.
- **Common traps**: Omitting or mis-choosing the base case (off-by-one starting index); using the inductive hypothesis to "prove" itself rather than the next case; assuming weak induction suffices when the step needs all smaller cases; misapplying the Master Theorem when f(n) does not fit any case; circular invariants that assume the postcondition.

## Chapter 6: Relations, Orders, and Modular Arithmetic
- **Core questions**: What distinguishes an equivalence relation from a partial order, and what does each one organize? How do equivalence classes partition a set? Why can you add and multiply but not freely divide in modular arithmetic, and when does a modular inverse exist?
- **Key concepts**: Binary relations; reflexive, symmetric, antisymmetric, transitive properties; equivalence relations, equivalence classes, partitions; partial and total orders; Hasse diagrams; divisibility; congruence modulo n; modular addition and multiplication; gcd and the Euclidean algorithm; modular inverses; Fermat's Little Theorem (a^(p-1) congruent to 1 mod p for prime p, a not divisible by p); cryptographic motivation.
- **Applied skills**: Check the three or four defining properties to classify a relation; list equivalence classes and confirm they partition the set; draw a Hasse diagram from a partial order; run the Euclidean algorithm to compute gcd and back-solve for a modular inverse; reduce and solve simple linear congruences.
- **Common traps**: Confusing symmetric with antisymmetric, or assuming a relation must be one or the other; thinking equivalence classes can overlap; dividing both sides of a congruence by a number that shares a factor with the modulus; assuming a modular inverse always exists rather than only when gcd(a,n)=1; treating partial orders as total (forgetting incomparable elements).

## Chapter 7: Graphs and Trees
- **Core questions**: What invariants (degree sequence, connectivity, bipartiteness) tell you about a graph before you draw it? When does a graph have an Euler circuit versus a Hamiltonian cycle? Why does a tree on n vertices always have exactly n-1 edges?
- **Key concepts**: Graph terminology; degree and the handshaking lemma (sum of degrees = 2|E|); walks, trails, paths, cycles; connectedness; directed and bipartite graphs; Euler paths and circuits (all degrees even for a circuit; exactly two odd for a path); Hamiltonian paths (preview); graph coloring; planarity and Euler's formula v - e + f = 2; trees, rooted trees, spanning trees, binary trees; traversal; adjacency-list and adjacency-matrix representations.
- **Applied skills**: Apply the handshaking lemma to argue about degree sequences and the number of odd-degree vertices; decide Euler-circuit existence from degrees; prove the tree edge-count property by induction; test bipartiteness via two-coloring; model a dependency, scheduling, or network problem as a graph and choose a representation.
- **Common traps**: Confusing Euler (every edge once) with Hamilton (every vertex once); forgetting the connectivity requirement when applying the even-degree Euler condition; claiming a degree sequence with an odd number of odd vertices is realizable; assuming every connected graph is a tree, or that trees can have cycles; mixing up adjacency-matrix and adjacency-list cost trade-offs.

## Chapter 8: Discrete Models in Computer Science
- **Core questions**: Which discrete structure best models a given computing problem - a relation, a graph, an automaton, a recurrence? How do logic and invariants establish that a program is correct? Where do modular arithmetic and counting show up in real systems like hashing and cryptography?
- **Key concepts**: Boolean logic and circuits; finite-state machines and a preview of regular languages; the relational model behind databases; program specifications and invariants; hashing via modular arithmetic; graph algorithms; recurrence-based runtime analysis; discrete probability (preview); a first look at the limits of computation.
- **Applied skills**: Design a finite-state machine for a simple protocol or pattern; express a query in set-and-relation language; state and use a loop invariant to argue partial correctness; derive and solve the recurrence of a recursive algorithm; name the discrete structure underlying an unfamiliar computing task.
- **Common traps**: Designing a finite-state machine that is non-deterministic or missing transitions when a deterministic complete one is required; confusing a loop invariant (true every iteration) with the postcondition (true at the end); assuming a hash function with a poor modulus distributes uniformly; reading asymptotic notation as exact running time; treating "no algorithm I can think of" as a proof of impossibility.

## Capstone
Students produce a discrete-mathematics modeling and proof project for a computational or mathematical system. The project must (1) define the objects involved in precise notation, (2) state assumptions and the claim in formal language, (3) use at least three course tools - drawn from logic, proof methods, counting, induction, recurrences, relations, modular arithmetic, graphs, or trees - and (4) present rigorous proofs or fully justified computations, including a counterexample or boundary case where relevant. Strong topics include analyzing the recurrence of a divide-and-conquer algorithm and bounding its running time, proving correctness of a small algorithm via a loop invariant, modeling access control as a relation or partial order and proving a property, designing and verifying a finite-state validator, a toy RSA or Diffie-Hellman example built on modular arithmetic and the Euclidean algorithm, a graph-coloring or scheduling model with a proven bound, or a combinatorial count of system configurations verified against small cases. Deliverables are graded on formal modeling, correct use of definitions, proof rigor, mathematical communication, and the strength of the connection to a genuine computational application.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written problem sets graded for definitions, notation, proof structure, and explanation quality - not just final answers.
- Short quizzes targeting the named common traps: converse versus contrapositive, quantifier negation, permutation versus combination, induction base cases, modular division, Euler versus Hamilton.
- Proof-revision assignments where students improve a flawed draft for clarity, quantifier discipline, and edge-case handling.
- Computational labs (enumeration, graph traversal, recurrence simulation, Euclidean-algorithm checks) used to build intuition and verify small cases, supporting but never replacing proof.
- Capstone rubric covering formal modeling, correct definitions, proof rigor, communication, and CS connection.
