# Anatomy and Physiology
**ID**: `anatomyAndPhysiology` | **Discipline**: Science | **Year**: University Freshman / High School Senior

## Course Aim
Build a rigorous, usable understanding of the human body as an integrated set of structures, signals, fluids, barriers, and feedback systems. Students learn to connect microscopic anatomy to organ-level function, interpret basic lab and clinical evidence, and explain how homeostasis fails in disease.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test human anatomical terminology, tissues, organ systems, homeostasis, physiology mechanisms, introductory pathophysiology, lab interpretation, or clinical structure-function reasoning. Keep the course grounded in evidence: students should draw, trace, compare, measure, and explain, not simply name parts.

## Chapter 1: Anatomical Language, Tissues, and Homeostasis
- **Key concepts**: Anatomical position, directional terms, body planes, cavities, serous membranes, levels of organization, epithelial tissue, connective tissue, muscle tissue, nervous tissue, extracellular matrix, homeostasis, negative feedback, and positive feedback.
- **Practice questions**: Use directional terms in a clinical description; match tissue structure to function; interpret a feedback-loop diagram.
- **Student output**: A tissue and homeostasis atlas page with labeled micrographs, function claims, and one feedback example.

## Chapter 2: Integumentary and Skeletal Systems
- **Key concepts**: Epidermis, dermis, hypodermis, keratinocytes, melanocytes, glands, thermoregulation, wound healing, bone matrix, osteoblasts, osteocytes, osteoclasts, ossification, axial and appendicular skeleton, joints, and synovial movement.
- **Practice questions**: Explain skin barrier failure; compare compact and spongy bone; classify joints from movement and structure.
- **Student output**: A fracture or wound-healing case brief connecting cells, tissue repair, imaging, and functional recovery.

## Chapter 3: Muscles, Movement, and Biomechanics
- **Key concepts**: Skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle; sarcomeres; actin; myosin; sliding filament model; excitation-contraction coupling; neuromuscular junction; motor units; twitch summation; fatigue; levers; and posture.
- **Practice questions**: Trace events from motor neuron firing to contraction; interpret force-length or fatigue data; connect joint anatomy to movement limits.
- **Student output**: A movement analysis diagram showing muscle action, joint motion, neural control, and mechanical tradeoffs.

## Chapter 4: Nervous System and Sensory Integration
- **Key concepts**: Neurons, glia, resting membrane potential, action potentials, synapses, neurotransmitters, central and peripheral nervous systems, spinal cord, brain regions, cranial nerves, spinal nerves, autonomic divisions, reflex arcs, and sensory receptors.
- **Practice questions**: Predict effects of ion-channel changes; compare sympathetic and parasympathetic responses; trace a reflex or sensory pathway.
- **Student output**: A neurophysiology case map linking symptom, pathway, tissue, and likely functional disruption.

## Chapter 5: Endocrine Control and Blood
- **Key concepts**: Hormone classes, receptors, second messengers, hypothalamus-pituitary axes, thyroid, adrenal glands, pancreas, gonads, negative feedback, plasma, erythrocytes, leukocytes, platelets, hemostasis, blood typing, and basic blood tests.
- **Practice questions**: Interpret hormone feedback scenarios; explain diabetes or thyroid dysfunction using receptor and feedback logic; read a simple CBC pattern.
- **Student output**: An endocrine or hematology interpretation memo with mechanism, evidence, and limits of the data.

## Chapter 6: Cardiovascular and Respiratory Systems
- **Key concepts**: Heart chambers, valves, cardiac cycle, conduction system, ECG basics, arteries, veins, capillaries, blood pressure, capillary exchange, respiratory tract anatomy, ventilation, gas exchange, hemoglobin, oxygen dissociation curve, carbon dioxide transport, and breathing regulation.
- **Practice questions**: Trace blood through pulmonary and systemic circuits; interpret blood pressure or ECG features; explain ventilation-perfusion and gas transport changes.
- **Student output**: A cardiopulmonary case explanation using anatomy, physiology, one quantitative measure, and a clinical sign.

## Chapter 7: Lymphatic, Immune, Digestive, and Metabolic Systems
- **Key concepts**: Lymph vessels, lymph nodes, spleen, thymus, innate immunity, adaptive immunity, inflammation, antibodies, B cells, T cells, immunological memory, GI tract anatomy, accessory organs, enzymes, absorption, liver function, nutrient metabolism, and energy balance.
- **Practice questions**: Distinguish innate and adaptive defenses; trace digestion and absorption of a meal; connect liver or immune dysfunction to observable findings.
- **Student output**: A systems flowchart following a pathogen, nutrient, or inflammatory signal through tissues and organs.

## Chapter 8: Urinary, Reproductive, Fluid Balance, and Integration
- **Key concepts**: Kidney anatomy, nephron structure, filtration, reabsorption, secretion, countercurrent multiplication, RAAS, electrolytes, acid-base balance, male and female reproductive anatomy, gametogenesis, ovarian and menstrual cycles, pregnancy basics, and developmental overview.
- **Practice questions**: Explain urine formation from nephron regions; interpret dehydration or acid-base scenarios; compare hormonal control of reproductive cycles.
- **Student output**: An integrated homeostasis portfolio that connects kidneys, lungs, hormones, circulation, and reproduction to a patient-style scenario.

## Capstone
Students produce an evidence-based human systems case study. The final product must include a structure-function diagram, a homeostatic feedback model, interpretation of at least one lab or vital-sign dataset, and a brief explanation of how disease or injury disrupts normal physiology.

## Assessment Ideas
- Practical quizzes using anatomical models, images, histology, and clinical language.
- Mechanism prompts that ask students to trace events across cells, tissues, organs, and systems.
- Lab and clinical data interpretation from vitals, CBCs, urinalysis, ECG strips, pulmonary values, or endocrine panels.
- Diagram revisions where students improve a flawed pathway or feedback model.
- A cumulative oral or written case defense focused on evidence and uncertainty.
