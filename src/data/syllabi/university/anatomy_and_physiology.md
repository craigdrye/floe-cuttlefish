# Anatomy and Physiology
**ID**: `anatomyAndPhysiology` | **Discipline**: Science | **Year**: University Freshman / High School Senior

## Course Aim
This course builds a rigorous, usable understanding of the human body as an integrated set of structures, signals, fluids, barriers, and feedback systems. Rather than memorizing parts in isolation, students learn to connect microscopic anatomy to organ-level function, trace a signal or substance across cells, tissues, organs, and systems, and explain how the body holds key variables — temperature, pH, blood pressure, glucose, fluid volume — inside narrow survivable ranges. Anatomy answers *what is it and where*; physiology answers *what does it do and how is it controlled*. The two are taught together because structure and function are inseparable: the shape of a capillary, the thinness of an alveolar membrane, and the length of the loop of Henle each exist to make a specific function possible.

A second goal is evidence literacy. Students should be able to read a basic clinical dataset — vital signs, a complete blood count, a urinalysis, an ECG strip, a hormone panel, a blood-gas value — and reason from it to a mechanism rather than guessing at a label. Every system is framed around its controlled variable and its negative-feedback loop, so that disease is taught as feedback failure: what variable drifted, which sensor or effector failed, and what observable sign that failure produces. This makes pathophysiology a logical extension of normal physiology instead of a separate body of facts.

By the end of the course, a student should be able to use anatomical language precisely, predict how a perturbation (a blocked channel, a missing hormone, a damaged valve, a leaking capillary) ripples through a system, and defend that prediction with anatomical, physiological, and quantitative evidence. The course assumes introductory biology and chemistry but builds the membrane, transport, and feedback concepts it depends on from scratch.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test human anatomical terminology, histology, organ-system structure and function, homeostatic feedback, physiological mechanism, introductory pathophysiology, lab and vital-sign interpretation, or clinical structure-function reasoning. Keep questions grounded in evidence and mechanism: students should trace, compare, predict, and interpret, not merely name parts. Favor "what happens next" and "why does this sign appear" prompts over pure recall. Distractors should encode the specific, common confusions students bring (reversing proximal/distal, confusing the filament that changes length, mixing up the two autonomic divisions, swapping the antigen-presenting MHC class), so a wrong answer diagnoses a real misconception. Quantitative anchors — −70 mV resting potential, the SA-node-first conduction sequence, Starling's net filtration equation, the right-shift mnemonic for the oxygen curve — should be exact and consistent with OpenStax and standard physiology references. Use SI and clinical units; spell out abbreviations on first use within a question.

## Chapter 1: Anatomical Language, Tissues, and Homeostasis
- **Core questions**: How do anatomists describe location unambiguously, and why does that matter clinically? What are the four primary tissue types and what does each one do? What is homeostasis, and how do negative- and positive-feedback loops keep variables in range or drive a process to completion?
- **Key concepts**: Anatomical position; directional terms (proximal/distal, superior/inferior, medial/lateral, anterior/posterior, superficial/deep); body planes (sagittal, frontal/coronal, transverse); dorsal and ventral cavities; serous membranes; levels of organization; the four tissues (epithelial, connective, muscle, nervous); extracellular matrix; set point, sensor, control center, effector; negative feedback; positive feedback (clotting, labor, action potential upstroke).
- **Applied skills**: Translate a plain-language clinical description into correct directional terms and back. Identify a tissue from a micrograph by its defining structural signature (tightly packed sheets vs. cells in abundant matrix vs. contractile fibers vs. branching processes). Read a feedback diagram and label sensor, integrator, and effector, then classify it as negative or positive.
- **Common traps**: Reversing proximal and distal, or describing position in a slumped posture instead of standard anatomical position; calling abundant extracellular matrix a feature of muscle rather than connective tissue; assuming positive feedback is "bad" — it is simply self-amplifying and is essential in clotting and childbirth; confusing the frontal (anterior/posterior) and transverse (superior/inferior) planes.

## Chapter 2: Integumentary and Skeletal Systems
- **Core questions**: How does skin function as a barrier, thermoregulator, and sensory surface, and what happens when the barrier fails? How is bone built, maintained, and remodeled, and how do the cells of bone keep calcium and structure in balance? How does joint structure determine the movement it allows?
- **Key concepts**: Epidermis, dermis, hypodermis; keratinocytes, melanocytes; sweat and sebaceous glands; thermoregulation via vasodilation, vasoconstriction, and sweating; phases of wound healing; bone matrix (collagen + hydroxyapatite); osteoblasts (build), osteocytes (maintain), osteoclasts (resorb); intramembranous vs. endochondral ossification; compact vs. spongy bone; axial vs. appendicular skeleton; joint classification by structure (fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial) and function (range of motion).
- **Applied skills**: Explain a specific consequence of barrier failure (a burn or chronic wound) in terms of fluid loss, infection risk, and thermoregulation. Compare compact and spongy bone by structure and load-bearing role. Classify a joint from a described movement and predict its stability–mobility tradeoff.
- **Common traps**: Confusing osteoblasts with osteoclasts (blast = build, clast = chew); thinking bone is inert rather than continuously remodeled tissue; assuming more mobile joints are more stable (the shoulder trades stability for range of motion); treating melanin as decorative rather than UV-protective.

## Chapter 3: Muscles, Movement, and Biomechanics
- **Core questions**: How does a motor-neuron signal become a muscle contraction? What actually shortens during contraction, and what stays the same length? How do muscle and bone act together as a lever system, and what sets the limits on force and movement?
- **Key concepts**: Skeletal, cardiac, smooth muscle; sarcomere organization (Z-disc, A band, I band, H zone); thin (actin) and thick (myosin) filaments; sliding-filament model; the neuromuscular junction and acetylcholine at nicotinic receptors; excitation–contraction coupling (action potential → Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum → troponin–tropomyosin shift → cross-bridge cycling, ATP-dependent); motor units and recruitment; twitch summation and tetanus; fatigue; lever classes and posture.
- **Applied skills**: Trace the full sequence from motor-neuron firing to sarcomere shortening, naming where Ca²⁺ and ATP act. Interpret which sarcomere band changes (the I band shortens; the A band, equal to thick-filament length, stays constant). Predict the effect of a neuromuscular toxin or a fatigue condition on force output. Connect joint anatomy and lever geometry to movement limits.
- **Common traps**: Saying filaments shorten — they slide; the A band length is fixed because it equals the thick-filament length. Thinking ATP is needed only for the power stroke — it is also required to *detach* myosin from actin (which is why rigor mortis occurs when ATP runs out). Confusing the neurotransmitter (ACh) trigger step with the contractile machinery. Forgetting that calcium, not the action potential itself, directly enables cross-bridge formation.

## Chapter 4: Nervous System and Sensory Integration
- **Core questions**: How does a neuron generate and propagate a signal? How is the resting membrane potential established, and what ion movements produce an action potential? How are the central, peripheral, somatic, and autonomic divisions organized, and how does a reflex arc convert a stimulus into a fast, protective response?
- **Key concepts**: Neuron structure (dendrites, soma, axon, terminals); neuroglia; resting membrane potential (≈ −70 mV, set by K⁺ permeability and the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase); graded potentials, threshold, all-or-none action potential (Na⁺ influx upstroke, K⁺ efflux repolarization); saltatory conduction; chemical synapses and neurotransmitters; CNS vs. PNS; brain regions; cranial and spinal nerves; somatic vs. autonomic; sympathetic (fight-or-flight) vs. parasympathetic (rest-and-digest); reflex arc (receptor → sensory neuron → integration center → motor neuron → effector); sensory receptor classes.
- **Applied skills**: Predict how blocking voltage-gated Na⁺ channels or altering K⁺ permeability changes the action potential. Contrast sympathetic and parasympathetic effects on a target organ (heart rate, pupil, gut). Trace a withdrawal reflex and explain why it can act before the brain "decides." Localize a deficit from a described sensory or motor sign.
- **Common traps**: Saying the inside of a resting neuron has *no* charged particles — it is full of them; the negative sign reflects net polarity by convention (inside relative to outside). Swapping the two autonomic divisions (parasympathetic *slows* the heart). Thinking action potentials vary in size — they are all-or-none; intensity is coded by frequency. Confusing depolarization (Na⁺ in) with repolarization (K⁺ out).

## Chapter 5: Endocrine Control and Blood
- **Core questions**: How do hormones reach and act on target cells, and why do steroid and peptide hormones use different mechanisms? How do hypothalamic–pituitary axes regulate downstream glands through feedback? What does blood carry, and how do its formed elements and clotting system work?
- **Key concepts**: Endocrine vs. nervous control (slow/widespread vs. fast/targeted); peptide/amine hormones (surface receptors, second messengers like cAMP) vs. steroid/thyroid hormones (intracellular receptors, gene transcription); hypothalamus–pituitary axis; thyroid, adrenal cortex and medulla, endocrine pancreas (insulin/glucagon), gonads; negative-feedback control of hormone levels; plasma; erythrocytes and hemoglobin; leukocytes; platelets; hemostasis (vascular spasm, platelet plug, coagulation cascade); ABO/Rh blood typing; the complete blood count (CBC).
- **Applied skills**: Predict whether a hormone acts at the membrane or in the nucleus from its chemical class. Use feedback logic to explain how a failing gland or a tumor shifts circulating hormone and tropic-hormone levels (e.g., primary hypothyroidism raises TSH). Explain type 1 vs. type 2 diabetes in receptor-and-feedback terms. Read a simple CBC pattern (anemia, leukocytosis) and propose a mechanism.
- **Common traps**: Assuming all hormones use second messengers — steroids cross the membrane and act on genes directly. Forgetting that in negative feedback a *low* target hormone usually drives a *high* tropic hormone. Confusing insulin (lowers blood glucose) with glucagon (raises it). Treating blood typing as about red-cell shape rather than surface antigens and the antibodies that react to them.

## Chapter 6: Cardiovascular and Respiratory Systems
- **Core questions**: How does the heart's structure and conduction system produce a coordinated, one-way cardiac cycle? How does blood pressure drive exchange at the capillaries? How do ventilation, gas exchange, and gas transport move O₂ to tissues and CO₂ out?
- **Key concepts**: Heart chambers and valves; pulmonary vs. systemic circuits; cardiac cycle (systole/diastole); conduction system (SA node → AV node → bundle of His → bundle branches → Purkinje fibers); ECG waves (P, QRS, T) and the PR interval; arteries, veins, capillaries; blood pressure and resistance; Starling forces and net filtration (NFP = (Pc − Pi) − (πc − πi)); respiratory tract and alveoli; ventilation mechanics; gas exchange across the respiratory membrane; hemoglobin and the oxygen–hemoglobin dissociation curve; CO₂ transport (mostly as bicarbonate); chemoreceptor control of breathing.
- **Applied skills**: Trace a red cell through pulmonary then systemic circuits, naming valves and vessels in order. Read ECG features (a prolonged PR interval points to AV-node delay; a widened QRS points to ventricular conduction). Apply Starling forces to predict edema (high capillary hydrostatic pressure in heart failure; low plasma oncotic pressure in liver disease). Predict a right shift of the oxygen curve and what it does for tissue O₂ delivery.
- **Common traps**: Naming the AV node as the primary pacemaker — the SA node initiates; the AV node delays and is a backup. Thinking the semilunar valves are open during diastole — they open in systole while the AV valves close. Believing most CO₂ travels dissolved — most is carried as bicarbonate. Reading a right shift of the O₂ curve as worse oxygenation — it means easier *unloading* to active tissue (the "CADET face Right" factors: ↑CO₂, ↑Acid/↓pH, ↑2,3-BPG, ↑Exercise, ↑Temperature).

## Chapter 7: Lymphatic, Immune, Digestive, and Metabolic Systems
- **Core questions**: How does the lymphatic system return fluid and stage immune surveillance? How do innate and adaptive immunity differ, and how do antibodies and the two T-cell classes recognize and clear threats? How does the GI tract mechanically and chemically break down a meal, absorb nutrients, and route them through the liver for metabolism?
- **Key concepts**: Lymph vessels, nodes, spleen, thymus; return of interstitial fluid (linking back to Starling forces); innate immunity (barriers, phagocytes, inflammation, complement) vs. adaptive immunity (specific, memory-forming); B cells and antibody (humoral) responses; helper T cells (CD4, recognize MHC class II on antigen-presenting cells) vs. cytotoxic T cells (CD8, recognize MHC class I on any nucleated cell); immunological memory and the basis of vaccination; GI tract regions and accessory organs; digestive enzymes (amylase, pepsin, lipase, proteases); absorption surfaces (villi/microvilli); liver functions (metabolism, detoxification, bile, plasma proteins); nutrient metabolism and energy balance.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguish innate from adaptive defenses by speed, specificity, and memory. Match a T-cell class to the MHC class it reads and the cell type it targets. Trace a carbohydrate, protein, or fat from mouth to bloodstream, naming the enzyme and site for each step. Connect liver or immune dysfunction (low albumin → edema; low CD4 count → opportunistic infection) to an observable finding.
- **Common traps**: Swapping the MHC pairings — CD8/cytotoxic reads MHC I; CD4/helper reads MHC II. Calling inflammation purely adaptive — it is a core innate response. Thinking antibodies kill cells directly — they mainly neutralize, opsonize, and flag for other effectors. Forgetting nutrient-rich venous blood from the gut passes through the liver (hepatic portal system) *before* the general circulation.

## Chapter 8: Urinary, Reproductive, and Whole-Body Fluid Balance
- **Core questions**: How does the nephron form urine through filtration, reabsorption, and secretion, and how does it concentrate urine? How do the kidneys, lungs, and hormones cooperate to defend fluid volume, electrolytes, and acid–base balance? How do reproductive anatomy and hormonal cycles control gamete production and the menstrual cycle?
- **Key concepts**: Kidney and nephron anatomy (glomerulus, proximal tubule, loop of Henle, distal tubule, collecting duct); glomerular filtration, tubular reabsorption (e.g., glucose via Na⁺-glucose cotransport in the proximal tubule), tubular secretion; countercurrent multiplication; ADH (aquaporin insertion → water reabsorption → concentrated urine); the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS: angiotensin II vasoconstricts; aldosterone drives Na⁺ and water retention and K⁺ excretion → raises blood pressure); acid–base regulation (kidneys secrete H⁺ and reabsorb HCO₃⁻ in acidosis; lungs adjust CO₂); male and female reproductive anatomy; spermatogenesis and oogenesis; the ovarian and menstrual cycles (FSH/LH, estrogen, progesterone); fertilization and an overview of development.
- **Applied skills**: Explain urine formation by nephron region and predict the effect of high vs. low ADH. Interpret a dehydration, volume-overload, or acid–base scenario and name the renal and respiratory compensations. Trace the RAAS from low renal perfusion to restored blood pressure. Compare the hormonal control of the ovarian and menstrual cycles and locate the LH surge.
- **Common traps**: Saying healthy kidneys *secrete* glucose — they reabsorb it; glucose in urine signals exceeded transport capacity (as in hyperglycemia). Confusing filtration (glomerulus, nonselective) with reabsorption and secretion (tubules, selective). Thinking ADH controls salt — it controls *water* via aquaporins; aldosterone handles Na⁺. Reversing the acidosis response (the kidney must *excrete* H⁺ and *keep* HCO₃⁻, not the reverse).

## Capstone
Students produce an evidence-based human-systems case study built around a single patient-style scenario (for example, a person with heart failure, untreated diabetes, dehydration, or a localized infection). The deliverable must integrate at least three systems and include: (1) a structure–function diagram showing the anatomy involved; (2) an explicit homeostatic feedback model naming the controlled variable, sensor, integrator, and effector, and identifying where the loop fails; (3) interpretation of at least one real or simulated dataset (vital signs, CBC, urinalysis, ECG strip, blood gas, or hormone panel) with reasoning from the numbers to a mechanism; and (4) a clear, mechanism-level explanation of how the disease or injury disrupts normal physiology and produces the observed signs. Students defend the case orally or in writing, and must state at least one limitation or source of uncertainty in their evidence. The capstone is assessed on accuracy of anatomy, soundness of the mechanism, correct use of the data, and honest reasoning about what the evidence can and cannot show.

## Assessment Ideas
- Practical quizzes using anatomical models, images, histology slides, and clinical language.
- Mechanism prompts that ask students to trace events across cells, tissues, organs, and systems ("what happens next?").
- Lab and clinical data interpretation from vitals, CBCs, urinalysis, ECG strips, pulmonary values, blood gases, or endocrine panels.
- Diagram-revision tasks where students find and fix a flawed pathway or feedback model.
- A cumulative oral or written case defense focused on evidence, mechanism, and uncertainty.
