# Linear Algebra
**ID**: `linearAlgebra` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Year**: University Sophomore

## Course Aim
Linear algebra is the mathematics of straight-line structure: it studies vectors, the linear maps that move them, and the subspaces those maps create and destroy. This course builds a rigorous working command of finite-dimensional linear algebra as both a computational language and a proof-based theory. Students learn to solve and classify systems of equations, reason fluently about span, independence, and dimension, read a matrix simultaneously as data, as a transformation, and as a collection of column vectors, and use determinants, eigenstructure, orthogonality, and factorizations to answer questions that arise across science, engineering, economics, computer graphics, statistics, and machine learning.

The deeper aim is to change how students see problems. By the end of the course a student should reflexively translate a concrete question — Does this system have a solution? Will this process settle down or blow up? What is the best straight-line fit to noisy data? Which directions in this dataset carry the most information? — into the right linear-algebra object: a column space, an eigenvalue, a least-squares projection, a singular value. The recurring theme is that almost every important matrix fact is really a statement about subspaces and the four fundamental ones (column space, row space, null space, left null space) in particular.

Throughout, the course holds two standards at once. Computations must be exact and disciplined: clean row reduction, correct signs, honest checking. And general claims must be proved from definitions, not asserted from examples. Students who finish the course can both compute an answer by hand and explain, with proof when needed, why the method works and when it fails.

## Course Design Notes
This is a proof-aware university course with weekly problem sets, short computational labs, and careful attention to definitions. Route questions here when students need vectors, matrices, Gaussian elimination, span, independence, bases, rank, null spaces, column spaces, linear transformations, determinants, eigenvalues, diagonalization, orthogonality, projections, least squares, symmetric matrices and quadratic forms, the Spectral Theorem, the Singular Value Decomposition, or applications such as Markov chains, discrete dynamical systems, networks, computer graphics, and principal component analysis.

The course is sequenced so that the geometric picture (vectors and transformations in R^2 and R^3) and the algebraic machinery (row reduction and matrix algebra) are introduced together and reinforced throughout. The Invertible Matrix Theorem and the Rank-Nullity Theorem act as spines that tie the middle chapters together; eigenvalues and orthogonality are treated as the two great structural lenses; and SVD is presented as the unifying decomposition that subsumes the others. Emphasis is placed on exact language ("spans" vs. "is a basis," "consistent" vs. "has a unique solution"), on matrix-vector geometry, on row-reduction discipline, on proving general claims, and on interpreting numerical output rather than trusting it blindly. Computational labs use Python (NumPy), Julia, MATLAB/Octave, or a CAS; software is used to scale up and to confront conditioning and round-off, never to replace understanding of small cases done by hand.

## Chapter 1: Vectors, Systems, and Row Reduction
- **Core questions**: What is the complete solution set of a linear system, and how do pivots and free variables determine whether it is empty, a single point, or an affine subspace? When does a vector lie in the span of others?
- **Key concepts**: Vectors in R^n, linear combinations, span, vector and matrix forms of a system (Ax = b), augmented matrices, elementary row operations, echelon and reduced row echelon form, pivot positions, free variables, consistency, homogeneous systems, and parametric vector solution sets.
- **Applied skills**: Row-reduce a system to RREF and read off the full solution set in parametric form; decide membership in a span by setting up and reducing the corresponding system; classify a system as inconsistent, uniquely solvable, or having infinitely many solutions directly from the pivot pattern; build a homogeneous system with a prescribed solution line or plane.
- **Common traps**: Treating a row of zeros equal to a nonzero number as "no equation" instead of the contradiction that signals inconsistency; confusing echelon form with reduced echelon form and stopping too early; assuming a system with more unknowns than equations must be consistent (it can still be inconsistent); writing the general solution as a single vector instead of base point plus a span of free-variable directions.

## Chapter 2: Matrices, Linear Maps, and Invertibility
- **Core questions**: When is a matrix transformation one-to-one, onto, or invertible, and how do these geometric properties translate into algebraic tests? Why is matrix multiplication composition of functions, and why is it therefore non-commutative?
- **Key concepts**: Matrix-vector products as linear combinations of columns, matrix multiplication as composition, identity and inverse matrices, transpose, block matrices, linear transformations and their standard matrices, one-to-one and onto characterizations, elementary matrices, and the Invertible Matrix Theorem as a web of equivalences.
- **Applied skills**: Find the standard matrix of a transformation from its action on basis vectors; compute an inverse by reducing [A | I] to [I | A^{-1}]; prove a given map is or is not linear straight from T(u+v) = T(u)+T(v) and T(cu)=cT(u); read off whether a transformation is injective/surjective from pivot structure; interpret 2x2 matrices as rotations, shears, reflections, and scalings.
- **Common traps**: Assuming AB = BA, or that AB = 0 forces A = 0 or B = 0; reversing the order in (AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}; thinking a non-square matrix can have a two-sided inverse; believing that because a transformation "looks geometric" it must be linear (translations are not); confusing the columns vs. rows interpretation of a matrix product.

## Chapter 3: Subspaces, Bases, Dimension, and Rank
- **Core questions**: What does it mean for a set of vectors to be exactly the right size to describe a space — neither redundant nor deficient — and how do the four fundamental subspaces of a matrix encode the structure of its linear map? Why does rank plus nullity always equal the number of columns?
- **Key concepts**: Subspaces and the subspace test, span, linear independence, bases, coordinates relative to a basis, dimension, the four fundamental subspaces (column space, null space, row space, left null space), rank, nullity, the Rank-Nullity Theorem, and change of basis.
- **Applied skills**: Find bases for the null space (from free-variable vectors) and column space (from pivot columns of the original matrix); test whether a set is a basis by checking count, independence, and span; compute coordinate vectors relative to a nonstandard basis and convert between bases; prove a subset is or is not a subspace using closure.
- **Common traps**: Taking the pivot columns of the RREF instead of the corresponding columns of the original matrix as a basis for the column space; assuming any spanning set is a basis or any independent set is a basis without checking the other condition; thinking a subset is a subspace because it is "closed under addition" while forgetting it must contain 0 and be closed under scalar multiplication; confusing the dimension of the column space (rank) with the number of columns.

## Chapter 4: Determinants, Volume, and Orientation
- **Core questions**: What single number tells you whether a matrix is invertible and by what factor it scales signed volume, and how do row operations change it? When is cofactor expansion the wrong tool?
- **Key concepts**: Determinants via cofactor expansion and via row reduction, the effect of each elementary row operation, determinants of triangular matrices, det(AB) = det(A)det(B), det(A^T) = det(A), the invertibility criterion det(A) != 0, signed area and volume scaling, orientation, and Cramer's Rule.
- **Applied skills**: Compute determinants efficiently by reducing to triangular form and tracking sign changes and scalings rather than blindly expanding; use multiplicativity and the transpose rule to shortcut computations; interpret the magnitude as a volume-scaling factor and the sign as orientation-preserving or -reversing; prove determinant identities for structured matrices (e.g., triangular, similar matrices).
- **Common traps**: Believing det(A+B) = det(A)+det(B) (it does not); forgetting that swapping two rows flips the sign, or that scaling a row by k multiplies the determinant by k; expanding a large determinant by cofactors when row reduction is far faster; concluding a matrix is invertible from a nonzero entry rather than a nonzero determinant; using Cramer's Rule for numerical work where it is wildly inefficient and unstable.

## Chapter 5: Eigenvalues, Eigenvectors, and Dynamical Systems
- **Core questions**: Which directions does a matrix merely stretch, by how much, and what does that reveal about repeated application A^n and long-run behavior? When can a matrix be diagonalized, and what goes wrong when it cannot?
- **Key concepts**: Eigenvalues, eigenvectors, eigenspaces, the characteristic polynomial det(A - lambda I) = 0, algebraic and geometric multiplicity, diagonalization A = PDP^{-1}, powers via diagonalization, complex eigenvalues and rotation-scaling blocks, Markov chains and steady states, and linear recurrence/discrete dynamical systems.
- **Applied skills**: Find eigenvalues from the characteristic polynomial and eigenspaces by solving (A - lambda I)x = 0; decide diagonalizability by comparing geometric and algebraic multiplicities; compute A^n via PDP^{-1} for closed-form dynamics; find the steady-state vector of a regular stochastic matrix; classify the long-run behavior (growth, decay, oscillation) of a discrete system from its dominant eigenvalue.
- **Common traps**: Concluding a matrix is not diagonalizable just because it has a repeated eigenvalue (it still is if the eigenspace is large enough); forgetting that real matrices can have complex-conjugate eigenvalues; mistaking the steady state for an arbitrary probability vector rather than the dominant eigenvector normalized to sum 1; assuming eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues could be dependent (they are always independent); reading eigenvalues off the diagonal of a non-triangular matrix.

## Chapter 6: Orthogonality, Projections, and Least Squares
- **Core questions**: What is the closest point in a subspace to a given vector, and why does that projection minimize error? When a system Ax = b has no solution, what is the most defensible "best" answer?
- **Key concepts**: Dot products, norms, distance and angle, orthogonal and orthonormal sets, orthogonal complements, orthogonal projection onto a subspace, the Gram-Schmidt process, QR factorization, the normal equations A^T A x = A^T b, least-squares solutions, and residuals.
- **Applied skills**: Build an orthonormal basis with Gram-Schmidt and produce a QR factorization; project a vector onto a subspace and split it into in-subspace and orthogonal parts; solve an inconsistent system by least squares via the normal equations; fit a line or polynomial to data and interpret the residual norm as goodness of fit.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting to normalize after orthogonalizing in Gram-Schmidt (orthogonal is not orthonormal); confusing the projection (which lies in the subspace) with the residual (which is orthogonal to it); applying the simple projection formula b·v/(v·v) v to a non-orthogonal basis; assuming the normal equations always have a unique solution when A's columns are dependent; minimizing the sum of raw residuals (they cancel) instead of squared residuals.

## Chapter 7: Symmetric Matrices, Quadratic Forms, and Spectral Theory
- **Core questions**: Why are symmetric matrices the best-behaved matrices in all of linear algebra, and how does the Spectral Theorem let you choose coordinates in which a quadratic form has no cross terms? How do you read the shape of a level set off the eigenvalues?
- **Key concepts**: Symmetric matrices, the Spectral Theorem (real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenvectors, orthogonal diagonalization A = QDQ^T), quadratic forms, the principal axes theorem, positive definite / semidefinite / indefinite classification, the eigenvalue test for definiteness, and links to constrained optimization (Rayleigh quotient).
- **Applied skills**: Orthogonally diagonalize a symmetric matrix and write its quadratic form in principal-axis coordinates; classify a quadratic form by the signs of its eigenvalues; test positive definiteness via eigenvalues or leading principal minors; identify whether a conic or quadric is an ellipse/ellipsoid, hyperbola, or parabola from the form; explain why symmetry guarantees real eigenvalues and an orthonormal eigenbasis.
- **Common traps**: Building the quadratic-form matrix without halving the off-diagonal (cross-term) coefficients; using a non-orthogonal change of variables to remove cross terms and then misreading the geometry; equating "all diagonal entries positive" with positive definite (false in general); forgetting that semidefinite (a zero eigenvalue) is the boundary case; assuming non-symmetric matrices also have orthogonal eigenvectors.

## Chapter 8: SVD, Numerical Thinking, and Applications
- **Core questions**: How can every matrix — square or not, invertible or not — be factored into a rotation, a stretch along orthogonal axes, and another rotation, and why does that decomposition reveal rank, the best low-rank approximation, and the directions of greatest variance? When should you distrust a computed answer?
- **Key concepts**: Singular values and singular vectors, the Singular Value Decomposition A = U Sigma V^T, the relationship between SVD and the eigendecomposition of A^T A, rank and the four fundamental subspaces from the SVD, the Eckart-Young best rank-k approximation, the condition number sigma_max/sigma_min, numerical stability and round-off, the pseudoinverse, and principal component analysis (PCA).
- **Applied skills**: Compute or interpret a small SVD and read rank, null space, and column space from it; form and compare rank-k approximations for compression; recognize an ill-conditioned problem from a large condition number and anticipate amplified error; connect singular values to explained variance in PCA and to least-squares stability via the pseudoinverse.
- **Common traps**: Confusing singular values (always nonnegative) with eigenvalues (which can be negative or complex); assuming the SVD requires a square matrix; treating a tiny but nonzero singular value as exactly zero, or vice versa, without considering conditioning; forgetting to center data before computing PCA; believing more digits of computation always means more accuracy when the problem itself is ill-conditioned.

## Capstone
Students complete a linear algebra modeling and analysis project that moves from a real question to a defended quantitative answer. The project must (1) define a vector space or matrix model and state its assumptions, (2) justify the relevant subspaces, transformations, or decompositions, (3) use at least three major course tools — for example row reduction, the Rank-Nullity Theorem, determinants, eigenanalysis, orthogonality/least squares, the Spectral Theorem, or SVD — and (4) communicate both exact mathematical reasoning (with at least one proof or rigorous derivation) and an honest applied interpretation that includes the model's limitations. Suitable topics include PageRank and network centrality, Markov-chain population or queueing models, image compression and denoising via truncated SVD, principal component analysis of a real dataset, least-squares regression with diagnostics, vibration/normal-mode analysis, electrical-network or graph-Laplacian problems, and 3D graphics transformation pipelines. Strong projects make explicit how a numerical result was validated and where conditioning or modeling error could undermine the conclusion.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written problem sets graded for correct computation, precise notation, proof quality, and interpretation.
- Short quizzes targeting recurring traps: echelon vs. RREF, column-space basis selection, sign tracking in determinants, diagonalizability decisions, projection vs. residual, and definiteness.
- Proof assignments on definitions and counterexamples, the Rank-Nullity Theorem, the Invertible Matrix Theorem equivalences, independence of eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues, and properties of symmetric matrices.
- Computational labs in Python/NumPy, Julia, MATLAB, or Octave that confirm hand calculations and then push into conditioning, round-off, and large-scale behavior.
- Capstone rubric covering model formulation, mathematical correctness, proof clarity, computational validation, and application-aware explanation of limitations.
