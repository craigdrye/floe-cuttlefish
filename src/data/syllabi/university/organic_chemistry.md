# Organic Chemistry
**ID**: `organicChemistry` - **Discipline**: Science - **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
Organic chemistry has a reputation as a memorization marathon, and that reputation is wrong. This course treats it as what it actually is: a small set of recurring patterns about how electron-rich and electron-poor regions of molecules find each other. Students learn to read a structure and predict its behavior from first principles — bonding, charge, sterics, and the relative stability of whatever the molecule turns into. The goal is fluency in curved-arrow logic, not a flashcard deck of named reactions.

By the end, a student should be able to look at an unfamiliar substrate and a set of reagents and reason out the major product, draw a defensible mechanism, assign stereochemistry, and explain why a competing pathway loses. They should be able to run that same reasoning backward — retrosynthesis — to plan a route to a target, and forward again to predict the spectra that would confirm they made it. These four moves (predict, mechanize, analyze stereochemistry, plan) are the spine of every chapter.

The course is also evidence-driven. Mechanisms are not stories we tell; they are claims constrained by rate laws, stereochemical outcomes, isotope effects, and spectra. Students are pushed to treat every "the reaction goes this way" statement as falsifiable — what observation would distinguish SN1 from SN2, syn from anti addition, kinetic from thermodynamic enolate? That habit is the difference between memorizing organic chemistry and understanding it.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve bonding and hybridization, resonance and formal charge, acid-base reasoning (pKa, ARIO, conjugate-base stability), conformations, stereochemistry (chirality, R/S, E/Z, diastereomers, meso), substitution and elimination competition, alkene/alkyne/radical chemistry, aromaticity and electrophilic aromatic substitution, carbonyl and acyl chemistry, enolates, spectroscopy (IR/MS/NMR), or multi-step synthesis and retrosynthesis. Questions that hinge on curved-arrow mechanisms, regiochemistry, or stereochemical outcomes belong here.

The chapters are deliberately front-loaded with reasoning tools (Chapters 1-3) before any named reactions appear, because students who skip the fundamentals end up memorizing. Difficulty escalates from "identify and explain" through "predict the major product" to "design a route and predict its spectra." Each chapter's Common traps section captures the specific error students actually make at that stage — these are not generic warnings but the recurring failure modes that distinguish a B from an A. Assessment should reward correct arrows and correct reasoning even when a final product is wrong, and should penalize a right product reached by a wrong mechanism.

## Chapter 1: Structure, Bonding, and Acid-Base Reasoning
**Core questions**: Why does structure determine reactivity? How do you locate the electron-rich and electron-poor regions of a molecule, and how do you predict where electrons will flow?
**Key concepts**: Lewis structures, formal charge, VSEPR geometry, hybridization (sp/sp2/sp3) and its effect on bond length, angle, and acidity; sigma vs pi bonding; electronegativity and bond/molecular dipoles; resonance and the rules for ranking contributors; curved-arrow notation; Bronsted-Lowry vs Lewis acids and bases; pKa and conjugate-base stability; the ARIO framework (Atom, Resonance, Induction, Orbital) for ranking acidity.
**Applied skills**: Draw and rank resonance contributors; compute formal charge; rank a set of acids using ARIO and predict the position of an acid-base equilibrium (it favors the weaker acid); identify the nucleophilic and electrophilic sites in a structure.
**Common traps**: Confusing resonance (electron delocalization, same skeleton) with equilibrium (different molecules); ranking acidity by the acid's stability instead of the conjugate base's; forgetting that high s-character (sp > sp2 > sp3) stabilizes negative charge, making terminal alkynes more acidic than expected; drawing curved arrows from positive to negative instead of from electron source to sink.

## Chapter 2: Nomenclature, Conformation, and Stereochemistry
**Core questions**: How does a molecule's three-dimensional shape — both its fixed configuration and its rotating conformations — control its energy and reactivity?
**Key concepts**: IUPAC naming; constitutional vs stereo isomers; Newman projections and torsional/steric strain (anti < gauche < eclipsed); cyclohexane chair conformations, axial/equatorial positions, 1,3-diaxial interactions, and ring-flips; chirality and stereocenters; R/S via Cahn-Ingold-Prelog; E/Z; enantiomers, diastereomers, and meso compounds; optical activity.
**Applied skills**: Name structures and convert between line, Newman, and chair drawings; identify the lowest-energy chair (largest group equatorial); assign R/S and E/Z; classify the relationship between any two stereoisomers; count expected stereoisomers (2^n, minus duplicates from meso symmetry).
**Common traps**: Assigning CIP priority by atomic mass instead of atomic number, and stopping at the first atom instead of exploring to the first point of difference; forgetting to reverse the R/S assignment when the lowest-priority group points toward you; assuming cis means both-equatorial on a ring (for 1,2-disubstituted cyclohexane, cis forces one axial/one equatorial); calling a meso compound a pair of enantiomers.

## Chapter 3: Reaction Energy and Mechanism Logic
**Core questions**: What makes a reaction fast versus favorable, and how do reagents and conditions decide which of several competing pathways actually runs?
**Key concepts**: Thermodynamics (ΔG, equilibrium) vs kinetics (rate, activation energy); reaction-coordinate diagrams; transition states vs intermediates; the Hammond postulate (the TS resembles the species nearest it in energy); kinetic vs thermodynamic control; roles of nucleophile, base, leaving group, and solvent; how polar protic vs polar aprotic solvents shift mechanism.
**Applied skills**: Read and annotate energy diagrams (locate rate-determining step, intermediates, products); predict whether a change in temperature, solvent, or reagent strength favors substitution vs elimination or kinetic vs thermodynamic product; justify reagent roles in a mechanism.
**Common traps**: Conflating thermodynamic stability (which product is lower in energy) with kinetic accessibility (which forms faster); reading the highest point on a diagram as an intermediate rather than a transition state; assuming a more exothermic reaction is always faster; ignoring that protic solvents stabilize and thereby slow strong anionic nucleophiles.

## Chapter 4: Substitution and Elimination
**Core questions**: Given a substrate, nucleophile/base, and solvent, which of SN1, SN2, E1, and E2 dominates — and what is the stereochemical outcome?
**Key concepts**: SN2 (concerted, backside attack, inversion, second-order, favored at methyl/1°); SN1/E1 (carbocation intermediate, racemization, first-order, favored at 3°); E2 (concerted, anti-periplanar geometry, second-order, strong base); carbocation stability and rearrangements (hydride/methyl shifts); substrate, nucleophile/base strength, and solvent effects; Zaitsev (more substituted, default) vs Hofmann (less substituted, bulky base) regiochemistry.
**Applied skills**: Build the four-way decision (substrate class -> nucleophile vs base, strong vs weak -> solvent) and predict the major product; draw complete mechanisms with correct arrows and stereochemistry; identify when a carbocation will rearrange; assign the geometry of E2 products from the required anti-periplanar arrangement.
**Common traps**: Forgetting that on a cyclohexane ring, E2 demands the leaving group and beta-H both axial (trans-diaxial), which can override Zaitsev; predicting SN2 at a tertiary center; missing carbocation rearrangements in SN1/E1; treating a bulky strong base (t-BuOK) as a good SN2 nucleophile rather than an elimination-forcing base.

## Chapter 5: Alkenes, Alkynes, and Radical Chemistry
**Core questions**: How do pi bonds act as nucleophiles, and how do regiochemistry and stereochemistry follow from the intermediate (carbocation, halonium, borane, radical)?
**Key concepts**: Electrophilic addition; Markovnikov (acid-catalyzed hydration, HX) via the more stable carbocation; anti-Markovnikov, syn hydroboration-oxidation; anti-addition halogenation via halonium ion and halohydrin formation; epoxidation (mCPBA, syn) and dihydroxylation; ozonolysis (oxidative cleavage to carbonyls); terminal alkyne acidity and acetylide alkylation; reduction (H2/Lindlar -> cis; Na/NH3 -> trans); radical halogenation, selectivity, and allylic substitution (NBS).
**Applied skills**: Predict regio- and stereochemistry for each addition; choose reagents to hit a specific OH placement and stereochemistry; plan short syntheses converting simple hydrocarbons into alcohols, dihalides, diols, or carbonyl fragments; use ozonolysis retro-analysis to deduce a starting alkene.
**Common traps**: Swapping Markovnikov (acid hydration) and anti-Markovnikov (hydroboration) orientations; calling syn-addition products cis on a ring without checking how OH and an existing substituent relate; forgetting that mCPBA epoxidation is stereospecific (trans-alkene -> trans-epoxide); confusing Lindlar (cis) with dissolving-metal (trans) alkyne reductions.

## Chapter 6: Conjugation, Aromaticity, and Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution
**Core questions**: What makes a ring aromatic, and how do existing substituents control the rate and position of further substitution?
**Key concepts**: Conjugation and resonance stabilization; aromaticity criteria (cyclic, planar, fully conjugated, 4n+2 pi electrons — Hückel's rule) vs antiaromatic (4n); aromatic ions and heterocycles; electrophilic aromatic substitution (nitration, halogenation, Friedel-Crafts) and the arenium/sigma-complex intermediate; activating ortho/para directors vs deactivating meta directors; halogens as deactivating ortho/para directors.
**Applied skills**: Classify aromatic/antiaromatic/nonaromatic and count pi electrons; predict the major EAS product including directing and activating/deactivating effects; rationalize directing effects with resonance structures of the arenium intermediate; sequence substituents to reach a specific disubstitution pattern.
**Common traps**: Counting pi bonds instead of pi electrons (benzene = 6, not 3); calling antiaromatic 4n systems aromatic; assuming all deactivators are meta directors (halogens are the exception — deactivating yet ortho/para); ignoring that the order of installation matters for getting the right substitution pattern.

## Chapter 7: Carbonyl, Acyl, and Enolate Chemistry
**Core questions**: Why is the carbonyl carbon electrophilic, and how do the two great carbonyl reactions — nucleophilic addition and nucleophilic acyl substitution — and enolate chemistry follow from it?
**Key concepts**: Carbonyl polarization and electrophilicity; nucleophilic addition to aldehydes/ketones (Grignard, hydride, cyanohydrin, acetal protection); nucleophilic acyl substitution and the reactivity ladder of acid derivatives (acyl chloride > anhydride > ester > amide); Fischer esterification and Le Chatelier control; alpha-acidity and enolates; aldol and Claisen condensations; E1cB dehydration to alpha,beta-unsaturated carbonyls; kinetic vs thermodynamic enolates.
**Applied skills**: Predict products of addition vs acyl substitution; rank acid-derivative reactivity by leaving-group ability; draw aldol/Claisen mechanisms and identify the new C-C bond; choose conditions to drive an equilibrium (excess reagent, remove water); select a base to set the kinetic vs thermodynamic enolate.
**Common traps**: Expecting Grignard reagents to add to carboxylic acids (they deprotonate them first — incompatible without protection); reversing the acid-derivative reactivity order; forgetting the conjugation of the alpha,beta-unsaturated product is the driving force for aldol dehydration; trying to base-catalyze Fischer esterification (it is strictly acid-catalyzed).

## Chapter 8: Spectroscopy and Structure Determination
**Core questions**: Given mass, IR, and NMR data, how do you converge on a single structure — and predict the spectrum a proposed structure would give?
**Key concepts**: Mass spectrometry (molecular ion, M+2 isotope patterns for Cl/Br, key fragmentations); degrees of unsaturation from molecular formula; IR diagnostics (broad O-H ~3300, sharp C=O ~1700, C≡C/C≡N ~2100-2250); 1H NMR (chemical shift, integration, n+1 splitting, coupling) and 13C NMR (carbon count, shift ranges); combining all evidence.
**Applied skills**: Calculate degrees of unsaturation; assign IR bands to functional groups; interpret splitting patterns with the n+1 rule; use integration ratios for relative H counts; solve an unknown structure from combined MS/IR/NMR; predict the spectrum of a given molecule.
**Common traps**: Treating IR carbonyl (~1700) and O-H (~3300) regions interchangeably; forgetting that integration gives ratios, not absolute counts; misapplying n+1 when neighbors are non-equivalent or exchangeable (OH protons often appear as broad singlets); overlooking the M+2 peak that flags chlorine or bromine.

## Chapter 9: Synthesis Strategy and Laboratory Thinking
**Core questions**: How do you plan a multi-step route to a target, and how do real-world selectivity, purification, and safety constraints shape that plan?
**Key concepts**: Retrosynthetic analysis and disconnections; synthons and reagent equivalents; chemoselectivity, regioselectivity, and stereoselectivity; protecting groups (acetals, silyl ethers); functional-group interconversions; purification (extraction, recrystallization, distillation, chromatography); percent yield and step economy; safety hazards and green-chemistry principles.
**Applied skills**: Propose a retrosynthesis and a forward route with mechanisms; deploy protecting groups to mask incompatible groups (e.g., protect a ketone before a Grignard); choose a purification method from the compound's properties; calculate overall yield across steps; diagnose and revise a route after a failed or low-selectivity step.
**Common traps**: Designing a route that destroys a needed functional group in an earlier step (no protecting-group planning); installing substituents in an order that gives the wrong EAS pattern; multiplying step yields incorrectly when computing overall yield; choosing a purification method incompatible with the compound (e.g., distilling a heat-sensitive product).

## Capstone
Students take on a target-molecule challenge from end to end. Given a moderately complex target, they (1) identify the key functional groups and stereocenters; (2) perform a retrosynthetic analysis and propose a forward route using reactions from the course; (3) justify the regio- and stereochemistry of each step with full curved-arrow mechanisms, naming the likely competing pathways and why they lose; (4) predict the IR and 1H/13C NMR spectra of the target as proof of structure; and (5) present a lab plan covering protecting-group strategy, purification at each stage, expected side reactions, overall yield, and safety considerations. The deliverable is assessed on mechanistic rigor and the quality of reasoning, not merely whether the final structure is reachable.

## Assessment Ideas
Use mechanism quizzes (graded on arrows and reasoning, not just products), stereochemistry drills, reagent-prediction sets, spectroscopy unknowns, retrosynthesis problems, and short oral mechanism defenses. Mix "predict the product," "draw the mechanism," "explain why the competing pathway loses," and "predict the spectrum" item types so students cannot lean on memorized outcomes. Reward correct mechanistic logic even where a final product is wrong; penalize correct products reached by an indefensible mechanism.
