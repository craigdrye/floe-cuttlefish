# MCAT Prep
**ID**: `mcat` · **Discipline**: Exam Prep · **Year**: University Senior

## Course Philosophy
The Medical College Admission Test (MCAT) assesses problem-solving, critical thinking, and knowledge of natural, behavioral, and social science concepts prerequisite to the study of medicine. This syllabus integrates massive content review with passage-based analysis strategies.

## Module 1: Chemical and Physical Foundations of Biological Systems (25%)
- General Chemistry: Atomic/electronic structure, periodic trends, stoichiometry, thermodynamics/thermochemistry, kinetics, equilibrium, acids/bases, electrochemistry
- Organic Chemistry: Nomenclature, stereochemistry, substitution/elimination, carbonyl chemistry (aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids), laboratory techniques (separations, spectroscopy)
- Physics: Kinematics, Newtonian mechanics, work/energy, thermodynamics, fluids (hydrostatics, fluid dynamics), electrostatics, circuits, waves/sound, light/optics, nuclear/atomic physics
- Data Analysis: Interpreting graphs, dimensional analysis, and performing calculations without a calculator
- **Questions**: Analyzing a passage on a novel battery and calculating standard cell potential, applying Bernoulli's equation to blood flow in a narrowed artery

## Module 2: Biological and Biochemical Foundations of Living Systems (30%)
- Biochemistry: Amino acids/peptides/proteins, enzyme kinetics (Michaelis-Menten, Lineweaver-Burk plots), carbohydrate structure/function, lipid metabolism, bioenergetics (glycolysis, Krebs cycle, oxidative phosphorylation), DNA replication/repair, transcription/translation
- Cellular Biology: Eukaryotic vs prokaryotic cell structure, cell cycle, mitosis/meiosis, viruses
- Genetics and Evolution: Mendelian genetics, population genetics (Hardy-Weinberg), molecular evolution
- Human Physiology: Nervous, endocrine, respiratory, cardiovascular, immune, digestive, excretory, reproductive, musculoskeletal, and integumentary systems
- **Questions**: Interpreting a Lineweaver-Burk plot to identify the type of enzyme inhibitor, tracing the hormonal regulation of the menstrual cycle based on a passage

## Module 3: Psychological, Social, and Biological Foundations of Behavior (20%)
- Psychology: Sensation and perception, learning and memory, cognition/language/consciousness, emotion and stress, developmental psychology, personality theories, psychological disorders, social psychology (conformity, obedience, group dynamics)
- Sociology: Theoretical approaches (Functionalism, Conflict Theory, Symbolic Interactionism), social institutions (family, education, religion, government, healthcare), culture, demographics, social stratification, inequality, and health disparities
- Biological bases of behavior: Neuroanatomy, neurotransmitters, and the endocrine system's effect on behavior
- **Questions**: Applying Conflict Theory to explain healthcare disparities detailed in a passage, identifying the specific memory distortion (e.g., source monitoring error) described in a clinical study

## Module 4: Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS) (15%)
- Foundations of Comprehension: Understanding the central theme, summarizing complex paragraphs, inferring the meaning of words from context
- Reasoning Within the Text: Identifying the author's argument, evaluating evidence, recognizing assumptions and rhetorical devices
- Reasoning Beyond the Text: Applying or extrapolating ideas from the passage to new situations, assessing how new information alters the author's argument
- Humanities vs Social Science passage strategies
- **Questions**: Determining the author's implicit assumption in a dense philosophy passage, evaluating how a hypothetical historical discovery would impact the passage's main thesis

## Module 5: Test Strategy and Experimental Design (10%)
- Passage Mapping: Strategies for reading dense scientific literature quickly (highlighting, outlining)
- Question Triage: Identifying easy vs hard questions, timing strategies (8 minutes per passage)
- Experimental Design: Identifying independent/dependent variables, controls (positive vs negative), identifying confounding variables, evaluating the validity of a study described in a passage
- Statistical significance (interpreting p-values and confidence intervals in passages)
- **Questions**: Identifying the purpose of a negative control in a described Western Blot experiment, explaining why a study's conclusion is invalid due to selection bias

## Stretch Zone
- Memorization techniques for high-yield pathways (e.g., all structures and enzymes of the Krebs Cycle)
- Advanced amino acid titration curves and pI calculations
