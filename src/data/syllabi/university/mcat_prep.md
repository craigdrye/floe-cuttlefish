# MCAT Prep
**ID**: `mcat` - **Discipline**: Exam Prep - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
The MCAT does not reward people who have memorized the most facts. It rewards people who can take a paragraph they have never seen, attach the right scientific model to it, and reason to an answer under a clock. This course is built around that reality. Across four scored sections — Chemical and Physical Foundations of Biological Systems, Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS), Biological and Biochemical Foundations of Living Systems, and Psychological, Social, and Biological Foundations of Behavior — roughly three-quarters of every science question is wrapped in a research passage. We treat the exam as applied scientific literacy: content is the toolkit, and passage reasoning is the job.

The course is sequenced to build two competencies in parallel. The first is durable content fluency — the formulas, mechanisms, and definitions you must recall without hesitation so that working memory is free for reasoning. The second is the meta-skill of MCAT thinking: reading a graph before the labels, predicting where a distractor will try to trap you, deciding in three seconds whether a question is worth two minutes, and treating a wrong answer in review as more valuable than ten right ones. Students who only drill content plateau in the high 490s; students who only drill strategy without a content base never get traction. We do both, deliberately, and tie every content chapter back to the passage and figure formats the exam actually uses.

Finally, this course takes scoring seriously as feedback rather than verdict. Each section is scaled 118 to 132 (midpoint 125), the total runs 472 to 528 (midpoint 500, the all-examinee mean), and the difference between a 505 and a 515 is almost entirely a difference in error discipline, not raw knowledge. You will leave able to read your own score report like a diagnostician — to name the gap behind every miss as content, careless, timing, or reasoning — and to convert that diagnosis into the next week's plan.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve MCAT-style content reasoning in any of the four sections: general and organic chemistry, physics applied to biological systems, biology and physiology, biochemistry and molecular biology, psychology and sociology, CARS passage analysis, experimental design and biostatistics, data and figure interpretation, no-calculator estimation, timing and triage, or score-report and missed-question analysis. The unifying thread is that almost nothing is asked in isolation — content lives inside passages, and reasoning skills are practiced on real content. Keep that pairing intact: a pure trivia recall item with no application context belongs in a discipline-specific track, not here.

CARS is roughly a quarter of the exam and uses no outside knowledge, so CARS items here pair an original short passage (AAMC passages are copyrighted) with a single question type — main idea, tone, inference, strengthen, weaken, application, or assumption. Science items should favor calculation, graph reading, inhibitor/curve identification, and "which conclusion is supported" formats over bare recall. Where a fact is load-bearing (a formula, a pKa, an ATP yield), it must be current and exact; the exam punishes the half-remembered version.

## Chapter 1: The Exam, the Diagnostic, and Your Study System
**Core questions**: How is the MCAT structured and scored, and what does a section score actually measure? How do you read a diagnostic score report to set content priorities? What review loop turns practice into score gains?
**Key concepts**: Four sections; ~59 questions each in the science sections and 53 in CARS; each section scaled 118-132 (midpoint 125); total 472-528 (midpoint 500); percentile vs. scaled score; diagnostic full-length; error log; spaced repetition; the four error categories (content gap, careless, timing, reasoning); weekly content-vs-practice balance.
**Applied skills**: Take and categorize a diagnostic; build a review calendar that front-loads weak sections; tag every missed question with a cause; decide when to review content versus do passages.
**Common traps**: Treating the scaled score as a percentage correct; chasing total question volume instead of reviewing depth; reviewing only what you got wrong and ignoring lucky guesses; planning by topic checklist rather than by diagnosed weakness.

## Chapter 2: Chemical Foundations and No-Calculator Quantitative Reasoning
**Core questions**: How do periodic trends and bonding predict reactivity? How do you handle equilibria, acids/bases, and buffers in a biological context? How do you estimate a numeric answer in seconds without a calculator?
**Key concepts**: Atomic structure and periodic trends; stoichiometry and limiting reagent; thermodynamics (ΔG, ΔH, ΔS); kinetics vs. thermodynamics; Le Chatelier and equilibrium shifts; Ka/pKa, pH, and the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation (pH = pKa + log [A⁻]/[HA]); buffer capacity near pKa; dimensional analysis and order-of-magnitude estimation.
**Applied skills**: Interpret titration and buffer passages; estimate logs and powers of ten mentally; identify the limiting reagent; connect an equilibrium shift to a physiological consequence.
**Common traps**: Confusing rate (kinetics) with spontaneity (thermodynamics); forgetting a buffer resists change only within ~1 pH unit of its pKa; sign errors on ΔG = ΔH − TΔS; decimal-place slips when skipping the calculator.

## Chapter 3: Physics in Biological Systems
**Core questions**: How do mechanics, fluids, and energy laws describe the body? How do circuits, waves, and optics show up in instrumentation and physiology? When can you reason qualitatively instead of plugging in?
**Key concepts**: Kinematics and Newton's laws; work, energy, and power; fluid statics and dynamics (continuity, Bernoulli, Poiseuille); pressure and the cardiovascular analogy; thermodynamics; electrostatics and DC circuits (Ohm's law, series vs. parallel); waves, sound, Doppler; geometric optics and the thin-lens equation (1/f = 1/dₒ + 1/dᵢ, m = −dᵢ/dₒ).
**Applied skills**: Apply continuity and Bernoulli to blood flow; combine resistors and find branch currents; locate and size an image from a lens; predict a Doppler frequency shift direction.
**Common traps**: Adding parallel resistances arithmetically instead of by reciprocals; forgetting Bernoulli trades pressure for velocity (faster flow → lower pressure); sign conventions for lenses/mirrors; treating a Doppler shift as magnitude-only when direction matters.

## Chapter 4: Biology, Physiology, and Organ-System Integration
**Core questions**: How do organ systems maintain homeostasis through feedback? How do you trace a hormone or signal from stimulus to effect? How do you read a physiology graph (e.g., a dissociation curve) for what shifted and why?
**Key concepts**: Cell structure, membranes, and transport; cell cycle and division; classical genetics and Hardy–Weinberg; nervous, endocrine, cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, immune, digestive, reproductive, and musculoskeletal systems; negative vs. positive feedback; the oxygen–hemoglobin dissociation curve and the Bohr effect (↑CO₂, ↑H⁺, ↑temp, ↑2,3-BPG → right shift, lower O₂ affinity).
**Applied skills**: Trace hormone feedback loops; predict the direction of a curve shift from a physiological change; integrate two organ systems in one passage; reason from a knockout or lesion to function.
**Common traps**: Reversing the direction of a feedback loop; reading a right-shifted O₂ curve as higher affinity instead of lower; conflating "secreted by" with "acts on"; assuming a hormone's effect without checking the receptor location.

## Chapter 5: Biochemistry, Metabolism, and Molecular Biology
**Core questions**: How does protein structure determine function and regulation? How do you read enzyme-kinetics plots and identify an inhibitor? How does carbon and energy flow through central metabolism?
**Key concepts**: Amino acid properties and ionization at physiological pH; the four levels of protein structure; enzymes and Michaelis–Menten (Km, Vmax); competitive vs. noncompetitive vs. uncompetitive inhibition (and their effects on Km/Vmax); glycolysis net yield (2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2 pyruvate per glucose); citric acid cycle; oxidative phosphorylation (~30–32 ATP per glucose); the central dogma; common lab techniques (electrophoresis, Western blot, chromatography, PCR).
**Applied skills**: Read a Michaelis–Menten or Lineweaver–Burk plot and name the inhibition type; compute a net charge on an amino acid at a given pH; trace a regulated metabolic step; interpret a gel or blot in a passage.
**Common traps**: Saying competitive inhibition lowers Vmax (it doesn't — it raises apparent Km); forgetting the side chain when computing amino acid net charge; quoting the outdated "38 ATP" figure for glucose; confusing Km (affinity proxy) with catalytic rate.

## Chapter 6: Psychology, Sociology, and the Behavioral Sciences
**Core questions**: How do sensation and perception turn stimuli into experience? How do learning, memory, and cognition differ mechanistically? How do social structures and determinants shape health?
**Key concepts**: Psychophysics — absolute threshold, just-noticeable difference, Weber's law, signal detection theory; learning (classical vs. operant conditioning, habituation); memory systems and the hippocampus; cognition, intelligence, and bias; emotion and stress; lifespan development; personality theories; psychological disorders; social psychology (attribution, conformity, attitudes); demographics, social institutions, stratification, and social determinants of health.
**Applied skills**: Match a scenario to the correct learning or perception process; distinguish closely related terms (e.g., habituation vs. extinction); connect a social-determinant variable to a health outcome; identify the theory behind a described study.
**Common traps**: Calling reduced response to a repeated harmless stimulus "extinction" instead of habituation; confusing the absolute threshold with the difference threshold; treating Weber's law's JND as a fixed amount rather than a constant proportion; swapping correlation of a determinant for causation.

## Chapter 7: Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills (CARS)
**Core questions**: What is the author's central claim and tone? What does a question type (inference, strengthen, weaken, assumption, application) actually require? How do you eliminate the seductive wrong answer?
**Key concepts**: Main idea vs. supporting detail; author purpose and tone; structure and argument flow; inference within the passage's logic; strengthen/weaken by addressing the specific claim or inferential gap; necessary assumptions; application to a new scenario; the no-outside-knowledge rule; common answer-choice traps (extreme language, half-right, out-of-scope, opposite).
**Applied skills**: Summarize a dense paragraph in one sentence; predict an answer before reading choices; identify which finding most directly bears on the author's specific claim; carry an argument by analogy into a new domain.
**Common traps**: Importing outside knowledge or personal opinion; choosing a true statement that doesn't answer the question; picking the answer that restates the position the author rejects; treating a sympathetic-but-tangential fact as a strengthener.

## Chapter 8: Experimental Design, Data Interpretation, and Biostatistics
**Core questions**: What is being manipulated, measured, and controlled? What does a result actually license you to conclude? How do you read a figure for the claim it supports?
**Key concepts**: Independent vs. dependent variable; positive vs. negative control; confounders and the correlation–causation gap; internal vs. external validity; reliability; randomization and bias; p-values and statistical vs. practical significance; confidence intervals; figure and table reading; identifying the supported conclusion.
**Applied skills**: Name the variable, control, or confounder in a study; critique a causal claim from observational data; read error bars and axes before trusting a trend; choose the conclusion the data support and reject overreach.
**Common traps**: Reading a small p-value as proof of importance or of the null's truth; mistaking the positive control for the experimental group; ignoring a confounder that explains both exposure and outcome; concluding causation from a correlational design.

## Chapter 9: Timing, Triage, Full-Lengths, and Test-Day Execution
**Core questions**: How do you allocate time per passage and question? When do you flag, skip, or commit? How do you build endurance for a 7.5-hour exam and turn a full-length into a review tool?
**Key concepts**: Per-question time budgets per section; passage mapping vs. read-and-attack; flag-and-return triage; guessing strategy (no wrong-answer penalty — never leave blanks); stamina and break planning; the full-length review protocol; converting error categories into the next week's study targets; test-day logistics and routine.
**Applied skills**: Pace a section against a clock and recover from a slow start; triage a brutal passage without bleeding time; run a structured full-length review that separates content gaps from reasoning gaps; build a final test-week taper.
**Common traps**: Sinking five minutes into one question and starving later ones; leaving questions blank despite no penalty; reviewing a full-length only by score instead of by error type; cramming new content in the final week instead of consolidating.

## Capstone
Students assemble a final MCAT readiness portfolio that demonstrates both content fluency and reasoning discipline. It includes: a diagnostic-to-current score-report history with each section's trajectory; content maps for the weakest two science topics; a CARS log tracking passage summaries and missed-question types over time; an experimental-design and biostatistics checklist applied to three real practice passages; a full-length review packet that categorizes every miss as content, careless, timing, or reasoning; and a final test-week plan with a pacing strategy, break schedule, and consolidation (not cramming) calendar. The portfolio is assessed on the quality of diagnosis and adjustment — the ability to explain why each error happened and what the plan does about it — as much as on raw score gain.

## Assessment Ideas
Use timed passages, no-calculator estimation drills, graph- and figure-interpretation sets, enzyme-curve and circuit identification problems, CARS question-type logs, full-length reviews, and oral explanations of missed questions ("teach the trap"). Weight the quality of review and the accuracy of self-diagnosis as heavily as score improvement.
