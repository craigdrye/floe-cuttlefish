# Marketing Principles
**ID**: `marketing` | **Discipline**: Business | **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
Marketing Principles introduces students to how organizations understand customers, create value, communicate clearly, deliver offerings, and measure results. The course treats marketing as a disciplined business function that combines research, analytics, creativity, operations, and ethics. Students learn to move from market evidence to segmentation, targeting, positioning, and the marketing mix while considering customer welfare, privacy, inclusion, sustainability, and social impact.

By the end of the course, students should be able to conduct basic customer and market research, define attractive segments, build a positioning argument, make product, price, place, and promotion decisions, interpret marketing metrics, and defend a practical marketing recommendation with evidence.

## Course Design Notes
- Use current products, local organizations, business-to-business examples, nonprofit examples, and consumer brands to show how marketing decisions vary by context.
- Keep the marketing mix connected to customer research and positioning rather than treating the 4 Ps as isolated tactics.
- Build repeated practice with personas, perceptual maps, value propositions, channel choices, campaign briefs, and metric interpretation.
- Treat ethics, data privacy, accessibility, representation, dark patterns, and truthful communication as recurring design constraints.
- Ask students to justify recommendations with customer evidence, financial logic, and operational feasibility.

## Chapter 1: Marketing, Value, and Strategy
### Key concepts
- Marketing as creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging value
- Customer needs, wants, demand, exchange, value proposition, and customer experience
- The marketing concept and market orientation
- Strategic planning, mission fit, objectives, SWOT, competitive advantage, and portfolio thinking
- Microenvironment and macroenvironment analysis, including competitive, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental forces
- Ethical marketing responsibilities to customers, firms, and society

### Practice questions
- How is marketing broader than advertising or selling?
- What customer problem does a chosen brand solve, and how does it create value?
- Which environmental trend creates the greatest opportunity or risk for a business?

### Student output
Students prepare a short situation analysis for an organization, including customer value, competitors, SWOT, and one ethical issue to monitor.

## Chapter 2: Customer Research and Market Insight
### Key concepts
- The marketing research process: problem definition, research design, data collection, analysis, and action
- Primary and secondary research
- Qualitative methods, including interviews, focus groups, observation, and open-ended feedback
- Quantitative methods, including surveys, experiments, panels, and behavioral data
- Sampling, bias, validity, reliability, and limits of self-reported data
- Customer journey mapping, pain points, jobs to be done, and insight statements

### Practice questions
- What research question should be answered before launching a new product?
- When is qualitative research more useful than a survey?
- How can leading questions or biased samples distort marketing decisions?

### Student output
Students design a small research plan, draft five neutral customer questions, and convert findings into two actionable customer insights.

## Chapter 3: Consumer and Organizational Buying Behavior
### Key concepts
- Consumer decision process: need recognition, information search, evaluation, purchase, and post-purchase behavior
- Psychological influences: motivation, perception, learning, attitudes, and decision shortcuts
- Social and cultural influences: reference groups, culture, identity, household roles, and social proof
- Business buying centers, derived demand, procurement criteria, and relationship selling
- Customer trust, perceived risk, satisfaction, loyalty, and cognitive dissonance
- Ethical boundaries in persuasion, nudging, vulnerability, and behavioral targeting

### Practice questions
- What factors explain why two customers respond differently to the same offer?
- How does a business purchase differ from a consumer purchase?
- When does personalization become manipulative?

### Student output
Students map a customer decision journey for a product or service and identify one marketing action for each major stage.

## Chapter 4: Segmentation, Targeting, and Positioning
### Key concepts
- Segmentation bases: geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioral, needs-based, and firmographic
- Segment attractiveness, measurability, accessibility, size, growth, profitability, and strategic fit
- Targeting strategies: mass, differentiated, concentrated, niche, and micromarketing
- Personas, use cases, and buyer roles
- Positioning, points of parity, points of difference, perceptual mapping, and brand associations
- Positioning statements and value proposition design

### Practice questions
- Which segmentation variable best explains demand for a selected product?
- What makes a segment attractive but difficult to serve?
- How can a perceptual map reveal a positioning opportunity?

### Student output
Students define two target segments, select one priority segment, build a persona, and write a positioning statement supported by a perceptual map.

## Chapter 5: Product, Brand, and Customer Experience
### Key concepts
- Product levels: core benefit, actual product, augmented product, and customer experience
- Goods, services, experiences, platforms, and product-service bundles
- Product life cycle and adoption categories
- Branding, brand equity, brand identity, packaging, naming, and brand architecture
- New product development, testing, launch, iteration, and failure learning
- Service quality, customer support, accessibility, and inclusive design

### Practice questions
- What is the core benefit of a product beyond its physical features?
- How should marketing strategy change across the product life cycle?
- How can packaging, service, or onboarding affect brand perception?

### Student output
Students create a product brief that identifies the target customer, core benefit, feature set, brand promise, and launch risks.

## Chapter 6: Pricing and Revenue Strategy
### Key concepts
- Pricing objectives: profit, revenue, market share, survival, customer value, and positioning
- Cost-based, competition-based, and value-based pricing
- Price elasticity, willingness to pay, reference prices, and perceived fairness
- Penetration pricing, skimming, bundling, subscriptions, freemium, dynamic pricing, and promotional pricing
- Break-even analysis, contribution margin, customer lifetime value, and price testing
- Ethical issues in pricing transparency, discrimination, scarcity claims, fees, and vulnerable customers

### Practice questions
- When is value-based pricing more appropriate than cost-plus pricing?
- How does price signal quality or risk to customers?
- What pricing practice might be legal but still damage trust?

### Student output
Students recommend a pricing strategy for a new or revised offer, calculate a simple break-even point, and explain the customer trust implications.

## Chapter 7: Place, Channels, and Distribution
### Key concepts
- Marketing channels, intermediaries, direct and indirect distribution
- Intensive, selective, and exclusive distribution
- Retail, ecommerce, marketplaces, wholesalers, resellers, agents, and platform channels
- Omnichannel customer experience and channel conflict
- Supply chain basics, fulfillment, service levels, inventory availability, and last-mile considerations
- Channel metrics, including conversion, sell-through, availability, margin, and return rates

### Practice questions
- Why might a premium brand choose selective distribution?
- What channel conflicts can arise between direct ecommerce and retail partners?
- How do fulfillment choices affect customer satisfaction and profitability?

### Student output
Students design a channel plan for a target segment, comparing at least two channel options by reach, cost, control, customer fit, and operational risk.

## Chapter 8: Promotion, Analytics, and Responsible Growth
### Key concepts
- Integrated marketing communications and message consistency
- Promotion mix: advertising, public relations, sales promotion, personal selling, direct marketing, content, social, search, email, events, and partnerships
- Creative briefs, media planning, funnels, customer acquisition, retention, and loyalty
- Marketing analytics: impressions, reach, frequency, click-through rate, conversion rate, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value, return on ad spend, attribution, and cohort analysis
- Testing and optimization, including A/B tests and campaign dashboards
- Ethics in data privacy, consent, representation, truth in advertising, influencer disclosure, and algorithmic bias

### Practice questions
- Which promotion tools best match awareness, consideration, conversion, and retention goals?
- How should a team decide whether a campaign was profitable?
- What data should marketers avoid collecting, even if it might improve targeting?

### Student output
Students create an integrated campaign brief with target audience, message, channel mix, budget logic, success metrics, testing plan, and ethical safeguards.

## Capstone
Students develop a practical marketing plan for a product, service, nonprofit, campus initiative, or small business. The plan includes customer research, segmentation, target selection, positioning, product or service recommendations, pricing logic, channel strategy, promotional campaign, analytics dashboard, and ethics review. The final presentation should defend the recommendation with evidence and explain tradeoffs across customer value, business viability, operational feasibility, and social responsibility.

## Assessment Ideas
- Research design memo and customer interview protocol
- Segmentation and positioning worksheet
- Product brief and brand critique
- Pricing case with break-even or contribution analysis
- Channel strategy comparison
- Campaign brief with analytics plan
- Short quizzes on key vocabulary and decision frameworks
- Final marketing plan presentation and written rationale

## Research Notes
- OpenStax, Principles of Marketing, for foundational coverage of customer value, research, segmentation, positioning, the marketing mix, and marketing ethics.
- OpenStax, Introduction to Business, for strategy, competition, entrepreneurship, and business function context.
- American Marketing Association definitions, ethics resources, and professional guidance for current terminology and responsible practice.
- Public campaign examples, brand websites, annual reports, and platform ad libraries for applied analysis of positioning, promotion, and metrics.
- Principles of Marketing course materials from university open courseware programs for supplemental cases and classroom activities.
