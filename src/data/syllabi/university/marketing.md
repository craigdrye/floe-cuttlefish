# Marketing Principles
**ID**: `marketing` | **Discipline**: Business | **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
Marketing Principles treats marketing as a disciplined business function rather than a synonym for advertising. The central claim of the course is the one the American Marketing Association makes in its official definition: marketing is the activity, set of institutions, and processes for creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, and society at large. In plainer terms, marketing creates and captures value, and every defensible marketing decision begins with evidence about a real customer and a real market. The phrase "society at large" is not decoration: it is why ethics, fairness, and trust are course-long constraints rather than an appendix. Students learn to move in a connected chain from market evidence, to segmentation, targeting, and positioning, to the marketing mix (product, price, place, promotion), to measurement and iteration. The course insists that the 4 Ps are downstream consequences of a positioning argument, not a menu of disconnected tactics.

The course is analytical and decision-oriented. Students practice the small number of frameworks that working marketers actually use under deadline pressure: situation analysis, the marketing research process, the consumer decision journey, segment attractiveness screening, perceptual mapping, value-based pricing with break-even and contribution logic, channel comparison, and funnel and unit-economics analysis (CAC, LTV, ROAS, retention). Throughout, students are asked to justify recommendations on three axes at once: customer value (does it solve a real job?), business viability (does the math work?), and operational feasibility (can the firm actually deliver it?).

Ethics is a structural constraint rather than a closing lecture. Privacy, consent, truthful claims, accessibility, inclusive representation, fair pricing, influencer disclosure, dark patterns, and algorithmic bias recur as design limits inside every topic. By the end of the course, a student should be able to take an ambiguous brief, gather and interpret customer evidence, define an attractive segment, construct and defend a positioning statement, make coherent mix decisions, build a simple measurement plan, and explain the tradeoffs and the trust implications of the recommendation.

## Course Design Notes
- Anchor every chapter in concrete, current cases drawn from consumer brands, B2B firms, nonprofits, public-sector campaigns, and small local businesses so students see how context changes the right answer.
- Keep the marketing mix tethered to research and positioning. A pricing or channel decision that cannot be traced back to a target segment and a positioning claim should be treated as an unsupported assertion.
- Build repeated, cumulative reps with the load-bearing artifacts: situation analysis, research plan, journey map, persona, perceptual map, positioning statement, product brief, break-even calculation, channel comparison, and campaign brief with a measurement plan.
- Force the distinction between leading and lagging indicators, between correlation and causal lift (incrementality), and between vanity metrics and decision-relevant metrics whenever analytics appears.
- Treat ethics as a recurring constraint: in every chapter ask "what is the cost to customer trust, and who could be harmed?" before accepting a recommendation.
- Reward students for naming assumptions, quantifying claims, and acknowledging what their evidence does not prove.

## Chapter 1: Marketing, Value, and Strategy
### Core questions
- How is marketing broader than advertising or selling, and what does it mean to "create and capture value"?
- What is the difference between a market orientation and a product orientation, and why does it change strategy?
- Which forces in the macro- and microenvironment most shape a chosen firm's opportunities and risks right now?

### Key concepts
- The exchange model of value: creating, communicating, delivering, and capturing value; needs vs wants vs demand
- Marketing concept and market orientation vs product, production, and selling orientations
- Strategic fit: mission, objectives, competitive advantage, and portfolio thinking (e.g., BCG growth-share matrix: stars, cash cows, question marks, dogs)
- SWOT (internal strengths/weaknesses vs external opportunities/threats) and PESTLE macro scanning (political, economic, social, technological, legal, environmental)
- The microenvironment (customers, competitors, suppliers, intermediaries, publics) vs the macroenvironment
- Customer value, customer lifetime relationship, and the firm's ethical responsibilities to customers and society

### Applied skills
- Write a one-page situation analysis: customer value created, key competitors, a SWOT, and one PESTLE trend that matters.
- Classify a firm's units on a BCG matrix and recommend invest/hold/harvest/divest with reasons.
- Distinguish internal factors (SWOT strengths/weaknesses) from external factors (opportunities/threats, PESTLE) without misfiling them.

### Common traps
- Equating marketing with promotion; forgetting that product, price, and place are marketing decisions too.
- Putting external trends (regulation, demographics, competitors) inside "strengths/weaknesses," which are internal-only.
- Listing SWOT items with no link to a decision, turning analysis into a content-free checklist.
- Confusing the marketing concept (start with customer needs) with the selling concept (push existing inventory).

## Chapter 2: Customer Research and Market Insight
### Core questions
- What decision are we trying to make, and what research question must be answered before we act?
- When is qualitative research the right tool, and when does a survey or experiment serve better?
- How do sampling, wording, and self-report limitations distort a finding, and how do we guard against it?

### Key concepts
- The marketing research process: define the problem, design the research, collect data, analyze, and turn findings into action
- Primary research (new data for this question) vs secondary research (existing data collected for another purpose)
- Qualitative methods (interviews, focus groups, observation, diary studies) vs quantitative methods (surveys, experiments, panels, behavioral/clickstream data)
- Sampling and representativeness, sampling vs non-sampling error, validity, reliability, and the gap between stated and revealed preference
- Question design: neutral vs leading wording, double-barreled questions, and acquiescence bias
- Customer journey mapping, jobs-to-be-done, pain points, and writing a sharp insight statement

### Applied skills
- Draft a focused research plan that names the decision, the research question, the method, and why that method fits.
- Write five neutral customer-interview questions and rewrite a leading question into a neutral one.
- Convert raw findings into two falsifiable, action-oriented insight statements (not restatements of the data).

### Common traps
- Choosing a method before defining the decision, then collecting data nobody can act on.
- Treating a focus group or a small qualitative study as a precise estimate of market size or share.
- Trusting stated intent ("yes, I'd buy it") as if it were revealed behavior.
- A biased sample (e.g., surveying only existing happy customers) that quietly guarantees the answer you hoped for.

## Chapter 3: Consumer and Organizational Buying Behavior
### Core questions
- Why do two customers respond differently to the same offer?
- How does a B2B purchase differ from a consumer purchase in who decides and what they weigh?
- Where is the line between helpful personalization and manipulation?

### Key concepts
- Consumer decision process: need recognition, information search, evaluation of alternatives, purchase, post-purchase behavior
- The consideration/evoked set and how brands enter or fall out of it
- Psychological influences: motivation, perception, learning, attitudes, and decision heuristics; high- vs low-involvement decisions
- Social and cultural influences: reference groups, social proof, culture, identity, and household roles
- B2B buying: the buying center (users, influencers, deciders, buyers, gatekeepers), derived demand, and longer relationship-driven sales
- Trust, perceived risk, satisfaction, loyalty, and post-purchase cognitive dissonance (buyer's remorse)

### Applied skills
- Map a real customer's decision journey and attach one marketing action to each stage.
- Identify the buying-center roles in a B2B purchase scenario and tailor a message to each role.
- Diagnose whether a decision is high- or low-involvement and adjust the recommended marketing emphasis.

### Common traps
- Treating all buyers as rational utility maximizers and ignoring heuristics, emotion, and social proof.
- Assuming the B2B buyer and the B2B user are the same person.
- Confusing post-purchase dissonance (psychological doubt) with product failure or with simple forgetting.
- Using a behavioral nudge that exploits a vulnerable customer and calling it "personalization."

## Chapter 4: Segmentation, Targeting, and Positioning
### Core questions
- Which segmentation variable best explains demand for a specific product, and why that one?
- What makes a segment attractive yet hard to serve, and how do you decide whether to chase it?
- How can a perceptual map reveal an open positioning opportunity?

### Key concepts
- Segmentation bases: geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioral, needs-based, and firmographic (B2B)
- Criteria for a usable segment: measurable, accessible/reachable, substantial, differentiable, and actionable
- Targeting strategies: undifferentiated/mass, differentiated, concentrated/niche, and micromarketing
- Personas grounded in research, buyer roles, and use cases
- Positioning: points of parity and points of difference, perceptual mapping, and brand associations
- The positioning statement and value proposition design

### Applied skills
- Choose and defend the single most explanatory segmentation base for a given product.
- Screen two or three segments against attractiveness criteria and pick a priority segment with a rationale.
- Build a research-grounded persona and write a positioning statement supported by a perceptual map (with two meaningful, customer-relevant axes).

### Common traps
- Segmenting on demographics by default when the real driver is behavior or need.
- Confusing the four STP concepts: segmentation (cut the market), targeting (choose segments), positioning (occupy a place in the mind).
- Building a persona from a stock photo and a stereotype instead of evidence.
- Choosing perceptual-map axes (e.g., logo color) that customers do not actually use to choose.

## Chapter 5: Product, Brand, and Customer Experience
### Core questions
- What is the core benefit a product delivers, beyond its physical features?
- How should marketing strategy shift across the stages of the product life cycle?
- How do packaging, onboarding, and service quality move brand perception?

### Key concepts
- The three product levels: core benefit, actual product, augmented product; the role of the total customer experience
- The goods-services spectrum, experiences, platforms, and product-service bundles; service intangibility and inseparability
- Product life cycle (introduction, growth, maturity, decline) and the diffusion of innovation adopter categories (innovators, early adopters, early/late majority, laggards)
- Branding: brand equity, brand identity vs image, brand associations, brand architecture, line vs brand extensions, naming, and packaging
- New product development: idea, concept testing, development, test market, launch, and structured learning from failure
- Product-market fit, service quality (e.g., SERVQUAL gaps), accessibility, and inclusive design

### Applied skills
- Separate a product's core benefit from its actual and augmented layers for a real offer.
- Recommend how price, promotion, and distribution emphasis should change as a product moves from growth to maturity.
- Write a product brief: target customer, core benefit, feature set, brand promise, and the top launch risks.

### Common traps
- Describing features (what it is) when asked for benefits (what the customer gets).
- Treating the product life cycle as a fixed timeline rather than a pattern that strategy can extend or accelerate.
- Confusing a line extension (new variant in the same line) with a brand extension or with diversification.
- Equating brand awareness with brand preference or purchase; recognition is necessary but not sufficient.

## Chapter 6: Pricing and Revenue Strategy
### Core questions
- When is value-based pricing more appropriate than cost-plus, and what evidence supports it?
- How does price signal quality, and when does a low price destroy perceived value?
- What pricing practice can be legal yet still corrode customer trust?

### Key concepts
- Pricing objectives: profit, revenue, market share, survival, value capture, and positioning signal
- The three pricing logics: cost-based (cost-plus, markup), competition-based, and value-based (anchored to willingness to pay)
- Price elasticity of demand: how strongly quantity demanded responds to a price change; elastic vs inelastic and what drives each
- Reference prices, perceived fairness, charm/psychological pricing, and the decoy effect (asymmetric dominance)
- Strategy levers: penetration (low launch price for share) vs skimming (high launch price, lowered over time), bundling, subscription, freemium, dynamic pricing, and promotional pricing
- Break-even and contribution margin: break-even units = fixed costs / (price − variable cost per unit); contribution margin and its link to lifetime value

### Applied skills
- Recommend a pricing logic for a new offer and justify it from customer value and competitive context.
- Compute a break-even point and a contribution margin per unit and interpret what they imply for volume targets.
- Distinguish penetration from skimming in a launch scenario and explain which fits the product and market.

### Common traps
- Defaulting to cost-plus and ignoring what the customer is actually willing to pay.
- Mislabeling penetration and skimming (low-then-rising vs high-then-falling).
- Treating "high price = high quality" as automatic; perceived value still has to be earned.
- Hidden fees, drip pricing, fake scarcity, or personalized price discrimination that is legal but trust-destroying.

## Chapter 7: Place, Channels, and Distribution
### Core questions
- Why might a premium brand deliberately choose selective or exclusive distribution?
- What channel conflicts arise when a firm sells direct online and through retail partners?
- How do fulfillment and availability choices affect both satisfaction and profitability?

### Key concepts
- Marketing channels and intermediaries; direct vs indirect distribution and the value intermediaries add (sorting, bulk-breaking, reach)
- Distribution intensity: intensive, selective, and exclusive, and when each fits
- Channel types: retail, ecommerce, marketplaces, wholesalers, resellers, agents, and platform channels
- Omnichannel coordination, the unified customer experience, and channel conflict (horizontal and vertical)
- Supply-chain and fulfillment basics: service levels, inventory availability, lead time, and last-mile considerations
- Channel economics and metrics: conversion, sell-through, availability/in-stock rate, channel margin, and return rate

### Applied skills
- Match distribution intensity to a brand's positioning and explain the tradeoff.
- Design a channel plan for one target segment comparing at least two options on reach, cost, control, customer fit, and operational risk.
- Anticipate a channel conflict between direct and partner channels and propose a mitigation (pricing, assortment, or role separation).

### Common traps
- Maximizing reach by going intensive when the brand's positioning demands scarcity and control.
- Ignoring channel partners' incentives, then being surprised when a direct channel triggers conflict.
- Treating "omnichannel" as "be on every channel" rather than coordinating a consistent experience.
- Optimizing one channel's local metric while degrading total margin, availability, or returns.

## Chapter 8: Integrated Communications and Campaign Design
### Core questions
- Which promotion tools best serve awareness, consideration, conversion, and retention goals respectively?
- How do you write a creative brief that keeps message, audience, and objective aligned?
- How do you keep a campaign honest: truthful claims, disclosed sponsorships, and consent-based targeting?

### Key concepts
- Integrated marketing communications (IMC) and message consistency across touchpoints
- The promotion mix: advertising (paid), public relations (earned), sales promotion (short-term incentives), personal selling, direct/email, content, search, social, events, and partnerships/influencers
- Paid, owned, and earned media; the creative brief; media planning (reach, frequency, GRPs)
- The funnel and AIDA-style stages; matching tools and messages to acquisition, conversion, and retention goals
- Message fit: matching language and proof to the target segment's decision criteria
- Communication ethics: truth in advertising, substantiated claims, and influencer/sponsorship disclosure; under the FTC's updated Endorsement Guides a material connection must be disclosed "clearly and conspicuously" (in a video that means inside the video, not buried in a caption), and fabricated or suppressed reviews are themselves deceptive

### Applied skills
- Map promotion tools to funnel stages and justify the mix for a stated objective and budget.
- Write a creative brief: audience, single-minded message, objective, channels, and success metric.
- Audit a sample ad or influencer post for disclosure, claim substantiation, and representation.

### Common traps
- Spending on conversion tactics when the real gap is awareness (or vice versa).
- Treating PR (earned, lower control) as if it were advertising (paid, full control).
- Inconsistent messaging across channels that fragments the positioning.
- Undisclosed sponsorship, a disclosure too faint or too late to be "clear and conspicuous," unsubstantiated claims, or a slick funnel that hides costs from the buyer.

## Chapter 9: Marketing Analytics and Responsible Growth
### Core questions
- How does a team decide whether a campaign was actually profitable?
- Which metrics drive decisions, and which are vanity metrics that only look impressive?
- What data should marketers refuse to collect, even if it would sharpen targeting?

### Key concepts
- Funnel and exposure metrics: impressions, reach, frequency, click-through rate (CTR), conversion rate (conversions / eligible users)
- Unit economics: customer acquisition cost (CAC = acquisition spend / new customers), customer lifetime value (LTV), and the LTV:CAC ratio (a common health benchmark is roughly 3:1) and CAC payback period
- Return on ad spend (ROAS = revenue attributable to ads / ad spend) and the difference between ROAS and true profitability
- Attribution models (last-click, first-click, multi-touch) and their limits; incrementality and causal lift vs correlation
- Cohort analysis, retention vs churn, and leading vs lagging indicators
- A/B testing: control vs variant, a single defined success metric, sample size, statistical significance, and avoiding peeking
- Data ethics: privacy, consent, data minimization, sensitive-data restrictions, and algorithmic bias in targeting; rising constraints from privacy regimes (e.g., GDPR-style consent, state-level US privacy laws) and the industry shift away from third-party cookies toward first-party and consented data

### Applied skills
- Compute and interpret CAC, LTV, the LTV:CAC ratio, and ROAS for a scenario, and judge whether growth is sustainable.
- Critique an attribution claim and design a simple incrementality test (e.g., holdout group) to check it.
- Distinguish a vanity metric from a decision-relevant one and propose the better metric.

### Common traps
- Reading high ROAS as proof of profit while ignoring margins and incrementality.
- Crediting all conversions to the last click and overrating the bottom-of-funnel channel.
- Confusing correlation (sales rose during the campaign) with causal lift (sales rose because of it).
- Collecting sensitive or excessive data because it is technically possible, ignoring consent and minimization.

## Capstone
Students develop a complete, defensible marketing plan for a product, service, nonprofit, campus initiative, or small business of their choice. The plan must trace one connected argument from evidence to action: (1) a situation analysis with SWOT and one decisive macro trend; (2) primary or secondary customer research and two insight statements; (3) segmentation, a screened target segment, and a research-grounded persona; (4) a positioning statement supported by a perceptual map; (5) product or service recommendations tied to the core benefit; (6) a pricing recommendation with a break-even or contribution calculation and a stated trust implication; (7) a channel strategy comparing at least two options; (8) an integrated campaign brief mapped to funnel stages; and (9) a measurement plan with named metrics (CAC, LTV, conversion, retention) and at least one A/B or incrementality test.

The deliverable is a written plan plus a presentation that defends the recommendation explicitly on three axes: customer value, business viability, and operational feasibility. Students must surface their key assumptions, quantify their central claims, and include a short ethics review naming who could be harmed and how the plan protects customer trust (privacy, truthful claims, fair pricing, accessibility, disclosure). Strong capstones are internally consistent: the price, channel, message, and metrics all follow from the same positioning and the same target customer.

## Assessment Ideas
- Situation analysis memo with SWOT and one PESTLE trend tied to a decision
- Research design memo and a neutral customer-interview protocol
- Segmentation, targeting, and positioning worksheet with a perceptual map
- Product brief and a brand critique
- Pricing case requiring a break-even and contribution-margin calculation
- Channel strategy comparison scored on reach, cost, control, fit, and risk
- Integrated campaign brief with a funnel-mapped measurement plan
- Analytics problem set: compute CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC, ROAS, and interpret an attribution/incrementality scenario
- Short quizzes on vocabulary and decision frameworks
- Final marketing plan and presentation with written rationale and ethics review

## Research Notes
- OpenStax, *Principles of Marketing*, for customer value, the research process, consumer behavior, STP, the marketing mix, and ethics.
- OpenStax, *Introduction to Business*, for strategy, competition, and business-function context.
- Kotler & Armstrong, *Principles of Marketing*, and Kotler & Keller, *Marketing Management*, for STP, the 4 Ps, brand equity, and the diffusion of innovation.
- American Marketing Association definition of marketing and AMA Statement of Ethics for current terminology and responsible practice.
- US Federal Trade Commission guidance on truth in advertising and endorsement/influencer disclosure for the communications-ethics material.
- Platform ad libraries, brand sites, and annual reports for applied analysis of positioning, promotion, and metrics.
