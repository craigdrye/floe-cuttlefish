# Neuroscience
**ID**: `neuroscience` | **Discipline**: Science | **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
This course builds a rigorous, mechanistic, and systems-level understanding of how nervous systems generate sensation, movement, cognition, emotion, and behavior. The unifying intellectual move is to connect levels of analysis: molecules to membranes, membranes to cells, cells to circuits, circuits to systems, and systems to behavior and clinical phenotype. Students should finish able to start from a behavior, symptom, or experimental result and reason downward to a plausible mechanism, and to start from a molecular fact and reason upward to its functional consequence.

A second, equally important aim is evidentiary discipline. Neuroscience is awash in correlational data, vivid imaging, and overreaching headlines, so the course trains students to distinguish what an experiment actually demonstrates from what it suggests. Throughout, students separate observation from inference, correlation from causation, and necessity from sufficiency, and they learn which methods can support which kinds of claims. A neuron that lights up on an fMRI is not the same as a neuron shown to be required for a behavior.

By the end, students should be conversant in the field's quantitative backbone (equilibrium potentials, summation, signal-to-noise in imaging) while remaining honest about its limits. The course is interdisciplinary but biological at its core: mechanisms, data, and causal evidence anchor every topic, and clinical disorders serve as natural experiments that reveal how healthy circuits work.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test neuroanatomy, cellular and molecular neuroscience, electrophysiology, synaptic transmission, sensory and motor systems, plasticity and development, cognition and emotion, methods, neuropharmacology, neurological and psychiatric disease, or evidence-based interpretation at an upper-division undergraduate level. Items should reward causal and mechanistic reasoning over rote labeling: prefer "predict what happens if" and "what does this trace/result show" over "name this structure." Questions that demand integration across levels (e.g., a channel mutation that changes a behavior, or a lesion that explains a deficit) are the target difficulty.

Calibrate to a junior-year major who has had introductory biology and a semester of physiology or cell biology. Calculation items (Nernst, Goldman intuition, summation) should be tractable by hand or with simple log estimates. Method and disorder items should test interpretation and limits, not just definitions. Keep facts current and avoid debunked tropes (strict left/right "brain types," the 10%-of-the-brain myth, rigid one-gene/one-behavior claims, the discredited serotonin "chemical-imbalance" framing of depression as settled mechanism). When a question touches a contested claim, the correct answer should reflect the appropriately cautious scientific consensus.

## Chapter 1: Neural Cells, Neuroanatomy, and Organization
- **Core questions**: What are the cell types of the nervous system and what does each do? How is the brain organized from gross anatomy to functional systems, and how does localized damage produce predictable deficits?
- **Key concepts**: Neuron doctrine; neuronal polarity (dendrites, soma, axon hillock, axon, synaptic terminals); glia and their roles (astrocytes at the tripartite synapse, oligodendrocytes vs. Schwann cells for CNS/PNS myelin, microglia as resident immune cells); meninges, ventricles, CSF, and the blood-brain barrier; CNS vs. PNS and somatic vs. autonomic divisions; cortical lobes; thalamus, hypothalamus, basal ganglia, hippocampus, amygdala, cerebellum, brainstem.
- **Applied skills**: Localize a lesion from a symptom profile; map information flow through a named system; match glial cell type to a clinical consequence of its failure (e.g., demyelination by oligodendrocyte vs. Schwann cell loss).
- **Common traps**: Confusing oligodendrocytes (CNS, many axons each) with Schwann cells (PNS, one internode each); thinking glia are merely passive "glue"; assuming gross anatomical size maps onto functional importance; conflating the cerebrum with the whole brain.

## Chapter 2: Membranes, Ion Channels, and Electrical Signaling
- **Core questions**: Where does the resting potential come from, and how do neurons convert graded inputs into all-or-none action potentials that travel reliably down an axon?
- **Key concepts**: Ion gradients and selective permeability; the Na+/K+ pump (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in); equilibrium potential and the Nernst equation; the Goldman equation and why rest sits near E_K but not exactly at it; voltage-gated Na+ and K+ channels; threshold, the all-or-none spike, absolute and relative refractory periods (Na+ channel inactivation vs. K+-driven hyperpolarization); cable properties, length and time constants; myelination and saltatory conduction; patch-clamp logic.
- **Applied skills**: Estimate E_ion from concentrations using the 61 mV/log rule; predict membrane-potential changes from channel manipulations or ion shifts; explain conduction velocity from diameter and myelination; interpret a labeled voltage trace.
- **Common traps**: Believing the resting potential equals E_K exactly (leak Na+ and the pump shift it); attributing the absolute refractory period to the pump rather than to Na+-channel inactivation; assuming larger stimuli produce larger action potentials; forgetting that the action potential reverses sign (overshoots above 0 mV) rather than just rising to 0.

## Chapter 3: Synapses, Neurotransmitters, and Neuropharmacology
- **Core questions**: How does an electrical signal cross a synapse and become a graded postsynaptic response, and how do drugs and toxins exploit each step?
- **Key concepts**: Chemical vs. electrical synapses; the release cascade (Ca2+ influx through voltage-gated channels, synaptotagmin as the Ca2+ sensor, SNARE-mediated vesicle fusion); ionotropic vs. metabotropic receptors and their speed/duration tradeoff; EPSPs and IPSPs; spatial and temporal summation; clearance by reuptake and enzymatic degradation; major transmitters (glutamate, GABA, acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine) and neuromodulation; agonists, antagonists, allosteric modulators, reuptake inhibitors.
- **Applied skills**: Predict the net effect of a receptor blocker, agonist, or reuptake inhibitor; classify a drug's mechanism from its effect; read summation from a dendritic-input diagram; explain why a transmitter can be excitatory at one receptor and inhibitory at another.
- **Common traps**: Calling a transmitter "excitatory" or "inhibitory" as if it were a property of the molecule rather than the receptor (e.g., ACh, dopamine); confusing reuptake with degradation; thinking metabotropic = slow-and-weak rather than slow-but-amplifying and long-lasting; assuming an agonist always increases firing (a GABA agonist inhibits).

## Chapter 4: Sensory Systems and Perception
- **Core questions**: How is physical stimulus energy transduced into a neural code, and how do receptive fields, tuning, and adaptation shape what we perceive?
- **Key concepts**: Transduction mechanisms across modalities; receptive fields, lateral inhibition, and contrast enhancement; rate vs. temporal vs. labeled-line coding; adaptation; somatosensation, nociception, proprioception; the retina, rods vs. cones, phototransduction (the dark current, cGMP, hyperpolarization to light), parallel visual pathways; cochlear hair cells and tonotopy; vestibular signaling; chemosensation (taste, smell); cortical maps and the disproportionate homunculus.
- **Applied skills**: Trace a pathway from receptor to cortex; explain how a stimulus feature becomes a firing pattern; interpret tuning curves and receptive-field data; predict the perceptual consequence of a transduction or pathway lesion.
- **Common traps**: Assuming vertebrate photoreceptors depolarize and fire to light (they hyperpolarize and do not fire action potentials); thinking the homunculus mirrors body size rather than receptor density and acuity; confusing place coding with rate coding; treating "more receptors firing" as the only way to encode intensity.

## Chapter 5: Motor Systems, Autonomic Control, and Homeostasis
- **Core questions**: How do the nervous system and body convert intention and homeostatic need into coordinated action, and how do motor structures divide the labor of movement?
- **Key concepts**: Lower vs. upper motor neurons and their distinct lesion signs; the neuromuscular junction; spinal reflexes and central pattern generators; the corticospinal tract; basal ganglia (direct "go" / indirect "no-go" pathways, disinhibition logic) vs. cerebellum (timing, calibration, error correction); motor learning; sympathetic vs. parasympathetic organization; hypothalamic regulation of feeding, temperature, and the stress (HPA) axis; sleep-wake control; neuroendocrine integration.
- **Applied skills**: Distinguish upper- from lower-motor-neuron signs; contrast basal-ganglia vs. cerebellar deficits (resting vs. intention tremor, bradykinesia vs. ataxia); explain autonomic shifts during stress or homeostatic challenge; reason through a disinhibition circuit.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the deficits of cerebellar damage (ataxia, intention tremor) with basal-ganglia disease (rigidity, bradykinesia, resting tremor); thinking the direct pathway excites the thalamus directly (it disinhibits via a double-negative); treating sympathetic activation as universally "bad" rather than adaptive mobilization.

## Chapter 6: Plasticity, Learning, Memory, and Development
- **Core questions**: How does experience change synapses and circuits, and how do those changes implement learning, memory, and the wiring of the developing brain?
- **Key concepts**: Hebbian learning; long-term potentiation and depression; the NMDA receptor as a coincidence detector (glutamate + depolarization to expel the Mg2+ block, Ca2+ entry) and AMPA-receptor trafficking as the expression mechanism; dendritic-spine remodeling; critical and sensitive periods; axon guidance (growth cones, netrins, semaphorins) and synaptic pruning; adult neurogenesis (and its contested extent in humans); memory taxonomy (declarative vs. nondeclarative, working memory) and systems consolidation; lessons from patient H.M.
- **Applied skills**: Explain coincidence detection at the molecular level; predict the effect of NMDA or AMPA receptor manipulation on LTP vs. baseline transmission; compare memory systems and the deficits that dissociate them; evaluate evidence for experience-dependent plasticity.
- **Common traps**: Saying NMDA receptors carry the bulk of the fast EPSP (AMPA do; NMDA gate plasticity); claiming "neurons that fire together wire together" without the timing/causality caveat (spike-timing dependence); treating critical periods as hard windows with no residual plasticity; overstating human adult neurogenesis as settled fact.

## Chapter 7: Cognition, Emotion, Language, and Social Neuroscience
- **Core questions**: How do distributed networks support attention, decision-making, emotion, and language, and where does localization help versus mislead?
- **Key concepts**: Attention and executive function; prefrontal cortex and cognitive control; reward circuitry and the dopamine reward-prediction-error account; emotion and fear conditioning through the amygdala; language networks (Broca's area and nonfluent production vs. Wernicke's area and impaired comprehension, the arcuate fasciculus); hemispheric specialization; theory of mind and social cognition; debates on consciousness; the limits of strict localization.
- **Applied skills**: Integrate behavioral and neural evidence rather than reading one off the other; distinguish aphasia profiles from a deficit description; critique an overreaching localization or imaging claim; reason about reward as prediction error rather than mere pleasure.
- **Common traps**: Reverse inference (concluding a mental process from which region "lit up"); equating dopamine with pleasure rather than with prediction error and motivation; mapping rich faculties onto single regions ("the morality center"); endorsing pop "left-brain/right-brain personality" claims.

## Chapter 8: Methods and Their Limits
- **Core questions**: What can each neuroscience method actually measure, at what spatial and temporal resolution, and which claims (correlational, necessary, sufficient) can it support?
- **Key concepts**: Lesion and inactivation studies (necessity); extracellular and single-unit recording; EEG and MEG (millisecond timing, coarse localization); fMRI BOLD as an indirect, slow hemodynamic proxy; PET tracers; DTI for white-matter tractography; calcium imaging; optogenetics and chemogenetics as causal, cell-type-specific manipulation (sufficiency and necessity); animal models and their validity; human-subjects ethics; the reproducibility crisis, multiple comparisons, and underpowered designs.
- **Applied skills**: Match a method to a research question by required resolution and causal status; read the limits of an imaging or animal-model result; explain why optogenetics can establish causation that fMRI cannot; spot a multiple-comparisons or reverse-inference problem.
- **Common traps**: Treating BOLD as a direct readout of neural firing or as millisecond-accurate; confusing the high temporal resolution of EEG with high spatial resolution; reading correlation (fMRI activation) as causation; assuming a result in one species or model transfers without validity argument.

## Chapter 9: Disorders and Translational Neuroscience
- **Core questions**: How do specific circuit and molecular failures produce neurological and psychiatric phenotypes, and how does mechanism inform (and limit) treatment?
- **Key concepts**: Alzheimer's disease (amyloid and tau, hippocampal/entorhinal vulnerability) and the limits of the amyloid hypothesis; Parkinson's disease (nigrostriatal dopamine loss, the rationale and limits of L-DOPA); epilepsy as excitation/inhibition imbalance; stroke (ischemia, the penumbra, excitotoxicity); multiple sclerosis (CNS demyelination); depression and addiction as circuit disorders (and why "chemical imbalance" is an oversimplification); neurodevelopmental conditions; the logic and ethics of clinical trials.
- **Applied skills**: Connect a disorder's symptoms to a circuit or molecular lesion; explain a therapy's mechanism and its predictable side effects; reason about why a mechanistically sensible drug can still fail in trials; critique a single-cause story for a complex disorder.
- **Common traps**: Locating Parkinson's memory-style cell loss in the hippocampus (it is nigrostriatal dopamine); treating the amyloid or serotonin hypotheses as proven sole causes; assuming demyelination kills neurons directly rather than slowing/blocking conduction; confusing symptom relief with disease modification.

## Capstone
Students produce a neuroscience research dossier on a chosen behavior, disorder, or neural system. The dossier must integrate every level of the course: a molecular or cellular mechanism, the relevant circuit anatomy, behavioral or clinical evidence, at least one experimental method with an explicit statement of what that method can and cannot show, and a cautious interpretation that separates established findings from open questions. Strong dossiers propose a falsifiable next experiment, name the control that would distinguish necessity from sufficiency, and identify at least one popular overclaim about their topic and explain why the evidence does not support it. The deliverable is assessed on mechanistic accuracy, cross-level integration, and evidentiary honesty rather than breadth of name-dropping.

## Assessment Ideas
- Diagram and pathway questions that require function and consequence, not just labels.
- Electrophysiology and synaptic problem sets (equilibrium potentials, summation, channel manipulations, trace interpretation).
- Data interpretation from imaging, lesion, behavioral, pharmacological, and recording studies, including stating each method's limits.
- Short critiques targeting controls, causal claims, reverse inference, and overinterpretation of imaging.
- A final dossier presentation that defends one mechanistic claim with evidence and names the experiment that would falsify it.
