# AP Calculus AB
**ID**: `apCalculusAB` · **Discipline**: Mathematics · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 17-19; AP exam taken in HS senior year, recurring as freshman Calculus I prep)
**Aligns with**: College Board AP Calculus AB Course and Exam Description (Units 1-8); equivalent to a one-semester university Calculus I

## Course Aim
Calculus is the mathematics of change, and AP Calculus AB asks one disciplined question over and over: *what is happening to a quantity at an instant, and how much accumulates over an interval?* Limits make the idea of "at an instant" precise, derivatives turn that idea into instantaneous rate of change, and integrals run the machine in reverse to measure accumulation. The aim of this course is not to memorize a list of rules but to build a single connected toolkit in which a slope, a rate, an area, and a net change are recognized as different faces of the same two operations. Students should finish able to look at a function and fluently ask: where is it defined, where is it smooth, where is it growing or bending, and what does its accumulated effect add up to.

The signature skill of AB calculus is moving between representations. The same function lives as a formula, a graph, a table of values, and a sentence about a real context with units, and a strong student translates among all four without losing the thread. A velocity graph is also a position-accumulation story; a table of temperatures is also a place to estimate a derivative; an FRQ stem about a draining tank is also a separable differential equation. This course trains that translation explicitly, because the AP exam and university Calculus I both reward students who can set up the right object and justify it, not just those who can grind an algebra step.

Finally, the course takes justification seriously. Calculus theorems — the Intermediate Value Theorem, the Mean Value Theorem, the Extreme Value Theorem, and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus — are not decoration; they are the licenses that let you assert that a value exists, that a guarantee holds, or that an antiderivative computes an area. Students should leave able to write the short, precise justification sentences that AP free-response rubrics demand and that distinguish a calculus argument from a lucky number.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test AP Calculus AB content: limits and continuity, the definition and rules of differentiation, composite/implicit/inverse differentiation, contextual applications (motion, related rates, linear approximation, L'Hôpital), analytical applications (extrema, concavity, optimization, MVT), integration and accumulation, separable differential equations and growth/decay, and applications of integration (net change, area, volume). Notation discipline (Leibniz vs. prime notation, dx, +C, definite vs. indefinite), units, and AP-style justification language are all in scope.

Keep the course rigorous but never mystical: every rule should connect back to a slope, a rate, an accumulation, an approximation, or a model. Calibrate to the current exam, which is roughly half multiple choice (45 questions, a no-calculator Part A and a calculator-required Part B) and half free response (6 questions split across calculator and no-calculator parts), and which is delivered in a hybrid digital format (multiple choice in Bluebook, free response on paper). Weight practice toward the units the exam weights most heavily — integration/accumulation, analytical applications of derivatives, and contextual applications — while never skipping the limits-and-continuity foundation that everything else stands on. Distinguish "set up the integral" from "evaluate it," because the AP rubric does. Prefer items that force interpretation with units over items that reward bare symbol-pushing.

## Chapter 1: Limits and Continuity
- **Core questions**: What does it mean for a function to approach a value, and how is that different from the value the function actually takes? When does a limit fail to exist, and what kind of discontinuity results?
- **Key concepts**: One-sided and two-sided limits; algebraic techniques (factoring, conjugates, common denominators); limits from graphs and tables; limits at infinity and end behavior; infinite limits and vertical asymptotes; horizontal asymptotes; the formal three-part definition of continuity; removable, jump, and infinite discontinuities; the Intermediate Value Theorem and the Squeeze Theorem.
- **Applied skills**: Evaluate limits from formulas, graphs, and tables; classify each discontinuity by type; locate and justify asymptotes from limit behavior; write an IVT justification sentence that explicitly checks continuity on a closed interval.
- **Common traps**: Assuming lim f(x) equals f(a) (it need not — that equality is exactly what continuity asserts); reporting "DNE" when only a one-sided limit exists; confusing a removable discontinuity (hole) with a vertical asymptote; invoking IVT without stating that the function is continuous on the closed interval.

## Chapter 2: Defining the Derivative and Basic Rules
- **Core questions**: How does the slope of a secant line become the slope of a tangent line, and why is that limit the right definition of instantaneous rate of change? When does a derivative fail to exist even though the function is continuous?
- **Key concepts**: The difference quotient and both limit definitions of the derivative; derivative at a point vs. derivative as a function; the tangent line and local linearity; differentiability implies continuity (but not the converse); corners, cusps, vertical tangents; the power, constant-multiple, sum, product, and quotient rules; derivatives of sin, cos, e^x, and ln x.
- **Applied skills**: Compute a derivative directly from the definition; match a function's graph to its derivative's graph and back; differentiate mixed expressions with the product and quotient rules; read a derivative value as a rate with correct units.
- **Common traps**: Treating the derivative of a product as the product of derivatives (it is not — use the product rule); dropping the chain-style care on the quotient rule's order (low·d-high minus high·d-low, all over low squared); assuming continuity guarantees differentiability; forgetting that the derivative of e^x is e^x while the derivative of a^x carries a ln a factor.

## Chapter 3: Composite, Implicit, and Inverse Differentiation
- **Core questions**: How does the chain rule let you differentiate functions built by composition, and how do you find a slope on a curve that is not solved for y? How is the derivative of an inverse function related to the derivative of the original?
- **Key concepts**: The chain rule and layered (multi-step) composition; implicit differentiation and dy/dx as a quantity that may depend on both x and y; derivatives of inverse functions via the reciprocal-slope relationship; derivatives of inverse trig functions (arcsin, arctan); introductory logarithmic differentiation; higher-order derivatives and their meaning.
- **Applied skills**: Differentiate deeply nested compositions cleanly, peeling one layer at a time; find dy/dx and tangent lines on implicit curves; use a table of values to compute (f⁻¹)'(a); compute and interpret a second derivative.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting the inner derivative when applying the chain rule (the single most common AB error); when differentiating implicitly, neglecting the dy/dx that the chain rule attaches to every y-term; evaluating (f⁻¹)'(a) at the wrong input — it uses the x where f equals a, not a itself; sign errors on inverse-trig derivatives.

## Chapter 4: Contextual Applications of Differentiation
- **Core questions**: How do position, velocity, acceleration, and speed relate as derivatives, and what do their signs tell you about motion? How do you connect the rates of two changing quantities that are linked by an equation?
- **Key concepts**: Rectilinear motion (position, velocity, acceleration, and speed as |velocity|); particle "speeding up vs. slowing down" via the signs of velocity and acceleration; related rates; local linearity, linear (tangent-line) approximation, and differentials; L'Hôpital's Rule for 0/0 and ∞/∞ indeterminate forms; rate interpretation with units.
- **Applied skills**: Analyze a moving particle and decide when it changes direction or speeds up; set up and solve a related-rates problem from a geometric relationship, differentiating with respect to time; approximate a function value with its tangent line and judge over/underestimate from concavity; apply L'Hôpital only after verifying an indeterminate form.
- **Common traps**: Equating "speeding up" with "positive acceleration" (the real test is whether velocity and acceleration share a sign); plugging in specific values *before* differentiating in related rates instead of after; using L'Hôpital on a limit that is not indeterminate (e.g., 3/0 or 2/5); confusing speed with velocity when reporting direction.

## Chapter 5: Analytical Applications of Differentiation
- **Core questions**: What do the first and second derivatives reveal about a function's shape, and how do you justify — not just locate — extrema and inflection points? What existence guarantees do the Mean Value and Extreme Value Theorems provide?
- **Key concepts**: Critical points; increasing/decreasing intervals from f'; the First and Second Derivative Tests; concavity and inflection points from f''; the Extreme Value Theorem (closed-interval/candidates method); the Mean Value Theorem and Rolle's Theorem; optimization; curve sketching; reasoning across f, f', and f'' graphs.
- **Applied skills**: Build and read a sign chart; justify a local max/min with a derivative-test sentence; run the candidates test for absolute extrema on a closed interval; translate an optimization word problem into a single-variable function and a constraint; check MVT hypotheses before asserting its conclusion.
- **Common traps**: Calling every critical point an extremum (a critical point can be neither, e.g., x=0 on y=x³); confusing where f' is increasing with where f itself is increasing; locating an inflection point where f''=0 without confirming a sign change in f''; stating MVT's conclusion without verifying continuity on [a,b] and differentiability on (a,b).

## Chapter 6: Integration and Accumulation of Change
- **Core questions**: How do Riemann sums turn the area-accumulation idea into a definite integral, and why does the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus tie antiderivatives to that accumulated area? What does an integral mean in context?
- **Key concepts**: Antiderivatives and indefinite integrals (with +C); Riemann sums (left, right, midpoint, trapezoidal) and over/underestimates; the definite integral as signed (net) area; properties of definite integrals; both parts of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus; u-substitution; the average value of a function; accumulation functions of the form g(x)=∫ₐˣ f(t)dt.
- **Applied skills**: Approximate a definite integral from tabular data and decide whether the estimate is high or low; evaluate definite and indefinite integrals, including by u-substitution; differentiate an accumulation function (FTC Part 1, with the chain rule when the upper limit is a function); interpret ∫ of a rate as a net change with units.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting +C on indefinite integrals; not changing the limits (or not back-substituting) when doing u-substitution; treating "area" and "net signed area" as identical when the function dips below the axis; mis-signing a left vs. right Riemann estimate for increasing/decreasing functions; ignoring the chain-rule factor when the FTC's upper limit is itself a function of x.

## Chapter 7: Differential Equations and Modeling
- **Core questions**: What does a slope field show about a differential equation's solution family, and how do separable equations and an initial condition pin down one particular solution? Why does exponential growth/decay arise whenever a rate is proportional to an amount?
- **Key concepts**: Slope fields and matching them to dy/dx; verifying a proposed solution by substitution; separable differential equations; initial conditions and particular solutions; exponential models y = y₀e^{kt} from dy/dt = ky; interpreting parameters and long-run behavior in context.
- **Applied skills**: Sketch or read a slope field and identify the matching equation; separate variables, integrate both sides (with +C), and apply the initial condition to solve for the constant; build and interpret an exponential growth/decay model from a proportionality statement.
- **Common traps**: Dropping the constant of integration and so producing the general family instead of the particular solution; applying the initial condition before integrating; mishandling the separation step algebraically (e.g., losing a variable that should move to one side); confusing linear and exponential change when a problem says "rate proportional to amount."

## Chapter 8: Applications of Integration and FRQ Studio
- **Core questions**: How do you choose and set up the right integral for net change, total distance, area between curves, and volume — and how do you communicate that solution to an AP rubric? When is setup worth credit even before evaluation?
- **Key concepts**: Net change vs. total distance (∫ of velocity vs. ∫ of |velocity|); average value applied in context; area between two curves; volume by disks and washers; volume by known cross sections; the distinction between *setting up* and *evaluating* an integral; calculator-active vs. analytic problems; FRQ communication (notation, units, justification, conclusion).
- **Applied skills**: Set up and evaluate area and volume integrals, choosing the correct integrand and limits; compute displacement and total distance from a velocity function; use the calculator to evaluate a definite integral and report a stored value, while showing the setup; write a complete FRQ response with correct notation, units, and a closing interpretive sentence.
- **Common traps**: Computing displacement when total distance was asked (forgetting the absolute value, or failing to split at sign changes); forgetting to square the radius — or to subtract squared radii — in disk/washer volume; mixing up which function is "top" in an area-between-curves integral; losing rubric points by giving a bare number with no units, no setup, or no justifying sentence.

## Capstone
Students assemble an **AP Calculus AB readiness portfolio** that proves mastery across the two core operations and all eight units. It contains: (1) one-page concept summaries for each unit, each including the unit's key theorem and a worked representative problem; (2) a corrected error log that sorts past mistakes into conceptual gaps, algebra slips, notation issues, and time-management problems, with a fix noted for each; (3) four timed, hand-graded free-response questions chosen to span one motion/derivative problem, one accumulation/FTC problem, one separable differential-equation problem, and one area-or-volume application — graded against an AP-style rubric that scores setup, notation, units, and conclusion; (4) one mixed multiple-choice reflection comparing a no-calculator and a calculator-active block; and (5) a short modeling write-up of a single real scenario (e.g., a draining tank or a cooling object) that uses *both* a derivative interpretation and an integral interpretation and states the result with units. The portfolio is the deliverable that demonstrates a student can set up, justify, compute, and interpret — the full calculus argument, not just the final number.

## Assessment Ideas
- Frequent representation-switching checks: graph to formula, table to derivative, context to integral.
- Short written justifications for IVT, MVT, the candidates test, concavity/inflection, and FTC.
- Timed calculator and no-calculator practice blocks that mirror the real exam split.
- FRQ rubrics that reward setup, notation, units, and conclusion — not just the final answer.
- Final mock-exam review sorted by concept gap, algebra slip, notation issue, or time-management problem.
