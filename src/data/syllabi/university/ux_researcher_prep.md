# UX Researcher Interview Prep
**ID**: `uxResearch` - **Discipline**: Design - **Year**: University Senior / Career Hopper

## Course Aim
Prepare students to interview for entry-level and early-career UX research roles with confidence, judgment, and a portfolio that shows how they think. The course builds practical fluency across research planning, recruiting, interviews, usability testing, surveys, synthesis, stakeholder collaboration, and case study storytelling, with repeated practice translating messy product questions into useful research.

## Course Design Notes
This is a career-prep studio, not a survey of every UX method. Route questions here when students need help with UXR interview preparation, research plans, discussion guides, usability study design, survey critique, synthesis, product collaboration, research storytelling, whiteboard prompts, take-home assignments, or portfolio case studies. Emphasize clear tradeoffs, ethical participant treatment, defensible method choices, and communication that helps product teams make better decisions.

## Chapter 1: The UX Research Role and Hiring Signal
- **Key concepts**: UX research responsibilities, generative and evaluative research, mixed methods, product development cycles, hiring rubrics, research maturity, impact language, collaboration with design, product, engineering, data science, and customer support.
- **Practice questions**: Explain what a UX researcher contributes beyond "talking to users"; identify the research signal in a job description; describe a project where research reduced product risk.
- **Student output**: A personal interview positioning brief with target roles, skill evidence, portfolio gaps, and a short "tell me about yourself" answer tailored to UXR.

## Chapter 2: Research Planning and Method Selection
- **Key concepts**: Problem framing, stakeholder intake, research questions, hypotheses, assumptions, decision points, method selection, constraints, timelines, sample strategy, screener design, risk, and success criteria.
- **Practice questions**: Choose a method when a product manager asks why users are not adopting a feature; turn a vague business concern into research questions; decide when a survey, interview, usability test, diary study, card sort, or tree test is appropriate.
- **Student output**: A one-page research plan with background, objective, research questions, method rationale, participant criteria, timeline, risks, and intended decisions.

## Chapter 3: Recruiting, Ethics, and Research Operations
- **Key concepts**: Participant criteria, screeners, sampling bias, incentives, consent, privacy, sensitive data, accessibility, note-taking roles, observer guidelines, research repositories, scheduling, tool setup, and lightweight ResearchOps.
- **Practice questions**: Write screener questions that avoid revealing the desired answer; spot ethical issues in a rushed study plan; explain how to protect participant data in a portfolio story.
- **Student output**: A recruiting and operations packet with screener, consent language, session checklist, observer note template, and data handling plan.

## Chapter 4: Interview Craft and Moderation
- **Key concepts**: Discussion guides, open-ended questions, neutral probes, active listening, the Five Whys, laddering, silence, rapport, bias control, follow-up questions, remote moderation, and handling participants who go off topic.
- **Practice questions**: Rewrite leading questions into neutral prompts; choose a follow-up that uncovers motivation rather than opinion; recover when a participant gives short answers or tries to please the interviewer.
- **Student output**: A discussion guide plus a recorded or role-played interview debrief that identifies strong probes, missed opportunities, and bias risks.

## Chapter 5: Usability Testing and Evaluative Research
- **Key concepts**: Task design, moderated and unmoderated testing, think-aloud protocol, success metrics, severity ratings, prototype fidelity, benchmark testing, accessibility checks, observation discipline, and distinguishing usability problems from preference.
- **Practice questions**: Design tasks for a checkout flow without telling the participant what to click; decide whether five participants are enough for a formative study; prioritize findings by user impact and product risk.
- **Student output**: A usability test plan with tasks, moderator script, success criteria, note grid, finding severity scale, and a concise findings readout.

## Chapter 6: Surveys and Quantitative UX Basics
- **Key concepts**: Survey purpose, question wording, response scales, sampling limits, statistical confidence, descriptive statistics, segmentation, benchmark metrics, NPS, SUS, CSAT, MaxDiff and conjoint awareness, and when quantitative evidence should not be overclaimed.
- **Practice questions**: Diagnose double-barreled, leading, or ambiguous survey items; choose between ranking, rating, and open-text questions; interpret survey results with a small or biased sample.
- **Student output**: A survey instrument with rationale for each section, a pilot-test revision log, and a short analysis plan for reporting results responsibly.

## Chapter 7: Synthesis, Insight, and Recommendation
- **Key concepts**: Qualitative coding, affinity mapping, thematic analysis, triangulation, insight quality, evidence strength, personas, journey maps, service blueprints, opportunity areas, prioritization, and separating observations from recommendations.
- **Practice questions**: Convert raw notes into themes; decide whether a pattern is strong enough to recommend action; write an insight that includes user behavior, evidence, and product implication.
- **Student output**: A synthesis board or memo with coded evidence, three to five insights, confidence levels, product recommendations, and open questions for further research.

## Chapter 8: Product Collaboration, Portfolios, and Interview Performance
- **Key concepts**: Stakeholder storytelling, research readouts, handling pushback, sample-size skepticism, roadmap influence, whiteboard research challenges, take-home assignments, behavioral questions, portfolio narrative, case study structure, confidentiality, and impact framing.
- **Practice questions**: Respond to "but this was only five users"; present negative findings to a stakeholder attached to a feature; outline a 30-minute research plan for a parking app, onboarding funnel, or enterprise dashboard.
- **Student output**: A polished UXR portfolio case study and a 15-minute presentation deck with context, problem, approach, findings, impact, limitations, and reflection.

## Capstone
Students complete a full interview-ready UX research case study from prompt to portfolio. The capstone includes stakeholder intake, research plan, screener, discussion guide or test script, at least one moderated research session or realistic simulation, synthesis artifact, findings readout, product recommendations, reflection on limitations, and a portfolio-ready case narrative. Students also complete a mock portfolio presentation and whiteboard research challenge.

## Assessment Ideas
- Research plan critiques focused on method fit, decision relevance, and scope control.
- Live interview or usability moderation scored for neutrality, probing, pacing, and participant care.
- Survey revision exercises that identify wording bias and analysis limits.
- Synthesis reviews that trace every insight back to evidence.
- Portfolio presentation rubric covering clarity, research judgment, collaboration, impact, limitations, and interview readiness.
