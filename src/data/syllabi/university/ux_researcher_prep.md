# UX Researcher Interview Prep
**ID**: `uxResearch` - **Discipline**: Design - **Year**: University Senior / Career Hopper

## Course Aim
This course prepares you to win an entry-level or early-career UX research role by learning to think like a researcher in front of a hiring panel. UX research interviews rarely reward people who can simply recite method definitions; they reward people who can take a vague product question, choose a defensible approach under real constraints, and explain how their findings should change a product decision. The course therefore trains judgment, not vocabulary: when to go generative versus evaluative, how to recruit the right participants without biasing the study, how to moderate without leading, and how to turn messy notes into insights a product team can act on.

You will build the artifacts that actually appear in interviews and portfolios: a research plan, a screener, a discussion guide, a usability test script, a synthesis board, a findings readout, and a case study. Each chapter pairs a craft skill with the way it shows up in hiring, so you practice the work and the storytelling together. By the end you should be able to walk into a whiteboard challenge, a take-home exercise, or a portfolio review and demonstrate the three things every panel is listening for: sound method choice, ethical and rigorous execution, and clear communication of impact.

The through-line is decision relevance. Good UX research is not "talking to users" or "running a test" — it is reducing the risk of a product decision with evidence, and being honest about how much confidence that evidence deserves. Everything in this course, from screener wording to sample-size pushback, comes back to that standard.

## Course Design Notes
This is a career-prep studio, not a survey of every UX method, and not an academic HCI course. Route questions here when learners need help with UXR interview preparation, research plans, discussion guides, usability study design, survey critique, qualitative synthesis, product collaboration, research storytelling, whiteboard prompts, take-home assignments, sample-size objections, or portfolio case studies. Prefer answers that surface tradeoffs over answers that name a single "right" method, because hiring panels probe reasoning, not recall. Emphasize defensible method selection, ethical and accessible participant treatment, separating observation from recommendation, and communication that helps product teams make better decisions. Where quantitative ideas appear (statistical significance, SUS, NPS, sample size), insist on honest scope: small qualitative samples are for discovering *why*, not for proving *how many*, and quantitative claims must not be overstated. Common traps in each chapter are written to mirror the mistakes interviewers actively listen for, so learners can rehearse avoiding them out loud.

## Chapter 1: The UX Research Role and the Hiring Signal
- **Core questions**: What does a UX researcher contribute beyond "talking to users"? What is the difference between generative and evaluative research, and where does each sit in the product cycle? What signal is a hiring rubric actually scoring?
- **Key concepts**: UXR responsibilities; generative vs. evaluative research; mixed methods; the double-diamond / product development cycle; research maturity in an organization; impact language ("research reduced the risk that…"); cross-functional partners (design, product, engineering, data science, support); the difference between a researcher and a research-doer.
- **Applied skills**: Read the research signal out of a real job description; tell a 90-second story about a project where research changed a decision; write a tailored "tell me about yourself" that leads with judgment, not tools.
- **Common traps**: Describing UXR as "asking users what they want"; confusing generative and evaluative work; listing methods you have touched without saying what decision each one informed; claiming impact you cannot trace to a product change.

## Chapter 2: Research Planning and Method Selection
- **Core questions**: How do you turn a vague business worry ("why aren't people adopting this?") into answerable research questions? Which method fits the question, the timeline, and the decision at stake — and why not the others?
- **Key concepts**: Problem framing; stakeholder intake; research questions vs. hypotheses vs. assumptions; decision points; method fit (interview, survey, usability test, diary study, card sort, tree test, analytics); constraints and timeboxing; sampling strategy; success criteria; the cost of the wrong method.
- **Applied skills**: Write a one-page research plan (background, objective, questions, method rationale, participants, timeline, risks, intended decisions); defend a method choice against a cheaper or faster alternative; scope a study down to fit two weeks.
- **Common traps**: Jumping to a method before naming the decision it serves; running a survey to answer a "why" question; conflating research questions with interview questions; promising statistical certainty from a qualitative plan; ignoring the constraint the PM actually cares about (time).

## Chapter 3: Recruiting, Ethics, and Research Operations
- **Core questions**: How do you recruit the right participants without telling them the answer you want? What are your obligations to participants, and how do those show up even in a portfolio?
- **Key concepts**: Participant criteria and screeners; sampling bias and self-selection; incentives; informed consent; privacy and sensitive data; accessibility and inclusive recruiting; note-taking and observer roles; research repositories; scheduling and tooling; lightweight ResearchOps.
- **Applied skills**: Write a short screener that disguises the target criteria using plausible distractor options; draft consent language in plain English; build a session checklist and an observer note template; write a data-handling plan that survives a portfolio review.
- **Common traps**: Screeners that telegraph the desired answer ("Do you love our app?"); over-long screeners that cause drop-off; recruiting only superfans or only convenient colleagues; skipping consent or anonymization; exposing identifiable participant data in a public case study.

## Chapter 4: Interview Craft and Moderation
- **Core questions**: What makes a question neutral rather than leading? How do you uncover motivation instead of opinion, and how do you recover when a participant gives you nothing — or tries to please you?
- **Key concepts**: Discussion guides; open-ended vs. closed questions; neutral probes; active listening and productive silence; the Five Whys and laddering; rapport; observer-expectancy and social-desirability bias; asking about past behavior over hypothetical future behavior; remote moderation; steering an off-topic participant.
- **Applied skills**: Rewrite leading questions into neutral prompts; choose a follow-up that surfaces the root motivation; use silence and "tell me more about that" to deepen an answer; debrief a recorded or role-played interview for strong probes, missed moments, and bias.
- **Common traps**: Leading or double-barreled questions; asking "would you pay for this?" and trusting the answer; pitching your design mid-interview; filling silence too fast; accepting the first surface answer instead of laddering to the why.

## Chapter 5: Usability Testing and Evaluative Research
- **Core questions**: How do you write tasks that test the design without revealing the answer? When are five participants enough, and when are they not? How do you separate a usability problem from a personal preference?
- **Key concepts**: Task design and realistic scenarios; moderated vs. unmoderated testing; the think-aloud protocol; success and error metrics; severity ratings; prototype fidelity; benchmark/summative testing; accessibility checks; observation discipline; Nielsen's finding that ~5 users in a qualitative formative study surface roughly 85% of usability problems (Nielsen, 2000) — and why that logic breaks for distinct user segments and for summative benchmarking.
- **Applied skills**: Design tasks for a checkout flow without telling the user what to click; decide whether five participants fit the study's goal; rate findings by user impact and product risk; write a concise readout that leads with the most severe issues.
- **Common traps**: Tasks that contain the answer ("click the blue Checkout button"); treating "5 users" as a universal law regardless of segments or summative goals; reporting preferences as usability defects; listing every issue flat instead of prioritizing by severity; the moderator helping the participant and contaminating the result.

## Chapter 6: Surveys and Quantitative UX Basics
- **Core questions**: When is a survey the right tool, and when does it create false confidence? How do you write items that measure what you think they measure, and how do you report results without overclaiming?
- **Key concepts**: Survey purpose and fit; question wording (double-barreled, leading, ambiguous items); response scales (Likert, ranking, rating, open text); sampling limits and self-selection; descriptive statistics and confidence intervals; segmentation; standardized metrics — SUS (population mean ≈ 68), NPS, CSAT; awareness of MaxDiff and conjoint for trade-off prioritization; the line between a finding and an overclaim.
- **Applied skills**: Diagnose and fix flawed survey items; choose between ranking, rating, and open text for a goal; pilot-test and keep a revision log; interpret results from a small or biased sample with appropriate hedging; explain what a SUS of 68 does and does not mean.
- **Common traps**: Treating SUS as "68% usable" rather than the historical 50th-percentile mean; trusting NPS as a usability measure; double-barreled or leading items; generalizing from a self-selected sample; using "statistically significant" loosely; ignoring effect size while celebrating a p-value.

## Chapter 7: Synthesis, Insight, and Recommendation
- **Core questions**: How do you turn raw notes into themes without forcing the data into your hypothesis? When is a pattern strong enough to recommend action, and how confident should you be?
- **Key concepts**: Qualitative coding (deductive vs. inductive); affinity mapping (bottom-up); thematic analysis; triangulation across sources; insight quality (behavior + evidence + product implication); evidence strength and saturation; personas and journey maps grounded in behavior; service blueprints; opportunity sizing; separating observation, interpretation, and recommendation.
- **Applied skills**: Code raw notes and cluster them into organic themes; write an insight that names the behavior, the evidence, and the product implication; assign a confidence level to each insight; produce a synthesis memo with 3–5 insights, confidence, recommendations, and open questions.
- **Common traps**: Cherry-picking quotes that fit the PM's hypothesis; grouping by demographic instead of behavior; presenting interpretation as observed fact; personas built on demographic fiction; recommending action from a single anecdote; hiding low confidence behind confident language.

## Chapter 8: Product Collaboration, Portfolios, and Interview Performance
- **Core questions**: How do you handle "that was only five users" or a stakeholder attached to a doomed feature? What turns a study into a portfolio case study and a whiteboard prompt into a strong answer?
- **Key concepts**: Stakeholder storytelling and readouts; handling pushback and sample-size skepticism; influencing the roadmap; whiteboard research challenges; take-home assignments; behavioral interview questions; case-study structure (context → problem → approach → findings → impact → limitations → reflection); confidentiality and how to present NDA'd work; impact framing.
- **Applied skills**: Answer the "only five users" objection by explaining saturation and the generative/evaluative goal; deliver negative findings to an attached stakeholder without alienating them; outline a 30-minute live research plan for a parking app, onboarding funnel, or enterprise dashboard; structure a portfolio case around decisions and impact.
- **Common traps**: Getting defensive or condescending about sample size; presenting findings without a "so what"; portfolios that linger on affinity maps instead of impact; blaming stakeholders for ignoring research; reading scripts aloud; over-sharing confidential details to seem impressive.

## Capstone
Complete one interview-ready UX research case study, from prompt to portfolio. Starting from a realistic product brief, run a full cycle: stakeholder intake, a one-page research plan, a screener, a discussion guide or usability test script, at least one moderated session (real or a realistic simulation), a synthesis artifact with coded evidence and confidence levels, a findings readout, prioritized product recommendations, and an honest reflection on limitations. Package it as a portfolio case study structured around decisions and impact (context → problem → approach → findings → impact → limitations → reflection). Then defend it in two interview simulations: a 15-minute portfolio presentation with live Q&A that includes at least one sample-size or "so what" challenge, and a timed whiteboard research challenge on an unseen prompt. You pass when an outside reviewer can trace every recommendation back to evidence, can see why each method was chosen over its alternatives, and would be willing to act on your readout.

## Assessment Ideas
- Research plan critiques scored on method fit, decision relevance, and scope control.
- Live interview or usability moderation scored for neutrality, probing depth, pacing, and participant care.
- Survey revision exercises that identify wording bias, scale misuse, and analysis limits.
- Synthesis reviews that trace every insight back to coded evidence and a stated confidence level.
- Portfolio presentation rubric covering clarity, research judgment, collaboration, impact, limitations, and composure under challenge.
