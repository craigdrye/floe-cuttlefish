# Art History
**ID**: `artHistory` | **Discipline**: Humanities | **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: a global Art History survey (e.g., ARTH 101 / Survey of World Art); textbook anchors include Gardner's *Art Through the Ages*, Stokstad & Cothren's *Art History*, and the Smarthistory open-access curriculum

## Course Aim
This course teaches students to look carefully, describe precisely, and interpret works of art in historical context. It treats art history not as a list of masterpieces to memorize but as a set of transferable analytical skills: visual analysis, chronological orientation, source evaluation, museum literacy, and evidence-based critique. Students encounter major works, makers, materials, and built environments from many regions, while also learning that survey categories, period names, and canons are scholarly constructions — useful, but provisional and open to revision.

The central discipline of the course is the move from *seeing* to *claiming*. A strong art historian can describe what is physically present in a work using shared formal vocabulary, then connect that evidence to function, patronage, technology, belief, and historical setting, and finally build an interpretive argument that another viewer could test against the object. We treat description and interpretation as distinct steps that must be kept honest: "the figure leans onto its right leg, dropping the left hip" is description; "the pose conveys relaxed naturalism characteristic of Classical Greek sculpture" is interpretation built on it.

By the end of the course, students should be able to analyze an unfamiliar work using formal vocabulary, situate it in a plausible cultural and historical setting, compare works across time and place without flattening their differences or ranking them on a single scale, evaluate the sources and labels that frame an object, and make an interpretive argument supported by visual and contextual evidence. They should also be able to recognize what they cannot yet know about an object and say so precisely.

## Course Design Notes
- Begin with looking before labeling. Students should describe form, material, scale, composition, and condition before reaching for meaning, judgment, attribution, or price.
- Balance chronology with theme. The chapters move broadly through time, but recurring questions about bodies, power, devotion, technology, exchange, patronage, and display keep the course from collapsing into a list of dates and styles.
- Keep the survey genuinely global. Avoid treating Europe as the default narrative and other traditions as side units. Foreground contact, trade, empire, translation, and local specificity, and resist applying one tradition's categories (e.g., "fine art" versus "craft," or the framed oil painting) as a universal yardstick.
- Use museums and archives as evidence, not just illustration. Labels, catalog entries, provenance records, conservation reports, installation choices, and digital collections are themselves sources students learn to read critically.
- Teach vocabulary as a tool, not a hurdle. Terms such as contrapposto, iconography, voussoir, tenebrism, and provenance are introduced so students can describe what they see — never as trivia for its own sake.
- Useful open resources include Khan Academy and Smarthistory, museum collection databases (the Met, British Museum, Louvre, Smithsonian, National Gallery of Art, Getty), conservation videos, UNESCO restitution materials, and LibreTexts art-history chapters. Students should treat all dates, titles, attributions, and cultural labels as evidence to verify, not facts to copy.

## Chapter 1: How to Look — Formal Analysis and Vocabulary
### Core questions
- What can you describe about a work before you know its title, maker, or date?
- How does medium shape what a work can and cannot do?
- What separates a description from an interpretation, and what evidence turns a personal reaction into a defensible claim?

### Key concepts
- The formal elements: line, shape, mass, volume, color, value, texture, composition, scale, proportion, balance, rhythm, movement, space, light, and viewpoint.
- Media and technique: painting, drawing, printmaking, photography, sculpture (carving versus modeling versus casting), ceramics, textiles, metalwork, architecture, performance, installation, video, and digital image-making.
- The interpretive layer: subject, style, iconography, function, patronage, audience, site, and ritual use.
- Description versus interpretation as a deliberate two-step method.
- Why scale, condition, and original setting change meaning, and why a photograph of a work is not the work.

### Applied skills
- Write a 300-word formal analysis of an unfamiliar work using specific visual vocabulary, ending with two careful interpretive possibilities rather than one premature conclusion.
- Distinguish, in your own sentences, which statements about a work are observable evidence and which are inferences.

### Common traps
- Jumping straight to "what it means" or "do I like it" before describing what is physically there.
- Confusing subject (what is depicted) with style (how it is depicted) — a battle scene can be painted in many styles.
- Treating a textbook reproduction as if it conveyed the true scale, color, surface, and viewing conditions of the original.
- Using vocabulary words as decoration ("the composition is very dynamic") without pointing to the visual feature that justifies the term.

## Chapter 2: Ancient Worlds and Early Visual Systems
### Core questions
- How do ancient images communicate authority when many lack written captions?
- What are the risks of interpreting ritual objects without surviving texts from the culture that made them?
- How do Egyptian, Greek, Chinese, and Mesoamerican approaches to the human body reveal different cultural priorities?

### Key concepts
- Prehistoric image-making and built environments: cave painting (e.g., Lascaux, Chauvet), portable figures, and megalithic sites, plus the limits of ritual interpretation.
- Mesopotamia, Egypt, the Aegean, Greece, and Rome: hierarchy of scale, registers, the Egyptian composite (twisted-perspective) figure, the Greek shift toward naturalism and contrapposto (e.g., the *Kritios Boy*, c. 480 BCE), the Classical ideal of proportion, and Roman veristic portraiture and concrete construction.
- South and East Asian early traditions: Indus seals, Ashokan pillars, the stupa as a Buddhist form, and early Chinese ritual bronzes and tomb objects (e.g., the terracotta army).
- Mesoamerican and Andean foundations: Olmec colossal heads, Maya, Teotihuacan, Moche, Nasca, and Inca monumentality, cosmology, and urban design.
- Chronology and period names as organizing tools that can also oversimplify cultural overlap and exchange.

### Applied skills
- Build a comparative object card for two ancient works from different regions, listing medium, approximate date range, function, context, and one specific formal comparison (not a value ranking).
- Read hierarchy of scale, frontality, and idealization as deliberate conventions rather than as technical "mistakes."

### Common traps
- Reading Egyptian frontality or hierarchy of scale as failed naturalism rather than as an intentional system for showing status and permanence.
- Assuming every ancient ritual object had the function it now appears to have in a glass case.
- Collapsing "Greek" and "Roman" art into one style; verism and idealization are different programs.
- Treating undated or unprovenanced prehistoric interpretation as settled fact.

## Chapter 3: Sacred Space, Image, and Devotion
### Core questions
- How does a sacred building choreograph a worshipper's movement and attention?
- Why might a tradition restrict figural imagery in one setting yet produce extraordinary visual culture in another?
- What changes when an object made for devotion is removed from ritual use and displayed in a museum?

### Key concepts
- Buddhist, Hindu, Jain, Christian, Islamic, Jewish, and Indigenous visual traditions as distinct systems of devotion, teaching, memory, and presence.
- The architecture of sacred space: basilica, mosque (with mihrab and minaret), Hindu temple and mandapa, stupa, shrine, monastery, pilgrimage route, courtyard, dome, and vault.
- Byzantine icons and mosaics, Islamic calligraphy and geometric and vegetal (arabesque) ornament, medieval illuminated manuscripts, Romanesque and Gothic churches, South Asian temples, and Buddhist cave sites (e.g., Ajanta, Dunhuang).
- Aniconism and iconoclasm, relics and reliquaries, liturgy, and the relationship between image and ritual presence.
- Material brilliance — gold ground, glass tesserae, pigment, ivory, enamel, textile — as part of a sensory and theological environment, not mere decoration.

### Applied skills
- Diagram a sacred space or devotional object, labeling how material, movement, light, and image work together to support its function.
- Explain a tradition's use of (or restriction on) figural imagery in terms of belief and function rather than as a lack of skill or imagination.

### Common traps
- Treating aniconism as "no art," when calligraphy, pattern, and architecture carry rich visual meaning.
- Reading a gold background as a literal sunset rather than as a symbolic, timeless, sacred field.
- Assuming a devotional object's museum context (lighting, isolation, label) reflects how it was originally seen and used.
- Confusing Romanesque (rounded arches, heavy walls) with Gothic (pointed arches, ribbed vaults, flying buttresses, expansive stained glass).

## Chapter 4: Courts, Cities, Trade, and Exchange (c. 1300–1700)
### Core questions
- How did patronage shape an artwork's subject, scale, and materials?
- What evidence shows exchange and influence between regions rather than isolated invention?
- How can students compare works across cultures without ranking them on a single European standard?

### Key concepts
- Renaissance and early modern Europe: linear perspective (orthogonals converging to a vanishing point), humanism, anatomy, oil paint, sfumato and chiaroscuro, workshop practice, print culture, and the patronage of churches, courts, and merchant families (e.g., the Medici).
- Islamic, Persian, and Mughal manuscript painting, Ottoman tilework (Iznik), Chinese literati painting, Japanese screens and woodblock prints, and Korean ceramics.
- African kingdoms and networks: Ife and Benin brass and bronze casting (lost-wax), Kongo and Ethiopian art, masquerade and regalia, and the difficulty of reading objects removed from performance contexts.
- Trade and exchange: the Silk Road, the Indian Ocean network, the Atlantic world, diplomatic gifts, collecting, colonial extraction, and hybrid visual forms.
- Cities and courts as engines of art: Florence, Venice, Istanbul, Isfahan, Beijing, Kyoto, Tenochtitlan, Cuzco, and Benin City.

### Applied skills
- Build a visual exchange map tracing one material, motif, technology, or object type across at least three places (e.g., blue-and-white porcelain, lost-wax casting, or paper).
- Use patronage and commission documents to explain why a work looks the way it does.

### Common traps
- Treating "the Renaissance" as a single European event rather than overlapping regional developments, and ignoring contemporaneous achievements in Asia, Africa, and the Americas.
- Calling perspective "more advanced" art rather than one culturally specific spatial convention among many.
- Reading hybrid colonial-era objects as either "pure" tradition or simple imitation rather than as evidence of contact and negotiation.
- Assuming an African court object was "anonymous folk craft" when it was a sophisticated, often signed or workshop-attributed, royal commission.

## Chapter 5: Modernity, Revolution, and the Public (c. 1700–1900)
### Core questions
- Why did some modern works provoke scandal at the salon or exhibition?
- How did photography (invented c. 1839) change the status of painting and of visual evidence?
- What makes a monument persuasive, contested, or vulnerable to later reinterpretation and removal?

### Key concepts
- Enlightenment, empire, revolution, nationalism, abolition, industrialization, urbanization, and the rise of the public exhibition and the modern museum.
- Movements and modes: Rococo, Neoclassicism, Romanticism, Realism, early photography, Impressionism (visible brushwork, changing light, modern life), Post-Impressionism, Symbolism, and Arts and Crafts.
- Art and politics: history painting, caricature, war imagery, monuments, propaganda, public memory, and counter-memory.
- Photography and mechanical reproduction: indexicality, truth claims, staging, circulation, archives, and colonial documentation.
- The modern viewer: salons, juries, the Salon des Refusés, academies, critics, newspapers, posters, and mass urban publics.

### Applied skills
- Write a short exhibition review of three works from the long nineteenth century, explaining how each engages modern public life, technology, or politics.
- Analyze a photograph's framing and caption to separate what it records from what it argues.

### Common traps
- Assuming photography simply "killed" painting, rather than redistributing its tasks and provoking new directions (Impressionism, abstraction).
- Treating a documentary photograph as neutral fact while ignoring framing, selection, and caption.
- Flattening the long nineteenth century into "Impressionism" and missing Realism, academic painting, and the politics that made works scandalous.
- Reading a monument as permanent and self-evident rather than as a contestable claim about who and what a public should honor.

## Chapter 6: Modernism, Avant-Gardes, and Global Responses (c. 1900–1945)
### Core questions
- Why did some artists reject naturalism, illusionism, and traditional craft hierarchies?
- How can a readymade be an artwork if the artist did not make it by hand?
- What ethical questions arise when modernists borrow or extract forms from colonized cultures?

### Key concepts
- European and US avant-gardes: Fauvism, Expressionism, Cubism (fragmentation and multiple viewpoints), Futurism, Constructivism, Suprematism, De Stijl, Dada (including Duchamp's readymade *Fountain*, 1917), Surrealism, and the Bauhaus.
- Modernism beyond the Euro-American center: Mexican muralism, the Harlem Renaissance, Japanese modern print culture, Indian and Latin American modernisms, African modernisms, and anticolonial visual culture.
- Abstraction, collage, the readymade, photomontage, performance, manifestos, design reform, and the broadening definition of "art."
- "Primitivism" and appropriation: distinguishing genuine influence and admiration from extraction, distortion, and erasure of source cultures.
- Art under war, displacement, revolution, censorship, and mass media (e.g., Picasso's *Guernica*, 1937).

### Applied skills
- Present a paired comparison between one canonical modernist work and one global or anticolonial response, focusing on form, context, and power rather than influence in one direction only.
- Explain a readymade or abstract work in terms of the idea, gesture, or system it foregrounds.

### Common traps
- Calling abstract or non-illusionistic work "easy" or "anyone could do that," ignoring its concept, context, and the conventions it breaks.
- Praising modernist "primitivism" without naming the colonial collections and power relations that made the source objects available.
- Assuming modernism happened only in Paris and New York and treating everywhere else as derivative.
- Treating a manifesto's claims as a neutral description of what the art actually achieved.

## Chapter 7: Contemporary Art, Visual Culture, and Technology (1945–present)
### Core questions
- How does an installation or performance change the viewer's role from spectator to participant?
- What happens to authorship and originality when images are sampled, reproduced, generated, or made collaboratively?
- How should critics evaluate very recent work when historical distance is limited?

### Key concepts
- Postwar and contemporary movements: Abstract Expressionism, Pop, Minimalism, Conceptual Art, performance, land art, feminist art, video and installation, postmodernism, identity-based practices, street art, social practice, and new media.
- The global contemporary system: biennials, residencies, diasporas, museums, art fairs, markets, state sponsorship, censorship, activism, and digital circulation.
- Technology and image culture: television, video, internet art, social media, NFTs, AI-generated imagery, surveillance, and machine vision — and the questions they raise about aura and reproduction (cf. Walter Benjamin).
- Critique and interpretation: authorship, originality, aura, audience participation, institutional critique, and the politics of display.
- Environmental art, memorials, archives, repair, and care, with art as public conversation.

### Applied skills
- Create a contemporary artwork dossier with images, artist statements or interviews where available, exhibition context, critical reception, and your own evidence-based critique.
- Apply Benjamin's questions about reproduction and aura to a born-digital or AI-generated work.

### Common traps
- Dismissing conceptual, performance, or new-media work as "not real art" because it lacks a traditional handmade object.
- Confusing market price or social-media virality with artistic significance.
- Assuming "contemporary global art" is neutral and borderless, ignoring the markets, states, and institutions that select and circulate it.
- Reaching a final verdict on very recent work without acknowledging the limits of immediate critical distance.

## Chapter 8: Museum, Source, and Critique Lab
### Core questions
- What does a museum label reveal, and what does it quietly leave out or assume?
- How can provenance change the meaning — and the ethical and legal status — of a work?
- What makes a visual argument convincing rather than merely confident?

### Key concepts
- Museum literacy: labels, wall texts, catalog entries, accession numbers, provenance, donor history, conservation status, display design, and assumptions about the audience.
- Primary versus secondary sources: artist writings, contracts, inventories, travel accounts, criticism, oral histories, collection records, technical reports, and scholarly essays.
- Research and display ethics: citation, image rights, cultural sensitivity, contested ownership, sacred materials, human remains, community consultation, and restitution. The Benin Bronzes — looted in the 1897 British punitive expedition and the subject of the 2022 Germany–Nigeria restitution agreement covering roughly 1,130 objects — are a live case study, including the dispute over whether returned works go to Nigeria's national museums or to the Oba of Benin.
- Writing art history: thesis, close looking, comparison, chronology, context, counterargument, and precise citation.
- Critique as disciplined judgment: moving beyond "I like it" toward claims about form, meaning, context, evidence, and stakes.

### Applied skills
- Revise an existing museum label or digital collection entry, adding missing context, clarifying the visual evidence, and noting one unresolved research question.
- Trace and evaluate the provenance of a contested object and articulate the ethical questions it raises.

### Common traps
- Treating a museum label as objective truth rather than as an authored, space-limited interpretation with its own point of view.
- Confusing attribution (who made it) with provenance (who has owned it and how it moved).
- Citing market value, attendance, or fame as if they were arguments about meaning or quality.
- Calling restitution "simple," ignoring genuine disputes over rightful custody, capacity, access, and the difference between ownership transfer and physical return.

## Capstone
Students complete a focused art-historical research project on one work, building, object group, or exhibition. The project must include: a rigorous formal analysis; accurate chronology; cultural and historical context; evaluation of at least three sources (with at least one primary source where available); a museum, collection, or provenance history where relevant; comparison to at least two other works that sharpens rather than decorates the argument; and a clear critical thesis. The project should explicitly note what remains uncertain — disputed attribution, lost original setting, incomplete provenance — rather than papering over gaps. Strong projects make the reader *see* the object more clearly and explain why its form, function, and history matter, and they hold description and interpretation honestly apart while connecting them with evidence.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly looking journal with sketches, vocabulary, and short descriptions.
- Image-identification quizzes that require visual evidence and reasoning, not memorized titles alone.
- Formal analysis paper on an unfamiliar work, graded on the description-to-interpretation move.
- Comparative object card or annotated timeline.
- Museum or digital-collection label critique and revision.
- Group presentation on exchange, patronage, or display.
- Final research project with an annotated bibliography and a revised thesis statement.

## Research Notes
- Khan Academy and Smarthistory are strong open anchors for introductory, object-focused, global survey material.
- Museum collection databases (the Met, British Museum, Louvre, Smithsonian, National Gallery of Art, Getty, and local university museums) provide images, catalog data, provenance notes, and bibliographies.
- LibreTexts art-history chapters support survey coverage and terminology; UNESCO materials are useful for restitution and heritage cases.
- Students should treat all dates, titles, attributions, and cultural labels as evidence to verify, and should note when an object's original setting, maker, title, or function is uncertain.
