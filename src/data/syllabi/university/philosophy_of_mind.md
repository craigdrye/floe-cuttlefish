# Philosophy of Mind
**ID**: `philJunior` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
Philosophy of mind asks what minds are and how they fit into the rest of nature. This course treats that question with the seriousness it deserves: not as a vague meditation on "the soul," but as a set of sharply defined problems about consciousness, mental representation, the self, agency, and machine intelligence. Students learn to state a thesis precisely (substance dualism, type identity, functionalism, illusionism), reconstruct the argument that supports it, and locate the exact premise an objection attacks. The recurring discipline of the course is keeping two kinds of question apart: empirical questions that neuroscience and cognitive science can settle by measurement, and conceptual questions about what would even count as an answer. Confusing the two is the single most common mistake in this field, and most famous debates turn on it.

By the end, a student should be able to take any thought experiment in the literature — Mary's Room, the Chinese Room, philosophical zombies, teletransportation, Otto's notebook, Frankfurt cases — and say exactly what conclusion it is meant to establish, which theory it threatens, and what a defender of that theory can say in reply. Students should also be able to read a result from cognitive science (predictive coding, the Libet readiness potential, blindsight, split-brain studies) and judge what philosophical conclusion it does and does not license. The aim is not to convert students to physicalism or dualism, but to make them fluent, charitable, and rigorous about the strongest version of every position.

The course is theory-first but empirically literate throughout. Where an experiment bears on an argument, we use it; where philosophers have over-read an experiment, we say so. Charity is a non-negotiable standard: a student who cannot state the best version of a view they reject has not yet understood it.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve dualism, physicalism, behaviorism, identity theory, functionalism, the computational theory of mind, the Chinese Room, the Turing test, consciousness, qualia, the hard problem, the knowledge argument, philosophical zombies, intentionality, mental representation, predictive processing, enactivism, personal identity, fission, teletransportation, the extended mind, free will and the neuroscience of decision, eliminativism, or AI consciousness and moral status.
Every question should reward careful argument reconstruction and the charitable, precise use of thought experiments. Prefer questions that test whether a student can identify which premise an objection targets, distinguish a conceptual claim from an empirical one, or spot when an experiment is being over-interpreted. Avoid pure vocabulary recall and avoid framing live debates (physicalism vs. dualism, compatibilism vs. incompatibilism) as if they had a settled "correct" winner; the correct answer should turn on what a position actually claims, not on which position is true. Do not route general ethics, epistemology, or philosophy of science here — those belong to their own tracks, except where they intersect mind directly (e.g., moral responsibility under determinism, or the status of folk psychology as a theory).

## Chapter 1: The Mind-Body Problem and Dualism
**Core questions**: What is the mind-body problem, and why is it a problem rather than a fact awaiting measurement? What does substance dualism claim, and how does it differ from property dualism? Can Descartes' conceivability argument survive the gap between "I can conceive of X without Y" and "X can exist without Y"?
**Key concepts**: Mental vs. physical states, subjectivity and objectivity, substance dualism (res cogitans / res extensa), property dualism, the conceivability argument, the divisibility argument, interactionism, the interaction problem, and the causal closure of the physical.
**Applied skills**: Reconstruct Descartes' argument from doubt and from divisibility as numbered premises. State the interaction problem as a dilemma (either the mental violates physical conservation laws, or it does no work). Distinguish substance dualism from property dualism by what each says is fundamental.
**Common traps**: Treating "I can doubt my body exists but not my mind" as proof they are different substances (conceivability is not separability). Confusing dualism with the claim that minds are merely complicated brains. Assuming the causal closure of the physical is obviously true rather than a substantive empirical-cum-metaphysical premise that dualists can deny.

## Chapter 2: Behaviorism and the Identity Theory
**Core questions**: Can mental states be defined entirely in terms of behavior and dispositions to behave? Why does the "Super-Spartan" who feels pain but never shows it sink logical behaviorism? Does the type identity theory ("pain = C-fibre firing") survive the multiple realizability objection?
**Key concepts**: Logical (analytical) behaviorism, behavioral disposition, Ryle's "category mistake" and "the ghost in the machine," the Super-Spartan / perfect actor objection, type identity vs. token identity, neural correlates, reduction, and multiple realizability.
**Applied skills**: Explain why a complete behavioral description leaves out the felt quality and the inner cause of pain. Distinguish type identity (every instance of a mental kind is the same physical kind) from token identity (each instance is some physical instance). Deploy multiple realizability — octopuses, hypothetical silicon minds — as an argument that mental kinds cannot be identical to specific physical kinds.
**Common traps**: Thinking multiple realizability refutes physicalism itself (it targets type identity, and motivates functionalism, which is still physicalist-friendly). Treating behaviorism as obviously silly rather than reconstructing why a careful philosopher held it. Confusing "pain typically causes pain behavior" (true) with "pain is pain behavior" (the behaviorist's contested claim).

## Chapter 3: Functionalism and the Computational Mind
**Core questions**: If mental states are defined by their causal role rather than their material, can anything with the right organization have a mind? What does the computational theory of mind add, and where does the Chinese Room claim it breaks down? Does passing the Turing test show understanding, or only the appearance of it?
**Key concepts**: Functional role, inputs/outputs/internal states, the Ramsey-Lewis recipe for defining mental terms, machine-state functionalism, the computational theory of mind, syntax vs. semantics, the Turing test, strong vs. weak AI, the Chinese Room, and the systems and robot replies.
**Applied skills**: Specify a mental state functionally (e.g., pain as "the state caused by tissue damage that causes wincing and the desire for it to stop"). Reconstruct Searle's Chinese Room as an argument that syntax is not sufficient for semantics. State the systems reply and Searle's rejoinder (internalize the whole room). Distinguish the claim that a machine could be intelligent from the claim that it could understand.
**Common traps**: Reading the Chinese Room as proof that machines can never think — Searle attacks symbol-manipulation as sufficient for understanding, not the possibility of artificial minds with the right causal powers. Treating the Turing test as a definition of intelligence rather than an operational, behavior-based criterion. Confusing simulation (modelling a hurricane) with duplication (making it rain).

## Chapter 4: Consciousness and the Hard Problem
**Core questions**: What is the difference between the "easy problems" of consciousness and the hard problem? What exactly does Mary learn when she leaves the black-and-white room, and does it refute physicalism? Are philosophical zombies conceivable, and does their conceivability show consciousness is non-physical?
**Key concepts**: Phenomenal consciousness vs. access consciousness (Block), qualia, the explanatory gap, Chalmers' hard problem, the knowledge argument (Mary's Room), the conceivability/zombie argument, the ability and acquaintance replies, illusionism, and panpsychism.
**Applied skills**: State the knowledge argument as: Mary knows all physical facts; Mary learns something on seeing red; so some facts are non-physical. Identify which premise each physicalist reply denies (the ability reply targets "learns a fact"; the phenomenal-concept strategy targets the inference from concepts to facts). Distinguish the hard problem (why is there experience at all?) from the easy problems (how does the brain discriminate, report, integrate?).
**Common traps**: Confusing access consciousness (information poised for reasoning and report) with phenomenal consciousness (what it is like). Treating zombie conceivability as automatically establishing zombie possibility (the same conceivability-to-possibility gap from Chapter 1). Assuming "we will explain it once neuroscience matures" already answers Chalmers, when his point is that the easy problems' methods seem structurally unable to reach the hard one.

## Chapter 5: Intentionality, Representation, and Embodied Cognition
**Core questions**: How can a mental state be *about* something — how does a thought reach out to the world? Are mental contents inner representations, or is cognition better understood as embodied, enactive, and world-involving? What does predictive processing imply about perception?
**Key concepts**: Intentionality and "aboutness," mental representation and content, the representational theory of mind, naturalizing content (causal and teleological theories), perception and the given, embodied and enactive cognition, predictive processing / the Bayesian brain, and the relation between phenomenology and cognitive science.
**Applied skills**: Explain intentionality using Brentano's "aboutness" and contrast representational with enactivist accounts. Use a perceptual illusion (the hollow-mask illusion, the checker-shadow illusion) to test whether perception is passive reception or active inference. Connect a cognitive-science finding to a philosophical thesis without overclaiming.
**Common traps**: Assuming "the brain represents the world" is a neutral observation rather than a contested theoretical commitment that enactivists reject. Treating predictive processing as having *solved* the hard problem when it addresses representation and inference, not phenomenal feel. Sliding from "cognition uses the body and environment" (mild and popular) to "the mind literally extends outside the skull" (the strong, separate thesis of Chapter 8).

## Chapter 6: Personal Identity and the Self
**Core questions**: What makes a person at one time the same person at a later time — the same body, the same psychology, or the same animal? Do fission and teletransportation cases show that identity is not what we really care about in survival? Is the self a persisting thing or a useful fiction?
**Key concepts**: Bodily/biological continuity, psychological continuity (memory, Locke), animalism, the bundle theory (Hume), Parfit's fission and teletransportation cases, the distinction between identity and survival, the narrative self, and the link between identity, responsibility, and prudential concern.
**Applied skills**: Run a fission case (one person, two equally good successors) and show why identity (a one-to-one relation) cannot hold while what we value in survival is preserved. Distinguish a numerical-identity question from a question about what matters. Apply each criterion to a hard case (advanced dementia, the teletransporter, a gradual neuron-by-neuron replacement) and predict its verdict.
**Common traps**: Treating teletransportation as a question of "would it really be me?" with an obvious yes/no answer, rather than as a probe of which criterion you implicitly hold. Assuming psychological continuity must be all-or-nothing when Parfit's point is that it comes in degrees. Conflating "survives" with "is strictly identical" — Parfit's central wedge.

## Chapter 7: Free Will, Agency, and the Neuroscience of Decision
**Core questions**: Is free will compatible with determinism? Does moral responsibility require the ability to do otherwise, or do Frankfurt cases show it does not? What, if anything, do the Libet experiments establish about whether our choices are free?
**Key concepts**: Determinism and indeterminism, hard determinism, libertarianism, compatibilism, the principle of alternative possibilities (PAP), Frankfurt-style cases, reasons-responsiveness, sourcehood vs. leeway accounts of control, and the Libet readiness-potential studies and their critiques.
**Applied skills**: Distinguish the metaphysical thesis of determinism from fatalism and from predictability. Reconstruct a Frankfurt case (a counterfactual intervener who never acts) as a counterexample to PAP. Evaluate what Libet's data show: that a readiness potential precedes reported awareness of intention — and identify the assumptions needed to leap from that to "free will is an illusion" (the "veto" window and recent meta-analytic doubts are the standard rejoinders).
**Common traps**: Equating compatibilism with libertarian free will, or assuming determinism obviously rules out responsibility. Reading Libet as a settled refutation of free will rather than a study of one narrow kind of arbitrary timed flick, with contested interpretation. Confusing "could have done otherwise" (leeway) with "the action issued from the agent in the right way" (sourcehood) — Frankfurt's whole point is to pry these apart.

## Chapter 8: Extended Mind, Eliminativism, and Future Minds
**Core questions**: Can the mind literally extend into notebooks, phones, and instruments? Is folk psychology a proto-scientific theory that could turn out false, as eliminativists claim? What would justify attributing consciousness or moral status to an AI or a non-human animal?
**Key concepts**: The extended mind thesis and the parity principle (Clark & Chalmers, Otto's notebook), active externalism vs. the coupling-constitution objection, eliminative materialism and the status of folk psychology, the gap between intelligence and sentience, criteria for moral status, and AI consciousness and animal minds.
**Applied skills**: Apply the parity principle to a candidate case (Otto's notebook, a smartphone, a calculator) and state the strongest objection (mere coupling is not constitution). Reconstruct the eliminativist claim that "beliefs" and "desires" are theoretical posits that could be eliminated, and the standard self-refutation worry against it. Specify what evidence would and would not support attributing phenomenal consciousness to a system, separating it from impressive behavioral performance.
**Common traps**: Inferring moral status from intelligence (a system can be brilliant and have no welfare; an animal can be cognitively simple and clearly sentient). Treating eliminativism as obviously self-refuting without engaging the reply that it targets a *theory* of mind, not the existence of mental life. Accepting a fluent chatbot's say-so about its own feelings as evidence of phenomenal consciousness — verbal report is exactly what such systems are optimized to produce.

## Capstone
Students write a philosophy-of-mind research paper or deliver a defended presentation that (1) states a clear thesis in one of the course's debates, (2) reconstructs the best argument for a rival view as numbered premises, (3) tests both views against a difficult real or imagined case — strong AI and the question of machine sentience, advanced dementia and personal identity, split-brain or blindsight findings and the unity of consciousness, animal minds and moral status, or teletransportation and survival — and (4) draws a conclusion that explicitly separates the empirical questions from the conceptual ones. The paper is assessed on the precision of its argument reconstruction, the charity shown to the opposing view, the rigor of the case analysis, and the clarity with which it marks what would count as evidence for and against each side.

## Assessment Ideas
Argument maps of dualism and its objections; theory-comparison memos (behaviorism vs. identity theory; functionalism vs. its rivals); structured debate briefs on machine understanding; a consciousness case study explaining one argument and one reply in plain language; thought-experiment analyses (fission, teletransportation, Frankfurt cases); a free-will responsibility memo applied to a legal or medical case; and a final paper or presentation. Across all assessments, grade on clarity, charity, and the disciplined separation of empirical from conceptual claims.
