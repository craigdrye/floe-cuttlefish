# Macroeconomics
**ID**: `macroeconomics` · **Discipline**: Economics · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18–19)
**Aligns with**: AP Macroeconomics CED (Units 1–6); typical intro university course (ECON 102 / ECON 1B); Mankiw Macro / Krugman Macro

## Course Philosophy
Macroeconomics is the study of the economy *as a whole* — output, employment, inflation, growth, money, and the interaction between government, central banks, and global trade. The central tension a student must internalise is **short run vs long run**: in the short run, prices/wages are sticky and demand drives output (Keynesian view); in the long run, prices adjust and output is determined by productive capacity (classical view). Most macro debates are really about how short the short run is and how strong each side's mechanisms are. The course's other big task is teaching students to read the news with a real model in their head — what the Fed does, why inflation reports move markets, how exchange rates respond to interest-rate differentials. Internally, every concept should connect to (a) a graph (AD/AS, money market, Phillips curve, loanable funds, FX), (b) a real-world story, and (c) a policy debate.

## Module 1: Basic Economic Concepts (Unit 1, 5–10%)
- Scarcity, opportunity cost, PPC; same building blocks as micro but applied to aggregate output
- **Comparative advantage** at the country level; sources of gains from trade; terms of trade
- Demand, supply, market equilibrium recap (single-market) — to set up the analogy with aggregate demand and aggregate supply
- The **circular flow model**: households, firms, government, foreign sector; injections and leakages
- The **business cycle**: expansion, peak, recession, trough; recession definition (two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth — and the more nuanced NBER definition)
- **Questions**: identify phases of the business cycle from a graph; compute opportunity cost / comparative advantage at country scale; classify circular-flow injections vs leakages

## Module 2: Economic Indicators and the Business Cycle (Unit 2, 12–17%)
- **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)**: definition (market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a period)
- **Three approaches**: expenditure (Y = C + I + G + NX), income, and value-added; why they sum to the same number
- What's *not* in GDP: intermediate goods, transfer payments, used goods, illegal/informal economy, household production; double counting risk
- **Nominal vs real GDP**; GDP deflator; chain-weighted real GDP (intuition)
- Real GDP per capita as a proxy for living standards; its limits (distribution, externalities, leisure, non-market work)
- **Unemployment**: labour force, participation rate, unemployment rate (U-3); discouraged workers, U-6 broader measure; types — frictional, structural, cyclical
- The **natural rate of unemployment** = frictional + structural
- **Inflation**: CPI vs GDP deflator vs PCE; substitution bias, quality bias, new-good bias in CPI
- Real vs nominal variables: real wage, real interest rate (Fisher: r = i − π^e), real GDP
- Costs of inflation: shoe-leather, menu, tax, redistribution from creditors to debtors (unanticipated inflation); deflation and its dangers
- **Questions**: compute GDP using each approach; calculate the unemployment rate, labour force participation rate, CPI inflation; distinguish nominal from real

## Module 3: National Income and Price Determination — AD/AS (Unit 3, 17–27%)
- **Aggregate Demand (AD)**: downward-sloping; reasons — wealth (Pigou) effect, interest-rate (Keynes) effect, exchange-rate (Mundell-Fleming) effect; *not* the same as the micro demand reason
- Shifters of AD via components: ΔC (consumer wealth, expectations, taxes), ΔI (interest rates, business expectations), ΔG (fiscal policy), ΔNX (exchange rates, foreign income)
- **Short-run aggregate supply (SRAS)**: upward-sloping; sticky-wage / sticky-price / misperceptions models
- Shifters of SRAS: input prices (oil shocks!), productivity, expectations of inflation, business taxes/regulation, supply shocks
- **Long-run aggregate supply (LRAS)**: vertical at potential output Y_f (full employment); shifts only with changes in productive capacity
- **Short-run vs long-run equilibrium**; recessionary gap (Y < Y_f) and inflationary gap (Y > Y_f); self-correction via SRAS adjustment
- **Multiplier effect**: marginal propensity to consume (MPC) and marginal propensity to save (MPS); spending multiplier 1/(1−MPC); tax multiplier −MPC/(1−MPC); balanced-budget multiplier
- **Fiscal policy**: discretionary (changes in G, T, transfers) vs automatic stabilisers (progressive taxes, unemployment insurance)
- Crowding out (loanable funds version) and the limits of fiscal policy
- **Questions**: predict AD/SRAS/LRAS shifts from a scenario; identify recessionary vs inflationary gap; compute multiplier effects; design a discretionary fiscal policy and trace its effects

## Module 4: Financial Sector — Money, Banks, and Monetary Policy (Unit 4, 17–22%)
- **Functions of money**: medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value
- **Measures of money**: M1, M2; what counts where; commodity vs fiat money
- **Time value of money**: present value, future value, discounting; bond pricing intuition (price ↑ ⇒ yield ↓)
- The **financial system**: banks, bond markets, stock markets; intermediation
- **Fractional reserve banking** and the **money multiplier**: 1/(reserve ratio); leakages reduce the realised multiplier
- **The Money Market**: demand for money (transactional, precautionary, speculative); MD slopes down in i; supply of money set by central bank; equilibrium nominal interest rate
- **Loanable funds market**: demand from borrowers (investment), supply from savers; equilibrium real interest rate; government borrowing crowds out
- **Central bank tools**: open-market operations (most important), reserve requirements, discount/policy rate, **interest on reserves (IORB)** — the modern primary tool, **forward guidance**, and **quantitative easing/tightening**
- **Monetary policy transmission**: ΔMS → Δi → ΔI and ΔC (durables) → ΔAD; also Δ exchange rates → ΔNX
- Expansionary vs contractionary monetary policy in AD/AS and money market diagrams
- **Money neutrality** in the long run; classical dichotomy; quantity theory of money: MV = PY
- **Questions**: trace a Fed action through MS → i → I → AD → Y, P; price a bond and compute yield; compute money-multiplier outcomes from a reserve change

## Module 5: Long-Run Consequences of Stabilisation Policies (Unit 5, 17–22%)
- The **Phillips Curve**: short-run downward-sloping (inflation–unemployment tradeoff); long-run vertical at the natural rate
- Adaptive vs rational expectations; how expected inflation shifts the SRPC
- **Stagflation** and supply shocks (1970s); the breakdown of a stable PC under shifting expectations
- The **Taylor rule** as a policy benchmark: i = r* + π + 0.5(π − π*) + 0.5(Y − Y*)/Y*
- Government **budget deficits and surpluses**; cyclically-adjusted (structural) deficit
- **National debt**: stock vs flow; debt-to-GDP ratio; intergenerational burden debate; Ricardian equivalence (controversy)
- Crowding out of investment via the loanable funds market; the long-run growth cost of persistent deficits
- **Economic growth**: sources — physical capital, human capital, technology, institutions
- Solow growth model (intuition): convergence, steady state, role of saving, depreciation, population growth, and technological progress; why technology is the only sustained growth source
- Endogenous growth (Romer) intuition: ideas as nonrival; R&D and human capital as engines
- **Questions**: shift SRPC from a change in expected inflation; analyze policy in PC framework; compute debt-to-GDP dynamics; reason about Solow steady state

## Module 6: Open Economy — International Trade and Finance (Unit 6, 10–13%)
- **Balance of payments**: current account (trade in goods/services, primary income, secondary income), capital account, financial account; identity CA + KA + FA = 0
- Why trade deficits aren't necessarily bad — what's on the other side of the ledger
- **Foreign exchange (FX) market**: appreciation/depreciation; demand for and supply of currency
- Determinants of exchange rates: interest-rate differentials, inflation differentials, expected future exchange rates, capital flows, trade flows; **purchasing power parity** (PPP) in the long run; **interest rate parity** in the short run
- Effect of exchange rate changes on net exports, AD; the J-curve; pass-through to inflation
- Monetary and fiscal policy under flexible exchange rates (Mundell–Fleming intuition): fiscal expansion appreciates currency and crowds out NX; monetary expansion depreciates currency and boosts NX
- Fixed vs flexible exchange-rate regimes; pegs, currency boards, dollarisation; the **trilemma** of international macro (cannot simultaneously have free capital flow, fixed exchange rate, and independent monetary policy)
- Trade restrictions: tariffs, quotas, subsidies, non-tariff barriers; effects on CS, PS, government revenue, DWL; political economy of protection
- **Questions**: trace a monetary policy change through the FX market and back to AD; compute current account from BoP entries; analyze the welfare effects of a tariff; reason about the trilemma

## Stretch Zone (push beyond intro macro)
- **Modern monetary policy** since 2008: zero lower bound, quantitative easing, balance sheet policy, asset purchase programs; the corridor vs floor system (IORB); the difference between the federal funds rate and the policy stance under abundant reserves
- **Inflation targeting**: history (New Zealand 1990, Fed implicit since 2012, explicit 2%), credibility, anchoring expectations
- **Heterodox views**: MMT (intuition + critiques), post-Keynesian, Austrian; what each gets right and wrong relative to the mainstream
- **Financial crises**: Minsky, Diamond–Dybvig bank-run model, the 2008 crisis as a shadow-banking liquidity crunch; macroprudential policy
- **Modern growth empirics**: why some countries are rich and others poor (Acemoglu & Robinson on institutions; Sachs on geography; Easterly on aid); the divergence of the great divergence
- **Hyperinflation case studies**: Weimar Germany, Zimbabwe, Venezuela, Argentina; the fiscal theory of the price level (intuition)
- **Real Business Cycle** vs **New Keynesian** models: what each takes seriously about the data; DSGE intuition (without the math)
- **Inequality and macro**: top-1% share, wealth vs income, Piketty's r > g; the macro consequences of distributional shifts
- **Climate and macro**: integrated assessment models, the social cost of carbon, the macroeconomics of decarbonization
- **Crypto and CBDCs** as monetary innovations; what a digital dollar would change
- Common student traps:
  - Treating "the deficit" and "the debt" as the same thing
  - Forgetting that an *expansionary* fiscal policy in the loanable funds market shifts demand right ⇒ real interest rate rises ⇒ investment falls (crowding out)
  - Drawing AD shifts from a *price level* change (that's a movement, not a shift)
  - Confusing nominal with real interest rates in the Fisher equation
  - Treating LRAS as ever shifting from demand-side changes
  - Misreading "the central bank lowers interest rates" as "the central bank lowers all interest rates by fiat" (it's a target hit through OMOs / IORB)
- The journalism filter: when a news article cites GDP, unemployment, CPI, or the Fed funds rate, the student should be able to predict what's missing or being slanted
