# Macroeconomics
**ID**: `macroeconomics` - **Discipline**: Economics - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: AP Macroeconomics CED; typical intro university course; Mankiw, Krugman, and OpenStax equivalents

## Course Aim
Teach students to understand the economy as a whole: output, employment, inflation, growth, money, financial systems, government budgets, central banks, and global linkages. Students learn to read economic news with models in mind and to distinguish short-run stabilization from long-run productive capacity.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve GDP, inflation, unemployment, business cycles, AD/AS, fiscal policy, money, banking, monetary policy, interest rates, Phillips curve, growth, debt, open-economy macro, exchange rates, trade, or macro policy debates. Every major idea should connect to a graph, a real-world story, and a policy tradeoff.

## Chapter 1: Macro Foundations and the Circular Flow
**Key concepts**: Scarcity, opportunity cost, comparative advantage, circular flow, households, firms, government, foreign sector, injections, leakages, business cycle, recession, expansion, and macroeconomic goals.
**Practice questions**: Classify circular-flow flows; identify business-cycle phases; compute country-level opportunity cost; explain the difference between micro and macro questions; connect trade to aggregate output.
**Student output**: A macro map showing how households, firms, government, banks, and foreign trade connect in one economy.

## Chapter 2: Measuring Output, Jobs, and Prices
**Key concepts**: GDP, expenditure approach, income approach, value added, final goods, nominal GDP, real GDP, GDP deflator, unemployment rate, labor force participation, frictional unemployment, structural unemployment, cyclical unemployment, CPI, PCE, inflation, and real versus nominal values.
**Practice questions**: Compute GDP from expenditure data; identify what is excluded from GDP; calculate unemployment and participation rates; convert nominal to real values; calculate inflation; explain limits of GDP as welfare measure.
**Student output**: An economic indicators dashboard with GDP, unemployment, inflation, and a short explanation of what each number does and does not show.

## Chapter 3: Aggregate Demand, Aggregate Supply, and Fiscal Policy
**Key concepts**: Aggregate demand, SRAS, LRAS, potential output, recessionary gap, inflationary gap, self-correction, multiplier, MPC, MPS, fiscal policy, automatic stabilizers, discretionary policy, taxes, spending, and crowding out.
**Practice questions**: Shift AD, SRAS, and LRAS from scenarios; identify output gaps; compute multipliers; design expansionary or contractionary fiscal policy; explain how crowding out limits fiscal stimulus.
**Student output**: A fiscal policy memo recommending a response to a recessionary or inflationary gap with graph and tradeoff discussion.

## Chapter 4: Money, Banking, and Financial Markets
**Key concepts**: Money, M1, M2, fiat money, banks, deposits, reserves, fractional reserve banking, money multiplier, bonds, stock markets, time value of money, present value, loanable funds, real interest rate, and nominal interest rate.
**Practice questions**: Classify money measures; trace deposit expansion; compare bond prices and yields; use a loanable-funds graph; explain how saving and borrowing determine real interest rates.
**Student output**: A financial-system explainer that follows one dollar from deposit to lending, interest, investment, and aggregate demand.

## Chapter 5: Central Banks and Monetary Policy
**Key concepts**: Central bank, open-market operations, policy rate, discount rate, reserve requirements, interest on reserves, forward guidance, quantitative easing, money market, monetary transmission, inflation targeting, expectations, and long-run money neutrality.
**Practice questions**: Trace a monetary policy action through interest rates, investment, aggregate demand, output, and prices; compare expansionary and contractionary policy; explain why expectations matter; distinguish target rate from market rates.
**Student output**: A central-bank decision brief based on inflation, unemployment, growth, and financial conditions.

## Chapter 6: Inflation, Unemployment, Debt, and Growth
**Key concepts**: Phillips curve, natural rate of unemployment, expected inflation, stagflation, budget deficit, national debt, debt-to-GDP ratio, structural deficit, economic growth, productivity, physical capital, human capital, technology, institutions, convergence, and Solow growth intuition.
**Practice questions**: Shift the short-run Phillips curve; compare demand-pull and cost-push inflation; calculate debt-to-GDP changes; explain sources of growth; distinguish temporary stimulus from long-run growth policy.
**Student output**: A long-run policy analysis comparing two growth strategies, such as education, infrastructure, research, immigration, savings, or institutional reform.

## Chapter 7: Open-Economy Macroeconomics
**Key concepts**: Current account, financial account, balance of payments, exchange rate, appreciation, depreciation, foreign exchange market, purchasing power parity, interest-rate parity, net exports, capital flows, tariffs, quotas, flexible rates, fixed rates, and policy trilemma.
**Practice questions**: Analyze an exchange-rate shift; compute current account entries; trace how interest-rate changes affect currency and net exports; explain why trade deficits have financial-account counterparts; evaluate tariffs and quotas.
**Student output**: An open-economy news brief connecting exchange rates, trade, capital flows, and domestic policy.

## Chapter 8: Crises, Inequality, Climate, and Macro Debates
**Key concepts**: Financial crisis, bank run, shadow banking, zero lower bound, quantitative easing, macroprudential policy, inequality, distribution, climate macroeconomics, social cost of carbon, heterodox macro, Modern Monetary Theory, real business cycle, New Keynesian models, and policy uncertainty.
**Practice questions**: Explain how a banking crisis becomes a macro crisis; compare mainstream and heterodox claims; evaluate climate risk as a macro problem; identify what a news article leaves out about inflation, unemployment, GDP, or central banks.
**Student output**: A macro debate brief that presents two serious positions, the model behind each, the evidence each needs, and the tradeoffs each accepts.

## Capstone
Students produce a macroeconomic country or policy report. The report must include an indicators dashboard, AD/AS analysis, fiscal and monetary policy discussion, financial-sector or debt context, open-economy linkages, and a final recommendation that separates model predictions from normative priorities.

## Assessment Ideas
Use indicator calculations, graph shifts, policy memos, central-bank simulations, data dashboards, news annotations, debate briefs, and a final country report. Assess graph accuracy, causal tracing, data interpretation, distinction between short and long run, and ability to name uncertainty.
