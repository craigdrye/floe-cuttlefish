# Macroeconomics
**ID**: `macroeconomics` - **Discipline**: Economics - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: AP Macroeconomics CED; typical first-year university principles course; Mankiw *Macroeconomics*, Krugman & Wells *Macroeconomics*, and OpenStax *Principles of Macroeconomics 3e* equivalents

## Course Aim
This course teaches students to think about the economy as a whole rather than as a collection of individual markets. By the end, a student should be able to take a piece of economic news — a jobs report, a central-bank decision, a debt-ceiling fight, a currency swing — and translate it into a model, trace the chain of cause and effect, and say what is likely to happen to output, employment, inflation, and interest rates, and over what time horizon.

The single hardest idea in macroeconomics is the distinction between the **short run** and the **long run**. In the short run, prices and wages are sticky, demand drives output, and policy can fight recessions and overheating. In the long run, output is pinned down by real factors — labor, capital, technology, and institutions — and money is roughly neutral. Almost every classic debate (does stimulus work? does printing money cause inflation? do deficits crowd out investment?) turns on which horizon you are arguing about. We treat this distinction as the spine of the course and return to it in every chapter.

We also insist on intellectual honesty about uncertainty. Macroeconomics is a young, contested field where reasonable economists disagree because data are noisy, experiments are impossible, and identical shocks play out differently across countries and eras. Students learn the mainstream AD/AS and money-market frameworks rigorously, but they also learn where those frameworks are weakest, which empirical questions are genuinely open, and how to separate a model's prediction from the analyst's political priorities.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve GDP and national accounting, inflation and price indices, unemployment and the labor market, business cycles, aggregate demand and aggregate supply, fiscal policy and the multiplier, money and banking, central banks and monetary policy, interest rates and loanable funds, the Phillips curve, long-run growth, government debt, open-economy macro, exchange rates, trade balances, or macro policy debates.

Three design commitments shape every chapter. First, **every major idea connects to a graph, a real-world story, and a policy tradeoff** — a concept the student cannot draw or apply to a news event is not yet learned. Second, we drill the **direction and mechanism** of effects, not just definitions: a student should never just know "expansionary monetary policy lowers rates" but be able to trace lower rates to higher investment to higher AD to higher output and, eventually, higher prices. Third, we surface **common traps explicitly** because macroeconomics is unusually rich in plausible-sounding errors (confusing stocks with flows, real with nominal, movement-along with shifts-of a curve, correlation with causation). Naming these traps is half the teaching.

Numbers used in worked examples (a 2% inflation target, the federal funds operating framework) reflect the current US institutional setup as of 2025-2026; instructors in other countries should substitute their own central bank's mandate and tools.

## Chapter 1: Macro Foundations and the Circular Flow
**Core questions**: What makes a question "macro" rather than "micro"? How does money flow between households, firms, government, banks, and the rest of the world, and what counts as an injection versus a leakage? What are the phases of the business cycle, and what are an economy's standing macroeconomic goals?
**Key concepts**: Scarcity, opportunity cost, comparative versus absolute advantage, the circular-flow model, households, firms, government, the foreign sector, financial intermediaries, injections (investment, government spending, exports), leakages (saving, taxes, imports), business cycle, peak, trough, expansion, contraction, recession, and the goals of growth, full employment, and price stability.
**Applied skills**: Classify a real transaction as an injection or leakage; identify the current business-cycle phase from output and unemployment data; compute a country's opportunity cost of producing a good and determine who has the comparative advantage; explain why a single-firm layoff is a micro story but an economy-wide rise in unemployment is a macro one.
**Common traps**: Confusing comparative advantage (lower opportunity cost) with absolute advantage (fewer total inputs); treating the trough as the worst possible moment rather than the turning point where decline ends; assuming saving is "good for the economy" without recognizing it as a leakage from the spending stream in the short run (the paradox of thrift).
**Student output**: A labeled macro map showing how households, firms, government, the financial sector, and foreign trade connect, with each flow tagged as an injection or leakage.

## Chapter 2: Measuring Output, Jobs, and Prices
**Core questions**: What does GDP count, and what does it deliberately leave out? How do we strip inflation out of a dollar figure to compare real activity across years? How do statisticians decide who counts as unemployed, and why can the headline rate hide labor-market weakness?
**Key concepts**: GDP, the expenditure approach (Y = C + I + G + NX), the income approach, value added, final versus intermediate goods, nominal versus real GDP, the GDP deflator, GDP per capita, the labor force, the unemployment rate, labor-force participation rate, frictional, structural, and cyclical unemployment, discouraged workers, the natural rate of unemployment, CPI, the PCE price index (the Fed's preferred gauge since 2012), inflation, and real versus nominal values.
**Applied skills**: Compute GDP from expenditure data and identify what is excluded (used goods, intermediate goods, financial transactions, transfer payments, household and underground production); convert nominal GDP to real GDP with a deflator; calculate the unemployment, participation, and employment rates from labor-market counts; compute an inflation rate from a price index; convert a past wage or price into today's dollars.
**Common traps**: Counting transfer payments (Social Security, unemployment benefits) inside G; adding the value of intermediate goods on top of final goods (double counting); confusing a rise in *nominal* GDP with real growth when prices have risen; assuming a falling unemployment rate is always good when it can reflect people leaving the labor force; treating CPI and the GDP deflator as identical (CPI uses a fixed consumer basket including imports; the deflator covers all domestic output).
**Student output**: An economic-indicators dashboard reporting GDP growth, unemployment, participation, and inflation for a chosen country, with a short note on what each number does and does not reveal about well-being.

## Chapter 3: Aggregate Demand, Aggregate Supply, and Output Gaps
**Core questions**: Why does the aggregate demand curve slope downward when individual demand curves and AD slope down for completely different reasons? Why is short-run aggregate supply upward-sloping but long-run aggregate supply vertical? How does an economy self-correct after a shock, and why might that take painfully long?
**Key concepts**: Aggregate demand and its components, the wealth (real-balances) effect, the interest-rate effect, the exchange-rate effect, short-run aggregate supply (SRAS), sticky wages and prices, long-run aggregate supply (LRAS), potential output, recessionary and inflationary gaps, demand shocks, supply shocks, stagflation, and short-run self-correction through wage and price adjustment.
**Applied skills**: Shift AD, SRAS, and LRAS in response to a described scenario and read off the new price level and output; identify whether an economy faces a recessionary or inflationary gap; distinguish a demand-driven recession (output and prices both fall) from a supply-driven one (output falls, prices rise); explain the self-correction mechanism and why economists disagree about its speed.
**Common traps**: Explaining the downward slope of AD with the microeconomic substitution argument (there is no "other economy" to substitute toward — the real reasons are the wealth, interest-rate, and exchange-rate effects); treating an LRAS shift as a short-run event; confusing a shift *of* a curve with a movement *along* it; assuming any price-level increase is "inflation" when a one-time AD shock raises the level without ongoing inflation.
**Student output**: A two-panel AD/AS diagram comparing a demand shock and a supply shock on the same economy, annotated with the resulting changes in output, the price level, and unemployment.

## Chapter 4: Fiscal Policy, Multipliers, and the Budget
**Core questions**: How does a change in government spending or taxes ripple through the economy, and why is the eventual effect larger than the initial injection? Why is a spending change more potent dollar-for-dollar than a tax change? When does deficit-financed stimulus crowd out the private spending it was meant to add?
**Key concepts**: Fiscal policy, expansionary versus contractionary policy, discretionary policy versus automatic stabilizers, the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) and to save (MPS), the spending multiplier 1/(1 − MPC), the tax multiplier, the balanced-budget multiplier, crowding out via the loanable-funds market, the budget deficit (a flow) versus the national debt (a stock), the structural versus cyclical deficit, the debt-to-GDP ratio, and Ricardian equivalence.
**Applied skills**: Compute the spending and tax multipliers from an MPC and find the total change in GDP from a fiscal action; design an expansionary or contractionary package to close a specified output gap; distinguish a discretionary policy (a new infrastructure law) from an automatic stabilizer (rising unemployment benefits in a downturn); trace crowding out from a larger deficit to higher loanable-funds demand to higher real interest rates to lower private investment.
**Common traps**: Treating the deficit and the debt as the same thing (deficit is this year's shortfall; debt is the accumulated stock); applying the spending multiplier to a tax change (the tax multiplier is smaller and negative because part of the tax cut is saved); forgetting that the multiplier shrinks as leakages — saving, taxes, imports — rise; assuming crowding out is total when at the zero lower bound or in deep recession it may be minimal.
**Student output**: A fiscal-policy memo recommending a response to a recessionary or inflationary gap, with a worked multiplier calculation, an AD/AS graph, and an honest discussion of crowding-out and timing-lag risks.

## Chapter 5: Money, Banking, and Financial Markets
**Core questions**: What makes something "money," and how do banks create most of it through lending? How are bond prices and yields related, and why do they move in opposite directions? What determines the real interest rate in the market for loanable funds?
**Key concepts**: The three functions of money (medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value), commodity versus fiat money, the monetary aggregates M1 and M2, fractional-reserve banking, reserves and required versus excess reserves, the deposit/money multiplier 1/(reserve ratio), the inverse relationship between bond prices and yields, present value and the time value of money, the loanable-funds market, saving as supply and investment as demand, and the real versus nominal interest rate (the Fisher relation: real ≈ nominal − expected inflation).
**Applied skills**: Classify assets into M1 and M2; trace deposit expansion through the banking system and compute the resulting money creation, including how cash drains and excess reserves shrink the real multiplier; explain why a rise in market interest rates lowers the price of an existing fixed-coupon bond; use a loanable-funds diagram to show how a change in saving or investment moves the real interest rate.
**Common traps**: Thinking banks lend out physical cash they have stored rather than creating deposits when they lend; confusing the real and nominal interest rate (borrowers and savers care about the real rate); assuming bond prices and yields move together; conflating the money market (which sets the nominal rate in the short run) with the loanable-funds market (which sets the real rate from saving and investment).
**Student output**: A financial-system explainer that follows one deposited dollar from the bank through lending, money creation, investment, and back into aggregate demand, with the money-multiplier math shown.

## Chapter 6: Central Banks and Monetary Policy
**Core questions**: What levers does a modern central bank actually pull to move the policy rate, and how has the toolkit changed since 2008? How does a rate change transmit through the economy to output and prices? Why do expectations and credibility matter as much as the policy action itself?
**Key concepts**: The central bank and its mandate, the policy (federal funds) rate, the modern ample-reserves operating framework, interest on reserve balances (IORB) as the rate floor, the overnight reverse repo (ON RRP) rate, open-market operations, the discount rate, forward guidance, quantitative easing and the zero lower bound, the monetary transmission mechanism, inflation targeting (the Fed's 2% PCE objective), the Taylor rule, rational and adaptive expectations, and long-run money neutrality.
**Applied skills**: Trace a policy-rate cut through lower borrowing costs to higher investment and consumption to higher AD to higher output and (eventually) higher prices; explain why, in the ample-reserves regime, the Fed steers rates by adjusting IORB rather than by fine-tuning reserve quantities through daily open-market operations; describe what quantitative easing adds when short rates are already at zero; explain why a credible inflation target can anchor expectations and make policy more effective.
**Common traps**: Describing money creation as the central bank "printing cash" (most policy works through reserves, interest rates, and expectations, not physical currency); confusing the policy target rate with the many market rates it influences; assuming monetary policy can permanently lower unemployment (in the long run it only affects nominal variables); forgetting recognition, implementation, and impact lags that make real-time stabilization hard.
**Student output**: A central-bank decision brief that weighs current inflation, unemployment, growth, and financial conditions and recommends a rate move, naming the tool used and the expected transmission path.

## Chapter 7: Inflation, the Phillips Curve, and Long-Run Growth
**Core questions**: Where does inflation come from, and why does the inflation-unemployment tradeoff vanish in the long run? What actually makes a country rich over decades, and why can't a nation simply save its way to permanent faster growth? Why do some poor countries catch up while others stagnate?
**Key concepts**: Demand-pull versus cost-push inflation, the short-run Phillips curve, the natural rate of unemployment, expected inflation and the expectations-augmented Phillips curve, the vertical long-run Phillips curve, stagflation, the quantity theory of money (MV = PY), economic growth, productivity, physical capital, human capital, technology, institutions, diminishing returns to capital, the Solow growth model and its steady state, conditional convergence, and growth policy (education, infrastructure, research, savings, openness, institutional reform).
**Applied skills**: Distinguish demand-pull from cost-push inflation on an AD/AS diagram; shift the short-run Phillips curve when expected inflation changes and explain why the long-run curve is vertical at the natural rate; use the quantity theory to predict the long-run price effect of money growth when V and Y are stable; identify, in the Solow framework, why a higher saving rate raises the *level* of income but not the long-run growth rate, and why technological progress is the only sustained driver of growth in output per worker.
**Common traps**: Believing the short-run inflation-unemployment tradeoff is exploitable in the long run (the 1970s disproved this); confusing a *level* effect (more capital raises output once, then diminishing returns bite) with a *growth-rate* effect; treating temporary demand stimulus as a growth policy when it shifts AD, not LRAS; assuming all inflation is monetary when supply shocks can raise the price level without sustained money growth.
**Student output**: A long-run growth analysis comparing two strategies for a chosen country (for example, education versus infrastructure, or savings incentives versus institutional reform), grounded in the Solow intuition and the level-versus-growth distinction.

## Chapter 8: Open-Economy Macroeconomics
**Core questions**: How is an exchange rate determined, and what makes a currency strengthen or weaken? Why does a current-account deficit mechanically require a financial-account surplus? How do interest-rate changes spill across borders into currencies, trade, and capital flows?
**Key concepts**: The balance of payments, the current account, the financial (capital) account, the BoP identity (CA + FA = 0), the foreign-exchange market, exchange-rate appreciation and depreciation, flexible versus fixed exchange rates, net exports, capital flows, purchasing power parity (PPP) and the law of one price, interest-rate parity, tariffs and quotas, and the policy trilemma (a country can pick at most two of fixed exchange rates, free capital mobility, and independent monetary policy).
**Applied skills**: Predict how a rise in domestic real interest rates affects capital inflows, the currency, and net exports; record a transaction correctly in the current or financial account; explain why a persistent trade deficit is financed by net foreign borrowing or asset sales (its financial-account counterpart); evaluate the winners, losers, and aggregate effects of a tariff; apply the trilemma to explain why a country pegging its currency with open capital markets must surrender monetary independence.
**Common traps**: Assuming higher interest rates always boost exports (they typically appreciate the currency, which *hurts* exports in the short run); treating a current-account deficit as automatically "bad" rather than as the counterpart of inbound investment; confusing appreciation (a stronger currency) with depreciation; thinking a country can simultaneously fix its exchange rate, allow free capital flows, and run an independent monetary policy.
**Student output**: An open-economy news brief connecting a recent exchange-rate move to interest-rate differentials, the trade balance, capital flows, and domestic policy options.

## Chapter 9: Crises, Inequality, and Contemporary Macro Debates
**Core questions**: How does trouble in the financial sector become an economy-wide crisis? Where do mainstream and heterodox schools genuinely disagree, and what evidence would settle their disputes? What macro problems — inequality, climate risk — sit awkwardly inside the standard toolkit?
**Key concepts**: Financial crises, bank runs and the maturity-mismatch problem, shadow banking, leverage and fire sales, the zero lower bound, quantitative easing, macroprudential policy and lender-of-last-resort, inequality and the income/wealth distribution, the macroeconomics of climate and the social cost of carbon, and the major schools of thought — Keynesian and New Keynesian, monetarist, real business cycle, and heterodox views including Modern Monetary Theory.
**Applied skills**: Explain how a localized banking shock propagates into a credit crunch, a fall in AD, and a recession; compare how a New Keynesian and a real-business-cycle economist would diagnose the same downturn and what each would recommend; assess a policy claim (for example an MMT argument about deficits) by stating its model, its assumptions, and the evidence it needs; identify what a news article about inflation, unemployment, GDP, or the central bank leaves unstated.
**Common traps**: Treating a financial crisis as merely a stock-market dip rather than a collapse of credit intermediation; assuming the economic consensus is monolithic when serious disagreement is real; dismissing a heterodox view without engaging its actual mechanism, or accepting one without demanding the same evidentiary standard; confusing a normative claim ("inequality is unjust") with a positive one ("inequality lowers aggregate demand").
**Student output**: A macro debate brief presenting two serious positions on a live question, the model behind each, the evidence each side would need to be right, and the tradeoffs each is willing to accept.

## Capstone
Students produce a macroeconomic country or policy report on a real economy of their choice. The report must include: (1) an indicators dashboard with GDP growth, unemployment, participation, and inflation, each interpreted rather than merely listed; (2) an AD/AS diagnosis of the economy's current position relative to potential output; (3) a fiscal and a monetary policy discussion with at least one worked quantitative element (a multiplier estimate or a transmission trace); (4) financial-sector or government-debt context; (5) open-economy linkages through the exchange rate, trade balance, and capital flows; and (6) a final recommendation that explicitly separates what the model predicts from the normative priorities driving the choice. The report is graded as much on naming uncertainty and rival interpretations as on getting the mechanics right.

## Assessment Ideas
Use indicator calculations, graph-shift exercises, multiplier and money-creation problem sets, policy memos, central-bank decision simulations, data dashboards, annotated news articles, debate briefs, and the final country report. Assess graph accuracy, correct causal tracing, data interpretation, the short-run versus long-run distinction, the stock-versus-flow and real-versus-nominal distinctions, and the student's ability to state where the analysis is uncertain.
