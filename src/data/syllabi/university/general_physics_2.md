# General Physics II
**ID**: `generalPhysics2` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: University Freshman
**Aligns with**: typical second-semester university physics covering electricity, magnetism, waves, optics, and modern physics

## Course Aim
Extend first-year physics from visible mechanical interactions into fields, circuits, light, and the evidence that led to modern physics. Students learn to reason about electric and magnetic fields, build and analyze circuits, connect Maxwell's ideas to electromagnetic waves, model optical systems, and understand why relativity and quantum physics became necessary.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve charge, electric fields, Gauss's law, potential, capacitance, DC circuits, Kirchhoff's laws, magnetic forces, sources of magnetic fields, induction, AC circuits, electromagnetic waves, geometric optics, interference, diffraction, relativity, quantum foundations, nuclear physics, or second-semester physics labs. Calculus-based routes can use flux integrals, line integrals, and differential relationships; algebra-based routes should emphasize symmetry, graphs, conceptual field maps, and circuit reasoning.

## Chapter 1: Electric Charge, Fields, and Potential
**Key concepts**: Charge, conductors, insulators, induction, Coulomb's law, superposition, electric fields, field lines, flux, Gauss's law, electric potential energy, electric potential, equipotentials, and the relationship between field and potential.
**Practice questions**: Find the net force on a charge; sketch electric-field patterns; use symmetry with Gauss's law; compare field and potential near conductors; calculate potential difference from energy change; explain why potential is a scalar while field is a vector.
**Student output**: A field-and-potential map for a charge arrangement, including sketches, calculations, and a short explanation of how a test charge would move.

## Chapter 2: Capacitance, Current, and DC Circuits
**Key concepts**: Capacitance, dielectrics, energy storage, current, resistance, Ohm's law, resistivity, power, series and parallel combinations, Kirchhoff's junction and loop rules, RC transients, time constants, and circuit measurement.
**Practice questions**: Reduce capacitor and resistor networks; compute stored energy; analyze a multi-loop circuit; predict brightness changes in bulb circuits; model capacitor charging and discharging; compare ideal and real meters.
**Student output**: A circuit-lab notebook with a designed DC circuit, predicted voltages and currents, measured data, percent differences, and a concise troubleshooting reflection.

## Chapter 3: Magnetic Fields and Magnetic Forces
**Key concepts**: Magnetic fields, Lorentz force, motion of charged particles in fields, magnetic force on current-carrying wires, torque on current loops, magnetic dipoles, Biot-Savart law, Ampere's law, solenoids, and the force between parallel conductors.
**Practice questions**: Use right-hand rules; determine circular or helical charge motion; calculate force on a wire segment; find magnetic fields for long wires and solenoids; compare electric and magnetic forces; explain why magnetic force does no work on a moving point charge.
**Student output**: A magnetic-field investigation using a compass, sensor, or simulation, with a diagram, data table, and explanation of how current geometry shapes the field.

## Chapter 4: Electromagnetic Induction and AC Circuits
**Key concepts**: Magnetic flux, Faraday's law, Lenz's law, motional emf, self-inductance, mutual inductance, energy in magnetic fields, RL circuits, LC oscillations, RLC circuits, reactance, impedance, resonance, phasors, transformers, and power transmission.
**Practice questions**: Calculate induced emf for changing fields and moving conductors; use Lenz's law to determine current direction; analyze RL and LC time behavior; find resonance frequency; compare RMS and peak values; explain transformer voltage and current changes.
**Student output**: An induction demonstration analysis that links a changing flux to measured or observed current, with an energy-conservation explanation of direction.

## Chapter 5: Electromagnetic Waves and Geometric Optics
**Key concepts**: Maxwell's synthesis, electromagnetic waves, speed of light, electromagnetic spectrum, intensity, radiation pressure, reflection, refraction, Snell's law, total internal reflection, mirrors, lenses, ray tracing, focal length, magnification, and image formation.
**Practice questions**: Relate wavelength, frequency, and speed; compare regions of the EM spectrum; trace rays through lenses and mirrors; solve refraction and total-internal-reflection problems; determine image distance and magnification; explain fiber optics.
**Student output**: An optical-system design for a simple camera, telescope, microscope, or corrective-lens setup, with ray diagrams and numerical predictions.

## Chapter 6: Interference, Diffraction, and Physical Optics
**Key concepts**: Wavefronts, Huygens' principle, superposition, coherent sources, Young's double-slit experiment, thin-film interference, single-slit diffraction, diffraction gratings, resolving power, polarization, and Malus's law.
**Practice questions**: Calculate fringe spacing; compare interference and diffraction patterns; determine film thickness for constructive or destructive interference; use diffraction-grating data to find wavelength; analyze polarizer combinations; connect pattern width to aperture size.
**Student output**: A light-pattern lab report using a slit, grating, or simulation to estimate wavelength or aperture spacing and discuss uncertainty.

## Chapter 7: Relativity and Quantum Foundations
**Key concepts**: Postulates of special relativity, simultaneity, time dilation, length contraction, relativistic momentum, mass-energy equivalence, blackbody radiation, Planck's hypothesis, photoelectric effect, photons, de Broglie wavelength, wave-particle duality, and uncertainty.
**Practice questions**: Apply time-dilation and length-contraction formulas; compare classical and relativistic predictions; analyze photoelectric-effect data; calculate photon energy and de Broglie wavelength; explain why classical wave theory failed for key experiments.
**Student output**: A historical-evidence brief explaining one experiment, what classical physics predicted, what was observed, and how the new theory resolved the conflict.

## Chapter 8: Modern Physics and E&M Lab Studio
**Key concepts**: Atomic spectra, Bohr model limits, introductory quantum states, particle in a box, radioactive decay, half-life, nuclear binding energy, fission, fusion, detectors, data fitting, uncertainty, calibration, and responsible interpretation of physical evidence.
**Practice questions**: Interpret emission spectra; estimate quantum energies in simple wells; model radioactive decay; calculate half-life from data; compare fission and fusion energetics; evaluate whether an experimental result supports a model.
**Student output**: A final lab or simulation portfolio connecting one E&M or modern-physics phenomenon to data, uncertainty, and a clear written model.

## Capstone
Design and defend an investigation or technical explanation that connects fields, circuits, waves, and modern evidence. Options include a wireless power demonstration, optical instrument, spectroscopy investigation, RC sensor, transformer model, photoelectric-effect analysis, or public-facing explainer on why fields and photons are not optional ideas.

## Assessment Ideas
Use circuit practicum checks, field-sketch concept questions, lab reports, derivation-light modeling prompts, and mixed problem sets that force students to choose among field, energy, wave, circuit, and quantum descriptions. Include at least one oral or written defense where students explain a result qualitatively before computing it.
