# General Physics II
**ID**: `generalPhysics2` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: University Freshman
**Aligns with**: typical second-semester university physics covering electricity, magnetism, waves, optics, and the experiments that forced modern physics (OpenStax *University Physics* Vols. 2-3; algebra- or calculus-based)

## Course Aim
General Physics I taught you to track a single object through space using forces, energy, and momentum. General Physics II asks a harder question: what happens when the "thing" doing the pushing is not an object at all, but a field that fills space and stores energy on its own? This course builds the field picture from the ground up — electric fields from static charge, magnetic fields from moving charge, and the self-sustaining electromagnetic wave that emerges when a changing electric field makes a changing magnetic field and vice versa. By the end you should be able to look at a charge configuration, a circuit, a lens, or an interference pattern and say not just *what* happens but *why*, in terms of fields, energy, and symmetry.

The second half of the course follows light. Once Maxwell's equations tell us light is an electromagnetic wave traveling at a fixed speed for every observer, two cracks open in classical physics that cannot be patched. The constancy of that speed forces special relativity — simultaneity, time dilation, and E = mc² — and the granular way light delivers energy in the photoelectric effect forces the photon and, with it, quantum mechanics. We treat these not as exotic add-ons but as the honest consequence of taking the wave equation seriously.

Throughout, the emphasis is on transferable judgment: knowing when a quantity is a vector that can cancel versus a scalar that always adds, when symmetry lets you replace a hard integral with a one-line argument, when a "denser medium" slows light versus bends it, and when a classical prediction and an experiment disagree. The goal is a student who reasons before computing and who can defend an answer qualitatively even when the algebra is ugly.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve charge, electric fields, Gauss's law, electric potential and capacitance, DC circuits and Kirchhoff's laws, RC transients, magnetic forces and the Lorentz force, sources of magnetic field (Biot-Savart, Ampère), electromagnetic induction (Faraday, Lenz), inductance and AC/RLC circuits, electromagnetic waves, geometric optics (mirrors and lenses), physical optics (interference, diffraction, polarization), special relativity, the photoelectric effect and photons, matter waves, or introductory atomic and nuclear physics. Both calculus-based and algebra-based routes belong here: calculus routes may use flux and line integrals and differential relations, while algebra routes should lean on symmetry, field maps, graphs, and circuit reasoning.

The chapters track a standard two-semester sequence so questions can be slotted by topic, but the design deliberately reuses a few unifying threads — vector-versus-scalar superposition, energy stored in fields, sign and direction conventions, and "what did classical physics predict and where did it fail" — so that later chapters reward students who internalized the earlier ones. Distractors should target the specific, predictable errors named in each chapter's Common traps rather than generic wrong numbers.

## Chapter 1: Electric Charge, Fields, and Potential
**Core questions**: How does charge create a field in the space around it, and how does that field create a potential energy landscape for other charges? When does symmetry let Gauss's law replace an integral?
**Key concepts**: Conductors vs. insulators, charging by induction, Coulomb's law, the electric field as force per unit charge, field lines, electric flux, Gauss's law, conductors in electrostatic equilibrium, electric potential energy, electric potential (a scalar), equipotential surfaces, and the field-potential gradient relationship E = -dV/dx.
**Applied skills**: Compute the net force or net field from several point charges using vector superposition; apply Gauss's law to spherical, cylindrical, and planar symmetry; relate work done to potential difference; sketch field lines and equipotentials and read one from the other.
**Common traps**: Treating the electric field (a vector) as though it adds like a scalar, so equal opposite fields are summed instead of cancelled — and treating potential (a scalar) as though it has direction and can cancel. Confusing field and potential at a point: the field can be zero where the potential is large (midpoint of two equal positive charges) and the potential can be zero where the field is large (midpoint of a dipole). Assuming Gauss's law gives the field anywhere, rather than only where symmetry makes the flux integral trivial.

## Chapter 2: Capacitance, Current, and DC Circuits
**Core questions**: How does a capacitor store energy in a field, and how do resistors, batteries, and capacitors share voltage and current once they are wired together? How does an RC circuit relax exponentially toward equilibrium?
**Key concepts**: Capacitance, dielectrics, energy stored U = ½CV², current, resistance, Ohm's law, resistivity, electrical power, series and parallel combinations of resistors and of capacitors, Kirchhoff's junction (charge) and loop (energy) rules, EMF and internal resistance, RC charging and discharging, and the time constant τ = RC.
**Applied skills**: Reduce resistor and capacitor networks (noting that capacitors combine oppositely to resistors); solve multi-loop circuits with Kirchhoff's rules; compute power dissipated and energy stored; model the exponential approach to final voltage or current in an RC circuit and read off τ.
**Common traps**: Adding capacitors in series the way you add resistors in series (capacitors in series add as reciprocals). Using P = V/R instead of P = IV or P = V²/R. Forgetting that after one time constant a charging capacitor reaches ~63% of final voltage, not 50% (half-voltage occurs at 0.69τ). Ignoring a battery's internal resistance so the terminal voltage is overstated.

## Chapter 3: Magnetic Fields and Magnetic Forces
**Core questions**: Why does a magnetic field push only on *moving* charge, and always perpendicular to the velocity? How does the geometry of a current determine the field it produces?
**Key concepts**: The magnetic force F = qv × B, the right-hand rule, circular and helical motion of charges in a field, force on a current-carrying wire, torque on a current loop and the magnetic dipole moment, the Biot-Savart law, Ampère's law, the field of a long straight wire and of a solenoid, and the force between parallel currents.
**Applied skills**: Apply right-hand rules to find force and field directions; predict the radius and period of charged-particle circular motion; compute the field of a long wire (B = μ₀I/2πr) and inside a solenoid (B = μ₀nI); use Ampère's law where symmetry allows; design a velocity selector by balancing electric and magnetic forces.
**Common traps**: Believing the magnetic force does work and changes a particle's speed — it never does, because F ⊥ v, so it changes direction only. Mixing up the right-hand rule for a negative charge (the force reverses). Confusing the field *of* a moving charge with the force *on* it. Treating the solenoid field as depending on its length rather than on turns per unit length n.

## Chapter 4: Electromagnetic Induction and AC Circuits
**Core questions**: How does a *changing* magnetic flux create an EMF even with no battery, and why does the induced current always oppose the change that made it? How do inductors and capacitors trade energy in AC and resonant circuits?
**Key concepts**: Magnetic flux, Faraday's law (EMF = -dΦ/dt), Lenz's law, motional EMF (= BLv), self- and mutual inductance, energy stored in an inductor U = ½LI², RL and LC time behavior, RLC circuits, reactance and impedance, resonance frequency f = 1/(2π√(LC)), phasors, RMS vs. peak values, transformers, and AC power transmission.
**Applied skills**: Compute induced EMF for a changing field or a moving conductor; use Lenz's law to get current direction and the associated force; find the resonant frequency and the impedance of a series RLC circuit; relate RMS to peak (V_rms = V_peak/√2); analyze a transformer's voltage and current ratios.
**Common traps**: Forgetting the minus sign / Lenz's law and concluding the induced current *aids* the change — which would violate energy conservation by accelerating the magnet for free. Dropping the square root in the resonance formula (using 1/(2π·LC) instead of 1/(2π√(LC))). Confusing RMS with peak or average values. Thinking a transformer can step up both voltage and current at once (it conserves power).

## Chapter 5: Electromagnetic Waves and Geometric Optics
**Core questions**: How do Maxwell's equations predict a self-propagating wave whose speed is fixed by μ₀ and ε₀? How do mirrors and lenses form images, and when is the image real, virtual, magnified, or inverted?
**Key concepts**: Maxwell's synthesis, the EM wave, c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀), the electromagnetic spectrum, intensity and radiation pressure, reflection, refraction and Snell's law (n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂), total internal reflection and the critical angle, the thin-lens and mirror equation 1/f = 1/dₒ + 1/dᵢ, magnification, and ray tracing.
**Applied skills**: Relate wavelength, frequency, and speed across the spectrum; apply Snell's law and find the critical angle θ_c = sin⁻¹(n₂/n₁); locate images with the thin-lens equation and characterize them with sign conventions; trace principal rays through converging and diverging lenses and mirrors; explain fiber optics via total internal reflection.
**Common traps**: Thinking light *speeds up* when entering a denser medium — it slows (v = c/n) and bends toward the normal. Reversing the bending direction (toward vs. away from the normal). Mis-applying the critical-angle comparison (TIR occurs only for angles *greater* than θ_c, and only going from higher to lower index). Forgetting that a converging lens makes a virtual image only when the object is inside the focal length; otherwise the real image is inverted.

## Chapter 6: Interference, Diffraction, and Physical Optics
**Core questions**: When do two light waves reinforce or cancel, and how does a wave bend around an obstacle or through a narrow slit? Why do the same equations describe maxima in one setup and minima in another?
**Key concepts**: Huygens' principle, coherence, the principle of superposition, Young's double-slit experiment, fringe spacing Δy = λL/d, path difference and phase, thin-film interference (and the half-wavelength reflection phase shift), single-slit diffraction, the diffraction grating, resolving power and the Rayleigh criterion, polarization, and Malus's law I = I₀cos²θ.
**Applied skills**: Compute double-slit fringe spacing and grating angles; determine film thickness for constructive or destructive interference, accounting for phase shifts on reflection; find the angular width of a single-slit pattern; predict transmitted intensity through stacked polarizers; estimate the resolution limit of an aperture.
**Common traps**: Confusing the single-slit minimum condition (a sin θ = mλ gives *dark* fringes) with the double-slit/grating maximum condition (d sin θ = mλ gives *bright* fringes). Forgetting the extra half-wavelength phase shift on reflection in thin-film problems, which flips constructive and destructive conditions. Assuming two non-coherent sources (two flashlights) can produce a stable interference pattern. Misusing Malus's law on unpolarized light, which simply halves on the first polarizer regardless of angle.

## Chapter 7: Special Relativity
**Core questions**: If the speed of light is the same for every observer, what must give — and the answer is that simultaneity, elapsed time, and measured length are all relative. How do energy and momentum behave near the speed of light?
**Key concepts**: The two postulates, the relativity of simultaneity, the Lorentz factor γ = 1/√(1 - v²/c²), time dilation (moving clocks run slow), length contraction (moving lengths shrink along the motion), relativistic momentum and energy, rest energy E = mc², and the invariant relation E² = (pc)² + (mc²)².
**Applied skills**: Compute γ for a given speed (e.g., v = 0.6c gives γ = 1.25); apply time dilation and length contraction with the proper time/length identified correctly; compare classical and relativistic predictions; compute rest energy and total energy.
**Common traps**: Mixing up which frame measures the *proper* time (the shortest, measured by a single clock present at both events) versus the dilated time, or the *proper* length (the longest, measured at rest with the object). Applying length contraction to dimensions perpendicular to the motion (only the along-motion dimension contracts). Treating γ as a small correction at everyday speeds being detectable, or thinking anything with mass can reach c. Adding velocities with simple addition instead of the relativistic velocity-addition rule.

## Chapter 8: Quantum Foundations
**Core questions**: Why did the smooth, continuous classical wave picture fail for blackbody radiation, the photoelectric effect, and atomic spectra — and how did energy quantization and the photon resolve it? In what sense does matter also behave as a wave?
**Key concepts**: The ultraviolet catastrophe and Planck's quantum hypothesis, the photon and photon energy E = hf = hc/λ, the photoelectric effect (threshold frequency, work function φ, K_max = hf - φ, stopping potential), wave-particle duality, the de Broglie wavelength λ = h/p, and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.
**Applied skills**: Compute photon energy in joules or eV (using hc ≈ 1240 eV·nm); find K_max and the stopping potential from frequency and work function; determine threshold wavelength λ₀ = hc/φ; compute a particle's de Broglie wavelength; interpret photoelectric data (current vs. intensity, K_max vs. frequency).
**Common traps**: Thinking a brighter (more intense) but below-threshold light will eventually eject electrons — it never does, because each photon individually lacks the energy; intensity sets the *number* of electrons, frequency sets their maximum *energy*. Expecting a time delay for emission (classical wave theory predicted one; experiment shows essentially none). Confusing the work function with the photon energy. Forgetting to convert eV ↔ joules consistently.

## Chapter 9: Atomic and Nuclear Physics
**Core questions**: How do quantized energy levels explain the discrete lines of atomic spectra, and what holds the nucleus together and powers its decay? How do half-life and binding energy govern radioactivity and nuclear energy?
**Key concepts**: The hydrogen spectrum and the Bohr model (energy levels Eₙ = -13.6 eV/n², photon emission/absorption), the limits of the Bohr model, the nucleus, the strong force, mass defect and nuclear binding energy (via E = mc²), the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve, radioactive decay (alpha, beta, gamma), the decay law and half-life, and the energetics of fission and fusion.
**Applied skills**: Predict spectral-line wavelengths from energy-level differences; use the decay law N = N₀(½)^(t/T) and half-life; compute binding energy from mass defect; explain why both fission of heavy nuclei and fusion of light nuclei release energy by reference to the binding-energy curve; balance a nuclear decay or reaction equation.
**Common traps**: Thinking a higher-energy photon corresponds to a *larger* wavelength (it is the opposite: E = hc/λ). Forgetting that a spectral line comes from a *transition between two levels*, not a single level. Believing half-life means everything decays in two half-lives (each half-life removes half of what remains). Assuming fusion and fission are opposites in energetics — both can release energy because both move nuclei toward the peak of the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve (iron-56).

## Chapter 10: E&M and Modern-Physics Lab Studio
**Core questions**: How do you turn a physical phenomenon into defensible data — with calibration, uncertainty, and a model you can compare against? When does an experimental result actually support a theory rather than merely fail to contradict it?
**Key concepts**: Measurement and instrument limits (ideal vs. real voltmeters and ammeters), calibration, random vs. systematic error, propagation of uncertainty, linearizing data to test a model (e.g., plotting K_max vs. f to extract h and φ), percent difference, and responsible interpretation of evidence.
**Applied skills**: Design a DC-circuit measurement and predict then compare voltages and currents; extract a physical constant from the slope or intercept of a linearized plot; estimate and propagate uncertainty; decide whether a discrepancy is within error or signals a real effect; write a concise model-vs-data conclusion.
**Common traps**: Reporting more significant figures than the measurement supports. Confusing precision (repeatability) with accuracy (closeness to truth). Loading a circuit with a non-ideal meter and blaming the theory for the discrepancy. Treating agreement within large error bars as strong confirmation when the test was simply not sensitive.

## Capstone
Design, build (or simulate), and defend an investigation that connects at least two of the course's pillars — fields, circuits, waves, and modern evidence — into a single coherent argument. Strong options include: a photoelectric-effect analysis that extracts Planck's constant from a K_max-vs-frequency plot; a diffraction-grating or double-slit experiment that measures an unknown wavelength with stated uncertainty; an RC-circuit sensor whose time constant you predict and verify; a working transformer or wireless-power demonstration explained by Faraday's and Lenz's laws; an optical instrument (telescope, microscope, or corrective-lens system) with ray diagrams and numerical predictions; or a spectroscopy investigation linking observed lines to energy-level transitions. The deliverable is a short report plus an oral or written defense in which you state the underlying physics qualitatively *before* showing the math, present data with honest uncertainty, and explain explicitly where a classical picture would have predicted something different.

## Assessment Ideas
Combine circuit practicums, field-and-ray sketch concept questions, lab reports with uncertainty analysis, derivation-light modeling prompts, and mixed problem sets that force students to *choose* among field, energy, wave, circuit, and quantum descriptions rather than being told which to use. Include at least one task where students must predict a result qualitatively and defend it before computing, and one where they identify which classical prediction an experiment falsified.
