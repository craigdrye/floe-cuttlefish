# Introduction to Psychology
**ID**: `introToPsychology` - **Discipline**: Social Science - **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Aim
This course introduces psychology as the scientific study of mind, brain, behavior, and experience. Its central claim is that human thought and action can be studied empirically — measured, predicted, and explained — without pretending that a person is ever fully captured by a single variable, diagnosis, or theory. Students learn the major perspectives that have shaped the field (biological, cognitive, behavioral, developmental, social, and clinical) and the methods that let researchers move from a vague question to defensible evidence.

The deeper aim is to make students fluent in *evidentiary reasoning about people*. A first-year student should leave able to read a study or a news headline and ask the right questions: What was actually measured? Who was in the sample? Was anything manipulated, or only observed? How large was the effect, and how much uncertainty surrounds it? Could a third variable, a biased sample, or a measurement artifact explain the result? These habits matter because psychological claims circulate widely and are easy to overstate — about willpower, intelligence, gender, mental illness, parenting, and persuasion — and because the cost of believing a wrong claim about people is high.

Throughout, the course balances rigor with humility. Psychological findings are treated as probabilistic evidence shaped by measurement, samples, culture, history, and ethics, not as laws of nature. Students practice distinguishing description from explanation, correlation from causation, and a symptom from a diagnosis, while keeping sight of the person behind the data.

## Course Design Notes
This course is built for a first university encounter with psychological science, and it assumes no prior coursework. Route questions here when students need foundations in the history and perspectives of psychology, research methods and statistics, the biological bases of behavior, sensation and perception, consciousness, learning, memory and cognition, lifespan development, motivation and emotion, personality, social psychology, psychological disorders, and treatment.

The pedagogy is deliberately scenario-driven rather than definition-driven. Strong items present a behavior, a study, or a media claim and ask the student to reason about it — to identify the confound, name the conditioning element, choose the appropriate research design, or separate situational from dispositional explanations — rather than to recite a definition. Questions should reward students who can apply a concept to a novel case and penalize rote pattern-matching. Two recurring threads run across every chapter: (1) what a given study design can and cannot show, and (2) the gap between a tidy theory and the messy, culturally situated person it tries to describe. Where the field has revised famous findings (replication failures, ethical re-evaluations of classic studies, updated diagnostic categories), the course teaches the revision, not the myth.

## Chapter 1: Psychology as a Science
- **Core questions**: What distinguishes psychology from folk wisdom or pop philosophy? How did the major perspectives — structuralism, functionalism, psychoanalysis, behaviorism, humanistic, cognitive, evolutionary, and biopsychosocial — each reframe the same behavior? Why does the field insist on testability, operational definitions, and replication?
- **Key concepts**: History of psychology; structuralism and functionalism; psychoanalysis; behaviorism; humanistic psychology; the cognitive revolution; evolutionary psychology; biopsychosocial models; the scientific method; theory versus hypothesis; operational definitions; falsifiability; empiricism; replication and the replication crisis; open science (preregistration, open data).
- **Applied skills**: Compare how two perspectives would explain one behavior (e.g., overeating). Translate a broad claim ("stress hurts memory") into a testable, operationally defined hypothesis. Explain why a single dramatic study should not change practice until it replicates.
- **Common traps**: Treating a *theory* as a proven fact rather than an organizing framework that generates testable predictions; assuming the most recent or most cited study is automatically the most reliable; conflating "scientific" with "uses brain scans"; thinking falsifiability means a claim is false rather than that it is *capable* of being shown false.

## Chapter 2: Research Methods, Measurement, and Ethics
- **Core questions**: When can a study support a causal claim, and when only an associational one? What makes a measure reliable, valid, both, or neither? What ethical obligations does a researcher owe participants, and why do they sometimes constrain the cleanest design?
- **Key concepts**: Descriptive methods (case study, naturalistic observation, survey); correlational designs and the correlation coefficient; the true experiment; independent and dependent variables; random sampling versus random assignment; confounds and the third-variable problem; reliability versus validity; operationalization; effect size; statistical significance and p-values; informed consent; confidentiality; deception and debriefing; IRB review; APA ethical principles.
- **Applied skills**: Spot the confound in a proposed study and propose a fix. Diagnose a "X causes Y" media headline that rests only on correlation, and name a plausible third variable or reverse-causation account. Explain how a bathroom scale that reads 5 lb light is reliable but not valid.
- **Common traps**: Confusing random *sampling* (who gets in — about generalizability) with random *assignment* (who goes to which condition — about causal inference); reading "statistically significant" as "large" or "important"; assuming a large sample fixes a biased sampling method; thinking deception is automatically unethical regardless of debriefing and IRB approval.

## Chapter 3: Biological Bases of Behavior
- **Core questions**: How does a chemical and electrical signaling system give rise to behavior and experience? What does it mean to say a brain region is "involved in" a function — and why is that not the same as that region "controlling" it? How should we interpret heritability estimates without sliding into genetic determinism?
- **Key concepts**: Neurons and glial cells; the action potential; neurotransmitters, receptors, agonists and antagonists, reuptake; the synapse; central and peripheral nervous systems; the endocrine system; brainstem, cerebellum, limbic system, and cerebral cortex; localization of function and neuroplasticity; genetics, epigenetics, heritability, twin and adoption studies; gene-environment interaction.
- **Applied skills**: Trace how altering neurotransmission (e.g., an SSRI raising synaptic serotonin) could shift behavior, without claiming the behavior is "just chemicals." Match brain regions to broad functions. Read a twin-study claim and state precisely what a heritability of 0.5 does and does not mean.
- **Common traps**: Reading heritability as a fixed percentage of an individual's trait that is "due to genes" (it is a population-level estimate within a specific environment); assuming localization means one region does one job alone; treating "biological" as a synonym for "unchangeable" (plasticity and epigenetics say otherwise); confusing reuptake inhibition with producing more neurotransmitter.

## Chapter 4: Sensation, Perception, and Consciousness
- **Core questions**: How does physical energy become a sensation, and how does the brain build a perceived world that goes beyond the raw input? Why do reliable illusions reveal the rules of perception rather than a malfunction? How do states of consciousness — sleep, dreaming, drug states — change information processing?
- **Key concepts**: Sensation versus perception; transduction; absolute and difference thresholds (Weber's law); sensory adaptation; signal detection theory (hits, misses, false alarms, correct rejections); top-down versus bottom-up processing; Gestalt grouping principles; depth cues and perceptual constancy; vision, audition, chemical senses, somatosensation, pain; circadian rhythm; the sleep cycle and REM; theories of dreaming; psychoactive drugs; tolerance, withdrawal, physical dependence, and addiction.
- **Applied skills**: Use signal detection theory to explain why a tired radiologist or a jumpy smoke alarm trades misses against false alarms. Apply Gestalt principles to an ambiguous image or an interface layout. Distinguish physical dependence (withdrawal on cessation) from addiction (compulsive use despite harm).
- **Common traps**: Equating sensation with perception (perception is interpreted, not raw); assuming illusions reflect broken eyes rather than the brain's reasonable inferences; treating tolerance and physical dependence as proof of addiction; believing the absolute threshold is a fixed value rather than the level detected 50% of the time.

## Chapter 5: Learning, Memory, Thinking, and Language
- **Core questions**: How do classical and operant conditioning differ, and how can you tell which is operating in a real scenario? Why is memory reconstructive rather than a recording, and what does that mean for eyewitness testimony? How do heuristics make us fast and also systematically biased?
- **Key concepts**: Classical conditioning (US, UR, CS, CR, acquisition, extinction, generalization); operant conditioning; positive and negative reinforcement and punishment; schedules of reinforcement; observational learning; encoding, storage, retrieval; working memory and long-term memory; explicit (declarative) versus implicit (procedural) memory; forgetting and interference; reconstructive memory and the misinformation effect; eyewitness reliability; concepts and problem solving; heuristics and cognitive biases; intelligence and its measurement; language development.
- **Applied skills**: In a scenario, label the US/CS/CR or the reinforcer/punisher and predict the behavioral effect. Explain how a confident eyewitness can be honestly wrong. Identify the heuristic (availability, representativeness, anchoring) driving a judgment error.
- **Common traps**: Equating negative reinforcement with punishment (negative reinforcement *increases* behavior by removing something aversive); assuming punishment teaches the desired alternative behavior (it mainly suppresses); treating memory as a video recording; reading a high IQ score as a fixed, culture-free measure of overall worth or potential.

## Chapter 6: Development Across the Lifespan
- **Core questions**: How do nature and nurture jointly produce change across a life? When is development best modeled in discrete stages versus continuous gradients? What does attachment research actually license us to conclude about caregivers?
- **Key concepts**: Prenatal development and teratogens; maturation versus learning; nature-nurture as interaction; temperament; attachment (secure, insecure; the Strange Situation); Piaget's stages and core critiques; Vygotsky's zone of proximal development and scaffolding; Erikson's psychosocial stages; adolescence (identity, brain maturation); adulthood and aging; cross-sectional versus longitudinal designs in developmental research.
- **Applied skills**: Compare a stage model and a continuous model on the same milestone. Interpret attachment evidence without simplistically blaming a caregiver or ignoring temperament and culture. Explain why a cross-sectional age difference might reflect cohort effects rather than aging.
- **Common traps**: Treating Piaget's ages as rigid deadlines rather than rough sequences that culture and task framing shift; reading attachment style as a permanent life sentence; assuming adolescent risk-taking is pure irrationality rather than partly a developing reward-and-control system; mistaking cohort effects for the effects of aging.

## Chapter 7: Motivation, Emotion, and Personality
- **Core questions**: What pushes and pulls behavior, and when do external rewards undermine internal motivation? How do biological arousal, cognitive appraisal, and culture combine to produce an emotion? What makes a personality measure useful for one purpose and useless for another?
- **Key concepts**: Drive-reduction and arousal theories; intrinsic versus extrinsic motivation and the overjustification effect; self-determination theory; hunger and achievement motivation; Maslow's hierarchy; theories of emotion (James-Lange, Cannon-Bard, Schachter-Singer two-factor); stress and the general adaptation response; psychodynamic, humanistic, trait, and social-cognitive views of personality; the Big Five (OCEAN); reliability, validity, and the limits of personality assessment (including projective tests).
- **Applied skills**: Predict when paying people for an activity they already enjoy will reduce their motivation. Apply the two-factor theory to a case where the same arousal is labeled fear or excitement. Judge whether a given personality instrument is appropriate for hiring, clinical, or research use.
- **Common traps**: Treating Maslow's hierarchy as a strict order that must be satisfied bottom-up (it is a heuristic, not a law); assuming all rewards boost motivation (overjustification can erode it); reading a Big Five score as a fixed type rather than a position on continuous dimensions; trusting projective tests as if they had the reliability of well-validated trait inventories.

## Chapter 8: Social Psychology, Culture, and Everyday Behavior
- **Core questions**: How much of behavior is explained by the situation versus the person, and why do we systematically misjudge that balance? How do norms, roles, and group settings change what individuals do? What did the classic conformity and obedience studies really show, once we account for ethics and replication?
- **Key concepts**: Social cognition and attribution; fundamental attribution error and actor-observer asymmetry; attitudes, cognitive dissonance, and persuasion; conformity (Asch) and obedience (Milgram); group processes — groupthink, social facilitation, social loafing, deindividuation; the bystander effect and diffusion of responsibility; stereotypes, prejudice, discrimination, and implicit bias; intergroup contact; culture, the self, and prosocial behavior; aggression and helping.
- **Applied skills**: Analyze a social behavior with parallel situational and dispositional explanations and weigh them. Explain how diffusion of responsibility, not apathy, can reduce helping when others are present. Critique a famous social-psychology study on ethical and replication grounds (e.g., what the Stanford Prison Experiment can and cannot support).
- **Common traps**: Committing the fundamental attribution error while analyzing it — over-attributing others' behavior to character and one's own to circumstance; treating conformity and obedience as the same phenomenon (peer matching versus an authority's command); reading the bystander effect as proof people are callous rather than as diffusion of responsibility; assuming an implicit-bias measure straightforwardly predicts an individual's discriminatory acts.

## Chapter 9: Psychological Disorders, Treatment, and Applied Psychology
- **Core questions**: How do we define abnormality without pathologizing difference, and what role do distress, dysfunction, and context play? Why does the diathesis-stress model fit the evidence better than purely biological or purely environmental accounts? How do we choose a treatment using both evidence and the client's context?
- **Key concepts**: Defining abnormality (distress, dysfunction, deviance, danger); the DSM-5-TR and diagnostic reliability; stigma and its effects on outcomes; the diathesis-stress and biopsychosocial models; major categories (anxiety, mood/depressive, trauma- and stressor-related, schizophrenia spectrum, personality, neurodevelopmental, substance-use disorders); psychotherapies (CBT, humanistic/person-centered, psychodynamic, group); biomedical treatments; evidence-based practice; cultural humility; applied fields (clinical, counseling, health, community, industrial-organizational).
- **Applied skills**: Separate a symptom from a diagnosis in a vignette and identify what additional information a clinician would need. Explain a mechanism by which stigma worsens course and outcome. Compare two evidence-based treatments for the same condition, weighing efficacy, client preference, and access.
- **Common traps**: Diagnosing from a single symptom rather than the full pattern, duration, distress, and impairment criteria; assuming a disorder is either "all biology" or "all environment" rather than diathesis-stress; treating the DSM as a list of fixed biological diseases rather than a clinically useful, revisable classification; confusing correlated risk factors with established causes; equating "evidence-based" with "best for every individual client."

## Capstone
Students complete an **evidence dossier** on a psychological question of public or personal importance — for example, "Do brain-training apps improve real-world cognition?" or "Does social media use cause adolescent depression?" The dossier must include: a focused, operationalized question; at least three credible sources spanning more than one research design; a methods comparison that states what each design can and cannot show; an effect-size-aware synthesis that quantifies uncertainty rather than declaring a winner; an ethics-and-equity note (sampling, consent, who is harmed by over- or under-claiming); and a public-facing explanation, written for a non-expert, that conveys the finding without overclaiming. The dossier is graded on evidence quality, calibrated uncertainty, humane and culturally aware interpretation, ethical awareness, and clarity.

## Assessment Ideas
- Low-stakes concept checks built on scenarios rather than definition recall alone.
- Methods and media-literacy critiques focused on variables, samples, measures, and causal language.
- Short data-interpretation tasks using graphs, effect sizes, and explicit uncertainty statements.
- Case applications that demand biological, cognitive, social, developmental, and cultural explanations in parallel.
- A capstone rubric assessing evidence quality, humane interpretation, ethical awareness, and clarity.
