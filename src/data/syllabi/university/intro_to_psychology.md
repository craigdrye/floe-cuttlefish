# Introduction to Psychology
**ID**: `introToPsychology` - **Discipline**: Social Science - **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Aim
Introduce students to psychology as the scientific study of mind, brain, behavior, and experience. Students learn major perspectives in the field while practicing careful interpretation of evidence, attention to context, and respect for the complexity of persons whose lives cannot be reduced to a single variable, diagnosis, or theory.

## Course Design Notes
This course is built for a first university encounter with psychological science. Route questions here when students need foundations in research methods, biological bases of behavior, sensation and perception, consciousness, learning, memory, development, personality, social psychology, mental health, treatment, or applied psychology. Emphasize what a study design can and cannot show, distinguish description from explanation, and treat psychological findings as probabilistic evidence shaped by measurement, samples, culture, and ethics.

## Chapter 1: Psychology as a Science
- **Key concepts**: History of psychology, structuralism, functionalism, psychoanalysis, behaviorism, humanistic psychology, cognitive psychology, evolutionary psychology, biopsychosocial models, scientific method, operational definitions, theory, hypothesis, replication, open science, and ethical review.
- **Practice questions**: Compare two psychological perspectives on the same behavior; turn a broad claim into a testable hypothesis; identify why replication matters for public trust.
- **Student output**: A brief research question memo with operational definitions, predicted evidence, ethical concerns, and one limitation.

## Chapter 2: Research Methods, Measurement, and Ethics
- **Key concepts**: Descriptive research, case studies, naturalistic observation, surveys, experiments, correlational designs, random sampling, random assignment, independent variables, dependent variables, confounds, reliability, validity, effect size, statistical significance, informed consent, confidentiality, deception, debriefing, IRB review, and APA ethical principles.
- **Practice questions**: Distinguish correlation from causation in a media headline; identify confounds in a proposed study; explain why a measure may be reliable but not valid.
- **Student output**: A methods critique of a short empirical report or news article, including design, measurement, sample, ethics, and the strength of the claim.

## Chapter 3: Biological Bases of Behavior
- **Key concepts**: Neurons, glial cells, action potentials, neurotransmitters, receptors, synapses, central nervous system, peripheral nervous system, endocrine system, brainstem, cerebellum, limbic system, cerebral cortex, localization, plasticity, genetics, epigenetics, heritability, and gene-environment interaction.
- **Practice questions**: Explain how neurotransmission can affect behavior without making a behavior "only chemical"; match brain regions to broad functions; interpret a twin-study claim cautiously.
- **Student output**: A biopsychosocial map of one behavior or mental process linking biology, environment, cognition, and culture.

## Chapter 4: Sensation, Perception, and Consciousness
- **Key concepts**: Sensation, perception, transduction, absolute thresholds, difference thresholds, sensory adaptation, signal detection theory, attention, vision, audition, chemical senses, somatosensation, pain, Gestalt principles, depth perception, perceptual constancy, circadian rhythms, sleep stages, dreams, hypnosis, meditation, psychoactive drugs, tolerance, dependence, and addiction.
- **Practice questions**: Use signal detection theory to explain a real decision; apply Gestalt principles to an illusion or interface; distinguish physical dependence from addiction.
- **Student output**: A perception and consciousness case analysis that connects sensory input, attention, interpretation, and evidence limits.

## Chapter 5: Learning, Memory, Thinking, and Language
- **Key concepts**: Classical conditioning, operant conditioning, reinforcement, punishment, schedules of reinforcement, observational learning, cognitive maps, encoding, storage, retrieval, working memory, long-term memory, explicit memory, implicit memory, forgetting, interference, reconstructive memory, eyewitness testimony, concepts, problem solving, heuristics, cognitive biases, intelligence, language development, and language use.
- **Practice questions**: Identify conditioning elements in a scenario; explain how memory can be useful and inaccurate; evaluate a claim about intelligence testing with attention to measurement and context.
- **Student output**: A learning or memory application brief that proposes an intervention, predicts outcomes, and names ethical or practical risks.

## Chapter 6: Development, Motivation, Emotion, and Personality
- **Key concepts**: Lifespan development, nature and nurture, prenatal development, attachment, temperament, Piaget, Vygotsky, Erikson, adolescence, adulthood, aging, motivation, hunger, achievement, self-determination, stress, emotion theories, appraisal, personality, psychodynamic theory, humanistic theory, trait theory, Big Five, social-cognitive theory, and personality assessment.
- **Practice questions**: Compare stage and continuous models of development; interpret attachment evidence without blaming caregivers simplistically; evaluate whether a personality measure is useful for a given purpose.
- **Student output**: A developmental profile that explains change over time using at least two theories, one empirical finding, and one cultural consideration.

## Chapter 7: Social Psychology, Culture, and Everyday Behavior
- **Key concepts**: Social cognition, attribution, fundamental attribution error, attitudes, cognitive dissonance, persuasion, conformity, obedience, groupthink, social facilitation, social loafing, bystander effect, stereotypes, prejudice, discrimination, implicit bias, intergroup contact, culture, self, relationships, aggression, helping, and prosocial behavior.
- **Practice questions**: Analyze a social behavior using both individual and situational explanations; explain how norms shape action; critique a famous social psychology study in light of ethics and replication.
- **Student output**: A social behavior analysis using evidence from at least one study, with separate sections for situation, person, culture, and uncertainty.

## Chapter 8: Psychological Disorders, Treatment, and Applied Psychology
- **Key concepts**: Defining abnormality, distress, dysfunction, DSM-5-TR, diagnostic reliability, stigma, diathesis-stress model, anxiety disorders, mood disorders, trauma-related disorders, schizophrenia spectrum disorders, personality disorders, neurodevelopmental disorders, substance use disorders, psychotherapy, cognitive behavioral therapy, humanistic therapy, psychodynamic therapy, group therapy, biomedical treatments, evidence-based practice, community psychology, health psychology, and industrial-organizational psychology.
- **Practice questions**: Distinguish symptoms from diagnosis; explain why stigma can worsen outcomes; compare treatment approaches using evidence and client context.
- **Student output**: A treatment and support plan for a fictional case that includes assessment questions, evidence-based options, cultural humility, and referral boundaries.

## Capstone
Students complete an evidence dossier on a psychological question of public or personal importance. The dossier must include a focused question, at least three credible sources, a methods comparison, a cautious synthesis of findings, an ethics and equity note, and a public-facing explanation that avoids overclaiming.

## Assessment Ideas
- Low-stakes concept checks using scenarios rather than definition recall alone.
- Methods and media-literacy critiques focused on variables, samples, measures, and causal language.
- Short data interpretation tasks using graphs, effect sizes, and uncertainty statements.
- Case applications that require biological, cognitive, social, developmental, and cultural explanations.
- A capstone rubric assessing evidence quality, humane interpretation, ethical awareness, and clarity.
