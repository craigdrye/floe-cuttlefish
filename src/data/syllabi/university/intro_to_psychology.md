# Introduction to Psychology
**ID**: `introToPsychology` · **Discipline**: Social Science · **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Philosophy
Introduction to Psychology provides a broad overview of the scientific study of mind and behavior. It covers the biological, cognitive, developmental, and social factors that shape human experience, emphasizing empirical research methods throughout.

## Module 1: Foundations and Biological Bases of Behavior (20%)
- History of Psychology: Structuralism, Functionalism, Psychoanalysis, Behaviorism, and the Cognitive Revolution
- Research Methods: The scientific method, experimental vs correlational designs, independent/dependent variables, and ethical guidelines (IRB, informed consent)
- Biopsychology: The structure of the neuron, action potentials, neurotransmitters, and the organization of the nervous system (CNS vs PNS)
- Brain Anatomy: The cerebral cortex (lobes), limbic system, and the endocrine system
- Neuroplasticity and the split-brain phenomenon
- **Questions**: Identifying the independent and dependent variables in a psychological study, explaining the function of the amygdala in emotional processing

## Module 2: Sensation, Perception, and Consciousness (20%)
- Sensation vs Perception: Bottom-up vs top-down processing, absolute thresholds, and sensory adaptation
- Vision: Anatomy of the eye, rods/cones, feature detectors, and color vision theories (Trichromatic vs Opponent-Process)
- Hearing and the other senses (chemical senses, somatosensation)
- Gestalt principles of perceptual organization, depth perception, and perceptual constancies
- States of Consciousness: Sleep stages (REM vs NREM), circadian rhythms, sleep disorders, and the function of dreams
- Psychoactive drugs and their mechanisms of action (depressants, stimulants, hallucinogens)
- **Questions**: Applying Gestalt principles to explain visual illusions, differentiating between physical dependence and psychological addiction

## Module 3: Learning and Cognition (20%)
- Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Unconditioned/Conditioned stimuli and responses, acquisition, extinction, spontaneous recovery, generalization, and discrimination
- Operant Conditioning (Skinner): Positive/negative reinforcement, positive/negative punishment, and schedules of reinforcement
- Observational Learning (Bandura's Bobo doll experiment)
- Memory: The Information Processing Model (encoding, storage, retrieval), sensory memory, working memory, and long-term memory (explicit vs implicit)
- Forgetting: Interference (proactive vs retroactive), amnesia, and the unreliability of eyewitness testimony (Elizabeth Loftus)
- Cognition and Language: Problem-solving strategies, heuristics (availability, representativeness), cognitive biases, and language acquisition
- **Questions**: Identifying the schedule of reinforcement in a real-world scenario, explaining how retroactive interference causes forgetting

## Module 4: Development, Personality, and Motivation (20%)
- Lifespan Development: Piaget's stages of cognitive development, Vygotsky's sociocultural theory, and Erikson's psychosocial stages
- Attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth's strange situation) and parenting styles
- Motivation: Drive-reduction theory, arousal theory, Maslow's hierarchy of needs, and the biology of hunger
- Emotion: James-Lange, Cannon-Bard, and Schachter-Singer two-factor theories
- Personality: Psychoanalytic theory (Freud's id/ego/superego, defense mechanisms), Humanistic theory (Rogers, self-actualization), and Trait theory (The Big Five - OCEAN)
- **Questions**: Analyzing a child's behavior using Piaget's concept of conservation, evaluating the validity of the Big Five personality test

## Module 5: Social Psychology and Psychological Disorders (20%)
- Social Psychology: Attribution theory (Fundamental Attribution Error), attitudes, cognitive dissonance (Festinger)
- Social Influence: Conformity (Asch experiment), obedience (Milgram experiment), groupthink, bystander effect, prejudice, and stereotyping
- Psychological Disorders: Defining abnormality, the DSM-5, and the diathesis-stress model
- Anxiety disorders, Depressive disorders, Bipolar disorder, and Schizophrenia
- Treatment: Psychotherapy (Psychoanalytic, Humanistic, Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy - CBT) and Biomedical therapies (psychopharmacology)
- **Questions**: Explaining the mechanism of cognitive dissonance in changing behavior, differentiating between the positive and negative symptoms of schizophrenia

## Stretch Zone
- Industrial-Organizational (I-O) Psychology
- Advanced behavioral genetics (twin and adoption studies)
