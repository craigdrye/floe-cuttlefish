# Philosophy (Introduction)
**ID**: `philosophy` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: typical PHIL 101 / Introduction to Philosophy / Problems of Philosophy

## Course Aim
This course treats philosophy not as a tour of famous opinions but as a craft: disciplined argument about questions that resist easy answers. Students learn to do the work philosophers actually do — reconstruct an argument into premises and conclusion, test it for validity and soundness, build a counterexample, read an opponent charitably before objecting, and then say honestly what would change their mind. The aim is to replace "that's just my opinion" with a clearer claim, a reason for it, the strongest objection to it, and a reply.

A student who finishes the course should be able to do four things. First, take a piece of ordinary prose — an op-ed, a sermon, a policy argument, a conversation — and put its reasoning into standard form so its hidden premises become visible. Second, locate the exact point of disagreement in a dispute and say whether it is factual, conceptual, or evaluative. Third, run a hard case through more than one influential framework (in knowledge, mind, ethics, religion, or politics) and report precisely where the frameworks converge and where they split. Fourth, defend a thesis in writing with a charitable reconstruction, the single best objection, a reply, and a revision note recording where the argument actually moved them.

The course surveys the central problems of the field — knowledge and skepticism; reality, time, causation, and identity; God; right and wrong; justice and the state; mind and consciousness — and closes by extending those tools beyond the Western canon to Buddhist, Confucian, Daoist, Islamic, African, existentialist, and feminist traditions. Throughout, the standard is the same: an argument is only as good as the objection it can survive, and a position you cannot state in its strongest form you have not yet earned the right to reject.

## Course Design Notes
The previous version of this syllabus had the right topics and a sound foundations-first sequence, but its chapters were thin and structurally inconsistent. Each chapter listed "Key concepts," "Practice questions," and a "Student output," which told students what to read and what to hand in but not what they should be able to *do* — and, critically, never warned them about the mistakes the chapter exists to prevent. This rewrite keeps the chapter order and the global breadth but rebuilds every chapter on the same four-part shape used across the Floe humanities sequence — **Core questions, Key concepts, Applied skills, Common traps** — so a student always knows the target skill, not just the reading.

The "Common traps" sections are deliberate and new. Almost every characteristic intro-philosophy error is predictable: confusing validity with truth, treating relativism as if it grounded tolerance, reading the Chinese Room as a claim that machines can't be useful, mistaking a thought experiment for a real-world recommendation. Naming these in advance is the fastest route to better reasoning, and they double as a diagnostic for grading. The sequence is still foundations-first: Chapter 1 builds argument method as a transferable toolkit; Chapters 2–7 apply it to the classic problems; Chapter 8 turns to philosophical writing and global extensions and sets up the capstone. Recurring moves — standard-form reconstruction, the counterexample, the charitable steelman, the objection-and-reply — reappear in every chapter until they become reflexes. Assessment rewards reconstructing a rival view fairly as much as defending one's own.

## Chapter 1: Doing Philosophy
**Core questions**
- What turns a mere assertion into an argument, and what does an argument owe its reader?
- Why can an argument be perfectly valid and still tell you nothing true about the world?
- When you disagree with someone, how do you find the real point of disagreement rather than talking past them?

**Key concepts**: thesis, premise, conclusion, standard form, deductive validity, soundness, inductive strength, abduction (inference to the best explanation), counterexample, necessary vs sufficient condition, thought experiment, the principle of charity, steelmanning, common fallacies (ad hominem, straw man, begging the question, equivocation, false dilemma).

**Applied skills**
- Rewrite a paragraph of ordinary prose in standard form, surfacing the suppressed premise that makes it work.
- Decide whether an argument is valid, and separately whether it is sound, and explain why the two can come apart.
- Construct a counterexample to a general claim, and distinguish necessary from sufficient conditions in a definition.

**Common traps**
- Treating validity as a synonym for truth: a valid argument can have wildly false premises ("All cats are gods; I am a cat; so I am a god" is valid).
- Attacking a weaker version of a view you invented (straw man) instead of the strongest version its defenders would accept.
- Calling any disagreement a "fallacy"; a fallacy is a specific defect in reasoning, not just a conclusion you dislike.

## Chapter 2: Knowledge and Skepticism
**Core questions**
- What does it take to *know* something, as opposed to merely believing it or getting lucky?
- If you cannot rule out that you are dreaming or a brain in a vat, do you still know anything about the external world?
- When should you defer to testimony or expertise, and when is deference an abdication of judgment?

**Key concepts**: knowledge, the justified-true-belief (JTB) analysis, Gettier cases (Edmund Gettier, 1963), the dream argument and the evil-demon / brain-in-a-vat skeptical scenarios, foundationalism vs coherentism, rationalism vs empiricism, a priori vs a posteriori, Hume's problem of induction, testimony, expertise, epistemic responsibility.

**Applied skills**
- Build an original Gettier case: a belief that is true and justified but, intuitively, not knowledge because the justification connects to the truth only by luck.
- Diagram a skeptical argument (dream, demon, vat) as premises and conclusion, then locate its most vulnerable premise.
- Evaluate a real piece of expert testimony for the conditions under which trusting it is epistemically responsible.

**Common traps**
- Treating Gettier cases as proof that knowledge is impossible; they show JTB is insufficient, not that we know nothing.
- Confusing "I can't prove the external world exists" (a justification problem) with "the external world doesn't exist" (a metaphysical claim).
- Using "true for me" as if it settled anything; if a claim is genuinely true it is not indexed to a believer.

## Chapter 3: Reality, Time, Causation, and Identity
**Core questions**
- What kinds of things ultimately exist — particular objects, properties, numbers, possibilities — and how would we tell?
- Is the future already fixed, and if every event has a prior cause, in what sense are your choices free?
- What makes you the same person as the child in your old photographs, given that almost nothing physical has stayed put?

**Key concepts**: metaphysics, ontology, universals vs nominalism, presentism vs eternalism (theories of time), causation, determinism, hard determinism, libertarian free will, compatibilism, personal identity over time, the bodily / memory / psychological-continuity / narrative criteria, the Ship of Theseus, teletransportation and fission cases (Parfit).

**Applied skills**
- Test a theory of personal identity against the Ship of Theseus and a teletransporter case, showing exactly where it gives a counterintuitive verdict.
- Distinguish hard determinism, libertarianism, and compatibilism by what each one accepts and denies, and place a given thinker correctly.
- Explain why a metaphysical disagreement (e.g., presentism vs eternalism) is not settled by any single observation.

**Common traps**
- Assuming compatibilism is just "we have a little freedom inside determinism"; it is the claim that free will and determinism are fully consistent.
- Treating identity as a matter of degree where it must be all-or-nothing, or vice versa (Parfit's fission cases are designed to break this intuition).
- Dismissing metaphysics as "unanswerable" while quietly relying on a metaphysical view of time, objects, or causation.

## Chapter 4: Philosophy of Religion
**Core questions**
- Can the existence of God be argued for from reason alone, and which argument is strongest?
- Does the amount and kind of suffering in the world count as evidence against an all-powerful, all-good God?
- When evidence runs out, is faith a reasonable response or an abandonment of reason?

**Key concepts**: the cosmological, teleological (design), ontological, and moral arguments; Pascal's wager; the logical and evidential problems of evil; theodicy (free-will and soul-making defenses); divine hiddenness; the Euthyphro dilemma; evidentialism vs fideism; religious diversity and the many-gods objection.

**Applied skills**
- Reconstruct one argument for God's existence in standard form and identify its weakest premise.
- State the logical problem of evil as a claimed inconsistency among three propositions, then explain how a theodicy tries to dissolve it and what it costs.
- Apply the Euthyphro dilemma to a divine-command theory of ethics and show why each horn is uncomfortable.

**Common traps**
- Treating the ontological argument as obviously a trick without saying which premise fails and why (it is harder to refute than it looks).
- Confusing a theodicy (an account of *why* God permits evil) with a mere denial that evil exists.
- Reading Pascal's wager as a proof of God rather than a decision-theoretic argument about what it is rational to *do* under uncertainty — which the many-gods objection targets.

## Chapter 5: Ethics and Moral Theory
**Core questions**
- When we call an act wrong, are we describing a fact, expressing an attitude, or making a mistake?
- What actually makes an action right — its consequences, the duty behind it, or the character it expresses?
- Why do thoughtful people, reasoning carefully, still end up disagreeing about hard cases?

**Key concepts**: metaethics vs normative vs applied ethics; moral realism, relativism, and error theory (Mackie); the is–ought gap; consequentialism and utilitarianism (act vs rule); deontology and Kant's categorical imperative; virtue ethics and practical wisdom; care ethics; contractualism; rights, duties, consequences, and character.

**Applied skills**
- Run one dilemma (e.g., a trolley variant, a promise-breaking case, a whistleblowing case) through utilitarian, Kantian, and virtue lenses and chart where the verdicts converge and split.
- Separate a first-order moral claim ("this is wrong") from a metaethical claim ("there are no moral facts") in a real argument.
- State the strongest objection to your own preferred theory — demandingness against utilitarianism, rigidity against Kant, vagueness against virtue ethics — and offer the best reply.

**Common traps**
- Collapsing relativism into tolerance; relativism cannot itself ground a universal duty to tolerate anyone.
- Treating utilitarianism as a literal happiness calculator while ignoring rule and indirect versions.
- Caricaturing Kant as "follow rules no matter the consequences," missing the universalizability test and the respect-for-persons formulation.

## Chapter 6: Justice, Power, and Political Philosophy
**Core questions**
- What, if anything, gives the state the right to coerce you, and what do you owe it in return?
- How much may a just society interfere with liberty in the name of equality, safety, or the common good?
- When does breaking the law become not just permitted but a duty?

**Key concepts**: political authority and legitimacy, consent and the social contract, negative vs positive liberty, Mill's harm principle, Rawls's "justice as fairness" and the veil of ignorance, the difference principle, Nozick's entitlement theory and the minimal state, Marx on class and alienation, feminist critiques, global justice, theories of punishment, civil disobedience.

**Applied skills**
- Compare Rawls and Nozick on a concrete distributive dispute, showing exactly which premise about justice divides them.
- Apply Mill's harm principle to a real liberty controversy and locate where reasonable people will disagree about what counts as "harm."
- Evaluate a case of civil disobedience against publicity, non-violence, and willingness to accept the penalty.

**Common traps**
- Confusing legitimacy (the procedure was defensible) with justice (the outcome was right) — a law can be one without the other.
- Treating the veil of ignorance as requiring strict equality; Rawls permits inequalities that benefit the worst-off.
- Reading negative liberty (freedom from interference) and positive liberty (freedom to achieve) as the same idea under two names.

## Chapter 7: Mind, Consciousness, and AI
**Core questions**
- How does a physical brain give rise to a subjective point of view — or does it?
- Could a system that behaves exactly like a mind nonetheless understand nothing?
- If a machine met every behavioral test, would that settle whether it has a mind or moral status?

**Key concepts**: the mind–body problem; substance and property dualism; physicalism, behaviorism, the identity theory, and functionalism; qualia and the "hard problem" of consciousness; Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?"; Jackson's Mary's Room (the knowledge argument); the conceivability / zombie argument; Searle's Chinese Room; eliminativism; the Turing Test; AI minds and moral status.

**Applied skills**
- Reconstruct the conceivability (zombie) argument against physicalism and identify the premise a physicalist must reject.
- Explain what functionalism gets right (multiple realizability) and where qualia-based arguments press on it.
- Use the Chinese Room and the Turing Test to articulate the difference between behaving intelligently and understanding, and apply it to a current AI system.

**Common traps**
- Reading the Chinese Room as the claim that computers are useless or unintelligent; Searle's target is *understanding* (semantics), not performance (syntax).
- Treating Mary's Room as a point about isolation or gender rather than about whether all facts are physical facts.
- Inferring from "the AI passed the Turing Test" straight to "the AI is conscious," skipping the gap the chapter exists to expose.

## Chapter 8: Philosophical Writing and Global Extensions
**Core questions**
- What makes a philosophy paper an argument rather than a summary, and what does a thesis with stakes look like?
- How do you compare a non-Western tradition responsibly, without flattening it into a Western category?
- What is the difference between changing your view because you lost an argument and changing it because you found a better one?

**Key concepts**: essay structure (thesis, charitable reconstruction, objection, reply, revision), using textual evidence and quotation, conceptual precision, comparative philosophy, Buddhist anattā (no-self), Confucian role ethics and ren, Daoist wu-wei, Islamic philosophy (the falsafa tradition), African ubuntu, existentialism and Sartrean bad faith, feminist epistemology and ethics, technology ethics.

**Applied skills**
- Write a thesis paragraph that states the claim, why it matters, and what would count against it.
- Integrate a quotation so it does argumentative work, then gloss it in your own words.
- Put a non-Western view (e.g., Buddhist no-self) into genuine dialogue with a Western one (e.g., Parfit on personal identity), naming both the resonance and the mismatch.

**Common traps**
- Writing a "book report" that explains what philosophers said without ever advancing or defending a claim of your own.
- Quote-mining: dropping a quotation in as decoration instead of analyzing it.
- "Comparative" work that uses the non-Western tradition only to confirm a Western thesis, rather than letting it challenge one.

## Capstone
Students produce a sustained philosophical inquiry — a paper or a defended presentation — on one major question of their choice (for example: "Is the JTB analysis fixable after Gettier?", "Does the Chinese Room refute strong AI?", "Are present generations wronging future ones?", "Can a divine-command ethics survive the Euthyphro dilemma?"). The work must (1) state the question precisely and say why it matters; (2) reconstruct at least two influential positions in standard form, each in the strongest version its defenders would accept; (3) advance the author's own thesis with reasons; (4) state and answer the single strongest objection to that thesis; and (5) defend a revised conclusion. A required reflective note records which premise or intuition actually moved during the inquiry and why — demonstrating the objection-and-reply method as a habit of mind, not just a paper format.

## Assessment Ideas
- Standard-form reconstruction portfolio (Chapter 1) graded on surfaced premises and named fallacies
- Build-a-Gettier-case exercise with a written diagnosis of the luck (Chapter 2)
- Personal-identity verdict table across Ship of Theseus, teletransporter, and fission cases (Chapter 3)
- Argument-map of one argument for and one against theism, weakest premise flagged (Chapter 4)
- Three-framework analysis of a single moral dilemma (Chapter 5)
- Rawls-vs-Nozick memo applied to a real institution or policy (Chapter 6)
- Mind theory-comparison chart with hard cases (Chapter 7)
- Seminar leadership graded on charitable reconstruction of an opposing view
- Final philosophical paper plus reflective revision note (capstone)
