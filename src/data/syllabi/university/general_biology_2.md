# General Biology II
**ID**: `generalBiology2` · **Discipline**: Science · **Year**: University Freshman

## Course Aim
General Biology II picks up where the cellular and molecular story of Biology I leaves off and asks the larger question: given cells that store information and transform energy, how did the staggering diversity of living things arise, how do whole organisms stay alive, and how do they fit together into ecosystems? The organizing idea is Dobzhansky's: nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution. Every later chapter — plant water transport, the amniotic egg, countercurrent gills, trophic energy flow — is framed as a solution that natural selection arrived at under real physical and ecological constraints.

The course is deliberately quantitative where biology is quantitative. Students should leave able to compute allele frequencies under Hardy-Weinberg, project logistic and exponential population growth, reason about energy loss across trophic levels, and read a phylogenetic tree the way a chemist reads a mechanism. These are not arithmetic drills; each calculation is tied to a biological claim a student must be able to defend or refute. The recurring move is "what does this number mean about the organism or population?"

Equally important is evidentiary literacy. A first-year biologist is constantly asked to distinguish a homology from an analogy, a correlation from a cause, a pattern that supports common descent from one that merely looks suggestive. The capstone and assessment design push students to build claims from evidence, state their assumptions, and acknowledge uncertainty — the habits that separate biology from natural history trivia.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they test evolution and population genetics, speciation and phylogeny, the diversity of life (prokaryotes, protists, fungi, plants, animals), plant structure and function, animal physiology and homeostasis, ecology, or conservation biology at a first-year college level. Keep six recurring threads visible across chapters: **structure-function**, **evolution**, **energy and matter flow**, **information**, **systems and interactions**, and **evidence and uncertainty**. A good question should usually touch at least two.

Calibrate to the OpenStax Biology 2e level: rigorous enough that majors are challenged, but assuming only Biology I (cells, metabolism, genetics, central dogma) as prerequisite. Favor scenario-based items over vocabulary recall — give students a population, a tree, a leaf, a food web, or a dataset and make them reason. Distractors should encode the specific, nameable misconception (drift vs. selection, homology vs. analogy, p^2 vs. 2pq, transfer vs. loss in the 10% rule), not just plausible-sounding noise. Math items should state the formula in the lesson so a student who missed it learns the tool, not just the answer.

## Chapter 1: Mechanisms of Evolution
- **Core questions**: What are the four mechanisms that change allele frequencies, and why do populations — not individuals — evolve? When does a trait spread because it raises fitness versus because of chance?
- **Key concepts**: Natural selection, descent with modification, genetic drift (including bottleneck and founder effects), gene flow, mutation as the raw source of variation, fitness, adaptation, sexual selection, directional/stabilizing/disruptive selection.
- **Applied skills**: Classify a described scenario into selection, drift, gene flow, or mutation; predict how a small population size amplifies drift; compute a weighted-average allele frequency after migration.
- **Common traps**: Calling any change in a population "natural selection" when it is actually drift or gene flow; thinking individuals evolve within a lifetime; treating mutation as goal-directed; assuming selection always increases complexity or "improves" a species.

## Chapter 2: Population Genetics and Hardy-Weinberg
- **Core questions**: What does it mean for a population to be in genetic equilibrium, and what does a deviation from Hardy-Weinberg tell you? How do you back out carrier frequency from the frequency of an observed phenotype?
- **Key concepts**: Allele vs. genotype frequency, the equations p + q = 1 and p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1, the five Hardy-Weinberg assumptions (large population, no migration, no mutation, random mating, no selection), the null-model role of HWE.
- **Applied skills**: Given q^2 (the recessive phenotype frequency), solve for q, p, and 2pq; decide whether observed genotype counts are consistent with equilibrium; identify which assumption a scenario violates.
- **Common traps**: Reporting q^2 or p^2 when the question asks for heterozygote frequency 2pq; assuming the dominant phenotype frequency equals p; forgetting that HWE is a *null* model whose violation is the interesting result; setting p = q = 0.5 by reflex.

## Chapter 3: Speciation, Phylogeny, and the History of Life
- **Core questions**: How do reproductive barriers turn one lineage into two, and how do we reconstruct relationships we never observed? What evidence distinguishes shared ancestry from coincidental resemblance?
- **Key concepts**: Biological species concept and its limits, prezygotic/postzygotic isolation, allopatric vs. sympatric speciation, cladistics, synapomorphies (shared derived traits) vs. plesiomorphies, monophyletic groups, sister taxa, homology vs. analogy/convergence, the "Big Five" mass extinctions (Permian-Triassic being the largest).
- **Applied skills**: Read a phylogenetic tree to find the most recent common ancestor and the closest relative; sort traits into ancestral vs. derived; tie a fossil or molecular datum to a specific evolutionary claim.
- **Common traps**: Reading "higher" or "more evolved" off the tips of a tree; calling analogous structures (bird vs. insect wing) homologous; assuming the species at the tip listed last is "most advanced"; treating branch order as a timeline of which species is older.

## Chapter 4: Microbial, Protist, and Fungal Diversity
- **Core questions**: How do the three domains differ, and why is metabolic diversity, not body plan, the headline story of prokaryotes? Where did the eukaryotic cell come from?
- **Key concepts**: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya; prokaryotic structure; horizontal gene transfer; metabolic strategies (aerobic/anaerobic, photo/chemo, auto/heterotroph, including anoxygenic phototrophy); endosymbiotic origin of mitochondria and chloroplasts; protist diversity; fungal hyphae, mycelium, and roles as decomposers and symbionts (mycorrhizae, lichens).
- **Applied skills**: Place an organism among the domains/eukaryotic groups from described traits; interpret a microbial metabolism scenario; explain a symbiosis in cost/benefit terms.
- **Common traps**: Believing all photosynthesis releases oxygen; thinking only plants use light energy; confusing the feeding mycelium with the reproductive mushroom; treating "prokaryote" as a single clade rather than two domains; calling all microbes pathogens.

## Chapter 5: Plant Evolution, Structure, and Function
- **Core questions**: What adaptations let plants colonize land, and how does a plant move water against gravity with no pump? Why did the dominant life stage shift from gametophyte to sporophyte over plant evolution?
- **Key concepts**: Alternation of generations (gametophyte-dominant bryophytes -> sporophyte-dominant ferns, gymnosperms, angiosperms), vascular tissue (xylem/phloem), cohesion-tension and transpiration, stomata, seeds, pollen, flowers and fruit, plant hormones, C3/C4/CAM strategies for hot-dry climates.
- **Applied skills**: Compare life cycles across plant groups; explain cohesion-tension and the role of stomata in the trade-off between CO2 and water; reason about why C4 and CAM reduce photorespiration and water loss.
- **Common traps**: Thinking water transport requires active pumping by the plant; confusing xylem (water, up) with phloem (sugar, both directions); assuming the gametophyte is diploid; believing the large visible moss plant is the sporophyte.

## Chapter 6: Animal Diversity and Body Plans
- **Core questions**: What sequence of innovations — tissues, symmetry, body cavity, segmentation — structures the animal tree? How does the protostome/deuterostome split organize the bilaterians?
- **Key concepts**: Animal tissue origins, radial vs. bilateral symmetry, diploblasty/triploblasty, acoelomate/pseudocoelomate/coelomate, protostome vs. deuterostome development, major invertebrate phyla (sponges, cnidarians, molluscs, annelids, arthropods, nematodes, echinoderms), chordate and vertebrate transitions, the amniotic egg.
- **Applied skills**: Diagnose a phylum from described traits (e.g., pseudocoelom + complete gut + molting cuticle -> nematode); map traits onto a phylogeny; explain the adaptive value of a structural transition such as the amniotic egg.
- **Common traps**: Equating "invertebrate" with a clade; confusing radial (cnidarians, adult echinoderms) with bilateral symmetry; thinking the first opening becomes the mouth in deuterostomes; believing the amniotic egg blocks gas exchange.

## Chapter 7: Animal Physiology and Homeostasis
- **Core questions**: How do organ systems sense and correct deviations to hold internal conditions stable? Why do exchange surfaces share recurring design principles across very different animals?
- **Key concepts**: Homeostasis and negative feedback, thermoregulation, digestion and nutrition, circulation (open vs. closed; single vs. double), gas exchange and countercurrent flow at gills, osmoregulation and the nephron/loop of Henle, nervous and endocrine control, surface-area-to-volume constraints.
- **Applied skills**: Trace a negative-feedback loop (stimulus -> sensor -> integrator -> effector -> response); explain why countercurrent exchange beats concurrent flow; predict how a desert mammal's nephron conserves water.
- **Common traps**: Confusing negative feedback (corrective) with positive feedback (amplifying); thinking countercurrent flow aims for equal O2 in blood and water; assuming larger animals exchange gases across the skin; treating homeostasis as a fixed point rather than a regulated range.

## Chapter 8: Ecology: Populations, Communities, and Ecosystems
- **Core questions**: When does a population grow exponentially versus logistically, and what sets its ceiling? How does energy move through a community, and why is it lost so fast up the food chain?
- **Key concepts**: Exponential (dN/dt = rN) vs. logistic (dN/dt = rN(1 - N/K)) growth, carrying capacity, r/K life-history strategies, niches and competitive exclusion, keystone species, primary vs. secondary succession, trophic levels, the ~10% energy-transfer rule, biogeochemical (carbon, nitrogen) cycles.
- **Applied skills**: Compute dN/dt and identify that growth rate peaks at N = K/2; predict community effects of removing a keystone predator; trace energy and a limiting nutrient through a food web; distinguish primary from secondary succession.
- **Common traps**: Subtracting 10% (loss) instead of taking 10% (transfer); forgetting the (1 - N/K) braking factor; expecting exponential growth indefinitely; confusing a keystone species (disproportionate effect) with merely abundant or dominant species; calling regrowth after a fire primary succession.

## Chapter 9: Conservation Biology and the Field Evidence Studio
- **Core questions**: How do we measure biodiversity and the threats to it, and how do we choose among interventions under uncertainty? What makes a sampling design trustworthy?
- **Key concepts**: Biodiversity (genetic, species, ecosystem), ecosystem services, habitat fragmentation and edge effects, invasive species, climate change impacts, restoration ecology, protected areas and corridors, conservation genetics (small-population effects, inbreeding), field sampling design, data quality, and conservation ethics/tradeoffs.
- **Applied skills**: Evaluate a proposed conservation intervention against costs and uncertainty; interpret abundance or biodiversity data; critique a sampling plan for bias; connect small-population genetics back to Chapters 1-2.
- **Common traps**: Treating "more species" as the only conservation goal while ignoring function and services; assuming correlation in field data proves a causal threat; overlooking selection/sampling bias in observational studies; ignoring genetic risks in small isolated reserves.

## Capstone
Students produce a **systems biology case study** on a single organism, population, or ecosystem that explicitly links four threads: an evolutionary explanation (mechanism and evidence), a structure-function or physiological adaptation, an ecological role (population, community, energy, or nutrient cycle), and a conservation or human-impact dimension. The deliverable must state its claims, show at least one quantitative analysis (allele frequency, population growth, or energy/nutrient budget), name its assumptions, and acknowledge what the available evidence cannot settle. The grade rewards integration across chapters and disciplined reasoning from evidence over breadth of facts.

## Assessment Ideas
- Phylogeny- and data-interpretation prompts with written justification of the evidence used.
- Comparative diagrams that link structure to function and to an evolutionary mechanism.
- Quantitative problem sets in population genetics and ecology, with biological interpretation required for full credit.
- Field-style observation or dataset briefs that ask students to critique a sampling design.
- A final systems case study or conservation defense grounded in evidence, assumptions, and uncertainty.
