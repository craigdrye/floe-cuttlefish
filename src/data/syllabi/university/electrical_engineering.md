# Electrical Engineering
**ID**: `electricalEngineering` - **Discipline**: Engineering - **Year**: University Senior

## Course Aim
This course builds the rigorous working foundation that a practicing electrical engineer carries into every later specialization: the ability to move fluidly between physical intuition, circuit models, mathematical analysis, simulation, and bench measurement. The spine is classical circuit theory — DC analysis, theorems, transients, AC steady state, and frequency response — but the goal is never to memorize formulas. It is to internalize the small set of conservation laws (KCL, KVL, energy continuity) and modeling abstractions (impedance, the ideal op-amp, the small-signal model) from which almost everything else can be re-derived under pressure.

A graduate of this course should be able to look at an unfamiliar circuit and decide, quickly and correctly, which tool fits: nodal versus mesh, Thevenin reduction versus brute force, time-domain ODE versus s-domain transfer function, phasor versus instantaneous. They should know when a result must be negative (an inverting amplifier), when a quantity cannot jump (capacitor voltage, inductor current), and when an answer with the wrong units is wrong before the arithmetic is even checked. The course treats sign conventions, units, and order-of-magnitude sanity checks not as bookkeeping but as the first line of defense against error.

The arc runs from the static (DC) through the dynamic (transients and AC) to the abstract (Laplace, frequency response, control) and finally the physical devices that make modern electronics possible (diodes, BJTs, MOSFETs) and the digital logic that organizes them. By the capstone, students design and defend a small but complete electrical system, justifying every component choice against requirements, analysis, and measured evidence.

## Course Design Notes
Route a question here when it involves DC circuits, KCL/KVL, nodal or mesh analysis, source transformation, Thevenin/Norton equivalents, maximum power transfer, op-amp configurations, capacitors, inductors, first- and second-order transients, AC phasors and impedance, complex/real/reactive power and power factor, three-phase systems, transfer functions, Bode plots, filters, Laplace methods, poles and zeros, stability, semiconductors and diodes, BJT/MOSFET biasing and small-signal behavior, Boolean algebra, combinational and sequential logic, sampling, or introductory feedback/control. Questions should demand the diagram and the units before the algebra; an answer that lands on the right number through the wrong reasoning (correct magnitude, missing sign; right divider ratio, wrong leg) should be reachable as a distractor so the bank teaches discrimination, not pattern matching.

Prefer numeric, single-answer problems with clean arithmetic where the *method* is the lesson, alongside conceptual questions that probe the boundary cases practitioners actually trip on: why a capacitor blocks DC, why feedback stabilizes gain, why an underdamped pole pair rings, why aliasing is irreversible. Distractors should encode specific, nameable mistakes (wrong sign convention, RMS-versus-peak confusion, decay-versus-rise exponential, parallel-versus-series combination) rather than random numbers.

## Chapter 1: Circuit Variables and DC Analysis
**Core questions**: How do charge, current, voltage, power, and energy relate, and how do KCL and KVL let us solve any DC resistive network? When is nodal analysis cleaner than mesh, and vice versa?
**Key concepts**: Charge and current (i = dq/dt), voltage as energy per charge, instantaneous power (p = vi), passive sign convention, Ohm's law, series/parallel reduction, voltage and current dividers, KCL and KVL, nodal analysis, mesh analysis, supernodes and supermeshes, dependent sources.
**Applied skills**: Set up and solve node-voltage and mesh-current systems; reduce ladder networks; apply dividers without re-solving the whole circuit; compute power delivered or absorbed and verify it sums to zero across the network.
**Common traps**: Sign errors from ignoring the passive sign convention (a source can absorb power); applying a voltage divider when current is drawn from the midpoint (the divider assumption breaks under load); confusing series and parallel combination rules for resistors; forgetting that a dependent source's controlling variable must be expressed in the chosen node/mesh unknowns.

## Chapter 2: Circuit Theorems and Operational Amplifiers
**Core questions**: How do superposition, source transformation, and Thevenin/Norton equivalents simplify analysis and reveal loading effects? Why does negative feedback let an op-amp impose precise, predictable gain despite a huge and ill-defined open-loop gain?
**Key concepts**: Superposition, source transformation, Thevenin and Norton equivalents, maximum power transfer (R_L = R_th), the ideal op-amp (infinite gain, infinite input impedance, virtual short), inverting, non-inverting, summing, and difference amplifiers, the two golden rules (no input current, equal input voltages under negative feedback).
**Applied skills**: Find Thevenin/Norton equivalents by open-circuit voltage and short-circuit current; design inverting and non-inverting gain stages to a spec; build a summing or difference amplifier for signal conditioning; predict output saturation against the rails.
**Common traps**: Dropping the inverting sign (an inverting amp output is negative for a positive input); zeroing sources incorrectly during superposition (voltage sources short, current sources open — not the reverse); using the divider leg resistance instead of the full Thevenin resistance for maximum power transfer; assuming the virtual short holds without negative feedback (it does not for a comparator).

## Chapter 3: Capacitors, Inductors, and First-Order Transients
**Core questions**: How do capacitors and inductors store energy, and what continuity constraints do they impose? How does a single energy-storage element produce an exponential approach to a new steady state?
**Key concepts**: Capacitance (i = C dv/dt), inductance (v = L di/dt), stored energy (½CV², ½LI²), continuity of capacitor voltage and inductor current, RC and RL circuits, natural and step response, time constant (tau = RC or L/R), initial and final values, the universal first-order formula x(t) = x(∞) + [x(0) − x(∞)]e^(−t/tau).
**Applied skills**: Identify initial and final values by inspection (open the cap / short the inductor at DC steady state); write the time constant from the Thevenin resistance seen by the element; sketch the rising or decaying exponential; solve for the time to reach a threshold voltage or current.
**Common traps**: Forcing a capacitor voltage or inductor current to jump at switching (it cannot); confusing the rising form V(1−e^(−t/tau)) with the decaying form Ve^(−t/tau); using the source resistor alone instead of the full Thevenin resistance seen by the storage element; reaching only ~63% at one time constant but reporting it as fully settled.

## Chapter 4: RLC Circuits and Second-Order Dynamics
**Core questions**: When two energy-storage elements interact, what determines whether the response rings, decays smoothly, or sits on the boundary? How does pole location encode time-domain behavior?
**Key concepts**: Second-order ODEs, natural frequency (omega_0 = 1/sqrt(LC)), damping ratio and the neper frequency alpha, overdamped/critically damped/underdamped classification, series versus parallel RLC, resonance, quality factor Q, characteristic roots, energy exchange between L and C, the link from root location in the s-plane to oscillation and decay.
**Applied skills**: Classify damping from alpha versus omega_0; write and solve the characteristic equation; sketch a step or natural response with the correct envelope and ringing; relate Q and bandwidth at resonance.
**Common traps**: Mixing up the alpha definitions for series (R/2L) versus parallel (1/2RC) RLC; calling a response underdamped when alpha > omega_0; equating resonant frequency with the damped oscillation frequency (they differ when damping is present); assuming higher resistance always means faster settling (in a parallel RLC it increases Q and ringing).

## Chapter 5: AC Steady State and Power
**Core questions**: How do phasors and impedance turn calculus into algebra for sinusoidal steady state? What do real, reactive, and apparent power actually represent, and why does power factor matter to a utility?
**Key concepts**: Sinusoids and Euler's identity, phasor representation, impedance and admittance, RMS values (V_rms = V_peak/sqrt(2) for a sinusoid), complex power S = P + jQ, real power P = V_rms I_rms cos(theta), reactive power Q, apparent power |S|, power factor and leading/lagging, power-factor correction with a shunt capacitor, balanced three-phase power (P = sqrt(3) V_L I_L cos(theta)).
**Applied skills**: Convert circuits to phasor/impedance form and solve with familiar DC techniques; compute the three powers and the power factor for a load; size a correction capacitor to a target power factor; analyze a balanced three-phase load using line quantities.
**Common traps**: Confusing peak and RMS values when computing power; using P instead of |S| (or vice versa) when sizing equipment; getting the leading/lagging convention backward (an inductive load lags, drawing positive reactive power); forgetting the sqrt(3) factor or mixing line and phase quantities in three-phase calculations.

## Chapter 6: Frequency Response, Filters, and Laplace Methods
**Core questions**: How does a circuit shape signals as a function of frequency, and how do transfer functions, poles, and zeros let us predict that shaping and judge stability? How do Bode plots make this readable at a glance?
**Key concepts**: Transfer function H(s), frequency response H(jomega), magnitude in decibels, Bode magnitude and phase asymptotes, the −3 dB cutoff (half-power, magnitude 1/sqrt(2)), low-pass/high-pass/band-pass/band-stop, first- and second-order rolloff (−20 dB/decade per pole), active filters, poles and zeros, the s-plane, BIBO stability (all poles in the left half-plane), initial-condition handling in the s-domain.
**Applied skills**: Derive a transfer function and find its cutoff(s); sketch Bode magnitude with correct breakpoints and slopes; identify filter type and order from H(s); read stability and transient character from pole locations.
**Common traps**: Reading the cutoff as where the output halves rather than where magnitude is 1/sqrt(2) (−3 dB); slope errors (each pole adds −20 dB/decade, each zero +20); confusing the cutoff frequency with the resonant peak in a sharp band-pass; declaring a system stable from a right-half-plane pole because the magnitude looked small.

## Chapter 7: Semiconductors and Analog Electronics
**Core questions**: How does the p-n junction produce one-way conduction, and how do BJTs and MOSFETs amplify or switch? How do we move from a nonlinear device to a linear small-signal model around a bias point?
**Key concepts**: Doping and the p-n junction, diode I-V and the ~0.7 V drop, rectifiers, clipping and clamping, BJT operating regions (cutoff/active/saturation) and beta, MOSFET regions (cutoff/triode/saturation) and threshold voltage, DC biasing and the Q-point, small-signal models, transconductance (gm = I_C/V_T, with V_T ~ 26 mV at room temperature), amplifier gain, and switching behavior.
**Applied skills**: Analyze diode circuits with the constant-drop model; design a half- or full-wave rectifier with smoothing; place and verify a transistor bias point; estimate small-signal gain from gm and load; decide whether a transistor is being used as an amplifier or a switch.
**Common traps**: Ignoring the diode forward drop (~0.7 V for silicon) when it matters; assuming a BJT is in the active region without checking V_CE against saturation; confusing the MOSFET triode and saturation regions (saturation is the amplifying region for a MOSFET, opposite to BJT naming); applying the small-signal model far from the bias point where linearity fails.

## Chapter 8: Digital Logic, Sampling, and Control Extensions
**Core questions**: How does Boolean algebra let us minimize and build combinational and sequential logic? What does the sampling theorem demand, and what does feedback control buy us?
**Key concepts**: Boolean algebra and DeMorgan's laws, logic gates, combinational logic, Karnaugh maps and minimization, latches and flip-flops (D, JK), registers and counters, clock edges, sampling and the Nyquist rate (f_s > 2 f_max), aliasing, Fourier-series intuition, negative feedback, the closed-loop transfer function, and qualitative PID action (proportional, integral, derivative).
**Applied skills**: Simplify Boolean expressions algebraically and with K-maps; design a small combinational circuit from a truth table; trace a counter or flip-flop through clock edges; identify an aliasing risk from a sample rate; explain qualitatively how each PID term shapes a control response.
**Common traps**: Misapplying DeMorgan's laws (the complement of a product is the sum of complements, not the product of complements); reading a flip-flop output at the wrong clock edge or before the next edge; assuming sampling above the signal frequency is enough (Nyquist requires above twice the highest frequency); believing aliased frequencies can be filtered out after sampling (they cannot — the information is already destroyed).

## Capstone
Students design, simulate, and document a small but complete electrical system — an instrumentation amplifier, an active filter to a frequency spec, a sensor interface, a regulated power supply, a digital controller, or a mixed-signal device of comparable scope. The deliverable is an engineering report containing: a requirements statement with quantitative targets; a schematic; hand analysis that predicts the key performance numbers (gain, cutoff, ripple, settling time, logic behavior); a simulation that corroborates the analysis; test or measurement evidence against the requirements; and an explicit discussion of design tradeoffs and the failure modes considered. The defense centers on a single question: does the measured behavior match the predicted behavior, and if not, can the student explain the discrepancy using the models from this course?

## Assessment Ideas
Use circuit problem sets graded on method and units (not just final numbers), lab notebooks comparing measured curves to analytic predictions, simulation cross-checks, design reviews with peer critique, debugging reflections that trace a fault to a root cause, and the capstone demonstration. Reward correct sign conventions, dimensional sanity checks, and physical explanation as heavily as algebraic accuracy.
