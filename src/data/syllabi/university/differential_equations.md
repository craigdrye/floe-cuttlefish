# Differential Equations
**ID**: `differentialEquations` - **Discipline**: Mathematics - **Year**: University Junior

## Course Aim
Differential equations are the language scientists and engineers use to write down change. Almost every quantitative model of the physical world — a cooling cup of coffee, a vibrating bridge, a spreading epidemic, a discharging capacitor, a competing pair of species — begins as a statement about rates: how fast something grows, decays, oscillates, or relaxes toward balance. This course builds a rigorous working command of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) as both solvable mathematical objects and faithful models of dynamic systems. By the end you should be able to look at a problem stated in words, translate it into an initial-value problem with explicit assumptions and units, decide whether a closed-form solution is reachable, and — when it is not — extract the qualitative truth (does it blow up, settle, or cycle?) and the quantitative answer numerically.

The course is built on a deliberate tension that runs through every chapter: solving an equation is not the same as understanding a model. A student can produce a flawless variation-of-parameters calculation and still have no idea whether the system is stable; another can sketch a phase portrait with no formula in sight and predict the long-run fate of a population correctly. Real competence requires both. We therefore move continuously between three modes of reasoning — analytic (exact symbolic solutions), qualitative (slope fields, phase lines, phase portraits, stability), and numerical (Euler, Runge-Kutta, error control) — and we insist that students reconcile them. A numerical curve that contradicts the eigenvalues is a bug to be hunted, not a result to be reported.

The mathematical arc runs from first-order equations and their geometry, through the full theory of second-order linear equations and their physical incarnation as oscillators, into the Laplace-transform machinery that tames discontinuous and impulsive forcing, and finally into linear and nonlinear systems where eigenvalues, nullclines, and linearization govern the phase plane. Applications are not decorative: mechanical vibration, RLC circuits, mixing tanks, pharmacokinetics, radioactive decay, logistic and predator-prey dynamics, and SIR epidemic models recur as the testbeds where the abstractions earn their keep. The aim is not memorized recipes but transferable judgment: knowing which tool fits, why it works, where it fails, and how to check the answer.

## Course Design Notes
This is a junior-level university differential equations course aligned to the standard sophomore/junior ODE sequence (MIT 18.03, OpenStax Differential Equations, Paul's Online Notes). It assumes single-variable calculus through integration techniques and a working acquaintance with partial derivatives, complex numbers (Euler's formula), and the basics of matrices and eigenvalues; the linear-algebra ideas are reintroduced as needed rather than presumed fluent. Route questions here whenever a learner is working with slope fields, Euler's method, separable or linear first-order equations, exact equations, Bernoulli substitutions, second-order linear equations, mechanical vibrations and resonance, Laplace transforms, step and impulse inputs, convolution, linear systems and phase portraits, eigenvalue classification of equilibria, nonlinear qualitative analysis, numerical integration, series solutions, or the interpretation of any ODE model.

The pedagogy is problem-set-driven with three threaded labs (a modeling notebook, an oscillation lab, and a computational lab) and a phase-plane portfolio. Throughout, emphasize the habits that separate strong students from weak ones: state assumptions and units before writing the equation, identify equation type before choosing a method, use initial conditions only after finding the general family, and always verify a candidate solution by substituting it back. Existence-uniqueness, stability, and the distinction between transient and steady-state behavior are recurring themes rather than one-off topics. Computational work may use Python (SciPy/NumPy), Julia, MATLAB, Octave, or a CAS; the tool is secondary to the discipline of comparing numerical output against analytic and qualitative predictions.

## Chapter 1: Modeling, Slope Fields, and First-Order Foundations
- **Core questions**: What does a differential equation assert, and what counts as a solution? How do order and linearity classify an equation before any solving begins? What can a slope field and an equilibrium analysis tell you about long-run behavior without a formula?
- **Key concepts**: Differential equations as rate laws; order and linearity; the meaning of a solution as a function (not a number) that satisfies the equation when substituted; general versus particular solutions and the role of arbitrary constants; initial-value problems; direction (slope) fields and solution curves; autonomous equations and the phase line; equilibrium solutions and their stability; the existence-uniqueness intuition (why solution curves of a well-posed equation do not cross); Euler's method as following the tangent; canonical models — exponential growth/decay (dy/dt = ky), radioactive decay (dN/dt = -kN, k>0), Newton's law of cooling (rate proportional to the temperature gap to ambient), and mixing tanks (rate in minus rate out).
- **Applied skills**: Classify an ODE by order and linearity at a glance; read a slope field to sketch solution curves through given points; locate equilibria of an autonomous equation and classify them as stable, unstable, or semi-stable from the sign of f(y); run Euler's method by hand for a few steps with a fixed step size; set up a cooling, decay, or mixing model from a word problem with explicit units and initial condition.
- **Common traps**: Reporting a number where a function is required (a solution is y(t), not a single value); confusing "order" (highest derivative) with the number of constants or the largest coefficient; assuming a positive growth rate means linear growth — proportional rate gives exponential; reading a horizontal slope-field segment as "y = 0" rather than "dy/dx = 0"; forgetting that Euler's method accumulates error and treating its output as exact.
- **Student output**: A first-order modeling notebook containing slope fields, Euler approximations, an equilibrium/stability analysis of an autonomous model, and a written interpretation of every parameter, unit, and initial condition.

## Chapter 2: Analytic Methods for First-Order ODEs
- **Core questions**: Given a first-order equation, which solving method does its structure call for? When can we reach a closed form at all, and how do we recognize and respect the limits of symbolic methods?
- **Key concepts**: Separable equations (dy/g(y) = f(x)dx) and the careful handling of constant solutions lost in division; first-order linear equations y' + p(x)y = q(x) and the integrating factor μ(x) = exp(∫p dx); exact equations M dx + N dy = 0 with the exactness test M_y = N_x and reconstruction of the potential function; substitutions that linearize — homogeneous (in the degree sense) equations via y = vx, and Bernoulli equations y' + p(x)y = q(x)y^n via v = y^(1-n); the logistic equation as a separable model; applied cases — terminal velocity, continuous mixing, and dosing.
- **Applied skills**: Solve a separable IVP and apply the initial condition to pin the constant; build and apply an integrating factor for a linear equation; test exactness and recover F(x,y) by partial integration plus the matching cross-term; choose v = y^(1-n) to convert a Bernoulli equation into a linear one; verify any candidate solution by direct substitution.
- **Common traps**: Dividing by a factor and silently discarding the equilibrium/constant solution it removes; using q(x) instead of the coefficient p(x) inside the integrating factor; declaring an equation exact without actually computing both M_y and N_x; misidentifying "homogeneous" (the substitution sense, f(tx,ty)=f(x,y)) versus "homogeneous" (the zero-right-hand-side linear sense); applying undetermined coefficients or Laplace to a first-order equation that simply separates.
- **Student output**: A methods-comparison problem set that, for each item, names the equation type, justifies the chosen method, solves carefully, and confirms the result by back-substitution.

## Chapter 3: Second-Order Linear Equations
- **Core questions**: Why does superposition make linear equations tractable, and how does the characteristic equation encode every solution of the constant-coefficient homogeneous case? How do we build a particular solution when forcing is present, and how do we know our two homogeneous solutions are genuinely independent?
- **Key concepts**: Linear second-order IVPs and the superposition principle; homogeneous constant-coefficient equations and the characteristic equation ar² + br + c = 0; the three root cases — distinct real (C₁e^(r₁x)+C₂e^(r₂x)), repeated real r (e^(rx) and xe^(rx)), and complex α±βi (e^(αx)cos βx and e^(αx)sin βx via Euler's formula); the Wronskian as a test for linear independence and a fundamental solution set; nonhomogeneous equations and the general-solution structure y = y_h + y_p; method of undetermined coefficients (matching the forcing form, with the multiply-by-x fix when a guess overlaps a homogeneous solution); variation of parameters using the Wronskian when the forcing does not fit a trial form; a preview of boundary-value problems.
- **Applied skills**: Solve homogeneous equations in all three root cases and write real-valued solutions for complex roots; select a correct undetermined-coefficients guess and apply the resonance-overlap correction; execute variation of parameters for an arbitrary forcing term; compute a Wronskian to confirm independence; impose two initial conditions to determine C₁ and C₂.
- **Common traps**: Collapsing distinct roots r₁, r₂ into a single e^((r₁+r₂)x) term instead of two independent exponentials; forgetting the extra factor of x for a repeated root, or for a forcing term that duplicates a homogeneous solution; leaving complex exponentials in the answer instead of converting to sine/cosine; applying initial conditions to y_h before adding y_p; mismatching the trial form to the forcing (e.g., guessing A sin for an exponential input).
- **Student output**: A second-order equation packet with exact solutions, method-selection notes, and explicit verification that both the differential equation and the initial conditions are satisfied.

## Chapter 4: Oscillations, Forcing, and Engineering Models
- **Core questions**: How do the physical parameters of a mass-spring-damper or RLC circuit determine its qualitative behavior, and what is the precise meaning of underdamped, critically damped, and overdamped? Why does resonance occur, and what distinguishes the transient from the steady-state response?
- **Key concepts**: The mass-spring-damper equation mx'' + cx' + kx = F(t) and its electrical analog L q'' + R q' + q/C = E(t); free versus forced motion; damping classification from the discriminant c² − 4mk (overdamped > 0, critically damped = 0, underdamped < 0) and the corresponding root structure; natural frequency ω₀ = √(k/m); amplitude, phase, and the envelope of decaying oscillation; beats when the forcing frequency is near ω₀; pure resonance and unbounded growth for an undamped system forced at ω₀; the bounded resonant peak in damped systems; transient (homogeneous, decaying) versus steady-state (particular, persistent) response; mechanical-electrical analogy and dimensional checks.
- **Applied skills**: Translate physical parameters (mass, damping constant, spring constant; or L, R, C) into the governing ODE and its characteristic roots; classify the damping regime and predict the motion's shape; compute the steady-state forced response and its amplitude as a function of forcing frequency; identify resonance and explain it in terms of forcing frequency relative to ω₀; separate transient from steady-state in a full solution.
- **Common traps**: Confusing the damping coefficient c with the damping ratio or with ω₀; calling a system "resonant" whenever forcing is present, rather than near the natural frequency; expecting unbounded resonance in a damped system (damping bounds the peak); dropping the transient because "it dies out" before checking the time scale of the question; sign or unit errors when mapping between the mechanical and electrical analogies.
- **Student output**: An oscillation lab that connects equations, parameter values, solution forms, graphs, and physical interpretation for one mechanical and one electrical system, including a resonance/frequency-response discussion.

## Chapter 5: Laplace Transforms and Discontinuous Inputs
- **Core questions**: How does the Laplace transform convert a differential equation with initial conditions into algebra, and why is it the natural tool for inputs that switch on, switch off, or strike instantaneously? How do shifting theorems, convolution, and the transfer-function viewpoint fit together?
- **Key concepts**: The Laplace transform L{f}(s) = ∫₀^∞ e^(-st) f(t) dt and its linearity; transform tables for e^(at), t^n, sin/cos; the derivative rules L{y'} = sY(s) − y(0) and L{y''} = s²Y(s) − s y(0) − y'(0), which build initial conditions directly into the algebra; inverse transforms via partial fractions; the first shifting theorem (s-shift, e^(at)f(t)) and the second shifting theorem (t-shift with the Heaviside step u(t−a)); piecewise/discontinuous forcing expressed with step functions; the Dirac delta δ(t−a) as an idealized impulse and L{δ(t−a)} = e^(-as); convolution (f∗g) and L{f∗g} = F(s)G(s); the transfer function H(s) and impulse response as the system's signature.
- **Applied skills**: Compute a transform from the definition for a basic function; solve a linear IVP entirely in the s-domain and invert with partial fractions; rewrite a piecewise forcing function using Heaviside steps and apply the t-shift theorem correctly; model and solve an impulse-driven system with the delta function; express a forced response as a convolution of input with impulse response.
- **Common traps**: Forgetting the initial-value terms in L{y'} and L{y''} (the whole advantage of the method is that they appear automatically); confusing the s-shift and t-shift theorems, or omitting the u(t−a) factor when applying the second shifting theorem; mishandling the e^(-as) factor on inversion; treating δ(t) as an ordinary function rather than an idealized impulse; algebra slips in partial-fraction decomposition that quietly break the inverse transform.
- **Student output**: A Laplace-transform problem set showing transform-domain algebra, inverse work, and graphs explaining how a step or impulse input shapes the response, including at least one convolution computation.

## Chapter 6: Linear Systems and Phase Plane Analysis
- **Core questions**: How does any higher-order linear equation become a first-order system x' = Ax, and how do the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of A determine the geometry of the phase portrait and the long-run fate of trajectories?
- **Key concepts**: First-order systems and the conversion of an n-th order equation to n first-order equations; matrix form x' = Ax; eigenvalues and eigenvectors as the natural modes; solution structure for real distinct eigenvalues (e^(λt)v), repeated eigenvalues (generalized eigenvectors), and complex eigenvalues (rotation plus exponential growth/decay); the fundamental matrix and the matrix exponential e^(At); phase portraits and the classification of the origin — node (real same-sign), saddle (real opposite-sign, always unstable), spiral (complex with nonzero real part), and center (pure imaginary); stability read directly from the signs of the real parts of the eigenvalues.
- **Applied skills**: Convert a second-order equation into a 2×2 first-order system; find eigenvalues and eigenvectors and assemble the general solution; classify the critical point at the origin and determine its stability; sketch a phase portrait consistent with the eigenstructure and interpret trajectory behavior; recognize complex eigenvalues as spiraling motion with rate set by the real part and rotation by the imaginary part.
- **Common traps**: Confusing a stable node (negative real eigenvalues, no rotation) with a stable spiral (complex eigenvalues, negative real part); forgetting that a saddle is always unstable regardless of the other eigenvalue's sign; drawing trajectories that ignore the eigenvector directions; mistaking a center (purely imaginary, neutrally stable closed orbits) for an asymptotically stable spiral; arithmetic errors in det(A − λI) that flip the stability conclusion.
- **Student output**: A phase-plane portfolio with computed eigenvalue solutions, critical-point classifications, software or hand-drawn phase portraits, and written explanations of each system's long-term behavior.

## Chapter 7: Nonlinear Systems and Qualitative Analysis
- **Core questions**: When closed-form solutions are out of reach, how do nullclines, equilibria, and linearization let us predict a nonlinear system's behavior? When is the linearization trustworthy, and when does it fail?
- **Key concepts**: Autonomous nonlinear systems and their equilibria (where both rates vanish); nullclines (curves where one component's rate is zero) and the flow directions they partition; local linearization via the Jacobian matrix evaluated at each equilibrium; stability inferred from the Jacobian's eigenvalues; the limitation that linearization is inconclusive at a center (purely imaginary eigenvalues) where nonlinear terms decide the outcome; canonical models — logistic with harvesting, Lotka-Volterra predator-prey, competing species, the SIR epidemic model, and reversible chemical kinetics; bifurcation as a qualitative change in equilibria/stability when a parameter crosses a threshold.
- **Applied skills**: Find all equilibria of a 2-D nonlinear system; draw nullclines and use them to infer the direction of motion in each region of the phase plane; compute the Jacobian, evaluate it at an equilibrium, and classify stability from its eigenvalues; interpret a parameter change in a population or epidemic model (e.g., how R₀ in SIR determines whether an outbreak grows); identify a borderline case where linearization cannot decide.
- **Common traps**: Treating the linearization as exact rather than valid only near the equilibrium; trusting linearization at a center, where it is provably inconclusive; finding only some equilibria (missing intersections of nullclines) and drawing wrong conclusions; sign errors in the Jacobian that invert stability; reading a predator-prey center as a stable fixed point rather than as sustained oscillation.
- **Student output**: A qualitative-analysis memo that uses nullclines, equilibrium classification, and parameter interpretation to explain a nonlinear model's behavior without relying on closed-form solutions, flagging any cases where linearization is indecisive.

## Chapter 8: Numerical Methods, Series Solutions, and Synthesis
- **Core questions**: When analytic methods fail, how do we approximate solutions reliably, and how do we know how much to trust the result? How do series methods extend our reach near ordinary and singular points, and how do all the course's methods reconcile on a single model?
- **Key concepts**: Local versus global truncation error and the role of step size; Euler's method (first-order, global error O(h)); improved/modified Euler (Heun, second-order); the classical fourth-order Runge-Kutta method RK4 (global error O(h⁴)) and why higher order buys accuracy per step; numerical stability and stiff equations where tiny steps are forced; power-series solutions about an ordinary point with coefficients fixed by a recurrence relation; regular singular points and a preview of the Frobenius method; Bessel and Legendre equations as named series-solution cases; the discipline of cross-checking numerical, analytic, and qualitative results against each other.
- **Applied skills**: Implement Euler and RK4 for an IVP and compare global error as step size shrinks (confirming the expected order of convergence); recognize a stiff problem from spurious blow-up under an explicit method; assume y = Σ aₙxⁿ and derive the recurrence relation for the coefficients near an ordinary point; decide when numerical evidence should be trusted and when it should be treated cautiously (near singularities, with stiffness, or when it contradicts the eigenvalue prediction).
- **Common traps**: Believing a smaller step size eliminates error entirely rather than reducing it; expecting Euler and RK4 to converge at the same rate (they differ by orders of magnitude); ignoring stiffness and blaming the model when the explicit method explodes; algebra errors in shifting series indices that corrupt the recurrence relation; reporting a numerical curve that disagrees with the analytic or phase-plane prediction without investigating the discrepancy.
- **Student output**: A computational lab (Python, Julia, MATLAB, Octave, or a CAS) that solves and visualizes an ODE model, compares Euler against RK4 with an error-versus-step-size study, and discusses numerical reliability and model assumptions.

## Capstone
Students complete a differential equations modeling project organized around a single dynamic system they choose and own end to end. A complete project must: (1) state the modeling assumptions, variables, parameters, and units, and explain why the chosen ODE or system of ODEs is appropriate; (2) write the initial-value problem precisely; (3) perform a qualitative analysis — equilibria, stability, and, for systems, a phase portrait or nullcline argument — and predict the long-run behavior before computing; (4) solve analytically wherever feasible (separation, linear/Laplace methods, or eigenvalue methods); (5) compute a numerical solution with an appropriate method and an error/step-size check; (6) reconcile the analytic, qualitative, and numerical results, explicitly resolving any disagreement; and (7) interpret the outcome in the original context, including a sensitivity comment on how a key parameter changes the conclusion.

Suitable topics include logistic population dynamics with harvesting, an SIR or SEIR epidemic model, single- or multi-compartment pharmacokinetics, Newtonian heating/cooling, chemical mixing in coupled tanks, a damped/forced mechanical oscillator, an RLC circuit driven by a step or impulse, Lotka-Volterra predator-prey or competing-species dynamics, or an economic-adjustment/control model responding to discontinuous input. The deliverable is a written report plus the supporting computation; assessment weighs model formulation, mathematical correctness, qualitative insight, numerical validation, contextual interpretation, and clarity of communication in roughly equal measure.

## Assessment Ideas
- Weekly written problem sets graded for setup, equation classification, method choice, algebra, initial-condition use, verification by substitution, and interpretation.
- Short quizzes on classification, separable equations, integrating factors, exactness, characteristic equations, Laplace transform rules, eigenvalue classification of equilibria, and numerical-method steps.
- Modeling labs requiring stated assumptions, derived equations, chosen units, plotted solutions, and parameter interpretation.
- Computational assignments comparing slope fields, phase portraits, and numerical approximations, including an explicit Euler-versus-RK4 error study.
- Capstone rubric covering model formulation, mathematical correctness, qualitative analysis, numerical validation, application interpretation, and communication.
