# Microeconomics
**ID**: `microeconomics` - **Discipline**: Economics - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: AP Microeconomics CED; typical first-year university principles course; Mankiw *Principles of Economics*, Krugman & Wells *Microeconomics*, and OpenStax *Principles of Microeconomics 3e* equivalents

## Course Aim
This course teaches students to reason about choice under scarcity: how households, firms, and institutions decide, how markets coordinate those decisions without anyone being in charge, when markets fail to produce good outcomes, and how policy reshapes incentives and welfare. By the end, a student should be able to take an ordinary situation — a surge in rent, a new soda tax, a ticket-resale ban, a wage hike — and translate it into supply and demand, trace the chain of cause and effect on price and quantity, and say who gains, who loses, and by how much.

The intellectual spine of the course is **marginal thinking**: rational actors weigh the benefit of one more unit against the cost of one more unit, and equilibrium is where those margins balance. Almost every result in the course is a marginal condition in disguise — a consumer equalizes marginal utility per dollar across goods, a competitive firm produces where price equals marginal cost, a monopolist where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, a firm hires labor until the wage equals marginal revenue product. We make this explicit so students see one idea, not eight unrelated rules. Alongside it we drill the **comparative-statics habit**: state the shock, ask which curve moves and in which direction, and read off the new equilibrium before reaching for a number.

We also insist on the line between **positive and normative** claims. "A binding price ceiling causes a shortage" is a prediction the model makes; "rent control is unfair" is a value judgment the model cannot settle. Students learn to keep the two apart, to name the assumptions a result rests on (perfect information, no externalities, price-taking behavior), and to recognize that efficiency and equity are different goals that policy must often trade against each other. Every major idea is anchored to a graph the student can draw, a real story they can recognize, and a "what changes if…" question they can answer.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve scarcity, opportunity cost, comparative advantage, the production possibilities curve, supply and demand, elasticity, consumer and producer surplus, taxes and subsidies, price ceilings and floors, utility and consumer choice, production functions and cost curves, perfect competition, monopoly, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, game theory, factor and labor markets, externalities, public goods, common-pool resources, asymmetric information, or inequality and market failure.

Three design commitments shape every chapter. First, **every concept connects to a graph, a story, and a comparative-statics question** — a student who cannot draw the curve, point to a real example, and predict the effect of a shock has not yet learned it. Second, we drill **direction and mechanism**, not just definitions: a student should not merely know "a tax creates deadweight loss" but be able to explain that the tax drives a wedge between what buyers pay and sellers receive, kills the mutually beneficial trades whose value lay between marginal cost and willingness to pay, and that the loss grows with elasticity. Third, we **name the common traps explicitly**, because intro micro is unusually rich in plausible-sounding errors — shift-versus-movement, sunk-cost reasoning, confusing accounting profit with economic profit, reading absolute advantage as comparative advantage, and treating a transfer as a deadweight loss. Naming the trap is half of teaching the concept.

Worked numbers (a federal minimum wage, illustrative tax rates) reflect the US institutional setting as of 2025-2026; instructors elsewhere should substitute local figures. The economics, not the numbers, is the point.

## Chapter 1: Scarcity, Choice, and Comparative Advantage
**Core questions**: Why does every choice have a cost even when nothing changes hands? What does a production possibilities curve reveal about efficiency, tradeoffs, and growth? Why can two parties both gain from trade even when one is better at producing everything?
**Key concepts**: Scarcity, resources, the economic problem, opportunity cost, marginal benefit and marginal cost, the production possibilities curve (PPC), efficient, inefficient, and unattainable points, the bowed-out shape and increasing opportunity cost, economic growth as an outward shift, specialization, absolute advantage, comparative advantage, terms of trade, gains from trade, and positive versus normative claims.
**Applied skills**: Compute opportunity cost from a PPC or a production table; classify points as efficient, inefficient, or unattainable; determine which party has the comparative advantage in each good and find a mutually beneficial range of trade terms; explain why a straight-line PPC implies constant opportunity cost while a bowed one implies increasing cost; sort statements into positive and normative.
**Common traps**: Reading **absolute** advantage (more total output) as **comparative** advantage (lower opportunity cost) — the party that is best at everything still gains by specializing where its opportunity cost is lowest; treating opportunity cost as the money spent rather than the *next best alternative forgone*; assuming a point inside the PPC is impossible rather than merely inefficient; smuggling a value judgment ("should") into what is really a prediction.
**Student output**: A trade-and-scarcity memo that uses a PPC and a comparative-advantage table to recommend who should produce what, and quotes a range of terms of trade that leaves both parties better off.

## Chapter 2: Supply, Demand, and Market Equilibrium
**Core questions**: What determines the position of a demand or supply curve, and what moves you along one versus shifting the whole curve? How does an unmanaged market settle on a single price, and what happens when it is pushed away from it? How do prices act as signals that coordinate strangers?
**Key concepts**: The law of demand and law of supply, demand and supply curves, the five demand shifters (income, prices of related goods, tastes, expectations, number of buyers) and the supply shifters (input prices, technology, expectations, number of sellers), normal versus inferior goods, substitutes and complements, movement along versus shift of a curve, market equilibrium, shortage and surplus, the price mechanism, and comparative statics.
**Applied skills**: Predict the new equilibrium price and quantity after a demand or supply shift; distinguish a change in quantity demanded (movement) from a change in demand (shift); work out simultaneous shifts and identify which of price or quantity becomes indeterminate; explain how a shortage or surplus generates the pressure that restores equilibrium.
**Common traps**: The **shift-versus-movement** error — a change in the good's *own* price moves you along the curve; everything else shifts it; confusing a fall in supply with a fall in quantity supplied; assuming both price and quantity always move in a definite direction when two curves shift at once (one of them becomes ambiguous); thinking a price is "too high" or "too low" without a benchmark — equilibrium is defined by quantity demanded equaling quantity supplied, not by fairness.
**Student output**: A market-event brief that takes one real-world change (a frost, a viral trend, a new tariff), draws the before-and-after supply-and-demand graph, and explains in words why price and quantity move as they do.

## Chapter 3: Elasticity, Surplus, Taxes, and Price Controls
**Core questions**: How responsive are buyers and sellers to a change in price, and why does that responsiveness decide everything about a policy's effect? Who really pays a tax — the side legally charged, or someone else? Why do price ceilings and floors, meant to help, routinely create the shortages and surpluses they were supposed to prevent?
**Key concepts**: Price elasticity of demand (midpoint method), elastic, inelastic, and unit-elastic demand, the total-revenue test, determinants of elasticity (substitutes, necessity, share of budget, time horizon), income elasticity, cross-price elasticity, price elasticity of supply, consumer surplus, producer surplus, total surplus and allocative efficiency, the tax wedge, statutory versus economic tax incidence, deadweight loss, subsidies, binding versus non-binding price ceilings and floors, rent control, the minimum wage, and black markets.
**Applied skills**: Compute elasticity with the midpoint formula `%ΔQ / %ΔP` using average base values; classify a good as elastic or inelastic and predict whether a price rise raises or lowers total revenue; identify whether a buyer or seller bears more of a tax from relative elasticities; measure consumer surplus, producer surplus, tax revenue, and deadweight loss off a graph; show why a binding ceiling creates a shortage and a binding floor a surplus.
**Common traps**: Confusing **statutory incidence** (who hands over the money) with **economic incidence** (who bears the burden) — burden falls on the *more inelastic* side regardless of who is taxed; treating tax revenue (a transfer) as part of deadweight loss; computing elasticity off the starting value instead of the midpoint, so the answer differs depending on the direction of the price change; calling a non-binding ceiling (set above equilibrium) a cause of shortage; assuming a minimum wage always cuts employment without checking whether demand is elastic or the market is a monopsony.
**Student output**: A policy comparison note that evaluates one intervention — a tax, subsidy, ceiling, or floor — using a labeled welfare graph, an incidence verdict justified by elasticities, and an explicit estimate of the deadweight loss.

## Chapter 4: Consumer Choice and Decision Rules
**Core questions**: How does a rational consumer spend a fixed budget to get the most satisfaction? Why does the law of demand fall out of diminishing marginal utility? Where do real people predictably violate the rational model, and does that break the predictions or just refine them?
**Key concepts**: Utility, total versus marginal utility, diminishing marginal utility, the budget constraint and opportunity set, the equimarginal principle (marginal utility per dollar equalized across goods), the substitution effect and income effect, normal and inferior goods, indifference curves and the marginal rate of substitution, consumer optimum, sunk cost, framing, and the behavioral critique of pure rationality.
**Applied skills**: Apply the equimarginal rule to allocate a budget and check whether a bundle is optimal; interpret how a price or income change pivots or shifts the budget line; decompose a price change into substitution and income effects qualitatively; spot sunk-cost and framing errors in a real decision; contrast a rational-choice prediction with a behavioral one.
**Common traps**: **Sunk-cost reasoning** — letting money already spent (a non-refundable ticket, a half-eaten meal) drive a forward-looking choice that should depend only on marginal benefit versus marginal cost from here; equalizing *marginal utility* across goods instead of *marginal utility per dollar* (the price matters); confusing total utility (still rising) with marginal utility (falling) when describing diminishing returns; assuming "irrational" behavioral findings overturn the model, when most just relocate the constraint or the perceived payoff.
**Student output**: A consumer-choice analysis of a genuine recurring decision — subscriptions, transport, food, study time — that names the budget, applies marginal reasoning, and flags at least one bias the student notices in their own past choices.

## Chapter 5: Production, Costs, and Perfect Competition
**Core questions**: How does a firm turn inputs into output, and why does cost eventually rise faster than output? When should a firm keep producing at a loss, and when should it close? Why does a perfectly competitive industry drive long-run economic profit to zero, and is that a bad thing?
**Key concepts**: The production function, marginal and average product, the law of diminishing marginal returns, fixed and variable costs, total, average (ATC, AVC, AFC), and marginal cost, the U-shaped cost curves and why MC cuts ATC and AVC at their minimums, economies and diseconomies of scale, the long-run average cost curve, the difference between accounting and economic profit, price-taking behavior, the marginal output rule (P = MC), the short-run shutdown rule, entry and exit, and long-run zero economic profit.
**Applied skills**: Derive marginal and average cost from a total-cost table; identify the profit-maximizing quantity where P = MC and read profit or loss off the graph; apply the shutdown rule (produce in the short run only if P ≥ AVC; exit in the long run if P < ATC); explain why MC intersects ATC at its minimum; trace the entry-and-exit adjustment that returns a competitive market to zero economic profit after a demand shift.
**Common traps**: Confusing **accounting profit** with **economic profit** — zero economic profit means the firm is covering its opportunity cost (a "normal" return), not that it is failing; applying the *short-run* shutdown rule (P vs AVC, because fixed costs are sunk) to a *long-run* exit decision (P vs ATC); thinking a firm maximizes profit by minimizing average cost rather than by setting MC equal to MR/price; treating fixed costs as relevant to the marginal production decision (they are not).
**Student output**: A firm-cost case study with a cost table, a labeled cost-curve graph, a profit-or-loss decision at the prevailing price, and a prediction of what happens to the industry in the long run.

## Chapter 6: Imperfect Competition and Game Theory
**Core questions**: Why does a firm with market power produce *less* and charge *more* than a competitive industry, and why is that inefficient even when no one is cheated? When can a seller profitably charge different buyers different prices? When firms are few enough to watch each other, how does strategy replace the impersonal market?
**Key concepts**: Monopoly, barriers to entry, market power, marginal revenue below price for a price-maker, the MR = MC rule, monopoly deadweight loss, the Lerner index `(P − MC)/P = −1/ε` (where ε is the price elasticity of demand, so a more inelastic demand supports a larger markup), price discrimination (first, second, third degree) and its conditions, natural monopoly and regulation, monopolistic competition, product differentiation, excess capacity, oligopoly, interdependence, the payoff matrix, dominant strategy, Nash equilibrium, the prisoner's dilemma, collusion and cartels, and tit-for-tat in repeated games.
**Applied skills**: Locate a monopolist's profit-maximizing quantity (MR = MC), read price off the demand curve, and shade the deadweight loss; explain why MR lies below price for any downward-sloping demand; check the three conditions for price discrimination (market power, identifiable segments, no resale); solve a 2×2 game for dominant strategies and Nash equilibrium; explain why a cartel's Nash equilibrium is to cheat, making collusion unstable.
**Common traps**: Assuming a monopolist can charge "any price it likes" — it is still bound by the demand curve and sets MR = MC; treating monopoly profit (a transfer from consumers) as the deadweight loss, when the loss is the triangle of trades that never happen because P > MC; expecting the socially efficient outcome from a Nash equilibrium (the prisoner's dilemma shows it can be jointly worse); calling any "best-against-one-opponent-move" choice a dominant strategy when dominance must hold against *every* opponent move.
**Student output**: A market-structure dossier on one real industry that classifies its structure, presents evidence of competition or market power (concentration, pricing, barriers), models one strategic interaction as a payoff matrix, and assesses the impact on consumers.

## Chapter 7: Factor Markets, Labor, and Inequality
**Core questions**: Why is the demand for labor "derived," and what makes a worker worth hiring? How does a competitive labor market set the wage, and how does a single dominant employer (a monopsony) suppress it? What do the Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient actually measure, and what do they leave out?
**Key concepts**: Derived demand, marginal product of labor, marginal revenue product (MRP = MP × MR or MP × P in competition), marginal factor cost (MFC), the hire-until-MRP-equals-wage rule, competitive labor supply and demand, monopsony and the wage gap below MRP, the minimum wage in competitive versus monopsony markets, human capital, compensating differentials, labor-market discrimination, the Lorenz curve, the Gini coefficient (0 = perfect equality, 1 = perfect inequality), poverty measures, and competing explanations of wages (productivity, bargaining power, institutions).
**Applied skills**: Compute MRP from a productivity table and find the profit-maximizing hiring level (wage = MRP in competition; MRP = MFC under monopsony); explain why a monopsonist pays below MRP and hires fewer workers, and why a minimum wage can *raise* employment there; interpret a Lorenz curve and the Gini coefficient and rank distributions by inequality; distinguish productivity-based, bargaining-based, and institutional accounts of a wage gap.
**Common traps**: Applying the competitive prediction "minimum wage cuts jobs" to a monopsony, where it can raise both wages and employment; confusing **marginal factor cost** with the wage under monopsony — hiring one more worker forces a raise for *all* workers, so MFC exceeds the wage; reading a high Gini as a statement about poverty (it measures dispersion, not living standards); treating the Lorenz curve's distance from the 45° line as anything other than inequality.
**Student output**: A labor-market analysis of one occupation or sector that lays out labor demand (MRP) and supply, identifies any market power on either side, evaluates a wage policy, and comments on how the result shows up in the distribution.

## Chapter 8: Market Failure, Public Policy, and Micro Frontiers
**Core questions**: When does a free market, working exactly as designed, still produce too much or too little? Why are clean air and national defense underprovided while overfishing and traffic are overconsumed? How does hidden information unravel markets before any trade even happens?
**Key concepts**: Negative and positive externalities, marginal private versus marginal social cost and benefit, the externality wedge, the Pigouvian tax and subsidy, tradable permits (cap-and-trade), the Coase theorem and the role of property rights and transaction costs, the rivalry-excludability grid (private, club, common-pool, public goods), the free-rider problem, the tragedy of the commons, asymmetric information, adverse selection (the "market for lemons"), moral hazard, signaling and screening, public-choice limits on government, and an introduction to mechanism design.
**Applied skills**: Graph an externality, mark the private and social optima, and size the welfare gain from a corrective tax or subsidy set equal to the marginal external cost or benefit; classify a good by rivalry and excludability and predict its provision problem; explain the tragedy of the commons and a property-rights or quota fix; distinguish adverse selection (hidden *type*, before the deal) from moral hazard (hidden *action*, after the deal) and name a remedy for each.
**Common traps**: Confusing **adverse selection** (hidden information *before* a transaction — lemons, sicker people buying more insurance) with **moral hazard** (hidden *action after* — driving recklessly once insured); thinking the optimal level of pollution is zero rather than where marginal social benefit equals marginal social cost; assuming a Pigouvian tax must exceed the external cost (it should *equal* it at the optimum); treating a public good's free-rider problem as solvable by a private market; forgetting that government intervention has its own failures (public-choice problems), so "market failure" does not automatically justify a policy.
**Student output**: A market-failure policy memo that diagnoses the specific failure, compares at least two interventions (e.g., a tax versus a permit market, mandate versus subsidy), and names the likely winners, losers, and unintended effects of each.

## Capstone
Students complete a microeconomic policy-and-market analysis of one contested real-world issue: housing and rent control, rideshare surge pricing, college tuition, healthcare and insurance markets, carbon pricing, gig-labor platforms, grocery prices, broadband access, or concert and sports-ticket resale. The deliverable must (1) build the relevant supply-and-demand or market-structure model with a labeled graph; (2) carry out an explicit welfare analysis — consumer surplus, producer surplus, deadweight loss, and incidence — for the status quo and at least one proposed intervention; (3) trace the incentive effects and at least one likely unintended consequence; and (4) maintain a clean separation between what the model *predicts* (positive) and what the student *recommends* (normative), stating the assumptions on which the prediction depends. Strong projects identify where the standard model is weakest for their case (information problems, behavioral effects, distributional concerns) rather than pretending the textbook result is the whole story.

## Assessment Ideas
Use graph-drawing drills (shift the right curve, the right way), short comparative-statics scenarios, calculation checks (elasticity, surplus, MRP, the multiplier of a tax wedge), market-event memos, welfare-and-incidence policy briefs, payoff-matrix puzzles, and the final applied analysis. Assess graph accuracy, the correctness of the mechanism in words, the choice of model, the consistency of marginal reasoning, and the student's ability to name the assumptions a result depends on and to keep positive and normative claims apart.
