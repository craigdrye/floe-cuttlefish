# Microeconomics
**ID**: `microeconomics` - **Discipline**: Economics - **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18-19)
**Aligns with**: AP Microeconomics CED; typical intro university course; Mankiw, Krugman, and OpenStax equivalents

## Course Aim
Teach students to reason about choice under scarcity: how households, firms, and institutions make decisions, how markets coordinate those decisions, when markets fail, and how policy changes incentives and welfare. Students practice marginal thinking, graph-based reasoning, and the distinction between positive analysis and normative judgment.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they involve scarcity, opportunity cost, comparative advantage, supply and demand, elasticity, surplus, taxes, price controls, production costs, perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly, game theory, factor markets, externalities, public goods, inequality, or market failure. Every major idea should connect to a graph, a story, and a comparative-statics question.

## Chapter 1: Scarcity, Choice, and Comparative Advantage
**Key concepts**: Scarcity, resources, opportunity cost, marginal benefit, marginal cost, production possibilities curve, efficiency, specialization, absolute advantage, comparative advantage, gains from trade, economic systems, and positive versus normative claims.
**Practice questions**: Compute opportunity cost from a PPC; identify efficient, inefficient, and unattainable points; determine comparative advantage from a table; explain why voluntary trade can help both sides; classify claims as positive or normative.
**Student output**: A trade-and-scarcity memo using a PPC and comparative advantage table to recommend a production choice.

## Chapter 2: Supply, Demand, and Market Equilibrium
**Key concepts**: Demand, supply, market equilibrium, shifters, movement along a curve, curve shifts, shortage, surplus, price signal, consumer surplus, producer surplus, total surplus, allocative efficiency, and comparative statics.
**Practice questions**: Predict equilibrium price and quantity after demand or supply shifts; distinguish shifts from movements; calculate surplus from a graph; analyze simultaneous shifts; explain how prices coordinate buyers and sellers.
**Student output**: A market event brief that uses a graph and written explanation to show how one real-world change affects price, quantity, and surplus.

## Chapter 3: Elasticity, Taxes, Subsidies, and Price Controls
**Key concepts**: Price elasticity of demand, total revenue test, income elasticity, cross-price elasticity, price elasticity of supply, tax incidence, deadweight loss, subsidy, price ceiling, price floor, rent control, minimum wage, and black markets.
**Practice questions**: Use the midpoint formula; classify elasticity; predict revenue changes; determine who bears a tax; identify welfare losses from taxes or price controls; explain why elasticity shapes policy effects.
**Student output**: A policy comparison note evaluating a tax, subsidy, ceiling, or floor with graph, incidence, and welfare language.

## Chapter 4: Consumer Choice and Decision Rules
**Key concepts**: Utility, marginal utility, diminishing marginal utility, budget constraint, opportunity set, marginal utility per dollar, income effect, substitution effect, indifference curves, and behavioral friction.
**Practice questions**: Apply the equimarginal rule; interpret a budget line; explain how price or income changes affect choices; identify sunk cost and framing errors; compare rational-choice and behavioral explanations.
**Student output**: A consumer-choice analysis of a real decision, such as subscriptions, transport, food, or study time, using marginal reasoning and constraints.

## Chapter 5: Production, Costs, and Perfect Competition
**Key concepts**: Production function, marginal product, diminishing marginal returns, fixed cost, variable cost, total cost, average cost, marginal cost, long-run cost, economies of scale, perfect competition, shutdown rule, entry, exit, and zero economic profit.
**Practice questions**: Derive cost curves from a table; identify profit, loss, shutdown, and exit regions; explain why MC crosses ATC at its minimum; analyze long-run adjustment after a market demand shift.
**Student output**: A firm-cost case study with table, graph, profit decision, and long-run prediction.

## Chapter 6: Imperfect Competition and Game Theory
**Key concepts**: Monopoly, barriers to entry, market power, marginal revenue, price discrimination, natural monopoly, regulation, monopolistic competition, product differentiation, oligopoly, payoff matrix, dominant strategy, Nash equilibrium, collusion, and prisoner's dilemma.
**Practice questions**: Compare market structures; find monopoly price, quantity, profit, and deadweight loss; identify conditions for price discrimination; solve a two-player payoff matrix; explain why collusion can be unstable.
**Student output**: A market-structure dossier on one industry, including evidence of competition, market power, strategy, and consumer impact.

## Chapter 7: Factor Markets, Labor, and Inequality
**Key concepts**: Derived demand, marginal revenue product, marginal factor cost, labor demand, labor supply, monopsony, minimum wage, human capital, discrimination, Lorenz curve, Gini coefficient, poverty, and income distribution.
**Practice questions**: Find the profit-maximizing hiring level; compare competitive labor markets and monopsony; analyze a minimum wage in different contexts; interpret a Lorenz curve; distinguish productivity, bargaining, and institutional explanations for wages.
**Student output**: A labor-market analysis of a chosen occupation or sector with demand, supply, wage, and policy considerations.

## Chapter 8: Market Failure, Public Policy, and Micro Frontiers
**Key concepts**: Negative externality, positive externality, marginal social cost, marginal social benefit, Pigouvian tax, subsidy, tradable permit, Coase theorem, public good, common-pool resource, free rider, adverse selection, moral hazard, information asymmetry, public choice, and mechanism design.
**Practice questions**: Graph an externality; design a corrective tax or subsidy; classify goods by rivalry and excludability; explain the tragedy of the commons; identify moral hazard and adverse selection; evaluate policy tradeoffs.
**Student output**: A market-failure policy memo that diagnoses the failure, compares at least two interventions, and explains likely winners, losers, and unintended effects.

## Capstone
Students complete a microeconomic policy and market analysis of a real issue: housing, rideshare pricing, college tuition, healthcare, pollution, labor platforms, grocery prices, broadband, sports tickets, or carbon permits. The project must use graphs, welfare analysis, incentives, and a clear separation between what the model predicts and what the student thinks should happen.

## Assessment Ideas
Use graph drills, scenario explanations, short calculation checks, market-event memos, policy briefs, payoff-matrix puzzles, and a final applied analysis. Assess graph accuracy, verbal interpretation, model choice, marginal reasoning, and the ability to name assumptions.
