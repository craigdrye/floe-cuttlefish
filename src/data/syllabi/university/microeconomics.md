# Microeconomics
**ID**: `microeconomics` · **Discipline**: Economics · **Stage**: University Freshman (ages 18–19)
**Aligns with**: AP Microeconomics CED (Units 1–6); typical intro university course (ECON 101 / ECON 1A); Mankiw Ch. 1–17 / Krugman & Wells equivalents

## Course Philosophy
Microeconomics is the study of *choice under scarcity* — how individuals and firms allocate limited resources, and how those decisions aggregate into prices, quantities, and welfare. The most important habit the course builds is **marginal thinking**: every decision is "should I do *one more*?", not "should I do this at all?". The second most important habit is **distinguishing positive from normative** claims. Students often arrive with strong moral intuitions about markets, and the course's job is not to dissolve them but to give students the analytical machinery (supply/demand, elasticity, surplus, externalities, market failures) to discuss those intuitions precisely. Internally, we should treat graphs as the dialect of economics — every concept must be tied to a graph, a story, and a comparative-statics question ("what happens if X rises?").

## Module 1: Basic Economic Concepts (Unit 1, 5–10%)
- **Scarcity** as the foundational economic problem; resources (land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship); economics as the science of allocation
- **Opportunity cost**: the value of the next-best alternative forgone; why "free" isn't free
- **Marginal analysis** and the marginal benefit = marginal cost decision rule
- **Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)**: efficient, inefficient, and unattainable points; the bowed-out shape and increasing opportunity cost
- Shifts in the PPC: change in resources, technology, trade
- **Comparative advantage** vs absolute advantage: who should produce what; gains from specialization and trade; terms of trade
- **Economic systems**: traditional, command, market, mixed; the role of property rights and prices as signals
- Positive vs normative economics; the danger of confusing "is" and "ought"
- **Questions**: compute opportunity cost, identify whether a point is on/inside/outside a PPC, determine comparative advantage from a table, classify statements as positive or normative

## Module 2: Supply and Demand (Unit 2, 12–15%)
- **Demand** and the law of demand; the demand curve as marginal benefit
- Determinants/shifters of demand: tastes, income (normal/inferior goods), prices of related goods (substitutes/complements), expectations, number of buyers
- **Supply** and the law of supply; the supply curve as marginal cost
- Shifters of supply: input prices, technology, taxes/subsidies on producers, expectations, number of sellers
- **Market equilibrium**: Pₑ and Qₑ; surplus (above) and shortage (below); price as the mechanism that clears markets
- Comparative statics: simultaneous demand and supply shifts and the indeterminacy of one of P or Q
- **Consumer surplus, producer surplus, total surplus**; allocative efficiency at equilibrium
- **Price ceilings** (rent control) and **price floors** (minimum wage): shortages, surpluses, deadweight loss; black-market reasoning
- **Taxes**: who bears the burden? Tax incidence based on relative elasticities; deadweight loss
- **Subsidies**: mirror analysis of taxes
- **Questions**: predict equilibrium changes from a shifter; identify CS, PS, DWL on a graph; compute tax incidence and DWL from given elasticities; reason about price ceiling outcomes

## Module 3: Elasticity and Consumer / Producer Choice (parts of Units 1–2, ~10%)
- **Price elasticity of demand**: midpoint formula; elastic, unit-elastic, inelastic, perfectly elastic, perfectly inelastic
- Determinants of PED: substitutes available, necessity vs luxury, share of income, time horizon, definition of the market
- Total revenue test: TR rises with price when demand is inelastic; TR falls when elastic
- **Income elasticity** (normal vs inferior, luxury threshold) and **cross-price elasticity** (substitutes positive, complements negative)
- **Price elasticity of supply** and its determinants (time, capacity)
- **Utility theory**: total vs marginal utility; diminishing marginal utility
- **Consumer choice**: utility maximization rule MU_x/P_x = MU_y/P_y; budget constraint; income and substitution effects
- (Indifference curves and budget lines are sometimes here, sometimes deferred — useful to include.)
- **Questions**: classify elasticity from numerical PED; predict TR change from a price change; apply the equimarginal principle to a two-good problem

## Module 4: Production, Cost, and Perfect Competition (Unit 3, 22–25%)
- The firm's objective: profit maximization (π = TR − TC); accounting profit vs **economic profit** (includes opportunity cost of owners' resources)
- **Production function** in the short run: total product, marginal product, average product; the law of diminishing marginal returns
- **Costs in the short run**: fixed (FC), variable (VC), total (TC); ATC = TC/Q, AVC = VC/Q, AFC = FC/Q, MC = ΔTC/ΔQ
- Geometric facts: MC cuts ATC and AVC at their minima; ATC is U-shaped from spreading fixed costs and diminishing returns
- **Long-run costs**: long-run average total cost (LRATC), economies of scale, constant returns, diseconomies of scale; minimum efficient scale
- **Perfect competition**: many firms, identical products, free entry/exit, price takers; firm faces a horizontal demand at market price
- Short-run profit maximization for a competitive firm: produce where P = MC (provided P ≥ AVC)
- Shutdown rule: shut down in the short run if P < AVC; exit in the long run if P < ATC
- Long-run equilibrium: entry/exit drives economic profit to zero; firms produce at minimum LRATC; allocative (P = MC) and productive (min ATC) efficiency
- **Questions**: derive MC, ATC, AVC from a TC table; identify profit/loss/shutdown regions on a graph; analyze long-run adjustment after a demand shift

## Module 5: Imperfect Competition (Unit 4, 15–22%)
- **Monopoly**: single seller, barriers to entry, price maker; demand curve is the market demand
- MR < P for a monopoly because of the price-reduction effect; MR curve has twice the slope of a linear demand
- Monopoly profit maximization: MR = MC, then read price off demand; graphing profit/loss
- Deadweight loss of monopoly; comparison to perfect competition
- **Price discrimination**: 1st, 2nd, 3rd degree; conditions for it (market power, ability to identify groups, no resale); effect on CS, PS, DWL
- Natural monopoly and regulation: marginal-cost vs average-cost pricing
- **Monopolistic competition**: many firms, differentiated products, free entry; long-run zero profit but excess capacity (P > MC, P = ATC at non-minimum scale)
- Advertising and product differentiation as core features
- **Oligopoly**: few firms, mutual interdependence; concentration ratios
- **Game theory**: simultaneous-move games, payoff matrices, dominant strategies, **Nash equilibrium**; the Prisoners' Dilemma; collusion vs competition
- Sequential games and credible threats (intuition); cartels and OPEC-style instability
- **Questions**: identify market structure from features; compute monopoly price/quantity/profit and DWL; find Nash equilibria in 2×2 payoff matrices; reason about advertising and product differentiation

## Module 6: Factor Markets (Unit 5, 10–13%)
- The firm's input decision mirrors the output decision: hire where marginal revenue product (MRP) = marginal factor cost (MFC)
- **Labour market** in perfect competition: market wage and quantity; firm faces a horizontal supply at the wage
- Labour demand as **derived demand** from the product market; shifts in labour demand from product price, productivity, prices of other inputs
- Labour supply: substitution vs income effects; backward-bending labour supply at high wages
- **Monopsony**: single buyer of labour; MFC > wage; underemployment and lower wage than competitive market
- Minimum wage in a monopsony vs competitive context — the surprising result that a binding minimum can raise both wage *and* employment under monopsony
- Capital and land markets — present value, rental rate, equilibrium
- Income distribution: Lorenz curve, Gini coefficient; sources of income inequality (intuitive)
- **Questions**: derive MRP from a production table; find the firm's optimal hiring; analyze the impact of a minimum wage in competitive vs monopsonistic labour markets

## Module 7: Market Failure and the Role of Government (Unit 6, 8–13%)
- **Externalities**: negative (pollution) and positive (vaccination, education); marginal social cost vs marginal private cost; deadweight loss from externalities
- Internalising externalities: Pigouvian taxes, subsidies, tradable permits, command-and-control regulation
- **Coase theorem**: private bargaining can solve externalities if property rights are clear and transaction costs are low; why this often fails in practice
- **Public goods**: nonrival and nonexcludable; the free-rider problem; under-provision by markets; government provision
- Common-pool resources and the **tragedy of the commons**
- **Asymmetric information**: adverse selection (the market for lemons), moral hazard, signalling
- **Income inequality** revisited: poverty lines, taxation (progressive, proportional, regressive), redistribution programs; the equity–efficiency tradeoff
- **Questions**: graph and compute DWL from a negative externality; design an appropriate Pigouvian tax; identify whether a good is rival/excludable; reason about adverse selection in insurance markets

## Stretch Zone (push beyond intro micro)
- **Behavioural economics**: bounded rationality, prospect theory, framing, anchoring, hyperbolic discounting; Kahneman & Tversky, Thaler; nudges and choice architecture
- **Mechanism design**: how to design auctions, matching markets (Gale-Shapley for medical residents, organ exchanges)
- **Game theory** beyond intro: mixed strategies, repeated games, the folk theorem, evolutionary stable strategies, signalling games (Spence), screening
- **General equilibrium** vs partial equilibrium; the two welfare theorems and what they assume; Edgeworth box and Pareto efficiency
- **Information economics**: principal–agent problems, contract theory, efficiency wages
- **Industrial organization**: predatory pricing, vertical integration, antitrust policy, network effects and platform economics
- **Behavioural pricing**: dynamic pricing, surge pricing, two-part tariffs, bundling, versioning
- **Empirical micro**: natural experiments, regression discontinuity, difference-in-differences, randomised controlled trials in development economics (Banerjee, Duflo)
- **Public choice**: government failure as the mirror of market failure; rent-seeking, regulatory capture, voting paradoxes (Arrow's impossibility theorem)
- Common student traps:
  - Confusing movement *along* a curve with a *shift* of the curve
  - Treating accounting profit as economic profit
  - Forgetting that producers don't maximize revenue; they maximize profit
  - Drawing MC and ATC inconsistent with each other (MC must cross ATC at its minimum)
  - In monopoly, drawing MR not steeper than D for a linear demand
- The political economy of policy: even Pareto-improving policies create losers who organize harder than diffuse winners (Olson)
