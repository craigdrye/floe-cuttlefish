# Philosophy of Science
**ID**: `philSophomore` - **Discipline**: Humanities - **Year**: University Sophomore

## Course Aim
Science is the most reliable knowledge-producing enterprise humans have built, yet almost nothing about *why* it works is obvious. Observations never logically force a single theory; experiments confirm hypotheses only against a backdrop of untested assumptions; "the data" rarely speaks for itself. This course asks the questions a working scientist usually has no time to ask: What separates science from imitations of it? When does evidence actually support a theory rather than merely accompany it? What is a scientific explanation, and is prediction enough? Why does scientific consensus shift, sometimes violently, and does that shifting threaten the claim that science tracks truth?

The course is built around a small number of deep problems — Hume's problem of induction, Popper's demarcation criterion, the Duhem-Quine thesis, the realism debate, Kuhnian revolutions — and around the cases that gave those problems their force: the eclipse test of general relativity, the Michelson-Morley experiment, continental drift, the discovery of Neptune versus the failed search for Vulcan, the replication crisis in psychology. Students learn to reconstruct an argument charitably, locate its weakest premise, and judge it against the historical record. The emphasis is on reasoning that transfers: a student who can tell a risky prediction from a safe one, or a genuine confirmation from a circular one, has a tool they will use in any quantitative field.

By the end, students should be able to do philosophy of science rather than recite positions in it — to take a contemporary controversy (a contested clinical trial, a disputed climate model, a "is it a science?" debate) and analyze it with the conceptual machinery of confirmation theory, explanation, theory change, and values in science. The course treats philosophy and scientific literacy as a single competence: you cannot evaluate the philosophy without understanding the science, and you cannot fully understand the science without seeing its philosophical commitments.

## Course Design Notes
Route questions here when they concern the logic and epistemology of science rather than the content of any one science: demarcation, induction, falsification, confirmation and Bayesian evidence, scientific explanation and laws, causation, theory change (Kuhn, Lakatos, Feyerabend), realism versus anti-realism, models and idealization, underdetermination, values and objectivity, the replication crisis, and the philosophy of special sciences (biology, physics, psychology, social science). Questions purely about formal logic, general epistemology (Gettier, skepticism), or ethics belong to the dedicated philosophy tracks; questions about the *technical* content of a science (how to balance a redox equation, what a p-value computes mathematically) belong to that science's track. The dividing line: this course asks whether and why a method or inference is *good science*, not how to execute it.

Keep every item anchored to a concrete case or a sharply stated argument. Avoid "name the philosopher" trivia in isolation — pair a thinker with the move they made and a case that tests it. Distractors should encode the standard confusions: verification mistaken for falsification, Kuhn conflated with Feyerabend, correlation read as the deductive-nomological model, "theory" as casual guess versus theory as a structured explanatory framework, a posterior confused with a likelihood. Difficulty should rise from recognizing positions, to applying them to fresh cases, to spotting where a famous argument actually fails.

## Chapter 1: What Makes Something Science? Demarcation and Falsifiability
**Core questions**: Is there a sharp line between science and pseudoscience, or only a family of better and worse practices? Does Popper's falsifiability criterion succeed, and what does it rule out that we want kept?
**Key concepts**: Demarcation problem; logical asymmetry of confirmation and falsification; Popper's risky prediction and bold conjecture; falsifiability vs. falsification; the 1919 eclipse test as a paradigm of a risky prediction; ad hoc auxiliary hypotheses; immunizing stratagems; Popper's critique of Marxism and Freudian psychoanalysis as unfalsifiable.
**Applied skills**: Reconstruct Popper's argument from the asymmetry between universal and existential statements; classify a claim as falsifiable or not and say what observation would refute it; identify an ad hoc rescue and distinguish it from a legitimate auxiliary revision.
**Common traps**: Thinking falsifiable means false, or that a falsifiable theory must be abandoned at the first anomaly; treating verification and falsification as symmetric; assuming Popper says we should never modify a theory (he objects only to *ad hoc* modification); calling astrology unscientific because it is *false* rather than because it makes no risky predictions.

## Chapter 2: The Problem of Induction
**Core questions**: Can past regularities rationally justify beliefs about the future or about unobserved cases? If induction cannot be justified deductively or non-circularly inductively, why is science not thereby irrational?
**Key concepts**: Hume's problem; the uniformity of nature as an unprovable premise; the distinction between deductive validity and inductive strength; enumerative induction; the circularity of justifying induction by its track record; Goodman's "new riddle" and the predicate *grue*; Popper's deductivist attempt to dissolve the problem; pragmatic and reliabilist responses.
**Applied skills**: State Hume's dilemma (demonstrative vs. probable reasoning) precisely; explain why "induction has worked before" begs the question; show how *grue* matches all evidence for green yet predicts differently.
**Common traps**: Believing Hume denied that we *do* reason inductively (he denied only that we can *justify* it); thinking adding more observations solves the logical gap; conflating the problem of induction with ordinary measurement error; treating Goodman's riddle as the same problem as Hume's rather than a problem about which generalizations are projectible.

## Chapter 3: Evidence, Confirmation, and Bayesian Reasoning
**Core questions**: When does evidence confirm a hypothesis rather than merely fit it? How should a rational agent update beliefs, and why does surprising evidence count for more?
**Key concepts**: Confirmation vs. mere consistency; hypothetico-deductive method and its problems; Bayes' theorem (posterior proportional to prior times likelihood); prior, likelihood, posterior, base rate; the base-rate fallacy; the role of P(E) and why unexpected evidence (low prior probability) is more confirming; Hempel's raven paradox; the value of severe tests and novel prediction over accommodation.
**Applied skills**: Compute a posterior from a prior and likelihoods (e.g., a diagnostic test with a low base rate); explain via Bayes why a successful risky prediction confirms more than an expected result; diagnose a base-rate neglect in a medical or forensic claim.
**Common traps**: Reading the likelihood P(E|H) as the posterior P(H|E) — the prosecutor's fallacy; ignoring the base rate so a "99% accurate" test seems to make a positive result decisive; thinking any true prediction confirms equally regardless of how surprising it was; confusing statistical significance (a p-value) with the probability the hypothesis is true.

## Chapter 4: Scientific Explanation, Laws, and Causation
**Core questions**: What turns a description into an explanation? Is showing that something *had* to happen (given laws and conditions) the same as explaining *why* it happened?
**Key concepts**: Hempel's deductive-nomological (covering-law) model; explanation as argument; the symmetry/asymmetry problem (flagpole and shadow); the problem of irrelevance; statistical-relevance and inductive-statistical models; causal and mechanistic explanation; laws of nature vs. accidental generalizations; counterfactual and interventionist accounts of causation; unificationist explanation.
**Applied skills**: Apply the D-N model to a case and expose its asymmetry failure (the shadow's length and the pole's height explain each other deductively, but only one direction is explanatory); distinguish a law from an accidentally true generalization; tell a mechanistic explanation from a merely law-subsuming one.
**Common traps**: Equating explanation with prediction (the D-N model conflates them, which is the source of its problems); assuming correlation plus a covering law equals causation; treating every true universal statement as a law of nature; thinking "it follows from the laws" answers a why-question even when the derivation runs backward.

## Chapter 5: Theory Change — Kuhn, Lakatos, and Feyerabend
**Core questions**: Does science grow by steady accumulation, or by ruptures that redefine the questions? Is theory change rational, or partly a matter of conversion and persuasion?
**Key concepts**: Kuhn's normal science, puzzle-solving, anomaly, crisis, paradigm, and scientific revolution; incommensurability and theory-ladenness of observation; Lakatos's research programmes with a hard core and protective belt, progressive vs. degenerating problemshifts; Feyerabend's epistemological anarchism and "anything goes"; the Copernican and chemical revolutions as cases.
**Applied skills**: Map a historical episode onto Kuhn's cycle and identify the anomaly that precipitated crisis; use Lakatos to judge whether a modification is progressive (predicts novel facts) or degenerating (only saves appearances); state what incommensurability does and does not claim.
**Common traps**: Reading incommensurability as "scientists from different paradigms cannot communicate at all" (it is about non-identical standards and meanings, not total mutual silence); conflating Kuhn's descriptive account with Feyerabend's normative anarchism; thinking Lakatos's "ad hoc rescue" verdict can be made on a single move rather than over a programme's trajectory; treating "paradigm shift" as any minor change.

## Chapter 6: Realism, Anti-Realism, Models, and Idealization
**Core questions**: Does the success of science give us reason to believe its theories are (approximately) true, including about unobservable entities? Or is empirical adequacy all we can or should want?
**Key concepts**: Scientific realism; the no-miracles argument (success would be a miracle if theories were not approximately true); the pessimistic meta-induction (past successful theories were false, e.g., phlogiston, caloric, the ether); van Fraassen's constructive empiricism and empirical adequacy; instrumentalism; observable vs. unobservable; structural realism as a middle position; the role of idealized models (frictionless planes, point masses, the ideal gas) and how false models can still be explanatory.
**Applied skills**: State and weigh the no-miracles argument against the pessimistic meta-induction; apply constructive empiricism to an unobservable posit; explain how an idealization that is literally false can yield understanding.
**Common traps**: Thinking realism requires believing current theories are *exactly* true rather than approximately so; treating "it's just a model" as automatically anti-realist; assuming empirical adequacy and truth are the same thing (van Fraassen's whole point is that they come apart); confusing instrumentalism with the claim that theories are meaningless.

## Chapter 7: Underdetermination, Values, and Objectivity
**Core questions**: If evidence never uniquely fixes a theory, what does? Can science be value-free, and should it be?
**Key concepts**: The Duhem-Quine thesis (hypotheses face the tribunal of experience only as a bundle, so a failed test never tells you *which* assumption to blame); underdetermination of theory by data; the value-free ideal and its critics; the distinction between epistemic values (predictive accuracy, scope, simplicity) and contextual/social values; Rudner's argument from inductive risk; standpoint and feminist philosophy of science; objectivity as a social-procedural achievement rather than a view from nowhere.
**Applied skills**: Use Duhem-Quine to show why a single anomalous result cannot logically refute a target hypothesis in isolation; identify where a value judgment legitimately enters (e.g., setting an acceptable error rate under inductive risk) versus where it corrupts inference; distinguish epistemic from non-epistemic values in a real study.
**Common traps**: Reading underdetermination as "any theory is as good as any other" (epistemic values still discriminate); thinking the presence of values automatically means bias; treating the value-free ideal as obviously correct or obviously refuted rather than as a contested ideal; confusing the inductive-risk argument (about accepting/rejecting) with a claim that data are fabricated.

## Chapter 8: Replication, Trust, and the Social Structure of Science
**Core questions**: Why did science develop peer review, replication, and pre-registration, and what happens when those mechanisms fail? When the data underdetermine the conclusion, how should a public reason about scientific consensus?
**Key concepts**: The replication crisis (notably in psychology and biomedicine); questionable research practices, p-hacking, HARKing, publication bias, and the "file drawer" problem; the difference between a p-value and a replication; pre-registration and registered reports; reproducibility vs. replicability; consensus formation and the epistemic role of disagreement; manufactured doubt and denialism; calibrated communication of uncertainty.
**Applied skills**: Explain why a string of significant results can still indicate p-hacking rather than a real effect; distinguish failure to replicate from fraud and from a true but small effect; assess when deferring to scientific consensus is rational and when it is not; critique a popular science article for overstated certainty or buried uncertainty.
**Common traps**: Treating one failed replication as proof the original was fraudulent (or one success as vindication); thinking a low p-value means the result will replicate; assuming consensus is mere conformity rather than a hard-won convergence of independent evidence; confusing legitimate scientific uncertainty with manufactured doubt designed to stall policy.

## Capstone
Students select one contemporary or historical scientific controversy — a contested clinical trial, a disputed climate or economic model, a "is X a science?" debate, a celebrated experiment, or a major theory change — and produce a rigorous case study that integrates four layers: (1) the *evidence* and how strongly it actually supports the rival hypotheses, analyzed with confirmation-theoretic or Bayesian tools; (2) the *method*, including which auxiliary assumptions are doing work and whether any tests were genuinely severe; (3) the *philosophical framework* that best illuminates the episode (demarcation, theory change, realism, underdetermination, values in science); and (4) the *social context* — peer review, consensus, communication, and trust. The deliverable is a reasoned verdict, not a survey: students must take a defensible position on what the case shows about how science works and defend it against the strongest objection.

## Assessment Ideas
Short argument reconstructions (state the premises and the weakest one); historical case notes mapped to a framework; Bayesian and base-rate exercises with worked posteriors; a falsifiability/demarcation case brief; a critique of a popular science article for its handling of uncertainty and values; seminar debates (realism vs. constructive empiricism; was a given episode a Kuhnian revolution?); and the final integrative case study. Grade philosophical precision and scientific literacy together — a technically correct argument resting on a misread of the science fails, and vice versa.
