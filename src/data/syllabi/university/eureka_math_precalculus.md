# Eureka Math Precalculus
**ID**: `eurekaMathPrecalculus` - **Discipline**: Maths - **Year**: University Sophomore (equivalent)

## Course Aim
Prepare students for calculus and mathematically serious modeling by making functions, transformations, trigonometry, complex numbers, vectors, matrices, and limits feel like one connected language. Students should leave able to move among symbolic, graphical, numerical, geometric, and computational representations, justify claims, and build models whose assumptions are visible.

## Course Design Notes
This course adapts the conceptual arc of Eureka Math Precalculus for advanced secondary, early university, or bridge-to-calculus settings. Route questions here when students need rigorous precalculus reasoning, function families, transformations, trigonometric modeling, complex numbers, vectors, matrices, polar or parametric forms, and a first encounter with limits. Use graphing technology, spreadsheets, CAS, or coding notebooks for exploration and model checking, but keep proof, exact reasoning, and interpretation central.

## Chapter 1: Functions, Transformations, and Mathematical Models
- **Key concepts**: Function notation, domain and range, composition, inverse functions, transformations, rate of change, families of functions, model assumptions, parameters, residuals, and multiple representations.
- **Practice questions**: Describe how parameters change a graph; compose two functions and interpret the result; decide whether an inverse is a function; critique a model outside its domain.
- **Student output**: A function family portfolio showing symbolic rules, graphs, transformations, parameter meanings, and one contextual model with limitations.

## Chapter 2: Polynomial and Rational Functions
- **Key concepts**: Polynomial structure, end behavior, zeros, multiplicity, factor and remainder theorems, complex roots, rational functions, holes, vertical and horizontal asymptotes, oblique asymptotes, sign charts, and graph construction.
- **Practice questions**: Build a polynomial from root and end-behavior conditions; use division to locate an oblique asymptote; explain the difference between a removable discontinuity and an asymptote.
- **Student output**: A graphing and proof brief for a polynomial or rational function, including algebraic evidence, technology checks, and a written interpretation.

## Chapter 3: Exponential, Logarithmic, and Logistic Models
- **Key concepts**: Exponential growth and decay, logarithms as inverses, log laws, change of base, half-life, doubling time, compound interest, linearization, logistic growth, carrying capacity, and parameter estimation.
- **Practice questions**: Solve exponential and logarithmic equations; estimate growth parameters from data; compare exponential and logistic models for the same situation.
- **Student output**: A modeling memo that fits and critiques an exponential, logarithmic, or logistic model using data and a graphing or spreadsheet tool.

## Chapter 4: Trigonometry on the Unit Circle
- **Key concepts**: Radian measure, unit circle definitions, sine, cosine, tangent, reciprocal functions, reference angles, exact values, periodicity, inverse trigonometric functions, identities, and trigonometric equations.
- **Practice questions**: Convert between degrees and radians; solve a trigonometric equation on a stated interval; prove an identity using unit circle or algebraic reasoning.
- **Student output**: A trigonometry reasoning notebook with exact-value work, identity proofs, equation solving, and a reflection on domain restrictions.

## Chapter 5: Periodic Functions and Harmonic Models
- **Key concepts**: Amplitude, period, frequency, phase shift, vertical shift, sinusoidal regression, circular motion, tides, sound, seasonal data, residual patterns, and competing periodic models.
- **Practice questions**: Write a sinusoidal model from a graph or table; interpret phase shift in context; decide whether a periodic model is appropriate for noisy data.
- **Student output**: A periodic-model report using real or simulated data, with parameter interpretation, graph, residual check, and a statement of uncertainty.

## Chapter 6: Complex Numbers and the Complex Plane
- **Key concepts**: Complex arithmetic, conjugates, modulus, argument, polar form, multiplication as rotation and dilation, powers and roots, De Moivre's theorem, Euler connections at an introductory level, and geometric proof.
- **Practice questions**: Explain geometrically why multiplying by i rotates a point; convert between rectangular and polar form; find and plot roots of a complex number.
- **Student output**: A complex-plane visual proof set showing arithmetic, rotations, powers, and roots with exact work and a technology-generated diagram.

## Chapter 7: Vectors, Matrices, and Linear Transformations
- **Key concepts**: Vectors in two and three dimensions, magnitude, direction, components, dot product, cross product, projections, matrices, determinants, inverses, systems of equations, transformations, composition, and matrix multiplication.
- **Practice questions**: Use a dot product to find an angle; use a cross product to find area or a normal vector; build a matrix for a rotation, reflection, dilation, or shear.
- **Student output**: A transformation lab that represents geometric actions with vectors and matrices, verifies them computationally, and explains determinant meaning.

## Chapter 8: Parametric, Polar, Conic, and Limit Ideas
- **Key concepts**: Parametric equations, projectile and motion models, polar coordinates, polar graphs, conic sections, focus-directrix definitions, eccentricity, numerical limits, graphical limits, algebraic limit techniques, continuity, and average versus instantaneous rate of change.
- **Practice questions**: Convert a simple parametric curve to rectangular form; graph and interpret a polar equation; identify conic parameters; evaluate a limit using a table, graph, or algebra.
- **Student output**: A pre-calculus-to-calculus bridge project that models motion or geometry in parametric or polar form and introduces a limit-based question.

## Capstone
Students complete a connected modeling and proof project using at least three major course strands. A strong project might model tides with trigonometry, represent rotations with complex numbers and matrices, or analyze motion with parametric equations and limits. The final submission must include exact mathematics, a technology artifact, a proof or derivation, a model critique, and a short presentation for a mathematically literate audience.

## Assessment Ideas
- Concept checks that require students to translate among graphs, formulas, tables, and verbal descriptions.
- Proof and justification prompts for identities, inverse restrictions, root behavior, and transformation claims.
- Technology labs graded on interpretation, not just screenshots or commands.
- Modeling memos that include assumptions, parameters, residuals, and limits of use.
- Cumulative oral or written defenses connecting precalculus ideas to calculus readiness.
