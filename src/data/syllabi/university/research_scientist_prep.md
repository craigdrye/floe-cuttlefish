# Research Scientist Interview Prep
**ID**: `research` - **Discipline**: Research - **Year**: University Senior / Career Hopper

## Course Aim
This course prepares students to move from university-level research training into research scientist interviews and early-career research roles. Students practice reading literature, forming defensible questions, designing studies, managing data and lab workflows, applying statistics, communicating results, and navigating the ethics and collaboration norms of modern research organizations.

The course is practical and project-based. Each chapter asks students to produce interview-ready artifacts: paper critiques, study plans, analysis notebooks, reproducibility checklists, presentation decks, grant-style aims, and collaboration memos.

## Course Design Notes
- Treat research skill as a workflow: question, evidence, method, data, analysis, communication, and revision.
- Use examples from the student's target domain, such as AI, biomedical science, social science, climate, robotics, quantitative research, or human-computer interaction.
- Balance rigor with career readiness: every technical activity should produce a portfolio artifact or interview story.
- Require students to explain assumptions, uncertainty, limitations, and ethical risks in every major deliverable.
- Include structured peer review so students practice giving and receiving technical criticism without treating critique as personal judgment.

## Chapter 1: Research Identity and Career Translation

### Key concepts
- Mapping academic experience to research scientist competencies
- Research taste: choosing questions that are important, tractable, and original
- Reading job descriptions for signals about domain depth, methods, tooling, and collaboration style
- Turning coursework, thesis work, lab work, internships, and open-source projects into evidence
- Building a research narrative for resumes, interviews, and job talks

### Practice questions
- What kinds of problems do you consistently notice before others do?
- Which project best shows your ability to handle ambiguity, and what did you decide when the path was unclear?
- How would you describe your research agenda to a principal investigator, an engineering manager, and a product leader?
- What evidence proves that you can work beyond assigned tasks?

### Student output
- Research scientist competency map
- One-page career narrative
- Resume bullet revision set focused on evidence, methods, and impact

## Chapter 2: Literature Review and Scholarly Judgment

### Key concepts
- Efficient paper reading: abstract, figures, methods, claims, limitations, and references
- Distinguishing contribution, novelty, evidence quality, and scope of inference
- Building literature maps by method, dataset, theory, result, and open question
- Identifying assumptions, confounds, missing baselines, and overclaimed conclusions
- Running a paper club discussion with a clear thesis and a productive critique

### Practice questions
- What is the strongest claim in this paper, and what evidence supports it?
- Which result would most change your mind if it failed to replicate?
- What baseline, dataset, control, or measurement would make the paper more convincing?
- What would you test next if you had one month and limited compute, samples, or field access?

### Student output
- Annotated bibliography of 12 to 15 sources
- Two-page paper critique
- Literature map showing methods, findings, gaps, and candidate research questions

## Chapter 3: Questions, Hypotheses, and Experimental Design

### Key concepts
- Moving from broad interests to specific, testable research questions
- Hypotheses, predictions, operational definitions, and measurable outcomes
- Experimental, quasi-experimental, observational, simulation, and qualitative study designs
- Controls, randomization, blocking, blinding, counterbalancing, and threat modeling
- Causal diagrams, confounding, colliders, mediators, and selection bias

### Practice questions
- What would count as evidence against your favorite hypothesis?
- Which variables must be controlled, measured, randomized, or explicitly left uncontrolled?
- How would you design a study when randomized assignment is impossible?
- What failure mode would make your result uninterpretable?

### Student output
- Study design brief with research question, hypothesis, design, measurements, and risks
- Causal diagram or design logic diagram
- Pre-analysis plan outline

## Chapter 4: Lab, Data, and Reproducibility Practices

### Key concepts
- Lab notebooks, data dictionaries, sample tracking, code repositories, and decision logs
- Version control for analysis code, protocols, figures, and manuscripts
- Data collection protocols, quality checks, missingness, exclusions, and provenance
- Reproducible computational environments and dependency management
- Secure, ethical, and compliant handling of sensitive data and research materials

### Practice questions
- Could another researcher reproduce your result from your notes, data, and code?
- What data checks should run before analysis begins?
- How will you record exclusions, changed protocols, failed runs, or unexpected observations?
- What access controls and retention rules apply to your dataset?

### Student output
- Reproducibility checklist
- Data management plan
- Repository or lab notebook template for a research project

## Chapter 5: Statistics, Modeling, and Inference

### Key concepts
- Descriptive statistics, uncertainty, effect sizes, confidence intervals, and power
- Regression, generalized linear models, mixed-effects models, and hierarchical structure
- Bayesian reasoning, priors, posterior inference, and model checking
- Multiple comparisons, missing data, robustness checks, and sensitivity analysis
- Simulation, bootstrapping, cross-validation, and error analysis

### Practice questions
- What is the estimand, and what assumptions connect it to the data?
- When would a mixed-effects model be better than a simple regression?
- How would you explain a statistically significant but practically tiny result?
- Which robustness check would most strengthen confidence in the finding?

### Student output
- Analysis notebook with simulated or real data
- Model choice memo
- Figure set with captions that state uncertainty and limitations

## Chapter 6: Ethics, Responsible Conduct, and Research Risk

### Key concepts
- Responsible conduct of research, including authorship, data integrity, peer review, and conflicts of interest
- Human subjects, animal research, biosafety, privacy, security, dual use, and domain-specific compliance
- Reproducibility, selective reporting, p-hacking, HARKing, and publication bias
- Equity, fairness, representation, and harm analysis in datasets and methods
- Escalation paths for questionable research practices or unsafe project direction

### Practice questions
- Who could be harmed if this research is wrong, misused, or overgeneralized?
- What would responsible authorship look like for this collaboration?
- Which research decisions should be documented before seeing the results?
- How would you respond if a supervisor asked you to omit a negative result?

### Student output
- Research ethics risk memo
- Authorship and contribution plan
- Responsible conduct scenario response

## Chapter 7: Communication, Papers, Grants, and Presentations

### Key concepts
- Scientific storytelling: problem, gap, method, evidence, contribution, and limitation
- Abstracts, introductions, methods sections, results, discussions, and figure captions
- Grant-style specific aims, significance, innovation, approach, and feasibility
- Job talks, chalk talks, poster sessions, and technical Q&A
- Writing for mixed audiences without flattening the science

### Practice questions
- What is the one-sentence contribution of your project?
- Which figure should a reader understand first, and why?
- What question are reviewers most likely to challenge?
- How would you revise your talk for a hiring committee outside your subfield?

### Student output
- Specific aims page or short proposal
- Research presentation deck outline
- Revised abstract and one publication-style figure caption

## Chapter 8: Collaboration, Interviewing, and Research Execution

### Key concepts
- Working across labs, disciplines, product teams, engineering teams, clinical teams, or field partners
- Project scoping, milestones, risk registers, and meeting notes
- Communicating uncertainty, disagreement, and failed experiments
- Technical interview formats: paper critique, research deep dive, coding or analysis exercise, methods discussion, and job talk
- Building interview stories around judgment, iteration, collaboration, and impact

### Practice questions
- Tell me about a time an experiment failed and how you changed direction.
- How do you decide whether to keep improving a method or move to a new question?
- What do you need from collaborators before analysis begins?
- How would you defend a controversial methodological choice in a hiring seminar?

### Student output
- Collaboration charter for a research project
- Interview story bank with evidence and reflection
- Mock job talk Q&A preparation sheet

## Capstone
Students complete a research scientist readiness portfolio built around one domain-relevant project. The portfolio includes a literature map, study design brief, reproducibility plan, ethics memo, analysis notebook or methods prototype, figure set, short proposal, and 10-minute research talk.

The capstone defense asks students to explain what they studied, why it matters, what evidence they trust, what remains uncertain, how the work could fail, and what they would do next with more time or resources.

## Assessment Ideas
- Literature critique scored for claim identification, evidence evaluation, limitation analysis, and next-step reasoning
- Study design brief scored for hypothesis clarity, measurement quality, controls, feasibility, and risk awareness
- Data and reproducibility package scored for traceability, documentation, code clarity, and integrity checks
- Statistical analysis scored for model fit, assumptions, uncertainty communication, and robustness
- Ethics memo scored for stakeholder analysis, responsible conduct, and realistic mitigation
- Research talk scored for structure, technical accuracy, figures, time discipline, and Q&A handling
- Peer review scored for specificity, fairness, and usefulness

## Research Notes
- NIH and NSF responsible conduct of research materials are useful anchors for research integrity, authorship, data management, peer review, and mentorship expectations.
- Nature and Science author guidance can frame strong scientific writing, figure design, transparent methods, and revision discipline.
- Domain-specific methods texts should be selected by cohort focus, with students comparing norms across fields rather than assuming one universal research template.
- Encourage students to study real lab websites, industry research blogs, conference papers, grant abstracts, and job talks to understand how research careers are communicated in public.
