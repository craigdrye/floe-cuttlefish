# Research Scientist Interview Prep
**ID**: `research` · **Discipline**: Research · **Year**: University Senior / Career Hopper

## Course Philosophy
This syllabus prepares candidates for Research Scientist roles in industry (e.g., AI labs, biotech, quantitative finance). It emphasizes the ability to read complex literature, design novel experiments, reason about causality, and communicate highly technical findings.

## Module 1: Literature Review and Technical Comprehension (20%)
- Reading dense papers efficiently: Abstract $\to$ Figures/Tables $\to$ Conclusion $\to$ Methods
- Identifying the core contribution, limitations, and unstated assumptions of a paper
- Deconstructing state-of-the-art (SOTA) models or methodologies
- "Paper Club" simulations: Presenting a paper to an audience of peers
- **Questions**: Critiquing the methodology of a seminal paper in a specific field, identifying potential confounding variables missed by the authors

## Module 2: Experimental Design and Causal Inference (25%)
- Formulating testable hypotheses for open-ended problems
- Designing robust experiments (RCTs, A/B testing variations)
- Causal Inference techniques for observational data: Propensity score matching, Instrumental Variables (IV), Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD), Difference-in-Differences (DiD)
- DAGs (Directed Acyclic Graphs) and identifying colliders/confounders
- **Questions**: Designing an experiment to isolate the causal effect of a new feature when A/B testing is impossible, drawing a DAG to identify sources of bias

## Module 3: Advanced Statistics and Math Foundations (20%)
- Probability theory and Bayesian inference (priors, posteriors, MCMC methods)
- Advanced regression techniques (Mixed-effects models, Generalized Linear Models)
- Optimization theory (Gradient descent, convexity, constrained optimization)
- Handling missing data (Imputation techniques, Missing Completely at Random vs Missing Not at Random)
- **Questions**: Deriving the update rule for a specific optimization algorithm, choosing between a mixed-effects model and a simple linear regression for hierarchical data

## Module 4: Applied Domain Knowledge (e.g., ML, Bio, Quant) (20%)
- Translating theoretical concepts into applied industrial solutions
- Algorithmic innovation: Modifying existing architectures for specific constraints (e.g., low latency, memory constraints)
- Coding for Researchers: Prototyping in Python/R, using Git, writing reproducible code
- **Questions**: Proposing a modification to a standard Transformer architecture to handle infinitely long context windows, writing a quick script to simulate a stochastic process

## Module 5: Technical Communication and "The Research Presentation" (15%)
- The Job Talk: Structuring a 45-minute presentation of original research
- Storytelling with data: Visualizing complex results effectively
- Handling the Q&A: Defending methodological choices without becoming defensive
- Behavioral questions for researchers (e.g., "Tell me about a time an experiment failed and how you pivoted.")
- **Questions**: Distilling a complex mathematical proof into a 2-minute elevator pitch for a non-technical product manager, fielding aggressive Q&A questions during a mock presentation

## Stretch Zone
- Grant writing and securing funding/compute resources
- Navigating the patent process vs open-source publication
